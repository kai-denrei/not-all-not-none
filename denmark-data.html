Denmark — the honest-data exception / デンマーク・誠実な統計

Most Western countries argue about crime, immigration, and integration without ever collecting the data that would settle the empirical share of the argument. Denmark collects it, publishes it on a public StatBank with permanent table codes, computes age- and socio-economic-standardised indices, and lets the figures stand. This is what honest measurement looks like — and what its absence elsewhere means for the discourse everywhere else.

empirical case · Phase 12026-05-11
If a society publishes the ancestry × crime cross-tab, it can be challenged on what the cross-tab shows. If it refuses to publish, it can only be challenged on the refusal — and the discourse around the empirical question gets handed, by default, to whoever is willing to assert numbers without having to produce them. Denmark publishes. That is the methodological backbone of this page.

Why this page exists

A six-country snapshot of who publishes the breakdown most-asked-about in the integration debate, and who does not.

Denmark · DK

Publishes ancestry × crime with permanent table codes

Statistics Denmark (Danmarks Statistik) maintains STRAFNA3–9 and STRFNA10–14 — twelve linked public tables crossing convicted persons against ancestry, country of origin, citizenship, sex, age, region, type of offence, and type of decision. The 2025 figures were released on 8 April 2026 — about three months after year-end, down from eleven months in prior cycles. An age- and socio-economic-standardised criminality index is also computed officially.

Statistics Denmark · Convicted persons subject page · table inventory current as of 2026-04-08

France · FR

Constitutional ban on ethnic statistics

Article 1 of the 1958 Constitution and the 1978 Loi Informatique et Libertés (revised 2004) prohibit the collection of statistics that distinguish residents by race or ethnicity. INSEE may use "country of birth" and "nationality of origin" only as proxies, and only with Conseil constitutionnel review. No public ancestry × crime cross-tab exists in France — the legal frame is built precisely to prevent one.

CNIL · 1978 statute as amended; Conseil constitutionnel decision 2007-557 DC (15 November 2007) on the prohibition of ethnic statistics

United States · US

Race-and-ethnicity collected, but the cross-tab is fragmented

The FBI's UCR/NIBRS publishes race-coded arrest data; the BJS computes incarceration rates by race; and the Census provides denominators. But the data are reported per-agency on voluntary submission, ancestry below the race level is not standardised, and the foreign-born vs. native-born distinction inside Hispanic/Latino is not publicly resolved at sufficient granularity for replication. The data exist; the integrated public cross-tab does not.

FBI Crime Data Explorer · BJS National Prisoner Statistics · ACS S0201

Germany · DE

Citizenship yes, ancestry-of-citizens no

The Polizeiliche Kriminalstatistik publishes suspect counts by citizenship — German vs. non-German — and crosses by nationality of origin for non-citizens. But Germans with migration background (the standard demographic concept used by Destatis for everything else) are statistically invisible in the police series. The two demographic concepts are not reconciled in the public crime release.

Bundeskriminalamt · PKS Jahrbuch · Destatis "Migrationshintergrund" microcensus concept

United Kingdom · UK

Ethnicity in stop-and-search; not in the conviction cross-tab

The Home Office publishes ethnicity for stop-and-search and prison-population snapshots, but the systematic ethnicity × convictions cross-tab is published only on irregular thematic releases ("Race and the Criminal Justice System") rather than as a maintained StatBank series with permanent table codes and annual update cadence. The series exists in form, but not in the persistence Denmark provides.

Ministry of Justice · "Statistics on Race and the Criminal Justice System" (latest 2023 release)

Japan · JP

Nationality of foreign offenders only

The Hanzai hakusho (White Paper on Crime) and the National Police Agency publish offence counts by nationality of the foreign offender (raini gaikokujin vs. teijū gaikokujin) but, by design, Japan does not maintain an ethnicity classification on its citizens (the koseki family-register system records only Japanese vs. non-Japanese). Ancestry × crime is conceptually absent from the official frame.

Ministry of Justice Japan · 犯罪白書 (Crime White Paper); 警察庁犯罪統計 (NPA crime statistics)

Method note. The six-country snapshot is not a ranking. France's prohibition rests on a defensible republican-equality argument; Japan's absence rests on a koseki-system structural fact. The point is not that Denmark is morally superior; the point is that only one of these six countries gives the public the data needed to challenge or confirm claims about ancestry × crime. Where the data are not collected, the public discourse runs on assertion. Where they are collected, it runs on cross-examination of numbers — which is what this site exists to enable.

The classification system

Three CPR-based origin categories crossed with a Western / non-Western split. The definitions are explicit, the fallback rules are documented, and the same scheme is used on both the crime side (STRAFNA9) and the population denominator side (FOLK2) — so rates are computable and the numerator-denominator match is clean.

The ancestry tree, as Statistics Denmark uses it

Population on 1 January │ ├── Person of Danish origin ← at least one parent is both a Danish citizen and born in Denmark │ ├── Immigrant ← born abroad; no parent is both a Danish citizen and born in Denmark │ ├── from Western country │ └── from non-Western country │ └── Descendant ← born in Denmark; no parent is both a Danish citizen and born in Denmark ├── from Western country └── from non-Western country Western = EU + Andorra, Iceland, Liechtenstein, Monaco, Norway, San Marino, Switzerland, UK, Vatican, USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand. Non-Western = all other countries, plus stateless persons.
Country of origin ≠ citizenship ≠ ancestry

Three separate official concepts. Country of origin is derived from parents' birth/citizenship with explicit fallbacks. Citizenship in dual cases defaults to Danish if any. Ancestry is the CPR-resolved three-part backbone above. Public crime tables expose all three lenses on the same convicted-persons domain.

The third-generation gap

Population tables include children of descendants (EB1–EB4) — the demographic third generation. The crime inventory does not currently publish an analogous third-generation crime table. So Statistics Denmark publishes the third-generation population but stops the crime breakdown at descendants. This gap is named explicitly in the documentation.

The data architecture

Twelve public crime tables, four public population tables, one official standardised index. Every cell is identifiable by table code; every code resolves to a permanent URL on statbank.dk. The system is built for replication.

What "index = 100" actually means

The standardised criminality index in Chart 2 below is normalised so that 100 = the whole-population age-adjusted average, not the Danish-origin average. This is the single most misread number on the dataset. Persons of Danish origin sit at index 88, not 100, because non-Danish groups are concentrated at high-crime young-adult ages and pull the population mean upward. A naive reader sees "Danish origin = 88" and reads it as "Danes commit 88% of crimes" — it actually means Danish-origin men are convicted at a rate 12% below the age-adjusted population mean.

The index also adjusts for age only (in the Integration Barometer time series) — not for socioeconomic composition. The age + SES correction exists in older Justice Ministry research and shrinks the multipliers further, but is not on the public barometer.

the trend most discourse misses

Cohort-by-cohort, the gap is narrowing

The Justice Ministry's fact-sheet figures 21–26 track cumulative share convicted by age 26 for successive birth cohorts. The 1996 cohort: 9.7% of Danish-origin men, 29.0% of non-Western immigrants, 28.3% of non-Western descendants. By the 2004 cohort, the cumulative share convicted by age 18 reads 4.1% Danish-origin, 8.4% non-Western immigrants, 12.6% non-Western descendants — the absolute multiplier has shrunk versus the equivalent point in earlier cohorts. The Justice Ministry's own summary: "this group [non-Western descendants] is markedly less crime-burdened today than earlier."

Why is this important? Because the policy debate around the 2018 "parallel society" laws, around stricter integration tests, and around tougher residency requirements typically cites the high multiplier as evidence that integration is failing. The cohort data inside the same official series shows the multiplier is falling — slowly, unevenly, but in the direction of integration succeeding rather than failing. An honest reading carries both findings.

What replicated cleanly: 10 of the 11 countries in the most-circulated independent chart appear directly in DST Table 6.6 with discrete indices: Denmark, Romania, Pakistan, Turkey, Iran, Morocco, Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, Somalia. The official numbers are below.

What did not replicate: Palestine has no discrete code in DST's official country list. Charts that show "Palestine" as a distinct point are necessarily either (a) treating Lebanon as 1:1 proxy, (b) applying the stateless-Palestinian adjustment to Lebanon, or (c) building a custom basket from STRAFNA4 raw cells across Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, and stateless codes. None of those three is the "official Palestine index" — DST does not publish one.

three structural observations on the data

What the verified per-country chart actually shows

1 · Descendant indices are generally higher than immigrant indices, but not uniformly. For Lebanon, Morocco, Syria, Pakistan and Sweden the descendant index is materially higher than the immigrant index — the standard "second-generation gap" pattern. For Romania, Vietnam, China and (marginally) Yugoslavia, the descendant index is lower than the immigrant index. The pattern is country-specific, not group-uniform — which is exactly the granularity that the aggregate "non-Western descendants" multiplier obscures.

2 · The Western/non-Western split is doing real work but is not the whole story. Romania (EU, classified Western by DST) sits at immigrant index 147 — above several non-Western countries. Sweden descendants are at 139 — above several Western-immigrant cells. The administrative split is a useful coarse categorization but country-by-country, the rank order does not follow Western/non-Western status cleanly.

3 · The "Pallesen ratio" and the "DST index" answer different questions. DST publishes index = 100 = whole-population mean. The widely-circulated independent chart uses ratio = 1.0 = Danish-origin rate. Mathematically: ratio ≈ DST_index ÷ 88, because Danish-origin sits at index ~88 below the population mean. So DST's Somalia descendant index of 320 corresponds to roughly a 3.6× ratio vs Danish origin at the aggregate penal-code level. The 20×–33× numbers in the most-circulated chart are not aggregate penal-code ratios — they are specific offence ratios (rape, robbery, attempted homicide) from STRAFNA4 cell-level data, which DST publishes for fewer offence types in Table 6.10 (15 countries × 5 offence categories). Comparing the two charts requires holding the offence type constant.

Recreating Pallesen's charts from primary sources

We downloaded 17 years of raw STRAFNA4 (persons guilty by country of origin and offence) and FOLK1C (population by country of origin), plus the Finansministeriet Bilag-tabel-B2 xlsx, and recomputed every ratio Pallesen plots. The recipe: pool 2008–2024, ages 15–79, divide each country's crime rate by Denmark's rate, treat Lebanon (5456) as the proxy for Palestine (since STRAFNA4 has no Palestine code — DST adds it only from 2022 onwards in FOLK1C). METHODOLOGY CHOICES — disclosure issues that survive even after the numbers verify
Age-standardisation — Choice, not flaw The chart shows raw rate ratios, not age-standardised indices. Both are valid; they answer different questions. Raw ratios = the actual conviction-per-population frequencies; age-standardised = "what the ratio would be if every group had the same age structure." The latter typically shrinks the multipliers ~30–50% because non-Western groups skew young/male, and young men commit more crime regardless of origin. The chart would be more useful if it labeled "raw rate ratio" or "age-adjusted index" explicitly. Both are honest representations of different facts.
Confidence intervals on ratio estimates — Not shown Small-country × rare-crime cells carry wide intervals. 95% CI on "Lebanon blackmail 28.5×" is roughly 17× to 45× depending on yearly distribution. The chart presents point estimates with no uncertainty bars. Visualising uncertainty would not change the rank order materially but would change how confidently magnitudes can be cited.
Pooling immigrants + descendants under "country of origin" — Choice, declared by source STRAFNA4's IELAND combines both immigrants and Danish-born descendants of a given origin. Pallesen does not separate them. This is the structure of the source table, not an analyst error — but it does inflate ratios for groups with a large descendant share (Lebanese-Palestinians, Turks, Pakistanis) because descendants skew younger.
Public code / notebook — Not published Pallesen has not published a GitHub repo, R/Python notebook, or CSV. He cites the source tables by name on his Substack and X feed. We replicated the computation independently here; the recipe is documented in the chart-source notes above so anyone with StatBank API access can re-run it.
Updated verdict. After downloading the raw STRAFNA4 + FOLK1C tables and the Finansministeriet Bilag B2 xlsx, recomputing every ratio, and matching them against Pallesen's chart, the answer is unambiguous: the headline numbers reproduce from primary sources, exact within rounding. Somalia rape 20× ↔ our 19.6×; Somalia robbery 33× ↔ our 33.3×; Somalia attempted homicide 27× ↔ our 26.9×; Somalia net cost ~$25k ↔ our $25,216. The methodology Pallesen uses is the structurally-obvious one (STRAFNA4 ÷ FOLK1C ÷ Denmark baseline, pooled 2008–2024, no age correction, Lebanon = Palestine proxy). The remaining concerns are about presentation, not arithmetic: missing age-standardisation labelling, missing confidence intervals, the small-country tail (Tunisia, Algeria, Uganda) where suppression thresholds differ, and the absence of public code. None of those concerns falsify the chart; they sharpen what the chart should be read as. Pallesen's data engineering is honest; Denmark's data infrastructure made the recreation possible. That second point is the whole reason this page exists.

Coverage, exclusions, timing

Every number on this page is bounded by explicit official caveats. Reading the numbers without reading the caveats produces the false-precision problem that wrecks most discourse on this question.

1 · Person-based, not incident-based

Convicted-persons statistics count persons found guilty during the calendar year, linked to the main case in a conviction complex. One person can appear multiple times across offence groupings if convicted in more than one offence area. Offence-specific person counts are therefore not additive across offence types.

2 · Residency restriction

Only persons resident in Denmark on 1 January and/or 31 December of the reference year are included. Persons who both immigrated and emigrated during the same year are excluded. The related convictions-level series (STRAF) does include tourists and others without a Danish personal identification number — a structural reason the two headline numbers differ.

3 · Low-fine exclusions

Road-traffic fines below DKK 2,500 (from 2012 onward; DKK 1,500 in 2001–2011; DKK 1,000 before 2001) and special-law fines below DKK 1,000 are not registered in the Central Criminal Register and therefore do not enter the statistics. The 2012 threshold change is an explicit comparability break in the time series.

4 · Police-activity confounder

Statistics Denmark explicitly warns that police activity — speed-control campaigns, nightlife-enforcement operations — can move convicted-persons counts, especially for traffic and special-law offences, without implying any matching change in underlying offending behaviour. The convicted-persons series measures detected-and-prosecuted behaviour, not behaviour as such.

5 · Legal-discontinuity breaks for sexual offences

Two named legal discontinuities affect sexual-offence comparability: a major criminal-code amendment effective 1 July 2013, and the new consent-based rape concept effective 1 January 2021. Pre/post comparisons across these dates are not direct.

6 · February extraction, no later revision

The data are extracted around 1 February following the reference year. Some convictions sentenced during the year are not yet registered at extraction, so totals are presumed slightly under-estimated — but Statistics Denmark says the under-estimate is roughly constant in scale year-over-year. Published figures are final and are not later revised.

7 · No international counterpart

Statistics Denmark states explicitly: there is no internationally comparable counterpart. The ancestry concept and the western/non-western split are national constructs. Cross-country claims using these numbers as a benchmark have to be carefully qualified — the Danish data are useful for Denmark and instructive for the methodological discussion, not directly portable.

8 · Crude rates ≠ the standardised index

A user can compute a crude per-1,000 rate by dividing STRAFNA9 by the matching FOLK2 cell. But crude rates do not reproduce the official age-standardised + socio-economic-adjusted criminality index, which weights each person by exact resident-period and adjusts for age × socioeconomic composition. Reporting raw counts as "the rate" without standardising is the most common misuse of this dataset.

The cross-examination

The position that ancestry × crime data should be collected and published is challenged from both directions. Below: the strongest serious objections to honest publication, named without softening, then cross-examined.

Where the honest-publication position strains

The case for publishing ancestry × crime statistics is strong, but not unanswerable. The serious objections — left and right of it — name real costs.

Objections from the left of honest publication (3)

The ecological-fallacy weaponisation

Group-level conviction rates do not predict individual behaviour. But every published group rate gets weaponised by political actors who use it as if it did — to justify discrimination in hiring, housing, policing, and immigration screening. Denmark's "ghetto laws" (2018) and "parallel-society" classifications cite exactly this data architecture. Publishing the data, the objection runs, is materially upstream of the discrimination.

The objection assumes the discrimination is downstream of the publication. The empirical record suggests the opposite causal direction: the political coalition that wanted the "ghetto laws" was built on assertion-level claims about crime by origin before the data architecture matured. Removing the publication does not remove the assertion; it just leaves the assertion uncontested. The honest answer to "this group commits X% of Y crime" is "the standardised index, after age and socioeconomic adjustment, is N", not "you may not ask." Where data exist, the discriminator has to engage them; where they don't, anything can be claimed.

The "no internationally comparable counterpart" admission as escape hatch

Statistics Denmark's own documentation says the data have no international counterpart. That is a structural feature, not a bug — but it is also the feature that lets advocates of publication insulate the Danish numbers from cross-national replication. If only Denmark publishes, only Denmark can be argued about; the rest of Europe can claim its silence is methodological humility rather than political choice.

True, and worth conceding. But the answer is not "Denmark should stop publishing", it is "France, Germany, the UK should start." The asymmetry is the political problem; the solution to political asymmetry is more publication, not less. The page's framing — Denmark as exception, not as model — concedes that the comparison cannot yet be made; it does not concede that the comparison should never be enabled.

The descendant-category problem

A person born in Denmark, schooled in Denmark, speaking Danish as first language, with one foreign-born grandparent on the wrong side of the citizenship test, can be classified as a "descendant from a non-Western country" — and therefore land in a public crime statistic that names them by lineage rather than by anything they did. This is the policy literature's most-cited objection: the category itself is a marker that follows people across generations regardless of behaviour or identification.

The objection is real, and Statistics Denmark partially answers it by publishing population-side third-generation tables (EB1–EB4) while declining to publish a matching third-generation crime table. That decision is not random. The third-generation crime question is sufficiently politically loaded that the agency has chosen to publish the demographic denominator and withhold the equivalent numerator. That choice is itself a methodological acknowledgement of the strain point — and the page names it rather than concealing it.

Objections from the right of honest publication (2)

The standardisation-as-laundering objection

The age- and socio-economic-adjusted index suppresses real signal. If a group has structurally different age composition or socioeconomic composition, that is a fact about the group; adjusting it out to produce an index of "what crime would be if age and class were equalised" buries the actual rate behind a counterfactual. The raw count, the objection runs, is what voters and policy-makers should see.

The objection has force on the framing, less on the methodology. The standardised index is published alongside raw counts, not instead of them — STRAFNA9 raw counts are publicly available on the same StatBank. The choice of which number to lead with is editorial, not statistical. The right answer is to publish both and be transparent about which question each one answers: raw counts answer "what happened?"; standardised indices answer "what happens after equalising for age and SES?" Treating either as the only honest number is the move that hides the actual story.

The third-generation withholding as politically motivated

The agency publishes the third-generation population (EB1–EB4) but not the third-generation crime. That is not a methodological constraint — it is a political choice, and the absence of a public third-generation crime series in 2026 (when the demographic third generation is large enough to study) is itself an editorial intervention into what the discourse can examine. The agency cannot claim methodological neutrality if it chooses which categories the public can see.

The objection is correct that the absence is an editorial choice, and Statistics Denmark's documentation does not pretend otherwise — it names the absence rather than concealing it. The honest reading is that the page sits inside a real tension: publishing everything maximises transparency; withholding the third-generation crime cross-tab is the agency's bet that the descendant category already exposes the empirical question without a generational marker that would be harder to defend. Naming that tradeoff openly — as this page does — is more honest than pretending the agency made the maximally-transparent choice.

The methodological point. Cross-examination is not refutation. The case for publication survives the strain points because the strain points are arguments about how to read the numbers, not about whether the numbers exist. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.
