# Unity, Diversity, Social Cohesion, and State Capacity

## Executive summary

The strongest conclusion from the literature is not “diversity is strength” or “unity is strength.” It is that diversity is usually a **coordination problem plus a capability opportunity**. Whether it becomes strength or weakness depends on a few recurring variables: the quality and impartiality of institutions, whether people meet each other in common institutions rather than separate enclaves, whether there is a credible shared identity above subgroup identity, and whether residential and political segregation are kept low enough that the state still feels common rather than factional. In other words, **organized unity beats both slogans**. citeturn8view0turn10search0turn11search0turn37view7turn37view8

At the neighborhood and local level, the evidence is fairly hard-edged. entity["people","Robert Putnam","political scientist"]’s “hunkering down” argument has not been cleanly overturned; later reviews and meta-analyses still find that ethnic diversity is, on average, associated with lower social trust, especially **trust in neighbors** and especially when diversity is measured at the **local** rather than national scale. But the same literature also shows that this is not the whole story: the effect is not uniform across all kinds of trust, it is often stronger in some national contexts than others, and it does **not** imply that interethnic contact is always bad. In fact, structured contact often improves trust. citeturn8view0turn10search0turn11search0turn37view13

The most important correction to both pro- and anti-diversity slogans is this: **segregation is often more corrosive than diversity itself**. A large European regional study finds that institutional quality is a much stronger predictor of trust than ethnic heterogeneity. A separate study finds that countries where ethnic and linguistic groups live more separately have substantially lower quality of government. That is a different claim from “mixed societies cannot work.” It means place, mixing, and fairness matter. citeturn37view7turn37view8

The comparative cases fit that pattern. High-capacity, successful cases such as entity["country","Singapore","country in Southeast Asia"], entity["country","Switzerland","country in Europe"], and entity["country","Canada","country in North America"] do not succeed by celebrating diversity in the abstract. They succeed by **engineering common identity and repeated cross-group cooperation** through law, schools, service, language rules, housing rules, and state legitimacy. By contrast, the strained cases in entity["country","Sweden","country in Northern Europe"], entity["country","France","country in Western Europe"], and entity["country","Denmark","country in Northern Europe"] are better understood as cases of **place-based segregation, integration gaps, and political backlash** than as simple proofs that diversity itself mechanically destroys society. The fragile cases—entity["country","Lebanon","country in the Middle East"], entity["country","Iraq","country in the Middle East"], and entity["country","Bosnia and Herzegovina","country in Southeastern Europe"]—show something still different: when state institutions are organized around communal patronage and vetoes, diversity can harden into permanent political fragmentation and weak state capacity. citeturn37view0turn37view1turn37view2turn36view1turn36view2turn34view0turn34view2turn36view5turn37view6turn36view8turn36view9turn36view10turn36view15turn36view16

The military literature is even more direct. **Esprit de corps is real, but it is not the same thing as demographic sameness.** Modern military sociology and RAND reviews consistently find that **task cohesion**—shared commitment to mission, competence, and trust in performance—is a stronger predictor of effectiveness than simple social liking. Diverse units can perform well when standards are clear, leadership is trusted, and the institution forces repeated cooperation under common risk. entity["politician","Harry S. Truman","33rd president of the United States"]’s desegregation order did not end friction, but the U.S. military became integrated because command authority, training, mission, and career incentives pushed integration from rhetoric into practice. citeturn37view11turn37view12turn9view0turn37view16turn37view17turn37view18

On innovation and economic performance, the evidence is mixed but usable. Diversity is most defensible as an advantage in **complex, creative, skilled, high-information environments**. It is much less defensible as a universal performance booster. Large meta-analyses find average positive effects that are usually **small and conditional**; they are stronger for deep-level or cognitive diversity and for complex tasks, and weaker or even negative for simple, low-interdependence work. The high-profile corporate “business case” literature—especially the widely cited reports from urlMcKinsey & Companyturn33view0—is mostly correlational and often presented more confidently in public discourse than the evidence warrants. citeturn7search4turn12search1turn37view9turn37view14turn37view15turn5search14

## What the strongest literature actually says

The best synthesis of the trust literature is still conditional rather than ideological. Putnam’s original 2007 paper argued that, in the short run, ethnic diversity tends to reduce social solidarity and social capital at the neighborhood level, with residents of all groups “hunkering down.” He also explicitly argued that this is not necessarily the long-run endpoint, because immigrant societies can build broader identities over time. Later review work by Portes and Vickstrom challenged overly broad uses of “social capital” and argued that trust and civicness have deeper historical roots than simple demographic composition. Van der Meer and Tolsma’s review of 90 studies found that support for the “constrict” thesis is most consistent for **neighborhood-bounded** cohesion; more common in the United States than elsewhere; and not a general finding that diversity lowers **interethnic** cohesion as such. Dinesen, Schaeffer, and Sønderskov’s meta-analysis of 1,001 estimates from 87 studies found a statistically significant negative relationship between ethnic diversity and social trust overall, stronger for trust in neighbors and for more local measures of diversity. citeturn8view0turn8view1turn11search0turn10search0

That means the non-slogan version is this: **local heterogeneity often imposes coordination costs**, particularly where trust is intimate, repeated, and place-based. It does **not** automatically mean national disintegration. The literature repeatedly distinguishes between generalized trust, neighborhood trust, civic participation, interethnic attitudes, and organizational performance. Too much public debate collapses all of these into one variable called “diversity.” The academic literature does not. citeturn11search0turn10search0turn8view1

The most important moderating variable is institutional quality. Charron and Rothstein, using 206 European regions, found strong and robust evidence that institutional quality—defined as an impartial, non-corrupt public sector—is the strongest determinant of regional trust variation, while ethnic heterogeneity was not the main driver once that variable was included. Alesina and Zhuravskaya found that more ethnically and linguistically segregated countries have substantially lower quality of government. So a good working model is: **raw diversity raises the coordination burden; segregation magnifies it; impartial institutions reduce it.** citeturn37view7turn37view8

This is also where contact theory matters. A field experiment in the armed forces found that soldiers randomly assigned to close contact with ethnic minorities showed increased trust toward a generic immigrant. That is an unusually valuable result because it gets closer to causality than most observational neighborhood studies. It suggests that diversity works better when interaction is **structured, repeated, equal-status, and mission-bound**, and worse when it is **distant, segregated, politicized, or mediated by fear and competition**. citeturn37view13

## Comparative cases from high-capacity to fragile states

The successful cases are not post-identity utopias. They are **highly managed pluralisms**. In entity["country","Singapore","country in Southeast Asia"], the state openly defines the country as a “small, multi-racial, multi-religious nation” and treats cohesion as an active governing task, not a passive byproduct. Ministry of Defence material describes Total Defence as involving every Singaporean in building a “strong, secure and cohesive nation,” and it explicitly ties daily civic behavior and National Service duties to maintaining ties “regardless of race and religion.” The same state apparatus says that building a shared identity strengthens collective resolve against threats to cohesion. Singapore also uses explicit housing management through the Housing & Development Board’s Ethnic Integration Policy. The point is not that harmony appears automatically. The point is that the state hard-wires mixing, common service, and common language/identity into institutional design. citeturn38view3turn35view0turn37view0turn37view1turn37view2

entity["country","Switzerland","country in Europe"] is the other classic success case, but again the mechanism is not “diversity” in the abstract. Swiss federal authorities describe the country as officially quadrilingual and say multilingualism is actively encouraged and enshrined in law. The Federal Office of Culture describes promotion of multilingualism and mutual understanding among linguistic communities as a pillar of language policy, grounded in constitutional articles that define the four national languages. Academic work on Switzerland makes the same point more bluntly: multilingualism survives there because protection of linguistic particularism is legally codified and made part of national identity. Switzerland works because linguistic diversity is nested inside strong cross-cutting institutions, federal autonomy, and a civic state that all major groups accept as legitimate. citeturn36view1turn36view2turn30search0

entity["country","Canada","country in North America"] is often used as the best multicultural case, and the data broadly justify that reputation—but only in a qualified sense. Statistics Canada reports that 23 percent of the population is immigrant-origin, and that 77 percent of immigrants report a very strong or somewhat strong sense of belonging to Canada, compared with 70 percent of the Canadian-born. Statistics Canada also notes that the stronger sense of belonging reported by immigrants is partly attributable to Canada’s multicultural policies. This looks like a real success in converting diversity into national attachment. But it is not proof that multicultural symbolism alone does the work; Canada also combines selective immigration, a strong citizenship regime, official bilingualism, and relatively high state legitimacy. citeturn34view0turn34view2turn8view6

entity["country","Japan","country in East Asia"] is useful mainly as a baseline, not as a direct counterexample. Official Japanese data show foreign residents rising to 4,125,395 at the end of 2025, but Japan still remains relatively less immigrant-diverse than the major Anglo societies, and official statistics continue to show comparatively low crime levels by advanced-country standards. The analytical point is that Japan does **not** prove homogeneity is always superior. It shows that a relatively low-diversity society avoids many integration burdens that more plural societies must actively solve. That makes it a baseline for comparison, not a transportable institutional model for already-diverse states. citeturn16search6turn17search46

The strained Western European cases need a more honest description than “collapse.” entity["country","Sweden","country in Northern Europe"] remains a capable state, and official homicide figures actually improved in 2025. But Sweden’s own housing agency says socio-economic residential segregation is most pronounced in many housing areas built during the Million Program era, and the government itself calls violence linked to criminal networks “one of the biggest societal challenges of our time.” Brå’s research on criminal networks shows how neighborhood-level criminal structures recruit children and entrench themselves locally. So Sweden is not a case of general state failure; it is a case where **segregated disadvantage plus gang recruitment created real local breakdown pressures inside a still-strong national state**. citeturn36view5turn37view6turn36view7turn22news37

entity["country","France","country in Western Europe"] shows a similar pattern under a different ideological model. The republican framework emphasizes a single civic identity, but official INSEE data show that in Grand Paris, one quarter of people leaving a priority urban neighborhood move to another such neighborhood, and those movers are more often poor than people moving outside those neighborhoods. A third of new arrivals in these neighborhoods live below the poverty threshold. That is not proof that French universalism is false in principle. It is proof that **formal civic equality does not automatically dissolve spatial concentration, poverty clustering, or local social separation**. citeturn36view8turn36view9

entity["country","Denmark","country in Northern Europe"] is especially revealing because the state itself adopted the language of “parallel societies.” The Danish government’s 2018 strategy explicitly promised “No ghettos in 2030” and said it was prepared to use “special rules for special areas.” Later litigation in the EU system focused on Danish public-housing rules for “transformation areas” defined in part by neighborhoods where more than 50 percent of residents were “immigrants and descendants from non-Western countries.” The lesson is double-edged. Denmark recognized a real problem—durable enclave formation. But the legal backlash shows the danger of trying to solve integration failure through blunt ethnicized categories. citeturn36view10turn21news35turn21search4

The fragile cases are qualitatively different. In entity["country","Lebanon","country in the Middle East"], the World Bank describes failed provision of key public services and attributes the service model’s fragility to elite capture and clientelist governance. In entity["country","Iraq","country in the Middle East"], the World Bank says the country remains caught in a fragility trap marked by instability, social unrest, and a deepening state-citizen divide. In entity["country","Bosnia and Herzegovina","country in Southeastern Europe"], the consociational settlement helped stop war, but later work argues that Bosnia’s ongoing problems are rooted in that governance structure itself. These are the clearest cases where identity is not just social background but the **operating system of politics**, and where power-sharing can buy peace while also freezing patronage, vetoes, and state weakness. citeturn18search5turn36view15turn36view16

The historical warning from entity["historical_event","Breakup of Yugoslavia","disintegration of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia"] cuts against simplistic stories. Demographic work on Yugoslavia shows that, in many regions between 1961 and 1991, ethnic diversity increased while segregation decreased. That means collapse cannot be reduced to “too much diversity” or “too much separation” in a simple mechanical sense. Elite competition, institutional breakdown, and politicized nationalism mattered more than the raw demographic pattern alone. citeturn25search7

## Military cohesion and esprit de corps

The military literature is one of the few places where this whole debate gets stripped of romance. The strongest finding is that **task cohesion matters more than social cohesion** for performance. MacCoun and Hix’s review found that meta-analytic evidence points to task cohesion—commitment to the mission—as the primary component in the cohesion-performance effect, and that social cohesion effects shrink or disappear once task cohesion is controlled for. RAND’s later review similarly states that the academic literature suggests task cohesion is a stronger predictor of performance than social cohesion. Another RAND report on special operations integration argues that the highest-performing units in stressful environments are likely to be highly task-oriented while also sustaining trust and social support. citeturn9view0turn37view16turn37view17turn37view18

That is the best research translation of “esprit de corps.” It is not mere sentiment. It is a blend of trust, mission commitment, competence, and confidence that everyone will do the job under stress. That is why diverse militaries can work when civilian politics assumes they cannot. The institution compresses status competition, enforces common standards, and channels identity toward mission. citeturn37view16turn37view17turn37view18

The U.S. case is especially instructive. The National Archives record of Executive Order 9981 states the governing principle clearly: equality of treatment and opportunity in the armed services without regard to race, with due regard for efficiency and morale. The same official history notes that there was strong resistance, but by the end of the Korean conflict almost all of the military was integrated. That does not mean integration was easy or instant. It means command structure, training, shared danger, and institutional enforcement made integration workable even in a society that was still segregated in civilian life. citeturn37view11turn37view12

The field experiment in the armed forces strengthens the case for structured contact. Soldiers randomly assigned to close contact with minority roommates became more trusting toward a generic immigrant. That result fits what the army often does at its best: it converts abstract out-groups into known teammates. The implication is not that any amount of diversity is harmless. The implication is that **common mission can domesticate difference when the institution is strong enough to force fair contact and credible reciprocity**. citeturn37view13

## Diversity and innovation without slogans

The innovation literature gives some support to diversity claims, but much less than corporate rhetoric usually suggests. A recent large registered-report meta-analysis found that the average relationships between demographic, job-related, and cognitive diversity and team performance are statistically significant and positive, but insubstantial on average, with more positive results in complex or creative tasks and in some cultural settings. A separate meta-analysis on culturally diverse teams found that surface-level diversity is not generally related to creativity and innovation, while deep-level diversity is positively related; it also found that surface-level diversity can be negative in simple tasks and that effects depend heavily on task interdependence and complexity. That is a long way from “diversity always improves results.” citeturn7search4turn12search1

At the macro level, there is also evidence that some forms of migration diversity can be economically productive. Alesina, Harnoss, and Rapoport found that the diversity of skilled immigration is positively related to income, total factor productivity, and patent intensity after many controls. But that is not the same variable as ethnic fractionalization in the older literature. It is closer to **skilled, complementary, economically integrated variety** than to unmanaged communal pluralism. citeturn37view9turn37view10

This is why the business-case literature is often overstated. McKinsey’s own reports say they are studying the relationship between leadership diversity and company performance, and the 2015 report was based on proprietary data from 366 public companies. The firm’s 2023 update still presents top-quartile board diversity as being associated with stronger odds of financial outperformance. But these are mostly correlational patterns, not clean causal demonstrations. Critics such as Alex Edmans have pointed out that positive correlations do not establish causation and may run in either direction. The safest reading is that diversity can be advantageous in some environments, but the effect is usually mediated by leadership quality, organizational design, inclusion, selection, and task structure. citeturn37view14turn37view15turn5search14

There is a similar lesson on “diversity programs.” Dobbin and Kalev’s critique, while not a meta-analysis of diversity itself, is important because it shows that many popular corporate interventions have weak or short-lived effects. So even in organizations where some forms of diversity help, **management slogans and compliance theater are not the same thing as institutional competence**. citeturn8view11

## Policy implications

If the goal is a blunt answer, it is this: **diversity scales only when institutions scale first, or at least simultaneously**. Strong states can sustain pluralism when they are impartial enough to be trusted, coercive enough to enforce common rules, and legitimate enough that major groups still want the state to exist as a common project. Weak states cannot. citeturn37view7turn37view8turn36view15turn36view16

Second, **shared identity matters most when cooperation is costly**. The comparative evidence suggests that slogans about “celebrating differences” are not enough. Successful plural societies build common identity through schools, citizenship, military or civil service, common command structures, common legal language, and common material stakes. entity["country","Singapore","country in Southeast Asia"] does this explicitly. entity["country","Switzerland","country in Europe"] does it through federal multilingual constitutionalism. entity["country","Canada","country in North America"] does it through citizenship, belonging, and relatively strong institutional trust. citeturn37view0turn37view1turn37view2turn36view1turn36view2turn34view0turn34view2

Third, **residential concentration is a strategic variable**. If policymakers want plural societies to remain cohesive, they should worry less about abstract national diversity and more about whether low-trust areas become physically and socially detached from mainstream institutions. The evidence from France, Sweden, Denmark, and the cross-national segregation literature all point in that direction. citeturn36view5turn36view8turn36view9turn36view10turn37view8

Fourth, **military-style lessons generalize surprisingly well**. Institutions integrate best when they are clear about mission, selective about standards, intolerant of predation and favoritism, and relentless about repeated cross-group cooperation. That is what turns heterogeneity from a liability into a controlled asset. It is also why sectarian patronage systems fail: they do the opposite. They reward faction before mission. citeturn37view16turn37view17turn37view18turn36view15turn36view16

The best-supported bottom line is therefore not a slogan but a sequence: **state capacity, impartial rules, low segregation, and shared identity first; then diversity can be an asset. Without those, diversity more often amplifies existing weaknesses than creates new strengths.** citeturn8view0turn10search0turn11search0turn37view7turn37view8turn37view13

## Open questions and limitations

A few issues remain genuinely unresolved. The literature still struggles to separate short-run shock effects from long-run adaptation; to distinguish selection from causation in neighborhood and organizational studies; and to compare national-level trust with local-level trust without mixing different mechanisms. It also remains difficult to separate the effects of diversity from those of poverty, rapid demographic change, elite rhetoric, housing-market structure, and weak local institutions. Those are not excuses for agnosticism; they are reasons to avoid slogans. citeturn10search0turn11search0turn8view1turn37view7

The case studies should also be read carefully. entity["country","Sweden","country in Northern Europe"], entity["country","France","country in Western Europe"], and entity["country","Denmark","country in Northern Europe"] are not failed states. Their problems are real, but mostly local, spatial, and institutional rather than total state collapse. entity["country","Lebanon","country in the Middle East"] and entity["country","Iraq","country in the Middle East"] are different because communal identity is built into the political operating system itself. That is why they belong in a separate analytical category. citeturn37view6turn36view8turn36view10turn18search5turn36view15
