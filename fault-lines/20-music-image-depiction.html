Music, image, depiction / 音楽・図像・描写: figurative art, music in public, mosque architecture

Where the Islamic aniconism tradition (variable across schools, strict in Salafi and Wahhabi readings, much more permissive in Sufi and Shia traditions) meets Western public-art, music, and depiction defaults — including the specific question of whether public mosques in The West should incorporate or reject figurative ornament.

The fault line, in one sentence

Western public culture treats figurative art, music in public spaces, and depiction of religious figures as default-permitted; the Islamic aniconism tradition (variable across schools — strict Salafi and Wahhabi prohibition, more permissive Sufi and Shia practice) prohibits figurative depiction of living beings in religious contexts and music in some interpretations, with the strictest interpretation extending to public-space restrictions and iconoclastic destruction (Bamiyan Buddhas 2001, Mosul antiquities 2014).

Steelmanned positions

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Aniconism is theological seriousness, not philistinism

The strongest version argues that the Islamic aniconism tradition is theologically grounded — the prohibition on creating images of living beings flows from the doctrinal commitment to monotheism and the rejection of idolatry that defines Abrahamic religion at its core. The strongest version concedes substantial intra-Islamic variation: Persian miniature painting, Mughal portraiture, contemporary Iranian cinema all violate strict aniconism and are recognized as legitimate within their traditions. The strict-aniconism position is one current within Islam, not the universal one. Music similarly: the strict prohibition coexists with the Sufi sama' tradition, classical Arabic and Persian musical scholarship, and contemporary Muslim popular music globally.

The strongest version concedes that the iconoclastic-destruction cases (Bamiyan 2001, Mosul Museum 2014, Palmyra 2015) were unambiguously wrong even by most internal-Islamic standards. Its operative claim is that aniconist preferences in mosque-design, religious-instruction-context, and personal practice are religious choices entitled to the same accommodation as any other religious practice — and that the broader framing of aniconism as anti-art is a Western-cultural caricature of a sophisticated theological-aesthetic position.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Aesthetic-religious accommodation yes, public-cultural restriction no

The liberal-democratic position is sympathetic on the religious-private-choice baseline: a mosque designed without figurative ornament is a religious aesthetic choice no different from a Quaker meeting-house designed without elaborate decoration. The strongest version draws limits where strict-aniconism demands move into restriction of public-cultural practice — calls for removal of public statuary, restriction of public music performance, restriction of figurative-art education in mixed-population schools.

The strongest version concedes that the iconoclastic-destruction cases are extreme and not representative of mainstream European Muslim opinion; they are the case for the principle, not its everyday application. The defensible settlement is religious-aesthetic choice in religious contexts as freely accommodated, public-cultural practice as default-permitted with the same protections that apply to free expression more generally — and the iconoclastic-destruction position categorically rejected as a legitimate religious-practice claim in Western context, while recognized as the minority strict-Salafi position it is.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band on this fault line runs from the cartoonist's drafting table to rue Nicolas-Appert and the schoolyard in Conflans, and every position on it should clear the same evidentiary bar.

Cross-examination

The depiction-and-music debate, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply — and against the body count the previous decade put on the table.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "moderation" bait-and-switch

The version offered to Western audiences emphasizes that strict aniconism is one current among several, that Persian miniatures and Mughal portraiture and Iranian cinema are recognized inside the tradition, and that mosque-architecture preferences are merely a private religious-aesthetic choice no different from a Quaker meeting-house.

The IFOP / Fondation Jean-Jaurès 2020 data on the same page tells the harder story: 69% of French Muslims rated Charlie Hebdo's republication "unnecessary provocation," 38% said religious rules should take precedence over Republic laws (57% in the 15–24 cohort), and 18% declined to condemn the 2015 attackers — twice the national rate. The diaspora-moderate framing collapses against polling of the diaspora itself; what is presented as a private aesthetic choice is, in the constituency where the position is held, a substantial illiberal-but-peaceful majority's view about what others may be allowed to publish. The "private religious choice" framing does not survive contact with the page's own friction data.

The unfalsifiable-consensus claim on taswir and music

The strict position presents the prohibition on taswir (image-making of living beings) and the categorization of music as lahw al-hadith (idle talk) as established consensus rooted in canonical Hadith — most prominently the Bukhari narrations on image-makers being "the most severely punished" on the Day of Judgement, and the Bukhari 5590 chain on stringed instruments.

Jonathan Brown's Hadith: Muhammad's Legacy in the Medieval and Modern World (Oneworld, 2009) and his Misquoting Muhammad (2014) document that the relevant musical-prohibition chains are weakly transmitted by the Hadith-criticism standards the same school applies elsewhere; G. R. Hawting's The Idea of Idolatry and the Emergence of Islam (Cambridge, 1999) shows the image-aversion emerged in a specific late-antique polemical context against pagan cultic image-use, not as a free-standing aesthetic ban. Yusuf al-Qaradawi himself — in his al-Halal wal-Haram fi'l-Islam chapter on music — concedes the permissibility of unaccompanied vocal music and of stringed instruments at weddings, contradicting the strict-Salafi reading from inside the conservative-Sunni mainstream. The "consensus" is one school's preferred reconstruction, not a textual fact; the position must defend the choice on its merits.

The privacy-not-punishment retreat

The contemporary apologetic version argues that aniconism is about personal piety and mosque interiors — that the position counsels Muslims to avoid figurative art and music in their own observance, not to impose those preferences on the public square or on non-Muslim cultural production.

This concedes the structure of the blasphemy regime while denying its existence. If the cartoons should "never have been published," if Samuel Paty should not have shown them to a class on freedom of expression, if Charlie Hebdo's editor required permanent police protection from 2011 until his murder in 2015 — then the position is not asking for personal accommodation; it is asking that the public square be configured around the same prohibition the strict reading imposes privately. The Munir al-Qattan / Conseil européen pour la fatwa et la recherche line that "freedom of expression cannot extend to insult of the Prophet" is the operative content; the privacy framing is the export presentation. The 69% "unnecessary provocation" figure is what the privacy framing looks like once it has been translated into a constituency demand.

The metaphysical-premise dodge

When pressed on the textual case for a categorical depiction or music ban, the position retreats to coherence on its own metaphysical premises — that depiction of the Prophet, or of living beings generally, is ontologically a different category of act than the secular-aesthetic frame can recognize, and that liberal critique applies premises the position does not share.

This is true and trivial. Faisal Devji's Landscapes of the Jihad (Cornell, 2005) and The Terrorist in Search of Humanity (Columbia, 2008) make the parallel observation that contemporary Salafi-jihadist iconoclasm — Bamiyan 2001, Mosul 2014, Palmyra 2015 — relies on exactly this premise-internal coherence to license destruction of objects the rest of humanity reads as cultural patrimony. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling. The substantive question is whether the premises survive examination outside the system that licenses them — and the position cannot answer that question without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion.

The "offence-is-violence" equivocation

The position's most charitable contemporary form holds that depiction of the Prophet is experienced as a form of violence against the believing community — and that the believing community is therefore entitled to a corresponding restraint from non-believers as a matter of basic civility, not as a religious-law imposition.

The Charlie Hebdo cluster — Kouachi brothers Jan 2015 (12 dead at the offices, 17 across three days), Mahmoud Sept 2020 (two near-fatal cleaver wounds at the former-offices address), Anzorov Oct 2020 (Samuel Paty beheaded for showing the cartoons in class) — is the empirical answer to the equivocation. Offence and violence are different categories. The cartoonists were murdered; Salman Rushdie remained under fatwa for thirty-three years before being stabbed fifteen times in Chautauqua in 2022, losing an eye and the use of one hand. The exit-cost asymmetry is not symmetric at all: those who depict bear the lethal risk in perpetuity; those offended bear, at most, the cost of looking away. A position that frames its own grievance in the vocabulary of violence while its violent fringe acts on that framing has not earned the equivocation; it has to choose between the framing and the disavowal.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency objection on blasphemy law

Western jurisdictions decriminalized blasphemy only recently — England and Wales in 2008, Ireland in 2018 (constitutional referendum), Denmark in 2017, Scotland in 2021. The conservative position can press: if the Western settlement on satirical treatment of religious figures is universal-and-self-evident, why did Western law itself protect Christianity from blasphemy within living memory?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest liberal answer is: we changed our mind — the same way the West revised its positions on slavery, women's suffrage, and religious tolerance. The right answer to "why did your own grandparents' law protect Christianity from blasphemy?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held," not "we held a different view that was equally valid." The position requires acknowledging that the liberal-democratic settlement on speech-and-the-sacred continues to evolve — and defending the current settlement on its merits, not on its provenance.

The selective-courage problem

Charlie Hebdo and the broader free-speech-on-Islam coalition defended the right to caricature Muhammad on principle. The same principle, applied evenly, requires the same defence of provocations against other faiths and groups — and the conservative critic can press that the same outlets and constituencies have often been markedly less robust on antisemitic provocation, on caricature of observant Christians, or on cartoon attacks on Hindu or Sikh religious figures.

The asymmetry exists and is documented (cf. the Maurice Sinet / Siné firing from Charlie Hebdo in 2008 over an antisemitism allegation; the European debates on prosecution of Holocaust denial vs. Muhammad caricature; the differential newsroom treatment of Christian-mocking and Muslim-mocking content). The honest liberal answer is not that the asymmetry doesn't exist; it is that the cure is more even-handed defence of provocative speech across the board, not less defence in the cases where the violent reply has been most lethal. The principle has to be defended where it costs most, or it isn't the principle being defended — but the defence is weaker if it is rationed by the courage of the satirist's targets.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

Mosque architecture debates and the music-in-public question

European mosque-architecture debates have engaged the question of whether new-build mosques should adopt figurative-or-non-figurative ornament — most contemporary European mosque architecture follows traditional aniconist conventions. The European Bamiyan reaction (2001) included substantial intra-Muslim condemnation of the Taliban destruction. Music-in-public-school debates (whether Muslim students should be exempted from music classes) have been litigated in some German Länder; the German Federal Constitutional Court has generally permitted accommodation requests but treated music education as a normal curriculum component absent specific demonstrated religious objection.

US

First Amendment baseline; minimal litigation

US First Amendment doctrine generally protects both religious-aniconist private practice and public-cultural-figurative practice. The Supreme Court has not directly ruled on Islamic-aniconism-related disputes; the music-in-school accommodation question is handled at school-district level case-by-case. Mosque-architecture and zoning disputes occasionally surface (the 2010 "Ground Zero Mosque" controversy was the highest-profile but the substantive design question was not the contested one).

Japan

Aesthetic-cultural accommodation through commercial infrastructure

Japan's substantial Buddhist and Shinto figurative-and-musical-religious-practice tradition has generated no significant friction with the small Muslim community on the aniconism question. Japanese mosques are built to traditional aniconist conventions; Japanese music-and-art institutions have not faced significant Muslim-objection cases. The 2001 Bamiyan destruction was widely covered in Japan with substantial public outrage.

A specific Japan-relevant case worth Phase-2 examination is the treatment of imperial and Buddhist statuary in Japanese-Muslim community discourse — the question has been almost entirely absent from public debate, which is itself a finding.

Case study: the Charlie Hebdo attacks and the Muhammad-depiction question

No incident in twenty-first-century Europe maps the depiction fault line as cleanly as Charlie Hebdo. A satirical magazine publishes drawings of the Prophet Muhammad. A series of mass-casualty attacks follows, spanning a decade. Sentiment polls in the wake of each attack show the distribution of opinion is exactly what the rainbow band would predict — overwhelming condemnation of the violence, real but minority sympathy for "the motives," and a much larger illiberal middle that says the cartoons should never have been published in the first place. The case is here because it is the empirical object on which the abstract aniconism debate has been tested in blood.

The cluster of attacks tied to Muhammad-depiction in France

November 2011 – October 2020 · 21 dead, 33+ wounded across the linked incidents · perpetrators all claimed religious-doctrinal warrant for the depiction grievance

Charlie Hebdo had been on al-Qaeda's published target list since at least 2013, after publishing a special "Charia Hebdo" issue in November 2011 (which triggered a firebombing of its offices the morning of publication) and republishing the Danish Jyllands-Posten Muhammad cartoons in 2006. Editor Stéphane "Charb" Charbonnier had a permanent police bodyguard. The 7 January 2015 attack was the predicted event the French security services had been bracing for — and they were proven right within five years by two further attacks tied to the same provocation.

Sentiment polls: the distribution the attacks produced

The polls below — UK ComRes 2015, French IFOP at three different moments — are the empirical answer to "what fraction of European Muslims hold which views on the depiction-and-attack question?" The rainbow-band reading is unavoidable: overwhelming majorities condemn the violence, but minorities ranging from one in ten to one in five say the magazine had it coming, and much larger majorities — often a clear majority of European Muslim respondents — say the cartoons should never have been published. Both halves of that picture matter for policy.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The depiction question is not a single clean disagreement to be resolved by negotiation. It is a distribution. The 2015 Kouachis, Coulibaly, Mahmoud, and Anzorov sit at the violent extreme — small numerically, lethal in consequence, claiming explicit religious-doctrinal warrant for their actions. The 11–18% French and UK Muslim respondents who decline to condemn the attackers, or who say the publishers "deserved" violence, occupy the next layer — non-violent themselves but ideologically adjacent enough that the recruitment funnel from this layer to the violent core is the central counter-radicalization problem European intelligence services have been working on for a decade. The 42–69% range — French Muslims saying the cartoons were "unnecessary provocation," UK Muslims "deeply offended," majorities of European Muslims who would prefer Western media simply not publish such images — is the illiberal-but-peaceful middle the hub describes. They are not the problem of terrorism. They are the problem of what kind of speech regime gets built where they are a substantial constituency. And the 68–95% counterweight figures — Muslims who reject the violence outright, declare loyalty to their country of residence, support the rule of host law — are the rainbow's left mass that any policy worth defending must be calibrated not to alienate.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Khaled Abou El Fadl — historical-critical work showing the substantial intra-Islamic disagreement on aniconism through the classical and medieval periods. · Hamza Yusuf — Sufi-aligned position broadly permissive of music and figurative art in non-religious contexts. · Mustafa Akyol — argues that strict Salafi aniconism is one historical current, not the classical mainstream. · Wijdan Ali (Jordanian art historian) — sustained scholarly work on the figurative-art tradition within Islamic civilization. · Iranian and Indian classical musicians — the operating contradiction with strict aniconism is the entire history of high Islamic-cultural musical-aesthetic production. · Indonesian and Malaysian Muslim cultural producers — substantial figurative-art and contemporary-music ecosystems in Muslim-majority democratic contexts; comparative model. · Mauritanian and Sufi-tradition clerical responses to ISIS iconoclasm — the most-direct intra-Muslim refutation of the iconoclastic-destruction position from inside the tradition.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The strict-aniconism distribution. What share of European Muslims hold strict-aniconism views vs. accommodating-permissive ones? Phase 2 should examine survey data on attitudes toward figurative art and music.
  • Mosque-architecture preferences in The West. European new-build mosque commissions and design competitions have produced striking variation. Phase 2 should examine specific cases — Cologne Central Mosque, the Mosque of Strasbourg controversy, the Lyon Grand Mosque — for what they reveal about the within-community aesthetic-and-political negotiation.
  • Music-in-school accommodation cases. German and UK educational case law has handled this on a piecemeal basis. Phase 2 should consolidate the pattern.
  • The Bamiyan reconstruction debate. UNESCO has gone back and forth on the question. Phase 2 should examine how the contemporary politics of the question reflect the broader iconoclasm-vs.-preservation question.
  • Indonesian and Malaysian comparative practice. Both countries have substantial figurative-art and music industries operating in Muslim-majority democratic contexts. Phase 2 should engage the comparative case for what it shows about the strict-aniconism position's actual demographic reach.
