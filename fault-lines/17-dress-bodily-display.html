Fault Line · 17

Public dress and bodily display / 公共の場の服装と身体表現: hijab, niqab, burkini, mixed swimming, school PE

The most-litigated dress fault line in The West: Islamic covering requirements (服装), from headscarf to face-veil to full-body swimwear, meeting European school, workplace, and public-space rules. France at the most-restrictive pole; the UK at the most-permissive.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1
Most Sunni and Shia jurisprudential traditions treat some form of female covering (ヴェール) as binding obligation; Western European jurisdictions handle this differently in schools, workplaces, and shared public infrastructure, with France at the most-restrictive pole and the UK at the most-permissive. The litigation (burkini at Grenoble, hijab at the CJEU, abaya in French schools) is the most-developed European case law on Islamic-practice accommodation.

Steelmanned positions

Each side in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamic articulation

Hijab is divine command; covering protects, uncovering exposes

The textual case the position actually makes. Quran 24:31 ("and let them draw their khimar over their bosoms") and 33:59 ("O Prophet, tell your wives, your daughters, and the women of the believers to draw upon themselves their jilbab — that is more suitable that they will be known and not be abused") are read in mainstream Sunni and Twelver Shia exegesis as making covering of hair and body a binding obligation (fard) on every post-pubescent Muslim woman before non-mahram men. The Hanafi/Shafi'i/Maliki/Hanbali readings exempt face and hands; Salafi-Hanbali and some Deobandi readings include them. The Aisha khimar hadith (Sahih al-Bukhari 4758) and the Asma bint Abi Bakr hadith are cited as confirmation. "The hijab is cultural, not religious" is reformist revisionism with no classical grounding, on this reading.

The strongest version goes further. Modesty (haya) is not a private preference but a structural feature of Islamic public ethics: it shields women from the sexualizing male gaze, signals submission to God rather than fashion, and resists the Western pornification of female bodies; that is the actual coercion problem. State bans on hijab, niqab, burkini, and abaya are the secular state forcibly uncovering Muslim women; the same coercive structure as Iran's compulsory hijab, only inverted. France banned the burkini at municipal pools while permitting topless sunbathing; banned the abaya but not crucifix necklaces; targets visibly Muslim women's bodies while leaving Catholic habits, Sikh dastars, and Hasidic dress undisturbed. The position names itself as the defender of female bodily sovereignty against a laïcité that has decided which bodies the public may and may not see.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Choice in both directions; narrow contextual limits

Adult women's dress in public is, by default, a personal choice protected by freedom of conscience and expression. The liberal position opposes both compulsory hijab (Iran 1979–present, Saudi Arabia until 2019, Aceh, Taliban Afghanistan) and compulsory uncovering (the French 2010 face-veil ban, the Swiss 2021 referendum, bans on headscarves in shared public space). The hard cases are not the adult-public-square baseline but three narrow edges: face-identification (security checkpoints, photo IDs, courtroom witness testimony), state-representative neutrality (judges, police, public-school teachers in their official capacity in laïcité regimes), and minors in compulsory schooling, where conscience-formation and child-protection arguments carry real weight.

What the position does not concede is that compulsory hijab is "just another cultural arrangement." The view that an unveiled woman invites harassment, that the state may whip her for showing her hair (Iran 2024), or that her testimony is worth half a man's because her honor depends on veiling: that is incompatible with constitutional equality, and the LGBT/feminist-Muslim insiders making the case are not Western proxies. The strongest liberal version holds two things at once: France's 2010 face-veil ban is paternalistic and disproportionate; and Iran's compulsory-hijab regime, including the post-2022 enforcement waves, is a human-rights violation the liberal-democratic order may name as such. The principle cuts the same in both directions: the woman chooses; the state's interest is narrow and instrumental, not ontological.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest form, in the language its own proponents use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own cross-examination is propaganda, not honest reading.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides, because intellectual honesty requires it; the point is not that both positions are equally defensible, but that both clear the same evidentiary bar.

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamic articulation

The "free choice" claim against the operative coercion record

The diaspora-apologetic version holds that hijab is a free religious choice; that Western state restrictions violate that freely-chosen practice; that the coercion problem runs only one direction.

In every jurisdiction where the conservative position has held state power (Iran 1979–present, Saudi Arabia until 2019, Taliban Afghanistan 1996–2001 and 2021–present, Aceh, ISIS-controlled territory 2014–2017, Sudan under the 1991 Public Order Act), hijab has been compulsory under criminal sanction: flogging, fines, imprisonment, and (Roya Heshmati, Jan 2024 Iran) 74 lashes for unveiling. The "free choice" framing is a presentation strategy for diaspora and export; it is not how the system behaves where it governs. Masih Alinejad's documentation, the post-2022 Mahsa Amini movement, and the 500+ prosecutions under Iran's 2024 "Noor Plan" are the empirical reply.

The unfalsifiable textual-consensus claim

The position rests on the claim that the four Sunni schools and Twelver Shia agreed that hair-covering is fard, settling the textual question and making any Muslim woman who declines to veil ipso facto non-observant.

The scholarship does not hold. Leila Ahmed, A Quiet Revolution (Yale, 2011); Khaled Abou El Fadl's work on constructed "consensus" claims; Asma Lamrabet, Women in the Qur'an (Square View, 2016); Fatima Mernissi's hadith critique in The Veil and the Male Elite (Basic, 1991): each documents that the relevant Quranic verses do not unambiguously specify hair as what is to be covered; that the khimar-as-hair-cover reading is one classical position among several; that mass veiling post-1979 is a 20th-century revivalist phenomenon, not the pre-modern norm across the Maghreb, Levant, or Southeast Asia; and that "veil-as-fard" was contested in classical Andalusian and Ottoman sources. Treating one classical reading as the only legitimate one is an interpretive choice. It has to be defended on the merits, not asserted as settled fact.

The "modesty protects women" claim against the harassment data

Hijab shields women from the sexualizing male gaze; the covering is a freely-chosen defensive shield that works.

The empirical record contradicts it. UN Women/Egypt 2013: 7,000 Egyptian women surveyed; 99.3% had experienced sexual harassment; 96.5% had been groped; over 80% of Egyptian women wear hijab. Iran, where hijab is compulsory, reports systematic street harassment regardless of covering. The "modesty deters harassment" claim gets the causation backwards: societies that mandate covering tend to be societies that hold women, not men, responsible for managing male sexual aggression; that is the variable producing the harassment rate, not the textile. The claim that the burkini is functionally protective survives only if one ignores that the structurally analogous argument is the marital-rape exemption. The protective frame transfers responsibility for male behavior onto female bodies and presents the transfer as a benefit.

The forced-uncovering symmetry argument, examined

French laïcité-driven uncovering (2004 schools, 2010 face-veil, 2023 abaya) is structurally identical to Iran's compulsory-hijab regime; both are the state dictating what women may wear; the West cannot condemn one while practicing the other.

The symmetry is partly real but load-bearing in a way the argument does not survive. Iran: compulsory covering enforced by morality police, criminal prosecution, flogging (74 lashes, Roya Heshmati Jan 2024), 500+ prosecutions post-2022. France: a €150 fine for niqab in public space (Loi 2010-1192); expulsion from school until the abaya is removed; no carceral consequence. Corporal punishment versus administrative fine; criminal sanction versus school dress code: calling these "the same kind of intrusion" is a category error the argument depends on. The narrower argument holds: France's restrictions are paternalistic, disproportionate, and inconsistent with their stated principle. That is defensible. "Macron is Khamenei in different clothing" is not.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The position makes no provision for a Muslim woman who concludes, after honest reflection, that the doctrinal requirement is wrong. The available moves are covering against conscience, family rupture, community ostracism, or leaving Islam, with apostasy consequences in some jurisdictions.

A doctrinal system that imposes lifelong covering from puberty, treats the question as settled before any individual reflection, and offers no internal exit without religious or social sanction is not "diversity within Islam"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one. The "she can remove it any time" answer collapses against honor-based-violence data, diaspora family-pressure fieldwork (UK, German, French Pew/IFOP), and the testimonies of Yasmine Mohammed and Sarah Haider on what unveiling actually costs inside their original communities. "Honest Muslim women want to wear it" is a position one may hold; it is not an argument that has met its interlocutor.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "narrow exceptions" that ratcheted

The liberal position presents its restrictions (face-ID at security checkpoints, state-agent neutrality, child-protective constraints at school) as narrow, principled, and contextually justified, defending the underlying free-choice baseline.

The French arc is the embarrassing data point. The Stasi Commission (Dec 2003) framed the 2004 law as banning all conspicuous religious symbols in public schools: a defensible neutrality position. The arc since has ratcheted exclusively against Islamic dress: the 2010 face-veil ban targeted niqab; the 2016 burkini controversies targeted Muslim women's swimwear; the Conseil d'État's 2022 Grenoble ruling permitted burkini bans in municipal pools while topless sunbathing remained legally protected at French beaches; the 2023 Attal abaya directive (upheld 7 Sep 2023) extended the ban to a garment not mandated by Islamic law. Joan Wallach Scott, The Politics of the Veil (Princeton, 2007), and Stéphanie Hennette-Vauchez's legal-history work both document that "neutral application across religions" has not held in practice: the law operates as a one-religion regime, whatever its drafters intended. The liberal position survives this only if it acknowledges that narrow exceptions ratchet under populist pressure and builds explicit ratchet-resistance into the principle.

The "she's not really choosing" claim and the autonomy paradox

A subset of the liberal position, most visible in French laïcité argumentation and some second-wave-feminist writing, holds that a woman who chooses hijab is not "really" exercising autonomy; that her choice follows from patriarchal socialization or false consciousness; that the state may override it in the name of an autonomy she has not yet attained.

This is the autonomy paradox: "we free you from false consciousness by forcibly removing your scarf." It is not defensible on liberal premises. Either the woman is a competent adult whose choices warrant respect, or she is not; if she is not, the state has replaced one paternalist authority (father, husband, imam) with another (the laïcité official), without solving the autonomy problem and adding the harm of overriding her stated wishes. The position that survives liberal premises is narrower: protect minors from family coercion; require face-identification for narrow security purposes; do not adjudicate whether an adult woman's reasons for her dress are authentic. Asma Lamrabet, Leila Ahmed, and the substantial body of pro-hijab Muslim feminist writing argue that veiling can be a freely-chosen feminist practice; the liberal position has to engage that case rather than dismiss it as managed false consciousness, even while staying rightly skeptical of documented family-pressure cases.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature; it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Hijab in schools (2004), niqab in public space (2011), burkini in pools (2022), abaya in schools (2023)

France's 2004 school law on conspicuous religious symbols, the 2011 niqab/burqa public-space ban (upheld at ECtHR, S.A.S. v. France 2014), the Conseil d'État's 2022 ruling permitting Grenoble's burkini ban in municipal pools, and Education Minister Attal's 2023 abaya/qamis directive: together the most-developed European jurisprudence on Islamic-practice restriction. Critics hold the ratchet has moved from "conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition" to "specifically female Islamic dress." Defenders say laïcité requires consistent application even when one religion is most affected.

CJEU rulings (Achbita 2017, WABE 2021) on workplace hijab have permitted neutral company-policy bans while blocking direct religious targeting.

Sources: France 24 on Grenoble burkini · ECtHR S.A.S. v. France (2014)
US

First Amendment baseline, EEOC accommodation cases, and the Abercrombie ruling

US doctrine starts from the First Amendment Free Exercise Clause and Title VII. EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch (SCOTUS 2015) confirmed an employer cannot reject a hijab-wearing applicant on dress-code grounds when the religious motivation is apparent. Public-school hijab-wearing is constitutionally protected; police and military uniform accommodations are negotiated case by case.

Source: EEOC religious garb guidance
Japan

No general regulation; institutional accommodation per case

Japan has no general regulation of Islamic 服装 (dress); the small Muslim population has not generated public-policy debate on it. Schools and workplaces accommodate case by case; most have permitted hijab on religious-practice grounds. Women-only pools and fitness facilities exist as commercial arrangements.

What would Japanese policy be at French-level Muslim demographic share? The honest answer is hypothetical; Japan has not had to decide.

Case study: France's dress-regulation arc 2003–2024, and Iran's mirror

The French arc runs from the Stasi Commission (Dec 2003) to the 2023 abaya directive: twenty years of legislative, judicial, and executive action that defenders call principled laïcité and critics call a one-religion regime. The Iranian post-Mahsa enforcement waves sit at the inverse pole: the same coercive structure, the opposite direction. Page 04 covers Mahsa's death and the 2022 protest movement; this case tracks the post-2022 enforcement arc and the comparative dress-regulation pattern.

The conservative-Islamic articulation holds hijab is a free choice and Western restrictions are paternalistic intrusion. The French arc and the Iranian inverse together expose what the symmetric framing both reveals and obscures. In twenty years, France has moved from a school-only ban on conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition to an executive directive against a garment not mandated by Islamic law, each step framed as neutral laïcité. In two years since Mahsa's death, Iran escalated: a new Hijab and Chastity bill, the "Noor Plan" enforcement wave, and the resumption of judicial flogging for unveiling. Both regimes regulate what women may wear in public; the methods differ by orders of magnitude (administrative fine vs. 74 lashes), but the structural move is the same on both sides: the state defines the licit female body.

  • 11 Dec 2003 The Stasi Commission (chaired by Bernard Stasi, mediator of the Republic) recommends prohibiting "conspicuous" religious symbols in public schools (kippah, large crosses, Sikh dastar, hijab), explicitly framed as neutral across religions. Chirac endorses. Olivier Roy and Joan Wallach Scott note at the time that application will predictably be asymmetric.
  • 15 Mar 2004 Loi 2004-228 ("Loi Stasi") bans conspicuous religious symbols in French public primary and secondary schools. Application is overwhelmingly directed at hijab; in the first year, ~600 students face dress-related sanctions, ~96% Muslim girls.
  • 11 Oct 2010 Loi 2010-1192 (the "burqa law") prohibits face concealment in public space across France. Sanction: €150 fine and a citizenship course. ~2,000 women estimated to wear niqab in France at the time. Critics (Hennette-Vauchez, Bowen) note the law cannot be defended on security grounds at this scale.
  • 13 Jul 2011 Belgium enacts a parallel face-veil ban; sanction up to €137.50 and possible 7-day imprisonment. Belgian Constitutional Court upholds in Dec 2012.
  • 1 Jul 2014 S.A.S. v. France: ECtHR Grand Chamber, 15 votes to 2, upholds the French face-veil ban under the "living together" (vivre ensemble) framework. Judges Nussberger and Jäderblom dissent, arguing the majority traded concrete individual rights for a sociological abstraction. The ruling becomes the European jurisprudential anchor.
  • 26 Aug 2016 Cannes, Villeneuve-Loubet, and Nice impose burkini-beach bans during the post-Nice-attack summer; Conseil d'État strikes them down. The municipal-pool question stays open until 2022.
  • 7 Mar 2021 Switzerland's face-covering referendum passes 51.2% to 48.8%; law enters force Jan 2025. Implementation is symbolic: Swiss federal data estimates ~30 niqab-wearers nationwide.
  • 21 Jun 2022 The Conseil d'État upholds Grenoble's burkini ban in municipal pools, ruling municipalities may invoke public-service neutrality. The ruling does not nationalize the ban; pools may permit or prohibit. The ratchet extends from school to leisure infrastructure.
  • 16 Sep 2022 Mahsa (Jina) Amini dies in Iran's Guidance Patrol custody after arrest for "improper" hijab (full case study on page 04). The "Woman, Life, Freedom" protest wave follows. Page 04 owns the death itself; this page tracks what came after.
  • 20 Sep 2023 The Iranian Majlis passes the "Hijab and Chastity bill" in initial reading: prison terms up to 10 years, fines up to ~€11,000, travel bans for unveiled women. The Guardian Council returns it once; the law enters force on a 3-year trial basis in late 2024. Amnesty International, the UN Special Rapporteur on Iran, and the Centre for Human Rights in Iran document the arc.
  • 27 Aug 2023 French Education Minister Gabriel Attal directs schools to ban the abaya and qamis as garments worn for religious ostentation, despite neither being mandated by Islamic law. The directive is challenged before the Conseil d'État.
  • 7 Sep 2023 The Conseil d'État upholds the abaya ban. Critics including Stéphanie Hennette-Vauchez and the Council of Europe's Commission against Racism note the directive shifts the ratchet from "religious symbol" to "any garment perceived to signal Muslim identity," dropping the principle that justified the 2004 law.
  • 3 Jan 2024 Iranian activist Roya Heshmati is sentenced to 74 lashes and a fine for appearing unveiled; the sentence is carried out; she publishes a written account. The case crystallizes the post-Mahsa enforcement direction.
  • 13 Apr 2024 Iran launches the "Noor Plan": coordinated morality-police enforcement using CCTV identification, vehicle confiscation, and 500+ documented prosecutions of unveiled women. Masih Alinejad's reporting and Iran Human Rights documentation track the surge.

Sentiment polls: what Muslim women actually think about hijab and bans

The conservative articulation holds Muslim women freely choose hijab; the laïcité argument holds many do not and that bans liberate. Both claims are partly true. Pew data shows hair-covering preferences running from 15% (Turkey) to 96% (Saudi Arabia); the 2020 GAMAAN-Iran shift is the single largest documented attitude swing.

What the arc shows. The conservative-Islamic articulation is right that France's arc has ratcheted from "conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition" (defensible neutrality, 2004) to "a non-religiously-mandated garment associated with Muslim identity" (indefensible on its own stated principle, 2023); the ratchet has moved almost entirely in one direction, against Muslim women's clothing. The Iranian inverse is not symmetric in scale: France fines and expels; Iran flogs and imprisons. But the structural move is the same: the state asserts jurisdiction over what a woman may wear in public. The liberal-democratic principle that protects the unveiled woman in Tehran has to apply in the same form to the veiled woman in Lyon. The polls show a divided Muslim population: 72% of Iranians oppose compulsory hijab; 96% of Saudi respondents endorse some head-covering; 35% of French Muslim women veil; 82% of the French public support the niqab ban. The fault line is not "Muslim women want hijab and Western states forbid it"; it is "the state, Iranian or French, has decided which female body presentations are permitted in public, and the women themselves are heterogeneous and largely not consulted." Roya Heshmati was flogged in Tehran in January 2024 for wearing what she chose; a French Muslim teenager was expelled from school for the same in September 2023. The methods are not equivalent. The question of who owns the female body in public space is.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The asymmetric-enforcement question. Are restrictions on Islamic dress enforced more aggressively than comparable restrictions on Sikh, Jewish, or Christian dress? Phase 2 should compare actual enforcement rates rather than asserting either way.
  • Iranian post-Amini movement implications. The 2022–23 mass refusal of compulsory hijab inside Iran is the largest contemporary case of women rejecting religious-dress compulsion. What does it imply for the European debate where the compulsion sometimes runs the other direction?
  • Schoolchildren as a special case. European debates have treated minors differently from adult-public-space cases. Phase 2 should engage the developmental and conscience-formation arguments specific to the schoolchild context.
  • Burkini as commercial vs. legal question. Most European pools are commercial; the legal-public-pool debate is narrower than the cultural debate suggests. Phase 2 should map the actual scope of the legal restriction against the wider controversy framing.
  • Police, military, and judicial uniform. Where state agents represent state authority directly, the neutrality argument is strongest. Phase 2 should map European variation in police and military hijab accommodation.
