Fault Line · 17

Public dress and bodily display: hijab, niqab, burkini, mixed swimming, school PE

The single most-litigated practical-friction fault line in The West — where Islamic dress requirements (variously interpreted from headscarf to face-covering to full body-covering swimwear) meet European school, workplace, and public-space dress regulations.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Most Islamic jurisprudential traditions require some form of female covering (the minimum standard usually the hijab, more conservative interpretations adding the niqab or face-veil); Western European jurisdictions vary widely in how they handle this in schools, public sector workplaces, and shared public infrastructure (especially swimming pools), with France at the most-restrictive pole and the UK at the most-permissive. The litigation around this fault line — burkini at Grenoble, hijab at the European Court of Justice, abaya in French schools — is the most-developed European case law on Islamic-practice accommodation generally.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamic articulation

Hijab is divine command; covering protects, uncovering exposes

The honest articulation begins with the textual case the position actually makes. Quran 24:31 ("and let them draw their khimar over their bosoms") and 33:59 ("O Prophet, tell your wives, your daughters, and the women of the believers to draw upon themselves their jilbab — that is more suitable that they will be known and not be abused") are read in mainstream Sunni and Twelver Shia exegesis as establishing covering of the hair and body (excluding face and hands in the Hanafi/Shafi'i/Maliki/Hanbali majority readings; including face and hands in the niqab-mandating Salafi-Hanbali and some Deobandi readings) as a binding religious obligation (fard) on every post-pubescent Muslim woman in the presence of non-mahram men. The hadith corpus — especially the khimar hadith of Aisha (Sahih al-Bukhari 4758) and the Asma bint Abi Bakr hadith on what should be visible — is read as confirming the obligation. The position holds that this is the four-schools mainstream, not a fringe reading, and that contemporary "the hijab is cultural, not religious" arguments are reformist revisionism with no classical grounding.

The strongest contemporary version goes further. Modesty (haya) is not a private aesthetic preference but a structural feature of Islamic public ethics: it protects women from the sexualizing male gaze, signals submission to God rather than to fashion industries, and resists the Western pornification of female bodies that the position reads as the actual coercion problem. State bans on hijab, niqab, burkini, and abaya are read as the secular state forcibly uncovering Muslim women — a violation that mirrors, in reverse, the Iranian regime's compulsory hijab. The position also names the asymmetry: France banned the burqini at municipal pools but tolerates topless sunbathing at beaches; banned the abaya in schools but permits crucifix necklaces; targets visibly Muslim women's bodies while leaving Catholic nuns' habits, Sikh dastars, and Hasidic dress essentially undisturbed. The position presents itself as the defender of female bodily sovereignty against a laïcité that has decided which female body presentations the public is required to look at and which it is required not to.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Choice in both directions; narrow contextual limits

Adult women's dress in public is, baseline, a matter of personal choice protected by freedom of conscience and expression. The liberal position therefore opposes both compulsory hijab (Iran 1979–present, Saudi Arabia until 2019, Aceh, Taliban Afghanistan) and compulsory uncovering (the French 2010 face-veil ban, the Swiss 2021 referendum, attempts to ban headscarves on adult women in shared public space). The harder cases are not in the adult-public-square baseline but at three contextual edges: face-identification (security checkpoints, photo IDs, courtroom witness testimony where face is evidence), state-representative neutrality (judges, police officers, public-school teachers in laïcité regimes acting in their official capacity), and minors in compulsory schooling (where the conscience-formation argument and the child-protection-from-family-coercion argument have actual bite, not just culture-war bite).

What the position does not concede is the relativist counter-claim that compulsory hijab is "just another cultural arrangement." The doctrinal view that an unveiled woman is fair game for harassment, that the state is entitled to whip her for showing her hair (Iran 2024), or that her testimony is worth half a man's because her honour depends on her veiling — that view is incompatible with constitutional equality protections, and the LGBT/feminist-Muslim insiders making the case are not Western proxies. The strongest liberal version is therefore willing to say two things simultaneously: France's 2010 face-veil ban is paternalistic, ratchet-friendly, and disproportionately punishes a few thousand women for a symbolic culture-war; and Iran's compulsory-hijab regime, including the post-2022 enforcement waves, is a serious human-rights violation that the liberal-democratic order is entitled to name as such without owing the position equal philosophical standing. The principle is the same in both directions: the woman chooses; the state's interest is narrow and instrumental, not ontological.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamic articulation

The "free choice" claim against the operative coercion record

The diaspora-apologetic version emphasizes that hijab is a free religious choice, that Muslim women freely choose covering, that Western state restrictions therefore violate that freely-chosen practice — implying the coercion problem runs only one direction (state against believer).

In every jurisdiction where the conservative position has actually held state power — Iran 1979–present, Saudi Arabia (until 2019 reform), Taliban Afghanistan 1996–2001 and 2021–present, Aceh province Indonesia, ISIS-controlled territory 2014–2017, Sudan under the 1991 Public Order Act — hijab has been compulsory under criminal sanction including flogging, fines, imprisonment, and (in the Roya Heshmati case Jan 2024 Iran) 74 lashes for unveiling. The "free choice" framing is a presentation strategy for diaspora and export, not a description of how the system behaves where it governs. Masih Alinejad's documentation, the post-2022 Mahsa-Amini movement, and the over 500 unveiled-women cases prosecuted under the 2024 Iranian "Noor Plan" enforcement wave are the empirical reply.

The unfalsifiable-textual-consensus move

The position rests on the claim that the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia agreed that hair-covering is fard (binding religious obligation), and that this convergence settles the textual question — making any Muslim woman who declines to veil ipso facto a non-observant Muslim.

Contemporary scholarship — Leila Ahmed's A Quiet Revolution: The Veil's Resurgence from the Middle East to America (Yale, 2011); Khaled Abou El Fadl's textual work on the constructedness of classical "consensus" claims; Asma Lamrabet's Women in the Qur'an: An Emancipatory Reading (Square View, 2016); Fatima Mernissi's hadith critique in The Veil and the Male Elite (Basic, 1991) — documents that the relevant Quranic verses do not unambiguously specify hair as the body part to be covered; that the khimar-as-hair-cover reading is one classical position among several; that the historical mass-veiling movement of post-1979 is a 20th-century revivalist phenomenon, not the pre-modern norm in the Maghreb, Levant, or Southeast Asia; and that "veil-as-fard" was contested in classical Andalusian and Ottoman sources. Treating one classical reading as the only legitimate one is an interpretive choice that has to be defended on the merits, not asserted as a textual fact.

The "modesty protects women" rationalization against the harassment data

The position holds that hijab protects women from the sexualizing male gaze and reduces sexual harassment — that the covering is, functionally, a defensive shield women freely choose because it works.

The empirical record contradicts the claim at its source. UN Women / Egypt 2013 surveyed 7,000 Egyptian women and found 99.3% had experienced sexual harassment; 96.5% had been groped — and over 80% of Egyptian women wear hijab. Iran (where hijab is compulsory) reports systematic street harassment of women regardless of covering. The "modesty deters harassment" claim mistakes the direction of causation: societies that mandate or socially enforce female covering tend also to be societies that hold women, not men, responsible for managing male sexual aggression — which is the actual variable producing the harassment rate, not the textile. The argument that the burqini at the pool is functionally protective survives only if one ignores that the structurally analogous claim is the marital-rape exemption: "she is safe because she is in the proper relationship." The protective frame transfers responsibility for male behavior onto female bodies and presents the transfer as a benefit to the bodies it constrains.

The forced-uncovering symmetry argument, taken seriously

The position argues that French laïcité-driven uncovering (2004 schools, 2010 face-veil, 2023 abaya) is structurally the same as the Iranian compulsory-hijab regime — both are the state telling women what they may or may not wear — and the West cannot consistently condemn one while practicing the other.

The symmetry is partly real and partly load-bearing in a way the argument does not survive. Iran's regime: compulsory covering enforced by morality police, criminal prosecution, flogging (74 lashes, Roya Heshmati Jan 2024), and at least 500 prosecutions in the post-2022 wave. France's regime: a fine of €150 for niqab in public space (Loi 2010-1192), expulsion from public-school class until the abaya is removed, no carceral consequence. The substantive asymmetry — corporal punishment vs. administrative fine, criminal sanction vs. school dress code — is large enough that calling them "the same kind of intrusion" is a category error the argument relies on. The argument would be stronger if it dropped the symmetry framing and made the narrower point: France's restrictions are paternalistic, ratchet-friendly, disproportionate, and inconsistent with their stated principle. That argument is defensible. "Macron is Khamenei in different clothing" is not.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The position offers no provision for a Muslim woman, born into the doctrinal regime, who concludes after honest reflection that the conclusion is wrong. The available moves are continued covering against conscience, family rupture, community ostracism, or leaving Islam — with the apostasy consequences attached in some jurisdictions.

A doctrinal system that imposes lifelong covering on a category of its own adherents from puberty, treats the question as theologically settled in advance of any individual reflection, and provides no internal mechanism for exit without religious or social sanction, is not "diversity within Islam"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one. The "she can choose to remove it any time" answer collapses the moment one looks at honour-based-violence data, family-pressure documentation (page 04 covers the Mahsa Amini case in detail; the diaspora pattern is documented across UK, German, French Pew/IFOP fieldwork), and the testimonies of women like Yasmine Mohammed and Sarah Haider who describe the cost of unveiling as catastrophic in their original communities. The repeated answer — "honest Muslim women want to wear it" — is a position one can hold; it is not an argument that has met its interlocutor on equal ground.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "narrow exceptions" that ratcheted

The liberal position presents its restrictions (face-ID at security checkpoints, neutrality for state agents, child-protective constraints in school) as narrow, principled, contextually justified — defending the underlying free-choice baseline.

The French case is the embarrassing data point. The Stasi Commission (Dec 2003) framed the 2004 law as banning all conspicuous religious symbols (kippah, large crosses, Sikh turbans, hijab) in public schools — a defensible neutrality argument. The arc since has ratcheted exclusively against Islamic dress: the 2010 face-veil ban (Loi 2010-1192) was framed against niqab specifically; the 2016 burkini-beach controversies targeted Muslim women's swimwear; the Conseil d'État's 2021 Grenoble pool ruling permitted burkini bans in municipal pools while topless sunbathing remained legally protected at French beaches; the 2023 Attal abaya directive (upheld by Conseil d'État 7 Sep 2023) extended the ban to a garment that is not even ostentatiously religious. Joan Wallach Scott's The Politics of the Veil (Princeton, 2007) and Stéphanie Hennette-Vauchez's legal-history work both document that the "neutral application across religions" claim has not held empirically; the law has functioned as a one-religion regime in operation, whatever its drafters' intent. The liberal position survives this only if it acknowledges that its narrow exceptions can ratchet under populist pressure and builds explicit ratchet-protection into the principle, rather than treating each new restriction as the last.

The "she's not really choosing" claim and the autonomy paradox

A subset of the liberal-democratic position (most visible in French laïcité argumentation, but also in some second-wave-feminist writing) holds that a woman who chooses hijab is not "really" exercising autonomy — that her choice is a product of patriarchal socialization, family pressure, or false consciousness, and that the state is entitled to override the choice in the name of an autonomy she has not yet attained.

This is the autonomy paradox: "we will free you from your false consciousness by forcibly removing your scarf." The argument is not defensible on liberal premises. Either the woman is a competent adult whose choices are entitled to respect or she is not; if she is not, the state has just substituted one paternalist authority (her father, husband, imam) for another (the laïcité official), without solving the autonomy problem and while adding the new harm of overriding her stated wishes. The position that survives liberal premises is narrower: the state may protect minors from family coercion; it may require face-identification for narrow security purposes; it may not adjudicate the authenticity of an adult woman's stated reasons for her own dress. Asma Lamrabet, Leila Ahmed, and the substantial body of pro-hijab Muslim feminist writing makes the case that veiling can be a freely-chosen feminist practice; the liberal position has to take that case seriously rather than dismiss it as managed false consciousness — even where it remains rightly skeptical of the family- and community-pressure cases that are also real.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Hijab in schools (2004), niqab in public space (2011), burkini in pools (2022), abaya in schools (2023)

France's law on conspicuous religious symbols in public schools (2004), the niqab/burqa public-space ban (2011, upheld at ECtHR in S.A.S. v. France 2014), the Conseil d'État's 2022 ruling permitting Grenoble's burkini ban in municipal pools, and Education Minister Attal's 2023 abaya/qamis ban in schools represent the most-developed European jurisprudence on Islamic-practice restriction. Critics argue the ratchet has moved from "conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition" to "specifically female Islamic dress." Defenders argue laïcité requires consistent application even when one religion is most affected.

The European Court of Justice rulings (Achbita 2017, Wabe 2021) on workplace hijab restrictions have permitted neutral company-policy bans while restricting direct religious targeting.

Sources: France 24 on Grenoble burkini · ECtHR S.A.S. v. France (2014)
US

First Amendment baseline, EEOC accommodation cases, and the Abercrombie ruling

US constitutional doctrine starts from the First Amendment Free Exercise Clause and Title VII Civil Rights Act protections of religious accommodation in employment. EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch (Supreme Court 2015) confirmed that an employer cannot refuse to hire a hijab-wearing applicant on grounds that the headscarf would violate dress code if the religious motivation was apparent. Public-school hijab-wearing is constitutionally protected; police-uniform and military-uniform accommodations have been negotiated case by case.

Source: EEOC religious garb guidance
Japan

No general regulation; institutional accommodation per case

Japan has no general regulation of Islamic dress; the small Muslim population has not produced a body of public-policy debate on the question. School and workplace accommodation operates case by case; mainstream Japanese schools have generally permitted hijab on a respect-for-religious-practice basis; women's swimming pools and women's-only fitness facilities exist as commercial accommodations.

A future-deep-dive question: what would Japanese policy be at French-level Muslim demographic share? The honest answer is hypothetical and Japan has not had to decide.

Case study: France's dress-regulation arc 2003–2024, and Iran's mirror

The most-developed European regulatory case on Islamic dress is the French arc from the Stasi Commission report (Dec 2003) through the 2023 abaya directive — twenty years of legislative, judicial, and executive action that the position's defenders call principled laïcité and its critics call a one-religion regime. The Iranian post-Mahsa enforcement waves (Sep 2023 onward) sit at the inverse pole: same coercive structure, opposite direction. Page 04 covers Mahsa's death and the 2022 protest movement; this case is the post-2022 enforcement arc and the comparative dress-regulation pattern.

The French arc and the Iranian inverse together expose what the symmetric framing both reveals and obscures. France has, in twenty years, moved from a school-only ban on conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition to an executive directive against a non-religiously-mandated garment, with each step framed as neutral application of laïcité.   • 11 Dec 2003 The Stasi Commission report (chaired by Bernard Stasi, mediator of the Republic) recommends prohibiting "conspicuous" religious symbols in public schools — kippah, large crosses, Sikh dastar, hijab — explicitly framed as neutral across religions. President Chirac endorses the report. Olivier Roy and Joan Wallach Scott both document at the time that the application would predictably be asymmetric.
  • 15 Mar 2004 Loi 2004-228 ("Loi Stasi") is enacted, banning conspicuous religious symbols in French public primary and secondary schools. Application is overwhelmingly directed at hijab; in the first year, ~600 students are referred for dress-related sanctions, of which ~96% concern Muslim girls.
  • 11 Oct 2010 Loi 2010-1192 (the "burqa law") prohibits the concealment of the face in public space anywhere in France. Sanction: €150 fine and a citizenship course. ~2,000 women estimated to wear niqab in France at the time. Critics (Hennette-Vauchez, Bowen) note the law cannot honestly be defended on its stated security grounds at this scale.
  • 13 Jul 2011 Belgium enacts a parallel face-veil ban; sanction includes a fine up to €137.50 and possible imprisonment up to 7 days. The Belgian Constitutional Court upholds in Dec 2012.
  • 1 Jul 2014 S.A.S. v. France — the ECtHR Grand Chamber, by 15 votes to 2, upholds the French face-veil ban under the framework of "living together" (vivre ensemble) as a legitimate aim. Judges Nussberger and Jäderblom dissent forcefully, arguing the majority sacrificed concrete individual rights for a sociological abstraction. The ruling becomes the European jurisprudential anchor.
  • 26 Aug 2016 Cannes / Villeneuve-Loubet / Nice burkini-beach bans during the post-Nice-attack summer; Conseil d'État strikes them down on 26 Aug 2016. The municipal-pool question remains open until 2022.
  • 7 Mar 2021 Switzerland's federal referendum on face-covering bans passes 51.2% to 48.8% nationwide. The cantonal vote was sharply divided; the law enters force in Jan 2025. Implementation is symbolic — Swiss federal data estimates ~30 niqab-wearers nationwide.
  • 21 Jun 2022 The Conseil d'État upholds Grenoble's ban on the burkini in municipal pools, ruling that municipalities may invoke neutrality of the public service to prohibit it. The ruling does not nationalize the ban — pools may permit or prohibit — but extends the regulatory ratchet from school to leisure infrastructure.
  • 16 Sep 2022 Mahsa (Jina) Amini dies in custody of Iran's Guidance Patrol after arrest for "improper" hijab (full case study on page 04). The "Woman, Life, Freedom" protest wave follows. Page 04 owns the death itself; this page tracks what came after.
  • 20 Sep 2023 The Iranian Majlis passes the "Hijab and Chastity bill" in initial reading: prison terms up to 10 years, fines up to ~€11,000, and travel bans for women who appear in public without hijab. The Guardian Council returns it once; the law enters force on a 3-year trial basis in late 2024. Amnesty International, UN Special Rapporteur on Iran, and Centre for Human Rights in Iran document the legislative arc.
  • 27 Aug 2023 French Education Minister Gabriel Attal directs schools to ban the abaya and qamis as garments worn for religious ostentation, despite their not being mandated by Islamic law. The directive is challenged before the Conseil d'État.
  • 7 Sep 2023 The Conseil d'État rejects the challenge and upholds the abaya ban. Critics including Stéphanie Hennette-Vauchez and the Council of Europe's Commission against Racism note the directive moves the ratchet from "religious symbol" to "any garment perceived to signal Muslim identity," eliminating the principle that justified the 2004 law in the first place.
  • 3 Jan 2024 Iranian activist Roya Heshmati is sentenced to 74 lashes plus a fine for appearing unveiled in public; the sentence is carried out, and she publishes a written account. The case crystallizes the post-Mahsa enforcement direction.
  • 13 Apr 2024 Iran launches the "Noor Plan" — a coordinated morality-police enforcement wave including CCTV-based identification, vehicle confiscation, and over 500 documented prosecutions of women appearing unveiled in public. Masih Alinejad's reporting and Iran Human Rights documentation track the surge.

Sentiment polls: what Muslim women actually think about hijab and bans

The conservative articulation claims Muslim women freely choose hijab; the laïcité argument claims many do not, and that bans liberate. Both claims are partly true and the polls show the substantial within-Muslim variation. The Pew Tunisia / Iran / Indonesia data shows hair-covering preferences span from 15% to 96%; the Iran 2014–2020 GAMAAN-survey shift is the single largest documented attitude swing.

What the arc shows about this fault line. The conservative-Islamic articulation is right that France's dress-regulation arc has ratcheted from "conspicuous religious symbols of any tradition" (defensible neutrality, 2004) to "a non-religiously-mandated garment associated with Muslim identity" (indefensible on its own stated principle, 2023) — and the ratchet has been almost entirely one-direction against Muslim women's clothing. The Iranian inverse is not symmetric in scale: the French regime fines and expels; the Iranian regime floggs and imprisons. But the structural move is the same — the state asserts jurisdiction over what a woman is permitted to wear in public — and the liberal-democratic principle that protects the unveiled woman in Tehran has to apply in the same form to the veiled woman in Lyon. The polls show a Muslim-majority population that itself is divided: 72% of Iranians oppose compulsory hijab while 96% of Saudi respondents endorse some head-covering; 35% of French Muslim women veil; 82% of the French public support the niqab ban. The fault line is not "Muslim women want hijab and Western states forbid it"; it is "the state — Iranian or French — has decided which female body presentations are permitted in public, and the women themselves are heterogeneous and largely not consulted on the answer." Roya Heshmati was flogged in Tehran in January 2024 for the same offence (wearing what she chose) for which a French Muslim teenager was expelled from school in September 2023. The methods are not equivalent. The question of who owns the female body in public space is.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The asymmetric-enforcement question. Are restrictions on Islamic dress enforced more aggressively than comparable restrictions on Sikh, Jewish, or Christian dress? Phase 2 should attempt actual enforcement-rate comparison rather than asserting either way.
  • Iranian post-Amini movement implications. The 2022–23 mass refusal of compulsory hijab inside Iran is the largest contemporary case of women rejecting religious-dress compulsion. What does it imply for the European debate where the compulsion is sometimes inverted?
  • Schoolchildren as a special case. The European debates have treated schoolchildren differently from adult-public-space cases. Phase 2 should engage the specific developmental and conscience-formation arguments about the schoolchild context.
  • Burkini as commercial vs. legal question. Most European pools are commercial; the legal-public-pool debate is narrower than the cultural debate suggests. Phase 2 should map the actual scope of the legal restriction against the broader cultural-controversy framing.
  • Police, military, and judicial uniform. Where state agents represent state authority directly, the neutrality argument is strongest. Phase 2 should map the European variation in police-and-military hijab accommodation.
