Fault Line · 01

Political authority / 政治的権威: popular sovereignty vs. divine sovereignty

Who writes the law: the demos (民意), revisable; or God (神権), fixed. Everything downstream rides on the answer.

scaffold · sources gathering Phase 1 · canonical template
Liberal democracies lodge authority in the people; revisable constitutions, elected legislatures. Islamist political theology lodges it in God; sharia, fixed. Parliaments apply, not legislate against. Everything else in this set (gender, blasphemy, apostasy, family law, finance, dress) is downstream.

Steelmanned positions

The strongest, most charitable form of each side. Not strawmen; not the worst proponent on a bad day. The position as its most thoughtful adherents would defend it.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Hakimiyya / 神権: sovereignty belongs to God alone

Sovereignty (al-hakimiyya) is not a constitutional convenience allocated by majority vote. It is a metaphysical fact. God is the lawgiver; any system that legislates against, or independently of, what God has revealed is jahiliyya, the pagan ignorance Islam came to abolish. Mawdudi and Qutb: "Islamic democracy" contradicts itself at the root. A polity cannot hold both that the people are sovereign and that God is sovereign. One gives way.

The strong version concedes shura (consultation), elections of officeholders, and ijtihād on cases the texts do not settle. It refuses one claim: that a parliament's vote overrides the Qur'an or the sunna on matters those sources have already decided. The objection is not to voting. It is to the metaphysical claim that 51% of human voices can repeal a divine command.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Popular sovereignty / 民意: the demos can revise its own laws

Liberal democracy's wager is not that majorities are wise; only that they are correctable. A polity anchored in a fixed, uncodifiable divine source has no internal mechanism for moral progress. It cannot revise slavery, apostasy law, the legal subordination of women; the source does not revise itself. The demos can. That is the point.

The strong version concedes that majorities have done monstrous things; constitutions, courts, and rights enumerations exist to constrain them. "The people" are not a unified moral subject. The claim is narrower: revisable authority in a self-correcting human institution is the precondition for any politics that can learn. Foreclose revision and you have chosen stasis over becoming better.

Why both compress. The hakimiyya position elides ijtihād and centuries of intra-Muslim disagreement about what divine law requires; it pretends the source-text speaks univocally when it manifestly does not. The popular-sovereignty position elides the record: majorities voting for slavery, disenfranchisement, internment. It pretends the corrective machinery worked promptly when it often took a century. Both steelmen are real; both bury their embarrassments.

The friction in The West

Where this fault line has surfaced as policy, court case, or political conflict; EU, US, Japan. Real incidents only.

EU · France

The 2021 "Principles of the Republic" law

France's 2021 anti-separatism law (laïcité) reasserts the primacy of republican secular authority over any competing religious authority. Religious associations declare foreign funding, accept bank-account review, face dissolution if they "promote ideas deemed as encouraging hate" against the Republic's laws. Universally framed; never names Islam; the target is widely understood.

The May 2025 Muslim Brotherhood report (cited in the hub) is the rationale: an organized, non-violent project to shift legitimate authority from the Republic to an Islamic-political reference frame. Pursued via municipal politics, schools, and associations rather than ballots.

Sources: Al Jazeera explainer (2021) · Institut Montaigne brief
US

Anti-sharia statutes and the First Amendment

Between 2010 and 2017, roughly a dozen US states passed or attempted bans on state courts "considering foreign law"; the category was transparently aimed at sharia. Oklahoma's 2010 amendment fell in Awad v. Ziriax (10th Cir., 2012) on Establishment Clause grounds. Targeting one religion's law violates neutrality.

US doctrine inverts the French one. The state may not single out sharia; neither may a sharia ruling override US law. Voluntary religious arbitration is permitted if it does not violate public policy; the same doctrine governs Beth Din (Jewish) and Catholic tribunals.

Source: ACLU case file, Awad v. Ziriax
Japan

Low public salience by demographic structure

Japan has roughly 230,000 Muslims (Tanada, 2018); about 0.18% of the population. Mostly first-generation skilled migrants and students from Indonesia, Pakistan, Bangladesh. The political-authority question (政治的権威) has not surfaced as a public controversy; the demographic mass for a parallel-authority claim does not exist. Japan's immigration regime selects for short-term workers and high-skill residents, not family-reunification chains.

A finding, not a gap. The EU and US debate the boundary because the boundary is contested. Japan does not, because almost no one is contesting it. The honest comparator: what would Japanese policy be if the Muslim share reached French levels? Hypothetical. Not yet a live debate inside Japan.

Source: Tanada Hirofumi, Islam in Japan, Waseda Univ. (2018 estimate, frequently re-cited).

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Muslim thinkers who address this fault line directly. Arguing, from inside the tradition, that hakimiyya as Mawdudi and Qutb formulated it is 20th-century innovation, not classical doctrine.

Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im (Sudanese, Emory Law). Islam and the Secular State (Harvard, 2008): state enforcement of sharia contradicts the Qur'anic insistence on voluntary belief; a secular state is the precondition for genuine observance. · Mohammed Arkoun (Algerian-French, Sorbonne). The Unthought in Contemporary Islamic Thought: Foucauldian critique of Islamist dogmatic-authority claims; "Islamic reason" frozen by exegetical orthodoxies that classical jurists themselves would have contested. · Abdolkarim Soroush (Iranian). Argued from inside Iran's clerical establishment that religious knowledge is human, fallible, historically situated; cannot ground a coercive theocracy. · Nasr Hamid Abu Zayd (Egyptian; declared apostate by an Egyptian court, 1995; fled to the Netherlands). Historicist Qur'anic hermeneutics; died in exile. · Asma Afsaruddin (Indiana University). Historian of Islamic political thought; the classical sources hold robust pluralist and consultative resources that the Mawdudi/Qutb hakimiyya doctrine actively suppresses. · Wael Hallaq (McGill / Columbia). More pessimistic. The Impossible State (2013): the modern state form is itself incompatible with sharia properly understood; cuts against both Islamists and liberal-reformers. A serious objection on its own terms. · Grace Mubashir & the New Age Islam circle. Contemporary South Asian critics; direct refutations of Qutb and Mawdudi on classical-jurisprudential grounds.

Sources collected

Primary religious-political texts; academic studies; survey data; policy documents. Phase 1 establishes the base. Phase 2 will deepen and triangulate.

Primary text · Islamist
Sayyid Qutb, Ma'alim fi al-Tariq (Milestones), 1964. The foundational mid-20th-century hakimiyya text. Written in Nasser's prisons. Established the move that any government not implementing sharia is in a state of jahiliyya.
Method: Primary theological-political treatise. Read it directly; don't rely on secondary summaries. A.B. Al-Mehri's English translation is widely available; multiple online editions exist.
Wikipedia overview & editions · ICIT Digital Library full text
Primary text · Islamist
Abul A'la Mawdudi, formulation of "theo-democracy." Articulated across The Islamic Law and Constitution, The Process of Islamic Revolution, and the Qur'anic commentary Tafhim al-Qur'an. The other 20th-century pillar of hakimiyya; pre-dates Qutb and shaped him.
Method: Read alongside Qutb. South-Asian and Arab versions of the same doctrine. Mawdudi is procedurally more accommodating (accepts elections, consultation); identical to Qutb on the core. Human assemblies cannot legislate against revealed law.
Cambridge: Maududi's "theodemocracy" (academic)
Academic critique · Reformist Muslim
Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im, Islam and the Secular State: Negotiating the Future of Shari'a, Harvard University Press, 2008.
Method: Sustained intra-Islamic argument. State enforcement of sharia contradicts Qur'anic voluntarism. The "Islamic state" concept is a borrowing from European political theory, not a feature of classical Islamic governance.
Harvard University Press · UNESCO Courier interview
Survey data · Cross-national
Pew Research Center, The World's Muslims: Religion, Politics and Society, April 2013. Country-level data on support for "sharia as the official law of the land." The single most-cited dataset on the political-authority question.
Method: 38,000+ face-to-face interviews; 80+ languages; 39 countries; 2008–2012. Support ranges from 8% (Azerbaijan) to 99% (Afghanistan). High-support clusters: South Asia (84% median), MENA (74%). Low-support: Central Asia (12%), SE Europe (18%).
Caveat the hub flagged: "Support for sharia" is not one thing. Pew's follow-up shows Indonesian respondents mostly mean family-law courts; Afghan respondents mostly mean full criminal code with hudud. One percentage compresses radically different policy preferences. Use the country-level breakdown, not the global average.
Pew Research overview · Full PDF report
Academic · Conceptual history
Andrew F. March, "Theorizing Popular Sovereignty in the Colony: Abul A'la Maududi's Theodemocracy," Review of Politics, 2019.
Method: Intellectual history. How Mawdudi adapted European sovereignty discourse (Bodin, Rousseau, Schmitt) into the vocabulary of hakimiyya. The doctrine is not classical Islam unchanged. It is a 20th-century synthesis that absorbs the European debate it claims to refute.
Cambridge Core article
Policy document · France
République française, Loi n° 2021-1109 du 24 août 2021 confortant le respect des principes de la République ("Anti-separatism" / "Principles of the Republic" law). The clearest case of a Western state legislating to defend secular constitutional authority against an alternative-authority project.
Method: Primary legislation. Read directly. Provisions are universally framed (any religion, any association); the political occasion is explicitly Islamist separatism. The gap between text and occasion is the source of the controversy.
Légifrance, full text of the law · Al Jazeera explainer
Court case · United States
Awad v. Ziriax, 670 F.3d 1111 (10th Cir. 2012). Struck down Oklahoma's "Save Our State Amendment" (2010), which barred state courts from considering "Sharia Law" or "international law." Singling out one religion violated the Establishment Clause.
Method: Federal circuit opinion. The US baseline: anti-sharia legislation that names sharia specifically is unconstitutional; voluntary religious arbitration is permitted if it does not violate public policy.
ACLU case page · Casetext opinion
Counter-position · Reformist Muslim
Wael B. Hallaq, The Impossible State: Islam, Politics, and Modernity's Moral Predicament, Columbia University Press, 2013.
Method: Sustained legal-historical argument. The modern nation-state (bureaucratic, sovereign, territorial) is structurally incompatible with sharia as classically understood. Cuts against both Islamists (who want a sharia state) and liberal-secularist reformers (who want a secular state with reformed sharia inputs). Worth engaging because it refuses both standard positions.
Columbia University Press

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Hakimiyya: 20th-century innovation, or classical doctrine? How much of Mawdudi–Qutb is genuine retrieval of pre-modern jurisprudence; how much is 20th-century synthesis absorbing European sovereignty theory? The intra-Islamic critique (An-Na'im, Arkoun, Hallaq, from different angles) leans toward synthesis. Phase 2 should weigh the evidence, not assert.
  • The Indonesia case. The world's largest Muslim population; a constitutional democracy; Pancasila negotiates the religion-authority question without picking pure secularism or pure theocracy. What does the compromise look like in legal practice? Stable, eroding, or strengthening?
  • UK sharia councils. The closest Western analog to a parallel-authority ecosystem operating in plain sight. The 2018 independent review (Siddique) and the contested 2018 Casey Review reach different conclusions on scope and harm. Phase 2 should map jurisdictional reach, the women's-rights debate, and practical interaction with English family law.
  • "Islamic democracy" in 2026. Tunisia post-Ennahda; Turkey after Erdoğan's constitutional changes; Malaysia; Bangladesh. Cases where the political-authority question is being negotiated in real time. Country-by-country status as of late 2026.
  • The Hallaq objection on its own terms. If the modern state form is genuinely incompatible with classical sharia, Islamist projects are incoherent; Western policy can stop pretending the choice is between two legitimate state-forms. Phase 2 owes Hallaq a serious treatment, not a one-line dismissal.
