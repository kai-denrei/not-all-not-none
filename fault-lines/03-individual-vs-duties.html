Fault Line · 03

Individual rights vs. religious duties / 個人の権利と宗教的義務: autonomy or communal conformity

Whether the moral unit is the rights-bearing individual whose conscience is sovereign, or the believer whose duties to God and community precede and constrain any individual claim.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic ethics treats the autonomous individual as the locus of rights — including the right to leave one's religion, marry across or outside it, eat what one chooses, and dissent from one's parents' creed — while political-Islamist ethics treats those choices as either non-rights (apostasy is not a "right" but a wrong with consequences) or duties owed to the umma. The friction is not that one side has rights and the other has duties; it is which entity gets to define which.

Steelmanned positions

Each in its strongest articulation.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

The umma constitutes the believer

The Western frame of the "rights-bearing individual" is a 17th-century Lockean construct, not a universal anthropology. Islam offers a different — and the strongest version would say truer — picture: the human being is constituted by relationships of obligation, to God first and to the umma second. Rights exist, but they are derived from duties rather than prior to them; one earns them by fulfilling the obligations the relationships impose.

The strongest version concedes substantial individual freedom in the private sphere — taste, vocation, friendship, much of family life — and points to classical Islamic legal protections of property, contract, and bodily integrity. What it refuses is the move that turns "I want to" into a trump card against duties owed to God, especially in the cases (apostasy, blasphemy, public dress, marriage outside the faith) where the individual claim and the communal duty most visibly collide.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Conscience cannot be conscripted

The liberal-democratic settlement does not deny that humans are constituted by relationships, duties, and traditions. Communitarian critics from MacIntyre to Sandel made that point against thin-Lockean liberalism a generation ago and most contemporary liberals concede it. What the settlement insists is narrower: that no relationship, duty, or tradition can be coercively enforced by the state or the community against the conscience of the individual subject to it.

The strongest version freely admits that the autonomous-individual frame produces atomization, weakened communal bonds, and other costs the communitarian critics describe correctly. It claims only that those costs are worth paying compared to the alternative — communities authorized to compel members' belief and behavior — and points to the long history of religious communities exercising precisely that authority badly when given it. The point is not the metaphysics; it is the constraint on coercion.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it.

Cross-examination

This fault line has the cleanest empirical anchor of any on the site — named ex-Muslims, named fatwa-targets, a body count. The conservative-Islamist articulation has nowhere to retreat that the case study does not follow it.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "duties enrich, not constrain" framing

The umma-constituted-believer picture concedes substantial individual freedom in private taste, vocation, friendship, and family life — and presents the duty-structure as a relational anthropology that enriches the believer rather than coercing them. Rights are derived from duties; the language of "constraint" is a Western projection that misreads a fundamentally different human picture.

The picture is presentable in the seminar room. It cannot survive its own page's case study. Hamed Abdel-Samad has lived under 24-hour Berlin police protection since 2013; Salman Rushdie was stabbed 15 times in 2022 in fulfilment of a 1989 fatwa; Imad Iddine Habib was sentenced in absentia to seven years' imprisonment for founding the Council of Ex-Muslims of Morocco; Maryam Namazie was attacked at Goldsmiths. Whatever "enrichment" the duty-structure offers those who accept its premises, what it visibly offers those who reject them is named, organised, lethal pressure. A relational anthropology whose answer to "I no longer believe" is a fatwa is not a richer human picture than the autonomy frame; it is the autonomy frame's primary historical adversary, with current operating capacity. The picture has to account for what it does to its dissenters, not for what it offers its conformists.

The "shunning is a private community matter" distinction

The page's own US friction card concedes that ex-Muslims in The West face community-imposed consequences — shunning, family rupture, threats — but not state penalty. The position can argue that this leaves liberal individual-rights protection structurally intact: the state does not punish, the community freely associates and disassociates, and that is itself a form of communal autonomy a liberal order should respect.

The distinction holds formally and falls apart operationally. If the community can credibly threaten total social annihilation — loss of family, marriage, custody, employment within communal economic networks, physical safety in some cases — for a doctrinal choice, then "the state does not punish" describes the legal architecture but not the lived constraint on conscience. The page's open-questions section names this honestly as one of the genuinely unresolved problems in liberal political theory. The position cannot have it both ways: either the communal-enforcement mechanism is real and operationally compelling (in which case "private community matter" is a euphemism for non-state coercion), or it is not real (in which case the EXMNA / CEMB / Zentralrat helpline caseload is fictive). The case-study record forecloses the second answer.

The "Western individualism is decadent" tu-quoque

The autonomous-individual frame produces atomization, loneliness, family breakdown, declining birth rates, and a measurable mental-health crisis among the young. The Islamic relational anthropology is, on these axes, healthier; the West has no standing to lecture about a frame whose own outcomes are visibly degenerative.

Half-correct on premise, irrelevant to the comparison. Western liberal societies do show the costs the position names — and the communitarian critique inside Western political philosophy (MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor) has made the same observations from inside the tradition. None of that bears on the question of whether communities should be authorised to coerce belief. A relational anthropology can be defended as preferable on flourishing grounds without the coercive enforcement apparatus; the position's own reformist current (Akyol, the Mu'tazilite tradition Akyol revives) does precisely this. The tu-quoque collapses the distinction between (a) believing the West has overshot autonomy at the expense of community and (b) endorsing the operational fatwa surface the case-study documents. The first is a defensible position widely held inside Western thought; the second is what Abdel-Samad lives under police protection because of.

The "apostasy is statistically rare so it's a non-issue" gambit

The cases the page names — Rushdie, Abdel-Samad, Habib, the Goldsmiths disruption, the Western ex-Muslim infrastructure — are a vanishingly small fraction of the global Muslim population. To organise the religion-state question around them is to let the most marginal currents define the centre.

The gambit confuses incidence with structural meaning. The doctrinal apparatus that licenses fatwa, apostasy execution, and communal enforcement is not the practice of most contemporary Muslims; it is, however, the operative law of multiple states (Iran, Saudi Arabia, Pakistan, Mauritania, Afghanistan) and the operative position of multiple internationally-organised currents (the Iranian state-fatwa apparatus, Tehreek-e-Labbaik, ISIS). The relevant measure is not "what fraction of Muslims act on it" but "what fraction of the doctrinal mainstream condemns the apparatus categorically and publicly." The Pew 2013 conditional-question result — 88% Egypt, 79% Afghanistan, 76% Pakistan supporting state apostasy penalty among sharia-as-state-law respondents — is the answer the position needs to address rather than minimise. The case-study people are not statistically representative because they are the survivors and dissenters; statistical representativeness is a property of the regime, not of those it targets.

The "rights derived from duties" metaphysical move

The strongest theological version retreats to the position that rights are not prior to duties but derived from them — a coherent picture on its own metaphysical premises about the human creature's relationship to God and umma, and one liberal critique applies premises the position does not share.

Coherent on premises and trivial as defence. Every system is internally coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether the premises are defensible — whether the theological case for treating individual conscience as derivative of communal duty (rather than merely traditionally so treated) survives examination, particularly given that the same scriptural tradition contains the explicit verse "there is no compulsion in religion" (Quran 2:256) which the reformist current (An-Na'im, Akyol) reads as foundational rather than abrogated. The position cannot make its case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion, and it cannot do so without contradicting an explicit textual constraint its own canon includes. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument; on this fault line, even the floor is contested inside the tradition.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency-of-individual-conscience objection

The liberal-individual-conscience frame is a 17th-century post-Westphalian achievement, established only after Europe exhausted itself on confessional war. Treating it as a universal human anthropology rather than a contingent settlement is itself a parochialism — the very move the position accuses the conservative-Islamist side of making in reverse.

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it is absent. The honest answer is that individual conscience as a state-protected right is recent, post-Westphalian, and worked out under specific historical pressure — and that the discovery is no less binding for being late, the way the abolition of slavery is no less binding for being a 19th-century achievement. The defensible move is to defend the principle on its merits, not its provenance, and to acknowledge that the deeper claim — that humans have a capacity for conscience that should not be coerced — is consistent with a wide range of metaphysical pictures (Christian, Mu'tazilite Islamic, secular humanist, Buddhist) and does not require the specific Lockean apparatus to support it. What the position cannot defend is the claim that individual conscience is a uniquely Western contribution to the species' self-understanding; reformist Muslims, ex-Muslims, and the Quran 2:256 tradition are all available counter-evidence inside the tradition the position is supposed to be foreign to.

The shunning question the page itself flags

The page's own open-questions section names a genuine unresolved problem: if the state may not punish apostasy but the community may shun the apostate to social annihilation, has the individual-rights baseline really been protected? The liberal frame has no fully satisfying answer; it relies on the assumption that exit from a coercive community is feasible, which the case-study record shows is precisely the question.

The honest reply is that the frame is incomplete here, and that the existing institutional architecture (asylum, public-funded police protection for fatwa targets, ex-Muslim organisations as alternative-community infrastructure, anti-discrimination law extended to apostasy-based discrimination) is the operational answer being assembled in real time and is itself partial. The defensible liberal position is not that the question is solved; it is that the framework is the only one in which the question can be openly named, debated, and partially answered without the questioner risking prosecution. A regime that punishes the asking of the question (most of the page's case-study source jurisdictions) has not solved the problem either — it has only foreclosed inquiry. The asymmetry is the entire substantive case for preferring the incomplete answer to the foreclosed one.

The cross-examination is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. On this fault line the asymmetry is sharpest — one side has named, organised, currently-operating coercion against dissenters; the other side has an incomplete framework that nonetheless lets the question be asked. The honest reading is not that both sides are equivalently flawed. It is that the people who took the risk of leaving — and in some cases died for it — are the ones who get to define what an honest answer looks like.

The friction in The West

EU · Egypt-NL

The Nasr Hamid Abu Zayd case (1995, exile to Netherlands)

An Egyptian sharia court declared the Cairo University Qur'anic-studies professor an apostate for his historicist reading of the text and annulled his marriage on the grounds that a Muslim woman cannot remain married to a non-Muslim. He fled to the Netherlands and held a Leiden chair until his death in 2010. The case is the textbook European example of what intra-Muslim individual conscience faces when the communal-duties frame is enforced by a state.

European receiving states routinely grant asylum to ex-Muslims, blasphemy defendants, and apostates from Iran, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Bangladesh — institutionalizing the individual-conscience principle as a refugee-protection ground.

Source: Nasr Abu Zayd biography & case
US

Ex-Muslims of North America & the apostasy-shunning question

Ex-Muslims in the US do not face state penalty (the First Amendment forecloses it) but face documented community-imposed consequences: shunning, family rupture, threats. Sarah Haider and Muhammad Syed founded Ex-Muslims of North America (EXMNA, 2013) precisely to provide community for those who lose theirs. The friction is not constitutional but social — and the question of whether community-level enforcement of religious belonging counts as a violation of conscience is unresolved in liberal political theory.

Japan

Low salience plus a different communitarian baseline

Japan's small Muslim population means ex-Muslim and conscience cases are individually rare and not a public-policy issue. A more interesting comparator question: Japan's own social fabric is significantly more communitarian than the Anglo-Saxon liberal model, so the abstract individual/community fault line lands differently. Japanese converts to Islam exist (~10,000 estimated) and exit issues are mostly individual-family matters, not state ones.

Case study: the ex-Muslim diaspora and the cost of leaving in The West

If individual-rights vs. religious-duties has an empirical object in The West, it is the ex-Muslim. Not the abstract apostate of the jurisprudence textbooks but the named, photographed, organisation-affiliated person who has publicly left Islam in London, Berlin, Cologne, Toronto, Washington, and stayed in The West to argue for the right to do so. The cluster below is the institutional history of that effort over roughly two decades — its founding organisations, its disrupted public events, its members living under permanent police protection, and the survey data on what the wider population of those raised Muslim in The West actually does. It is the on-the-ground answer to the textbook question of what individual conscience faces when it tries to leave.

The Western ex-Muslim infrastructure (2007–2025) and the intimidation pattern around it

January 2007 – May 2025 · CEMB London, Zentralrat der Ex-Muslime Cologne, EXMNA Washington, Council of Ex-Muslims of Morocco-in-exile · multiple jurisdictions · all named organisations and individuals are public, on-the-record

The story does not begin in 2007. It begins in 1989, with the Khomeini fatwa on Salman Rushdie — the moment that established, for everyone watching, that leaving or criticising Islam in The West would not be a purely private matter. What 2007 added was institutional infrastructure: in January, Iranian-born Mina Ahadi launched the Zentralrat der Ex-Muslime in Cologne; in June, Maryam Namazie launched the Council of Ex-Muslims of Britain (CEMB) at Westminster, modelled directly on Ahadi's. Both founders had themselves fled fatwa-issuing regimes — Ahadi sentenced to death in absentia by Iran after her husband and five guests were executed in 1981, Namazie a long-time Iranian dissident. The pattern that followed — disrupted campus events, permanent police protection for named individuals, families ruptured at scale documented by the organisations' own helplines — is what the abstract "freedom to leave" looks like once you stop talking about it in the seminar room.

  • 14 Feb 1989 Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini issues the fatwa against Salman Rushdie over The Satanic Verses, with a $3M bounty. Rushdie enters British police protection for nine years. The fatwa is the founding event of the Western ex-Muslim / Muslim-critic-of-Islam predicament: leaving or critiquing Islam in The West is now an extraterritorial security problem, not a private matter.
  • 21 Jan 2007 Mina Ahadi founds the Zentralrat der Ex-Muslime in Cologne — the first organised public ex-Muslim body in Europe. Ahadi had fled Iran in 1981 after her husband and five dinner guests were executed by the regime; she had been sentenced to death in absentia. The press conference was held under police protection; she has lived under police protection ever since.
  • 22 Jun 2007 Maryam Namazie launches the Council of Ex-Muslims of Britain (CEMB) at Westminster, explicitly modelled on Ahadi's German body. CEMB later reports that in 2007 it was "hard pressed to find 25 people" willing to publicly break the apostasy taboo; by the mid-2020s its public-membership model and helpline have made it the institutional reference point for British ex-Muslims.
  • 2013 Sarah Haider and Muhammad Syed co-found Ex-Muslims of North America (EXMNA) in Washington, DC — initially as a private support network for those losing family and community, later expanding to public advocacy. EXMNA's documented service model is itself the most rigorous available record of the social-cost question: shunning, marriage rupture, custody disputes, threats.
  • May 2013 Imad Iddine Habib founds the Council of Ex-Muslims of Morocco — the first such organisation in any Muslim-majority Arab country. Within months he is forced into hiding, then exile to the UK; he is later sentenced in absentia to seven years' imprisonment in Morocco for "violating Islamic morals." He becomes a CEMB spokesperson in London. The case is the cleanest single illustration that the apostasy problem in The West and in Muslim-majority states is one continuous problem with one set of refugees.
  • June 2013 Egyptian-German political scientist Hamed Abdel-Samad — author of Mohamed: Eine Abrechnung and Der islamische Faschismus — is targeted by twin fatwas from Egyptian clerics Mahmoud Shaaban and Assem Abdel Maged after a Cairo lecture comparing political Islam to fascism. The German Federal Government summons the Egyptian Chargé d'Affaires; Abdel-Samad enters 24-hour state-criminal-police protection in Berlin, where he remains to this day. The fatwa has not been rescinded.
  • 26 Sep 2015 Warwick University Student Union initially bans Namazie from speaking on the grounds that she would "incite hatred" against Muslims. The decision is reversed within days under public pressure — but the precedent is set: the Western universities are now part of the intimidation surface, not a refuge from it.
  • 30 Nov 2015 Namazie speaks on "apostasy, blasphemy and free expression in the age of ISIS" at Goldsmiths University, hosted by the Atheist, Secularist and Humanist Society. Members of the Goldsmiths Islamic Society arrive, switch off the projector mid-talk, heckle, and gesture aggressively at audience members. The Goldsmiths Feminist and LGBTQ Societies issue statements in support of the Islamic Society's "safe space" against Namazie — a moment that crystallises the alliance the wider hub describes as the blue-band's operational tell.
  • 22–24 Jul 2017 The International Conference on Freedom of Conscience and Expression at Logan Hall (UCL/Conway Hall venues), London, organised by CEMB and One Law for All — described by organisers as "the largest gathering of ex-Muslims in history" with 70+ speakers from 30 countries. The 99-balloon installation memorialises those killed or imprisoned for blasphemy and apostasy. Egyptian ex-Muslim YouTuber Ismail Mohamed is prevented by Egyptian authorities from leaving the country to speak.
  • 12 Aug 2022 Hadi Matar (24, Lebanese-American, New Jersey) stabs Salman Rushdie 15 times on stage at the Chautauqua Institution, NY, blinding him in one eye and partially paralysing his hand. Federal prosecutors later allege Matar was acting in furtherance of the 1989 Iranian fatwa, citing his consumption of a 2006 Hassan Nasrallah speech endorsing it. Matar is convicted of attempted murder February 2025, sentenced to 25 years May 2025; a federal terrorism case is concurrent. Thirty-three years after the fatwa was issued, the fatwa worked.
  • May 2025 The French Ministry of the Interior's Frères musulmans et islamisme politique en France report (75pp, made public 20 May 2025) names the institutional-capture project the ex-Muslim diaspora has been describing for two decades — and treats the non-violent intimidation surface, rather than terrorism alone, as the strategically more serious threat. The ex-Muslim organisations' two-decade documentary record becomes, retroactively, primary-source evidence for a state-level threat assessment.
"When the Council of Ex-Muslims of Britain started in June 2007, we were hard pressed to find 25 people who would come out publicly to break the apostasy taboo. Today we are part of an international ex-Muslim community — a tsunami of atheism." — Maryam Namazie, CEMB, looking back at the founding (interview, Freethinker, March 2022).

Sentiment polls: what the underlying populations actually say

The polls below answer the question that ex-Muslim organisations exist to make answerable: in The West, of those raised Muslim, what fraction actually leaves; of those raised Muslim, what fraction holds the fundamentalist beliefs that make leaving costly; and of the wider Muslim-majority context they came from, what fraction supports state-enforced apostasy penalties? The picture is the rainbow band: a substantial leaving-cohort exists in The West (Pew 2017: 24% of US-raised Muslims), the fundamentalist tail among European Muslims is much larger than is comfortable to say (Koopmans: ~44%), and the home-country baselines for state enforcement are extreme (Pew 2013: 88% Egypt). All three layers matter for policy and none of them is the whole story.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The individual-rights-vs.-religious-duties question is not, in The West, an academic debate between two equally-armed traditions. It is an asymmetry. On one side: the named, photographed ex-Muslims of CEMB, Zentralrat, EXMNA, the Council of Ex-Muslims of Morocco-in-exile — most of them women, most of them publishing under their own faces, several of them under permanent police protection in EU capitals, two of them (Rushdie, Abdel-Samad) the targets of operationalised state-issued fatwas that crossed continents and, in Rushdie's case in 2022, finally connected. On the other side: the wider population the Pew and Koopmans surveys describe — most of whom are not the problem, a substantial fundamentalist tail of whom are, and a Muslim-majority home-country baseline (Pew 2013) where state-enforced apostasy penalty is the modal view conditional on wanting sharia at all. The defensible Western position is not "both sides have a point" — it is that the right to leave has named human beings willing to die for it, the cost of leaving is documented, and the institutional infrastructure that makes leaving survivable is itself one of the things that distinguishes a liberal democracy from the regimes the founders of these organisations fled. Anything less is a failure of reciprocity to the people who took the risk first.

Case · Hamed Abdel-Samad fatwa
Hamed Abdel-Samad — Egyptian-German political scientist, target of June 2013 fatwas from clerics Mahmoud Shaaban and Assem Abdel Maged following his Cairo lecture comparing political Islam to fascism. Has lived under 24-hour Berlin state-criminal-police protection since.
Method: The single clearest standing-illustration in Europe that the fatwa surface is current, not a 1989 Rushdie-era artefact. The German Federal Government summoned the Egyptian Chargé d'Affaires; the fatwa has not been rescinded.

Case · Goldsmiths disruption / campus
National Secular Society and Eastlondonlines coverage of the 30 November 2015 disruption of Maryam Namazie's talk at Goldsmiths University by members of the Goldsmiths Islamic Society — and the subsequent statements of solidarity with the Islamic Society issued by the Goldsmiths Feminist Society and Goldsmiths LGBTQ+ Society.
Method: Contemporaneous reporting plus video evidence (the projector-shutdown is on tape). The case is the cleanest single illustration in Britain of the alliance between Islamist campus organising and the progressive-left "safe space" framing — what the wider hub describes as the blue-band's operational tell.

Case · Salman Rushdie 2022
Stabbing of Salman Rushdie, Chautauqua Institution, NY, 12 August 2022 — perpetrator Hadi Matar (24, Lebanese-American) convicted of attempted murder February 2025, sentenced to 25 years May 2025. Federal prosecutors allege furtherance of the 1989 Iranian fatwa.
Method: The case closes the loop the 1989 fatwa opened. Read together with Abdel-Samad: the operational fatwa surface against Western-resident Muslim and ex-Muslim critics is not a historical curiosity, it is current state-issued policy from at least one regime, with measurable consequences within US territory in the 2020s.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Does community shunning count as violation of conscience? If the state may not punish apostasy but the community may shun the apostate to social annihilation, has the individual-rights baseline really been protected? This is one of the genuinely unresolved questions in liberal political theory.
  • The communitarian critique on its own terms. MacIntyre, Sandel, and Taylor's arguments against thin-Lockean individualism overlap substantially with the Islamist critique. What does it look like to take both seriously without conceding the coercive enforcement question?
  • Numbers on UK / EU ex-Muslims. EXMNA, the Council of Ex-Muslims of Britain (Maryam Namazie), and similar German and Dutch organizations have collected anecdotal data; Phase 2 should look for any rigorous prevalence and outcomes survey work.
  • The Indonesia and Turkey cases. Both are Muslim-majority democracies with active individual-rights jurisprudence. What does the case law on apostasy, conversion, and inter-religious marriage actually look like, and where is it trending?
  • Saba Mahmood's Politics of Piety — the most-cited academic argument that the autonomy frame itself is a culturally specific imposition, illustrated through the Egyptian women's mosque movement. Phase 2 owes Mahmood's argument a real engagement.
