Fault Line · 16

Banking and finance / 銀行と金融: riba/interest prohibition, parallel financial institutions

Where the Islamic prohibition on interest (riba) has produced a parallel sharia-compliant finance ecosystem that operates within Western financial regulation but uses different instruments — to growing or shrinking institutional acceptance depending on the jurisdiction.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Conventional Western finance is structured around interest-bearing loans, bonds, and deposit accounts; Islamic jurisprudence prohibits riba (variously interpreted as all interest or as predatory interest specifically) and has produced a parallel suite of sharia-compliant instruments — murabaha cost-plus sale, ijara leasing, musharaka partnership, sukuk asset-backed certificates — operating inside Western regulatory frameworks. The friction is primarily commercial and operational rather than ideological; the question is whether the parallel system funds Islamist political organizations through certification revenues.

Steelmanned positions

Conservative-Islamist articulation

The riba prohibition is divinely-ordered moral economics; sharia-compliant finance is the operative answer

The honest articulation begins with the textual case the position actually makes. Quranic verses 2:275–280, 3:130, 4:160–161, and 30:39 are read as establishing the absolute prohibition of riba — interpreted in classical fiqh and the contemporary AAOIFI standards as the predetermined excess paid on a loan of money or fungible commodities — as one of the Quran's most categorical economic commandments, paired with the unique threat of "war from Allah and His Messenger" (2:279) for those who persist in it. Hadith literature elaborates that riba includes seven specific commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) and any unequal or deferred exchange of like for like; the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia converge on the substance and disagree only on application to fiat currency. The position holds that this is not a marginal or culturally-contingent prohibition but a mainstream-Sunni and mainstream-Shia operative consensus, that the prohibition shares moral-economic ancestry with the Aristotelian critique of usury and the medieval Catholic prohibition (which was the operative European law until the Reformation period), and that the contemporary sharia-compliant finance industry — Islamic banking, sukuk, takaful insurance, murabaha cost-plus, ijara leasing, musharaka and mudaraba partnership — is the operative jurisprudential answer to running an Islamic-compliant economic life within a global financial system structured around interest.

The strongest contemporary version concedes — through Mufti Taqi Usmani's November 2007 critique that ~85% of Gulf sukuk were not sharia-compliant — that contemporary practice has often substituted formal-rather-than-substantive compliance, that Mahmoud El-Gamal's Islamic Finance: Law, Economics, and Practice (Cambridge 2006) has identified murabaha-as-disguised-loan, tawarruq, and bay' al-inah as practices where the risk-sharing pretence collapses on examination, and that the AAOIFI Bahrain standards have been openly contested by the Malaysian school (which permits bay' al-inah and tawarruq) producing a real intra-Muslim methodological dispute about what "sharia-compliant" actually means. What is uniformly held in mainstream Sunni and Shia jurisprudence — and is the operative standard for AAOIFI, Bank Negara Malaysia, OJK Indonesia, the Iranian Central Bank, the State Bank of Pakistan, and the Islamic finance windows of HSBC Amanah, Lloyds, Standard Chartered Saadiq, Citi Islamic, and the Gulf majors — is that the riba prohibition is religiously irreducible, that the moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking has independent merit (the 2008 financial crisis as case in point — the 2010 IMF Working Paper Hasan & Dridi argued Islamic banks weathered the early phase better, though later research has substantially complicated the claim), and that the implementation flaws are addressable through transparent regulation and stricter standards rather than through dismissal of the framework. The position presents this as religious-minority economic self-determination within a global financial regulatory framework that has, in practice, accommodated the parallel system without significant difficulty.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Sharia-compliant accommodation is largely settled; the El-Gamal critique and the political-financing line

The liberal-democratic position is largely sympathetic on the financial-product question. Western financial regulation has accommodated sharia-compliant instruments without significant ideological difficulty: the UK was the first non-Muslim-majority country to issue a sovereign sukuk (2014, £200m, oversubscribed; refinanced 2021), the Bank of England maintains a sharia-compliant liquidity facility for Islamic banks since 2021, the FCA and PRA treat Islamic-finance products under the same regulatory framework as conventional products with adaptations for the underlying-asset structures of murabaha and ijara, and Frankfurt, Luxembourg, and Dublin have active Islamic-finance markets. The position is willing to grant — through Mahmoud El-Gamal's sustained academic critique — that the contemporary Islamic-finance industry has often replicated conventional finance through formal-rather-than-substantive reformulations, that the "Islamic finance weathered the 2008 crisis better" claim has been substantially complicated by post-2010 research (Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche's 2013 World Bank study found "few significant differences" in efficiency, asset quality, or stability between Islamic and conventional banks), and that Timur Kuran's The Long Divergence (Princeton, 2011) makes the structural-historical case that classical Islamic commercial-law institutions (the absence of corporate personhood, the partible-inheritance rule, the waqf as substitute for trust) operationally constrained the Muslim-majority world's financial development across the early-modern centuries.

What the position does not concede is the entire framing as a closed religious-minority self-determination question. The political-financing transparency line is the substantive issue: where halal-and-finance-certification bodies channel revenues to political organizations whose published positions on the other fault lines in this set are the political-Islamist articulation, the host society has a legitimate interest in transparency requirements that apply equally to all tax-advantaged religious or charitable associations. The 2021 French séparatisme law operationalised this at the certification-body level. The strongest version is willing to defend the substantive claim that sharia-compliant accommodation is fine on its merits — financial products are a domain where parallel infrastructure is operationally workable and ideologically low-friction — and that the political-financing question is bounded, addressable, and distinct from any objection to the religious-economic framework itself. The argument is made most credibly by the Muslim insiders themselves — Mahmoud El-Gamal, Tarek El Diwany on the methodological-betrayal critique, Mufti Taqi Usmani on the AAOIFI compliance standards, the Indonesian and Malaysian regulators who have built the most-developed parallel-banking architecture — and the Western liberal-democratic position should engage with their critiques rather than treat sharia-compliant finance as either uniformly progressive or uniformly suspect.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next sections present the empirical case study — the 2008-2010 "Islamic finance weathered the crisis better" claim arc, the November 2007 Usmani sukuk-compliance critique, and the AAOIFI/Malaysian-school divergence — and then stress-test both positions. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Case study: 2007–2010 sukuk-compliance crisis and the AAOIFI/Malaysian school divergence

The cleanest contemporary case for examining what sharia-compliant finance actually is — and the methodological dispute inside the industry about what "compliant" means. The November 2007 Mufti Taqi Usmani statement that ~85% of Gulf sukuk were sharia-non-compliant triggered an industry-wide crisis of methodology that interacted with the 2008 global financial crisis in ways the "Islamic finance weathered it better" claim has not fully digested. The AAOIFI Bahrain vs Malaysian school divergence on bay' al-inah and tawarruq is the structural fault inside the industry.

The Usmani critique, the 2008 crisis claim, and the AAOIFI/Malaysian split

Usmani statement at AAOIFI, Bahrain, November 2007 · Goldman Sachs sukuk plan withdrawal 2014 · Dana Gas sukuk dispute 2017 · Bank Negara Malaysia / OJK Indonesia parallel-architecture model · HSBC Amanah closure 2012 · Lloyds withdrawal 2018 · Standard Chartered Saadiq operating · UK sovereign sukuk 2014 (refinanced 2021)

In November 2007, at the AAOIFI conference in Bahrain, AAOIFI Sharia Board chairman Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani — the single most-influential contemporary classical-fiqh authority on Islamic finance — released a paper concluding that approximately 85% of Gulf sukuk being marketed as sharia-compliant were not actually sharia-compliant on AAOIFI standards. The principal violations he identified: purchase undertakings guaranteeing repayment of principal regardless of underlying asset performance (which converts the sukuk from an equity-like risk-sharing instrument into a debt-like fixed-income instrument), fixed-rate mark-up structures that tracked LIBOR, and asset-based rather than asset-backed structures that meant sukuk holders had no recourse to underlying assets in default. The 2009 AAOIFI revised standards substantially tightened compliance requirements; the global sukuk market briefly contracted before recovering. The 2008 financial crisis arrived weeks later. The "Islamic finance weathered the crisis better" claim — most-cited in the IMF Working Paper WP/10/201 (Hasan and Dridi, 2010) — became the industry's principal external talking point. Subsequent research (World Bank 2013 Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche; the Dubai 2009 default of Nakheel sukuk that triggered Dubai World restructuring) has substantially complicated the claim.

What the 2007–2010 arc shows about this fault line. The conservative-Islamist articulation, in its strongest form, presents sharia-compliant finance as a religiously-mandatory parallel infrastructure with a moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking that has independent merit. The empirical record across the arc — Usmani 2007 (~85% Gulf sukuk non-compliant), Dubai World / Nakheel 2009, IMF 2010 vs World Bank 2013, Goldman 2014, Dana Gas 2017, AAOIFI/Malaysian divergence on bay' al-inah and tawarruq — shows that the industry has matured, that the methodological self-critique from inside (Usmani, El-Gamal, El Diwany) has been substantial and ongoing, that the "sharia-compliant finance is structurally more stable" claim has been complicated rather than confirmed by post-crisis research, and that the operative regulatory accommodation in Western jurisdictions has been functionally settled with low ideological friction. The fault line is not "Islamic finance vs. Western finance" — it is the methodological dispute inside the Islamic-finance industry about what "sharia-compliant" actually means, the AAOIFI/Malaysian-school divergence on bay' al-inah and tawarruq that structures the industry's institutional architecture, and the political-financing transparency question (legitimately raised by the 2021 French separatism law) that is bounded and addressable. Mahmoud El-Gamal, Timur Kuran, Tarek El Diwany, and Mufti Taqi Usmani himself have done most of the substantive critical work; Western liberal-democratic engagement should treat the industry as the relatively-low-friction accommodation case that the empirical record shows it to be, while taking the political-financing concern seriously where the evidence supports it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The Usmani 85% finding against the industry's "sharia-compliant" branding

Sharia-compliant finance is presented as a religiously-validated parallel infrastructure operationally distinct from conventional finance.

Mufti Taqi Usmani's own November 2007 paper concluded ~85% of Gulf sukuk marketed as sharia-compliant were not actually compliant on AAOIFI standards — that the industry had widely substituted formal-rather-than-substantive compliance, with purchase undertakings guaranteeing principal converting equity-like risk-sharing into debt-like fixed-income, fixed-mark-up tracking LIBOR, and asset-based rather than asset-backed structures. Mahmoud El-Gamal's Islamic Finance: Law, Economics, and Practice (Cambridge 2006) had already documented the structural problem; Tarek El Diwany's The Problem with Interest made the methodological-betrayal critique from inside the UK-based community. The position cannot simultaneously claim the industry is religiously-validated and accept the inside critic's finding that 85% of its flagship instruments are not actually compliant on its own most-stringent standards. The honest version concedes that the industry is in continuous methodological dispute with itself, that AAOIFI-Bahrain and Malaysian-school practice substantially diverge, and that "sharia-compliant" is a contested rather than settled category.

The "weathered the 2008 crisis better" claim against the comparative record

Islamic finance is presented as structurally more stable than conventional finance because risk-and-profit-sharing arrangements (musharaka, mudaraba) avoid the leverage-based fragility of deposit-and-loan banking — with the 2008 crisis as case in point.

The IMF Working Paper WP/10/201 (Hasan and Dridi, 2010) showed Islamic banks performed better in profitability through 2008 but worse in credit and asset growth in 2009 due to "weaknesses in risk management practices." Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche (World Bank 2013, JBF) found "few significant differences in business orientation, efficiency, asset quality, or stability" across a 22-country panel 1995–2009 — qualifying the "weathered better" claim substantially. The Dubai World / Nakheel sukuk default (Nov 2009) demonstrated that sukuk holders' position in default is more analogous to unsecured creditor than equity-like risk-sharing partner. The "structurally more stable" claim is not supported by the comparative empirical record once the post-crisis years are included. The operative version is more modest: Islamic banks were less directly exposed to subprime-mortgage securitisation in the 2007 phase because they did not participate in those products on religious grounds — but the structural-stability claim that follows from that observation does not survive the Beck et al. analysis.

The Kuran structural argument the position has to engage

The riba prohibition is presented as a moral-economic position with broader appeal — paired with the Catholic medieval prohibition and the classical-Greek critique of usury — that has independent merit on contemporary economic grounds.

Timur Kuran's The Long Divergence: How Islamic Law Held Back the Middle East (Princeton 2011) argues that classical Islamic commercial-law institutions — the absence of corporate personhood, partible inheritance preventing intergenerational capital concentration, the waqf as institutional substitute for trust — operationally constrained the Muslim-majority world's financial development across 1500–1900, contributing structurally to the per capita GDP gap with Western Europe. The position cannot defend the moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking without engaging Kuran's structural-historical case that the alternative had real, sustained, measurable economic-development costs. The serious response is from the inside-the-industry methodological reformers (Bank Negara Malaysia's regulatory architecture, the Indonesian OJK parallel-banking model, the AAOIFI standards process) who treat the religious commitment as fixed and the institutional design as the open question. The position's strongest version operates at that level; the cultural-civilisational triumphalism that sometimes accompanies popular discourse on Islamic finance does not.

The Dana Gas case as a test of "sharia-compliance" as contractual basis

Sukuk are presented as religious-economic instruments grounded in classical-fiqh principles, providing operative certainty for both issuer and holders.

In June 2017, Dana Gas (UAE) attempted to declare its $700m mudaraba sukuk "no longer sharia-compliant" to escape repayment. The UK High Court ruled in favour of bondholders using English-law governing-law provisions; the Sharjah Court of First Instance later ruled against Dana Gas; the case settled in 2018. The case demonstrates the operative-fragility of "sharia-compliant" as a contractual basis when both sides have institutional incentives to redefine it — and demonstrates that the industry's reliance on Western-law governing-law clauses to enforce sharia-compliant contracts contradicts the "operative parallel infrastructure" framing the position advances on the marketing side. The honest position is that the industry is dependent on Western legal-system enforcement infrastructure and that the parallel-system framing is more rhetorical than operational at the points where the substantive contractual disputes get litigated.

The "Brotherhood-aligned certifier financing" question against the position's own self-presentation

Islamic finance is presented as a neutral commercial-religious accommodation with no political-financing dimension; the certification bodies are described as religious-compliance services.

Florence Bergeaud-Blackler's CNRS work (Le marché halal 2017, Le Frérisme et ses réseaux 2023) and the French séparatisme reporting documented that several major European certification bodies route revenue to organisations affiliated with the Muslim Brotherhood network. The 2021 French Loi 2021-1109 operationalised transparency requirements on this. The position cannot simultaneously claim the certification ecosystem is a neutral religious-compliance market and resist transparency requirements that would expose where the certification revenue actually goes. The honest version is that the financing concern is real but bounded, addressable through transparency rules indistinguishable from those required of every other tax-advantaged religious or charitable association, and distinct from any objection to the religious-economic framework itself.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "we accommodated without difficulty" framing as understatement

Western financial regulation has accommodated sharia-compliant instruments without significant difficulty — UK sovereign sukuk, BoE Alternative Liquidity Facility, FCA/PRA regulatory framework.

The accommodation has been operationally workable but politically uneven — Goldman's 2014 sukuk withdrawal under criticism, HSBC and Lloyds retreat from retail Islamic mortgages on commercial grounds, Republican-Party state-level attempts at anti-sharia constitutional amendments in the US (struck down by Awad v. Ziriax 2012), and the recurring popular-discourse conflation of Islamic finance with broader political-Islamist concerns. The honest position is that the technical accommodation has been settled while the political accommodation has been less so, and that the latter is what the position has to defend on its merits — not pretend the political dimension has been frictionless. The 2021 French separatism law is one operative response; the UK Bank of England 2021 ALF is another; the comparative-jurisdictional work of mapping which dimensions are technical and which are political is the load-bearing analytical task.

The political-financing line and the Trojan-horse risk

The transparency-of-financing argument is offered as a discrete concern about specific Brotherhood-linked certifiers, distinct from any objection to Islamic finance per se.

In practice, the same political coalitions that legitimately raise the financing concern also press for broader restrictions on Islamic-finance visibility — supermarket labelling, financial-product marketing to Muslim communities, public-sector procurement of sharia-compliant banking services. The position should distinguish, in its own analysis and rhetoric, between the financing-transparency concern (which is real and bounded) and the broader cultural-majoritarian programme that uses the financing concern as cover for objectives the open argument would have to justify on different grounds. The 2021 French law did the work of separating these in legislative text; the political discourse around it routinely conflates them. An honest liberal-democratic position holds the financing-transparency line and refuses the broader programme, even when it shares electoral coalition with that programme — and engages with the El-Gamal, Kuran, El Diwany, and Usmani inside-critic literature on its merits rather than weaponising it.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK

UK sovereign sukuk, Islamic-mortgage retrenchment, and the regulatory baseline

The UK was the first non-Muslim-majority country to issue a sovereign sukuk (2014, £200m, oversubscribed). The Bank of England maintains a sharia-compliant liquidity facility for Islamic banks. The retail Islamic-mortgage market peaked around 2010–2014 with HSBC and Lloyds as participants; both withdrew (HSBC 2012, Lloyds 2018) leaving the market to specialist providers (Al Rayan, Gatehouse). The retreat was commercial — low margins, regulatory complexity — not ideological.

UK regulation treats Islamic-finance products under the same FCA and PRA framework as conventional products, with adaptations for the underlying-asset structures of murabaha and ijara contracts.

Sources: Bank of England — What is Islamic finance · Islamic Finance Guru on UK banks
US

Domestic Islamic-finance market and the OCC interpretive ruling

The US Islamic-finance market is small but established: Guidance Residential, University Bank, and Lariba serve the residential-mortgage segment. The OCC issued an interpretive ruling in 1997 (later supplemented) authorizing national banks to offer ijara-based mortgage alternatives. The market has not grown to UK scale, partly due to the smaller and more dispersed US Muslim population, partly due to regulatory complexity.

Sukuk issuance by US issuers has been minimal; Goldman Sachs's 2014 sukuk plan was withdrawn under controversy.

Source: OCC interpretations
Japan

Limited but growing institutional engagement

Japanese institutional engagement with Islamic finance has been growing slowly: Tokyo issued sukuk-friendly tax legislation in 2011, and several Japanese trading companies (Sojitz, Sumitomo, Mitsui) have used sukuk for Asia-and-Middle-East financing. The Bank of Japan and FSA have produced limited operational guidance. Domestic retail Islamic-finance products are essentially absent due to the small Japanese Muslim population.

Japan's 2024 sovereign sukuk discussion (linked to Indonesian and Malaysian investor outreach) had not produced an issuance as of writing; Phase 2 should track.

Inside-critic primary · Usmani 2007
Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani, "Sukuk and their Contemporary Applications," paper presented at AAOIFI Sharia Board meeting, Bahrain, November 2007.
Method: Read the Usmani paper directly rather than the press summaries. Usmani is the most-influential contemporary classical-fiqh authority on Islamic finance and sat at the time as AAOIFI Sharia Board chairman; his finding that ~85% of Gulf sukuk did not comply with AAOIFI standards is the load-bearing inside-critic intervention of the modern era of the industry.

Comparative empirical · post-crisis
Thorsten Beck, Asli Demirgüç-Kunt, Ouarda Merrouche, "Islamic vs. conventional banking: Business model, efficiency and stability," Journal of Banking & Finance 37 (2013) 433–447 (originally World Bank Policy Research Working Paper 5446).
Method: Comparative empirical study across 22 countries 1995–2009. The standard academic reference qualifying the IMF 2010 "Islamic finance weathered the 2008 crisis better" framing. Found "few significant differences in business orientation, efficiency, asset quality or stability."
JBF article · World Bank WPS 5446
IMF · 2008 crisis claim · primary
Maher Hasan and Jemma Dridi, "The Effects of the Global Crisis on Islamic and Conventional Banks: A Comparative Study," IMF Working Paper WP/10/201, September 2010.
Method: IMF Working Paper that became the principal external source for the "Islamic finance weathered the crisis better" claim. The paper's actual finding is more qualified: Islamic banks performed better in profitability through 2008 but worse in credit and asset growth in 2009 due to "weaknesses in risk management practices." Read alongside Beck et al. 2013.
IMF WP/10/201 (PDF)
Structural-historical · Kuran 2011
Timur Kuran, The Long Divergence: How Islamic Law Held Back the Middle East, Princeton University Press, 2011.
Method: Sustained structural-historical analysis of how classical Islamic commercial-law institutions affected Muslim-majority world economic development across 1500–1900. The methodological-historical anchor for the case that the moral-economic claim has to engage with the operative cost record. Read also Kuran's earlier Islam and Mammon (Princeton 2004) for the political-economy framing.
Princeton — Kuran 2011
Foundational Western scholarship
Frank E. Vogel and Samuel L. Hayes III, Islamic Law and Finance: Religion, Risk, and Return, Kluwer Law International / Brill, 1998.
Method: The foundational Western academic engagement with Islamic finance. Vogel (Harvard Law) and Hayes (Harvard Business School) anchored the methodological framework that subsequent scholars including El-Gamal developed. Still useful as the baseline reference for the institutional and jurisprudential-history dimensions.
Brill — Vogel & Hayes 1998
UK · Bank of England
Bank of England, Alternative Liquidity Facility (December 2021) and ongoing operational guidance on Islamic-finance treatment.
Method: Primary central-bank source on the operational accommodation of Islamic-finance institutions in the UK. The 2021 ALF is the first such facility from a non-Muslim-majority central bank and a useful operational reference.
BoE ALF Market Notice · BoE — What is Islamic finance
Dana Gas case · 2017
Dana Gas PJSC v Deutsche Bank Trust Company Americas and Others, [2017] EWHC 2928 (Comm), UK High Court Commercial Division — and parallel Sharjah Court of First Instance proceedings.
Method: UK High Court ruling on the Dana Gas attempt to declare its $700m mudaraba sukuk no-longer-sharia-compliant to escape repayment. Demonstrates the operative-fragility of "sharia-compliant" as contractual basis when both sides have institutional incentives to redefine it; demonstrates the industry's reliance on Western-law governing-law clauses to enforce sharia-compliant contracts. Settled 2018.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The El-Gamal critique on its merits. If much of contemporary Islamic finance is conventional finance with religious branding, what does that imply both for the moral-economic argument and for the political-financing concern? Phase 2 should engage El-Gamal directly.
  • HSBC and Lloyds withdrawals — what changed. The major UK banks' exit from Islamic-mortgage products was commercial. What does the current market structure (Al Rayan, Gatehouse, others) tell us about the long-run viability of the parallel-financial-system model?
  • Certification-body governance audit. Which bodies, what governance, what revenue flows, what political organizations? Phase 2 should attempt a rigorous audit at the European-regulatory level.
  • Sukuk performance through 2024. Sovereign-sukuk track record (UK 2014, multiple OIC issuers, secondary-market liquidity). The instrument-class has matured; Phase 2 should examine whether it has produced the more-stable financing the moral-economic argument predicted.
  • Indonesian regulatory model. OJK's parallel banking-system architecture (BPRS, BUS, BPRS) is the most-developed regulatory model for a Muslim-majority country running parallel conventional-and-Islamic finance. What can Western regulators learn from it?
