Banking and finance / 銀行と金融: riba/interest prohibition, parallel financial institutions

The Islamic prohibition on 利子 (interest, riba) has produced a parallel suite of sharia-compliant instruments operating inside Western 金融 (finance) regulation. The friction is mostly commercial. The open question is political financing through certification revenue.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1
Conventional Western 銀行 (banking) runs on interest-bearing loans, bonds, and deposits; Islamic jurisprudence prohibits riba (all interest, or predatory interest, depending on the school) and has built a parallel suite of instruments in response: murabaha cost-plus sale, ijara leasing, musharaka partnership, sukuk asset-backed certificates. The friction is mainly commercial and operational; the sharper question is whether certification revenue flows to Islamist political organizations.

Steelmanned positions

Conservative-Islamist articulation

The riba prohibition is divinely ordered moral economics; sharia-compliant finance is the operative answer

The textual case is not marginal. Qur'anic verses 2:275–280, 3:130, 4:160–161, and 30:39 establish the prohibition of riba as one of scripture's most categorical economic commands, paired with the threat of "war from Allah and His Messenger" (2:279) for those who persist. Classical fiqh and the AAOIFI standards read riba as the predetermined excess on a loan of money or fungible commodities; hadith extends it to seven specific commodities and any unequal or deferred exchange of like for like. The four Sunni schools and Twelver Shia agree on substance; they dispute only application to fiat currency. This prohibition shares its ancestry with the Aristotelian critique of usury and the medieval Catholic ban, operative European law until the Reformation. The contemporary industry (Islamic banking, sukuk, takaful, murabaha, ijara, musharaka, mudaraba) is the jurisprudential answer to living within a global financial system built on interest.

The strongest version concedes, through Mufti Taqi Usmani's November 2007 finding that ~85% of Gulf sukuk were not actually compliant on AAOIFI standards, that practice has often substituted formal for substantive compliance; that El-Gamal's Islamic Finance: Law, Economics, and Practice (Cambridge 2006) identified murabaha-as-disguised-loan, tawarruq, and bay' al-inah as structures where the risk-sharing premise collapses; and that AAOIFI Bahrain standards diverge from the Malaysian school on bay' al-inah and tawarruq, producing a real intra-Muslim methodological split. What mainstream Sunni and Shia jurisprudence holds uniformly, and what AAOIFI, Bank Negara Malaysia, OJK Indonesia, the Iranian Central Bank, the State Bank of Pakistan, and the Islamic windows of HSBC Amanah, Standard Chartered Saadiq, Citi Islamic, and the Gulf majors all treat as operative, is that the riba prohibition is religiously irreducible; that the moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking has independent merit; and that implementation flaws are addressable through stricter standards, not by abandoning the framework. This is religious-minority economic self-determination within a regulatory structure that has, in practice, accommodated it.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Sharia-compliant accommodation is largely settled; the El-Gamal critique and the political-financing line

Western financial regulation has absorbed sharia-compliant instruments without much ideological friction. The UK issued the first non-Muslim-majority sovereign sukuk in 2014 (£200m, oversubscribed; refinanced 2021); the Bank of England launched a sharia-compliant liquidity facility in 2021; the FCA and PRA treat murabaha and ijara under the same framework as conventional products, with structural adaptations; Frankfurt, Luxembourg, and Dublin have active markets. The position grants El-Gamal's critique: the industry has often replicated conventional finance with religious branding. It grants Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche's 2013 World Bank finding of "few significant differences" in efficiency, asset quality, or stability. It grants Kuran's The Long Divergence (Princeton 2011): classical Islamic commercial-law institutions (absent corporate personhood, partible inheritance, waqf as substitute for trust) constrained the Muslim-majority world's financial development across the early-modern centuries.

What the position refuses is the claim that this is a closed self-determination question. Where certification bodies channel revenue to political organizations whose published positions on the other fault lines in this set are the political-Islamist articulation, the host society has a legitimate interest in transparency requirements applied equally to all tax-advantaged religious or charitable associations. The 2021 French séparatisme law did that at the certification-body level. The parallel-infrastructure model is operationally workable and ideologically low-friction on its merits; the political-financing question is bounded, addressable, and distinct. The most credible critics are Muslim insiders: El-Gamal on methodological betrayal, El Diwany on the same, Usmani on AAOIFI compliance, the Indonesian and Malaysian regulators who built the most developed parallel-banking architectures. Engage them; don't treat the industry as uniformly progressive or uniformly suspect.

Method note. The two cards above give each position its strongest form, on its own premises, without smoothing. The sections that follow present the empirical arc (Usmani 2007, the "weathered the crisis better" claim, the AAOIFI/Malaysian-school divergence) and then stress-test both. A steelman that cannot clear its own implications is propaganda, not analysis.

Case study: 2007–2010 sukuk-compliance crisis and the AAOIFI/Malaysian school divergence

The clearest contemporary case for examining what "sharia-compliant" actually means. Usmani's November 2007 finding that ~85% of Gulf sukuk were non-compliant triggered an industry-wide methodological crisis that intersected with the 2008 global financial crisis in ways the "weathered it better" claim has not fully absorbed. The AAOIFI Bahrain vs. Malaysian school divergence on bay' al-inah and tawarruq is the structural fault running through the industry today.

The Usmani critique, the 2008 crisis claim, and the AAOIFI/Malaysian split

Usmani statement at AAOIFI, Bahrain, November 2007 · Goldman Sachs sukuk plan withdrawal 2014 · Dana Gas sukuk dispute 2017 · Bank Negara Malaysia / OJK Indonesia parallel-architecture model · HSBC Amanah closure 2012 · Lloyds withdrawal 2018 · Standard Chartered Saadiq operating · UK sovereign sukuk 2014 (refinanced 2021)

In November 2007, at the AAOIFI conference in Bahrain, AAOIFI Sharia Board chairman Mufti Muhammad Taqi Usmani released a paper concluding that approximately 85% of Gulf sukuk being marketed as sharia-compliant were not on AAOIFI standards. The principal violations: purchase undertakings guaranteeing principal repayment regardless of underlying asset performance (converting equity-like risk-sharing into fixed income); fixed-rate mark-up structures tracking LIBOR; and asset-based rather than asset-backed structures leaving sukuk holders with no recourse to underlying assets in default. AAOIFI revised its standards in 2009, tightening compliance; the sukuk market briefly contracted. The 2008 global financial crisis arrived weeks after the Usmani paper. IMF Working Paper WP/10/201 (Hasan and Dridi, 2010) became the main external source for the "Islamic finance weathered the crisis better" claim. Subsequent research (Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche 2013; the Dubai World/Nakheel sukuk default of November 2009) has complicated the claim substantially.

What the 2007–2010 arc shows. The strongest conservative-Islamist articulation presents sharia-compliant finance as a religiously mandatory parallel infrastructure with a moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking. The arc, Usmani 2007 (~85% Gulf sukuk non-compliant), Dubai World / Nakheel 2009, IMF 2010 vs. World Bank 2013, Goldman 2014, Dana Gas 2017, and the AAOIFI/Malaysian divergence on bay' al-inah and tawarruq, shows an industry that has matured; inside critics (Usmani, El-Gamal, El Diwany) have been substantial and ongoing; the "structurally more stable" claim is complicated rather than confirmed; and regulatory accommodation in Western jurisdictions is functionally settled with low ideological friction. The fault line is not Islamic finance vs. Western finance. It is the methodological dispute inside the industry about what "sharia-compliant" means, the AAOIFI/Malaysian divergence that structures institutional architecture, and the political-financing transparency question, bounded and addressable, raised legitimately by the 2021 French separatism law. El-Gamal, Kuran, El Diwany, and Usmani have done most of the substantive critical work. Treat the industry as the low-friction accommodation case the record supports; take the political-financing concern seriously where the evidence warrants.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. Both positions are presented uncensored above; both are stress-tested below. The standard is not that they are equally defensible; it is that each must clear the same evidentiary bar.

Cross-examination

The positions named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The Usmani 85% finding against the industry's "sharia-compliant" branding

Sharia-compliant finance is presented as a religiously-validated parallel infrastructure operationally distinct from conventional finance.

Usmani's own November 2007 paper found ~85% of Gulf sukuk marketed as sharia-compliant were not, on AAOIFI standards: purchase undertakings guaranteeing principal converted equity-like risk-sharing into debt-like fixed income; fixed mark-up tracked LIBOR; asset-based rather than asset-backed structures left sukuk holders without recourse. El-Gamal's Islamic Finance: Law, Economics, and Practice (Cambridge 2006) had already documented the structural problem; Tarek El Diwany's The Problem with Interest made the methodological-betrayal critique from inside the UK-based community. The position cannot simultaneously claim religious validation for the industry and accept its most authoritative inside critic's finding that 85% of the flagship instruments fail the industry's own most stringent standards. The honest version concedes that "sharia-compliant" is contested rather than settled, that AAOIFI-Bahrain and Malaysian-school practice diverge substantially, and that the industry is in continuous methodological dispute with itself.

The "weathered the 2008 crisis better" claim against the comparative record

Islamic finance is presented as structurally more stable than conventional finance because risk-and-profit-sharing arrangements (musharaka, mudaraba) avoid the leverage-based fragility of deposit-and-loan banking; the 2008 crisis is cited as proof.

IMF WP/10/201 (Hasan and Dridi, 2010) showed Islamic banks performed better in profitability through 2008 but worse in credit and asset growth in 2009, due to "weaknesses in risk management practices." Beck, Demirgüç-Kunt, and Merrouche (World Bank 2013, JBF) found "few significant differences in business orientation, efficiency, asset quality, or stability" across a 22-country panel 1995–2009. The Dubai World / Nakheel sukuk default (November 2009) demonstrated that sukuk holders in default sit closer to unsecured creditor than equity-like risk-sharing partner. The "structurally more stable" claim does not hold once the post-crisis years are included. The defensible version is narrower: Islamic banks were less exposed to subprime-mortgage securitisation in the 2007 phase because they did not participate in those instruments on religious grounds. The structural-stability argument does not follow from that; Beck et al. 2013 shows why.

The Kuran structural argument the position has to engage

The riba prohibition is presented as a moral-economic position with broader appeal, paired with the Catholic medieval prohibition and the classical-Greek critique of usury, that has independent merit on contemporary economic grounds.

Kuran's The Long Divergence (Princeton 2011) argues that classical Islamic commercial-law institutions (absent corporate personhood; partible inheritance preventing intergenerational capital concentration; waqf as substitute for trust) constrained the Muslim-majority world's financial development across 1500–1900, contributing to the per capita GDP gap with Western Europe. The moral-economic critique of leverage-based banking cannot be defended without engaging Kuran's case that the alternative had real, sustained, measurable costs. The serious response comes from inside-the-industry reformers: Bank Negara Malaysia's regulatory architecture, the Indonesian OJK parallel-banking model, the AAOIFI standards process. They treat the religious commitment as fixed and the institutional design as the open question. The position's strongest form operates there; the cultural-triumphalist strand of popular Islamic-finance discourse does not.

The Dana Gas case as a test of "sharia-compliance" as contractual basis

Sukuk are presented as religious-economic instruments grounded in classical-fiqh principles, providing operative certainty for both issuer and holders.

In June 2017, Dana Gas (UAE) attempted to declare its $700m mudaraba sukuk "no longer sharia-compliant" to escape repayment. The UK High Court ruled for the bondholders on English-law governing-law provisions; the Sharjah Court of First Instance later ruled against Dana Gas; the case settled in 2018. "Sharia-compliant" is fragile as a contractual basis when both sides have institutional incentives to redefine it. The industry's reliance on Western-law governing-law clauses to enforce its own instruments contradicts the "operative parallel infrastructure" framing it advances in marketing. At the points where substantive contractual disputes get litigated, the parallel-system framing is more rhetorical than operational.

The "Brotherhood-aligned certifier financing" question against the position's own self-presentation

Islamic finance is presented as a neutral commercial-religious accommodation with no political-financing dimension; the certification bodies are described as religious-compliance services.

Florence Bergeaud-Blackler's CNRS work (Le marché halal 2017, Le Frérisme et ses réseaux 2023) and the French séparatisme reporting documented that several major European certification bodies route revenue to organisations affiliated with the Muslim Brotherhood network. The 2021 French Loi 2021-1109 applied transparency requirements on this basis. The position cannot claim the certification market is neutral while resisting transparency rules that would show where the revenue goes. The honest version: the financing concern is real but bounded, addressable through transparency rules no different from those required of any other tax-advantaged religious or charitable association, and separate from any objection to the religious-economic framework.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "we accommodated without difficulty" framing as understatement

Western financial regulation has accommodated sharia-compliant instruments without significant difficulty: UK sovereign sukuk, BoE Alternative Liquidity Facility, FCA/PRA regulatory framework.

The accommodation has been technically workable but politically uneven. Goldman's 2014 sukuk withdrawal under criticism, HSBC and Lloyds retreating from retail Islamic mortgages on commercial grounds, Republican-controlled state attempts at anti-sharia constitutional amendments (struck down by Awad v. Ziriax 2012), and the recurring popular-discourse conflation of Islamic finance with political Islamism all complicate the "without difficulty" framing. The technical accommodation has been settled; the political has been less so; that is the dimension the position must defend on its merits, not paper over. The 2021 French separatism law is one response; the Bank of England's 2021 ALF is another. Mapping which dimensions are technical and which are political is the load-bearing analytical task.

The political-financing line and the Trojan-horse risk

The transparency-of-financing argument is offered as a discrete concern about specific Brotherhood-linked certifiers, distinct from any objection to Islamic finance per se.

In practice, the same political coalitions that legitimately raise the financing concern also press for broader restrictions on Islamic-finance visibility: supermarket labelling, financial-product marketing to Muslim communities, public-sector procurement of sharia-compliant services. The position must distinguish in its own analysis between the financing-transparency concern (real and bounded) and the broader cultural-majoritarian programme that uses that concern as cover for objectives the open argument would have to justify on different grounds. The 2021 French law separated these in legislative text; the political discourse around it routinely conflates them. An honest liberal-democratic position holds the transparency line and refuses the broader programme, even when it shares an electoral coalition with that programme; and it engages El-Gamal, Kuran, El Diwany, and Usmani on their merits rather than weaponising them.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of the whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot clear its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature; it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth taking.

The friction in The West

EU · UK

UK sovereign sukuk, Islamic-mortgage retrenchment, and the regulatory baseline

The UK issued the first non-Muslim-majority sovereign sukuk in 2014 (£200m, oversubscribed; refinanced £500m 2021). The Bank of England operates a sharia-compliant Alternative Liquidity Facility for Islamic banks since 2021. The retail Islamic-mortgage market peaked around 2010–2014 with HSBC and Lloyds participating; both withdrew (HSBC 2012, Lloyds 2018) for commercial reasons, low margins and regulatory complexity, leaving the market to Al Rayan, Gatehouse, and smaller specialists.

FCA and PRA treat Islamic-finance products under the same framework as conventional, with structural adaptations for murabaha and ijara contracts.

Sources: Bank of England — What is Islamic finance · Islamic Finance Guru on UK banks
US

Domestic Islamic-finance market and the OCC interpretive ruling

Small but established. Guidance Residential, University Bank, and Lariba serve the residential-mortgage segment. The OCC issued an interpretive ruling in 1997 authorizing national banks to offer ijara-based mortgage alternatives. The market has not reached UK scale, partly due to a smaller and more dispersed Muslim population, partly due to regulatory complexity. Goldman Sachs withdrew its $2 billion sukuk plan in 2014 under sustained methodological criticism.

Source: OCC interpretations
Japan

Limited but growing institutional engagement

Japan issued sukuk-friendly tax legislation in 2011; Sojitz, Sumitomo, and Mitsui have used sukuk for Asia and Middle East financing. The FSA has produced limited operational guidance. Domestic retail Islamic-finance products are absent; the Muslim population is too small to support them.

Japan's 2024 sovereign sukuk discussions, linked to Indonesian and Malaysian investor outreach, had not produced an issuance as of writing. Phase 2 should track.

Method: UK High Court ruling on Dana Gas's attempt to declare its $700m mudaraba sukuk non-compliant to escape repayment. Shows the fragility of "sharia-compliant" as a contractual basis and the industry's reliance on Western-law governing-law clauses. Settled 2018.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The El-Gamal critique on its merits. If much of contemporary Islamic finance is conventional finance with religious branding, what does that imply for the moral-economic argument and for the political-financing concern? Phase 2 should engage El-Gamal directly.
  • HSBC and Lloyds withdrawals. Both exits were commercial. What does the current market structure (Al Rayan, Gatehouse, others) show about the long-run viability of the parallel-finance model in Western retail banking?
  • Certification-body governance audit. Which bodies, what governance, what revenue flows, which political organizations? Phase 2 should attempt a rigorous audit at the European regulatory level.
  • Sukuk performance through 2024. Sovereign sukuk track record (UK 2014, multiple OIC issuers, secondary-market liquidity). The instrument class has matured; Phase 2 should examine whether it has delivered the more stable financing the moral-economic argument predicted.
  • Indonesian regulatory model. OJK's parallel banking architecture is the most developed regulatory model for a Muslim-majority country running conventional and Islamic finance side by side. What can Western regulators take from it?
