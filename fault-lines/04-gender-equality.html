Fault Line · 04

Gender equality / 男女平等: equal citizenship vs. complementarian hierarchy

Whether men and women hold identical rights and standing before the law, or whether the genders are assigned divinely-grounded different roles whose hierarchy is a feature, not a bug.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic legal orders treat the two sexes as identically positioned rights-bearers — equal in voting, contracting, testifying, inheriting, divorcing, and child custody — while classical and political-Islamist family-law traditions assign asymmetric rights and duties (inheritance shares, marriage authority, divorce access, testimony weight) on the doctrinal ground that male and female roles are complementary by divine design, not interchangeable.

Steelmanned positions

The most defensible form of each.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Asymmetric roles with asymmetric obligations

The honest version of this position names the package frankly rather than reaching for the word "complementary" as a euphemism. Classical Islamic family law assigns the husband wali-style guardianship in marriage, talaq (unilateral access to divorce), a 2:1 inheritance share against a sister, 2:1 testimony weight in classical financial matters, and the permission of polygamy up to four wives. It assigns the wife mahr (a personal marriage gift owed to her, not her family), nafaqa (full lifetime financial maintenance for her and the children regardless of her own wealth), the right to retain all pre- and post-marital property without any obligation to spend it on the household, and a procedurally harder but recognized access to divorce (khul', faskh). The position holds that the package is doctrinally coherent — designed to fit together when enforced as a whole — and that fourteen centuries of Muslim family life structured by it are the empirical case for its workability when actually implemented as designed.

The strongest version concedes what the apologetic version hides. As implemented across most twenty-first-century Muslim-majority states, the package routinely combines the disadvantageous half of the female position (smaller inheritance, harder divorce, restricted custody after a fixed age) with the absence of the male obligations that were supposed to balance it (unenforced nafaqa, no recourse against arbitrary talaq, dowry treated as bridewealth paid to the bride's family). It concedes that the lived experience of women under this regime, in many places, does track the word "subordination" — and that "then implement it correctly" is a thin reply to women whose alternatives are not theoretical. What the position actually defends is the doctrinal coherence of the classical settlement on its own metaphysical premises about gender — not its current operation, and not the claim that asymmetric rules cannot, in practice, become subordinating. It loses the empirical argument the moment it is pressed on lived outcomes. It only wins the doctrinal argument against an interlocutor unwilling to reject the religious-anthropological premise outright.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Equal status before the law is non-negotiable

The liberal-democratic position is not that men and women are biologically identical — almost no one defends that view — but that legal status is not where biological differences should land. A wife's testimony in court counts as much as a husband's; a daughter inherits as much as a son; a woman initiates divorce on the same terms as a man; a mother retains custody on the same standard. The asymmetric package the Islamist position calls "complementary" looks, when actually litigated, asymmetric in one direction.

The strongest version concedes the historical record: Western legal systems achieved formal sex equality only in the 20th century and informal equality is still incomplete. The defensible claim is not that the West got there first or honestly — it is that revisable equal-status law is the only stable settlement compatible with women being citizens rather than wards, and the empirical record of complementarian regimes (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan) is the case for it.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it.

Cross-examination

The arguments above, tested. The conservative-Islamist articulation is probed first because that is where the steelman has historically done the most laundering work; the liberal-democratic articulation is then probed in turn.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "if implemented correctly" move

The package is doctrinally coherent and produces women's flourishing — when the male obligations (nafaqa, mahr protection, custody fairness) are actually enforced as the classical scheme designed them.

After fourteen centuries, the position cannot point to a single contemporary or historical Muslim-majority polity where the package has been implemented in a way that produces women's flourishing on metrics women themselves identify (life expectancy, education access, divorce parity, freedom of movement, freedom of marriage choice). The "if implemented correctly" move is unfalsifiable: every empirical record can be dismissed as miscalibration. A doctrinal claim that has had fourteen hundred years and dozens of attempts to demonstrate itself, and has not, has lost the right to keep deferring its empirical case to "next time, properly."

The "implementation problem" framing

Where the package produces subordination, the failure is in implementation (corrupt courts, weak enforcement of nafaqa, patriarchal local custom contaminating the doctrine), not in the package's design.

If a system has produced subordinating outcomes everywhere it has been implemented, across centuries and across radically different political conditions — Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan, classical Andalusia, the Ottoman millet system, contemporary informal sharia councils in Birmingham — the system's design is part of the answer, not an external variable. Calling this an "implementation problem" treats the empirical record as separable from the doctrinal framework — a separation the system's own theology rejects when it claims the framework is divinely revealed and complete.

The metaphysical-premise dodge

The doctrine is internally coherent on its own metaphysical premises — namely that men and women are divinely ordained to complementary roles with distinct legal capacities — and liberal critique applies premises the position does not share.

True and trivial. Every system is internally coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether those premises are defensible — whether the theological case for treating men and women as ontologically different in legal capacity (rather than merely traditionally so treated) survives examination. The position cannot make that case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling.

The exit-cost asymmetry

Women within the regime have legitimate paths of redress — khul' divorce, mahr enforcement, work permission, the qadi's discretion — and are not without recourse.

Khul' in most jurisdictions requires the husband's cooperation or compensation; mahr enforcement varies by court; work permission depends on his consent in several Gulf and South Asian implementations; and exit from Islam altogether — the position's logical-final exit — carries apostasy consequences in many implementations. The doctrinal scheme provides no provision for a Muslim woman who concludes that the gender-anthropology premise is simply wrong, and who wishes to live by a different premise without religious or legal sanction. A defense of a system that requires its female subjects to accept its gender metaphysics on pain of legal or social cost is not a defense — it is a description of the coercion problem.

The Western-women-too tu-quoque

Western legal equality conceals informal subordination — the second shift, the wage gap, the invisible labor of motherhood, sexualized commodification, declining family stability. The Islamic settlement is at least honest about role asymmetry.

True about Western informal asymmetry, irrelevant to the comparison. The question is which framework, as enforced, produces better measurable outcomes for women on metrics women themselves identify (life expectancy, education access, divorce parity, custody parity, freedom of movement, freedom of marriage choice, protection from honor-based violence). The empirical record across these metrics is not even close — the WEF Global Gender Gap, the UNDP Gender Inequality Index, life-expectancy gaps, female-labor-force-participation, age-at-first-marriage, all run sharply in one direction. The tu-quoque hides the comparison rather than answering it, and the position's serious advocates know this — which is why it is the lay-defense form of the argument, not the scholarly one.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency objection

Western formal sex equality is a 20th-century achievement (US 19th Amendment 1920, Married Women's Property Acts 1870–1882, marital rape recognized as crime only in the 1970s–1990s in most US states); informal equality is incomplete and contested. The conservative position can press: if the equal-citizenship framework is universal-and-self-evident, why did the West only discover it recently?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest answer is: we were wrong before — the same way the West was wrong about slavery, religious tolerance, racial segregation, and child labor. The right reply to "why didn't you have this view in 1850?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held," not "we held a different view that was equally valid." The position requires defending the current settlement on its merits, not its provenance — and acknowledging that liberal-democratic norms continue to evolve and are not always self-consistent.

The "wrong, not different" claim

Some doctrinal positions on gender (death penalty for adultery, child marriage, half-share testimony in financial matters, polygamy without consent) are wrong, not merely "another tradition." This is a substantive cross-civilizational moral claim with imperial echoes.

The claim is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can — Ayaan Hirsi Ali, Masih Alinejad, Asra Nomani, Irshad Manji, Mona Eltahawy, Taslima Nasrin, the Iranian women on the streets after Mahsa Amini, the diaspora honour-killing victims' surviving sisters and friends, Musawah and the Sisters in Islam reformist tradition. The argument has to be defended through these voices, not over them. It is dishonest to pretend the universalist framing has no civilizational asymmetry baked in; the honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry, defend the substantive claim on the merits, and refuse the relativist deflection that would silence Muslim women's voices in the name of respecting Muslim-majority cultures.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of the project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK

Sharia councils, divorce, and women's standing

The UK's network of ~85 informal sharia councils handles primarily women's applications for religious divorce (khul') — a divorce a Muslim wife typically cannot obtain from her husband without third-party mediation. The Casey Review (2016) and subsequent independent inquiries documented findings that some councils condoned wife-beating, ignored marital rape claims, and applied custody and inheritance frameworks materially inconsistent with English equality law.

Women using these councils generally do so because they need a religious divorce their imam will recognize, not because English civil divorce is unavailable to them. The friction is precisely there: a parallel jurisdiction operating on inegalitarian rules, voluntarily entered, applied to women whose alternative would be community-recognized marital limbo.

Sources: UK Parliament evidence on sharia councils
US

Religious arbitration and family-court enforcement

US courts will enforce religious-arbitration awards — Catholic, Jewish, Islamic — under the Federal Arbitration Act, but with the public-policy carve-out that awards violating fundamental constitutional rights are unenforceable. Litigation through the 2010s established that Islamic mahr (deferred dower) contracts are generally enforceable as prenuptials, while custody and child-support awards inconsistent with state best-interest standards are not.

The active dispute in US scholarship is whether voluntary entry into a religiously-asymmetric arbitration regime should be respected as autonomy or refused as effectively coerced — the same question the UK debate raises.

Source: Multiple state-level cases since ~2000.
Japan

Low immediate friction, persistent domestic gender gap

Japan ranks 118th of 146 in the WEF Global Gender Gap Report 2024 — the lowest among OECD economies. The Islamic gender-equality fault line has not surfaced as a public issue largely because Japan's own gender equality is not where the West sits, and the small Muslim population (~0.18%) has not produced visible friction cases.

A future deep dive should ask whether Japan's social-equilibrium approach to gender (less legal hierarchy, persistent informal hierarchy) gives it a different relationship to Islamic family-law norms than the European one — interestingly closer in some respects, sharper in others.

Case study: gender coercion from Tehran to the diaspora

Two empirical objects, one fault line. Inside Iran, the largest sustained women-led uprising in the Islamic Republic's history — sparked by the September 2022 death in custody of Jina Mahsa Amini, a 22-year-old Kurdish woman arrested for "improperly worn" hijab — produced ~500 protester deaths, 22,000+ arrests, and the European Parliament's first call to designate Iran's enforcement apparatus as terrorist. In the European and North American diaspora, a parallel and largely concealed pattern: a cluster of so-called "honour killings" of girls and young women whose offence was Westernization, refusal of forced marriage, or relationships with non-Muslim partners. The Iranian regime case gives the contemporary state-coercion anchor; the diaspora case gives the intra-Western evidence that the complementarian-coercion problem does not stop at the West's borders. The poll cards below sit on top of both.

Mahsa Amini, "Woman, Life, Freedom," and the diaspora honour-killings cluster

Iran, Sept 2022 – present · UK / Germany / Canada honour-killings cluster, 2002–2012 · scope: state coercion abroad + family coercion in The West · women-led contestation in both directions

On 16 September 2022, Jina Mahsa Amini died in a Tehran hospital three days after being detained by the Gasht-e Ershad ("Guidance Patrol") for hijab non-compliance. The UN Independent Fact-Finding Mission on Iran later concluded her death was "unlawful and caused by physical violence" for which the Iranian state is responsible. The protests that followed were the largest sustained women-led uprising in the Islamic Republic's history — and the regime response (mass shootings, sexual assault of detainees, public hangings of protesters) became one of the best-documented contemporary cases of state-enforced complementarianism. Inside The West, the same fault line surfaces in a different register: a cluster of family-honour killings of girls aged 16 to 23 — Heshu Yones (London 2002), Hatun Sürücü (Berlin 2005), Banaz Mahmod (London 2006), Aqsa Parvez (Toronto 2007), Shafilea Ahmed (Cheshire 2003, conviction 2012). The same coercion logic, executed by states in one geography and by families in another.

Sentiment polls: what the surveys actually show

The polls below are the empirical picture the Iran-and-diaspora cases sit inside. The Pew 2013 international baseline shows just how high "wife must always obey" runs in the regions of origin (74–87% across South Asia, MENA, Southeast Asia). The ICM/Channel 4 2016 survey of British Muslims shows that 39% — eight times the 5% non-Muslim British rate — hold the same view in The West. The IFOP 2019 polygamy data and the UK CPS 2024 honour-based-abuse statistics are the hard evidence that the diaspora-coercion pattern is real, measurable, and concentrated. The honest reading is that family coercion in The West and state coercion in Iran are not the same regime, but they share a doctrinal substrate that the polls trace continuously.

What the case shows about this fault line. Two pieces of evidence that look very different — Iranian state security forces beating a 22-year-old to death for a loose headscarf, and a British father stabbing his 16-year-old daughter 17 times for dating a Christian — sit on the same axis once you look at the polls. The Pew international baselines (74–93% "wife must obey" across the regions of origin) and the ICM 2016 finding that 39% of British Muslims hold the identical view (versus 5% of the wider population) are the bridge. The diaspora honour-killings statistics are systematically undercounted because the practice depends on community concealment, the victims' families are the perpetrators, and English juries returned "missing person" or "suicide" verdicts for years before Heshu Yones was correctly classified in 2002–03. The Iran case shows what the maximalist-state version of complementarian coercion looks like at scale; the diaspora cases show what its private-family version looks like in cities where the law in principle protects the daughter equally. The women on both sides of the line — Mahsa Amini's funeral chanters in Saqqez, Banaz Mahmod making her fifth police report, Masih Alinejad still publishing from a Brooklyn safe-house — are the same constituency, fighting the same regime, in the same lifetime. Any honest position on this fault line has to centre them, not deflect from them.

Hakim El Karoui & Institut Montaigne / IFOP, Un islam français est possible, September 2016 (rapport + annexes); follow-up IFOP polls 2019–2023 on French Muslim attitudes to family law, polygamy, and gender.
Method: Large national-sample survey with 1,029 self-identifying Muslim respondents drawn from a 15,459-person base. Generated the well-known three-cluster typology: ~46% "completely secular," ~25% "Islamic-pride / proselytizing but legalistic," ~28% "secessionist / Islam-as-identity-rejection-of-Republic." The 28% cluster is the empirical anchor for the discussion of where complementarian-family-law preferences sit in the French Muslim population.
Caveat: The survey instruments and the typology have been contested in French academic debate (notably by the sociologists working with the CNRS Trajectoires et Origines / TeO programme). The typology is interpretively defensible but not neutral; the underlying frequencies (29% sharia > republican law; 50% among under-25s) are robust across re-analyses. Phase-2 deep-dive should triangulate with TeO and with the IFOP 2023 follow-up.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The Musawah synthesis on its merits. The Muslim-feminist case for reconciling CEDAW with classical jurisprudence deserves serious engagement: where does it succeed and where does it strain? Is "Islamic feminism" a stable settlement or a transitional formation?
  • The Iran 2022–23 protests as data point. Largest contemporary mass mobilization of Muslim women against the complementarian state. What does the demographic and political structure of that movement tell us about the layered ecosystem the hub describes?
  • UK sharia council quantitative data. Beyond Casey's qualitative findings, what is the actual case-load, outcome distribution, and follow-up of women whose cases were heard? Phase 2 should locate or commission proper survey work.
  • The complementarian-vs.-equal frame in non-Islamic Western settings. Catholic and Orthodox Jewish family-law traditions hold positions structurally similar to the Islamic complementarian one. Why are the comparable controversies smaller — selection? size? the specific legal mechanisms? — and what does the comparison tell us?
  • Honor-based violence prosecution data. The hub's rehab section noted UK 2024: 2,755 honour-based offences recorded, only two FGM prosecutions since 1985. Phase 2 should triangulate this with EU-level EIGE and DG-Just data and assess the prosecution-rate gap.
