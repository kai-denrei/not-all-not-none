Fault Line · 04

Gender equality / 男女平等: equal citizenship vs. complementarian hierarchy

Whether men and women hold identical rights before the law, or whether divinely-assigned complementary roles make the hierarchy a feature, not a defect.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1
Liberal-democratic law treats both sexes as equal rights-bearers: equal in voting, contracting, testifying, inheriting, divorcing, and custody. Classical and political-Islamist family law assigns asymmetric rights and duties (inheritance shares, marriage authority, divorce access, testimony weight) on the ground that male and female roles are complementary by divine design (男女平等 / gender equality is precisely what this settlement rejects).

Steelmanned positions

Each position in its strongest form. Not a bad-day caricature; the argument as its best advocates defend it.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Asymmetric roles with asymmetric obligations

The honest version names the package frankly rather than hiding behind "complementary." Classical Islamic family law gives the husband wali-style guardianship, talaq (unilateral divorce), a 2:1 inheritance share over a sister, 2:1 testimony weight in financial matters, and permission of up to four wives. It gives the wife mahr (a marriage gift owed to her alone), nafaqa (full maintenance regardless of her own wealth), the right to keep all property with no obligation to spend it on the household, and a harder but recognized path to divorce (khul', faskh). The position holds that the package is doctrinally coherent: designed to work as a whole, and fourteen centuries of Muslim family life structured by it are the empirical case for its workability when actually implemented as designed.

The strongest version concedes what the apologetic version hides. Across most 21st-century Muslim-majority states, the package combines the disadvantageous female half (smaller inheritance, harder divorce, restricted custody after a fixed age) with the male obligations removed (unenforced nafaqa, no recourse against arbitrary talaq, dowry treated as bridewealth paid to the bride's family). It concedes that the lived experience of women under this regime, in many places, does track the word "subordination"; "then implement it correctly" is a thin reply to women whose alternatives are not theoretical. What the position defends is the doctrinal coherence of the classical settlement on its own metaphysical premises about gender; not its current operation, and not the claim that asymmetric rules cannot become subordinating in practice. It loses the empirical argument the moment it is pressed on lived outcomes. It only wins the doctrinal argument against an interlocutor unwilling to reject the religious-anthropological premise outright.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Equal status before the law is non-negotiable

The liberal position is not that men and women are biologically identical; almost no one defends that. The claim is that legal status is not where biological differences should land. A wife's testimony counts as much as a husband's; a daughter inherits as much as a son; a woman initiates divorce on the same terms. The package the Islamist calls "complementary" looks, when actually litigated, asymmetric in one direction.

The strongest version concedes the historical record: the West achieved formal sex equality (平等) only in the 20th century; informal equality is still incomplete. The defensible claim is not that the West got there first or honestly; it is that revisable equal-status law is the only stable settlement compatible with women being citizens rather than wards. The empirical record of complementarian regimes (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan) is the case for it.

Method note. Both cards present each position in its strongest form, on the premises its own proponents use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own cross-examination is propaganda, not honest engagement.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested by the standards a serious interlocutor would apply. Both sides.

Cross-examination

The arguments above, tested. The conservative-Islamist position is probed first because that is where the steelman has historically done the most laundering work; the liberal-democratic position is probed in turn.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "if implemented correctly" move

The package is doctrinally coherent and produces women's flourishing when the male obligations (nafaqa, mahr protection, custody fairness) are actually enforced as the classical scheme designed them.

After fourteen centuries, the position cannot point to a single Muslim-majority polity, contemporary or historical, where the package produced women's flourishing on metrics women themselves identify: life expectancy, education access, divorce parity, freedom of movement, freedom of marriage choice. The "if implemented correctly" move is unfalsifiable: every empirical failure is dismissed as miscalibration. A doctrinal claim that has had fourteen hundred years and dozens of attempts to demonstrate itself, and has not, has lost the right to keep deferring its case to "next time, properly."

The "implementation problem" framing

Where the package produces subordination, the failure is in implementation (corrupt courts, weak nafaqa enforcement, local patriarchal custom contaminating the doctrine), not in the package's design.

If a system has produced subordinating outcomes everywhere it has been tried, across centuries and radically different political conditions (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan, classical Andalusia, the Ottoman millet system, informal sharia councils in Birmingham); the design is part of the answer, not an external variable. Calling this an "implementation problem" treats the empirical record as separable from the doctrinal framework; a separation the system's own theology rejects when it claims the framework is divinely revealed and complete.

The metaphysical-premise dodge

The doctrine is internally coherent on its own metaphysical premises: men and women divinely ordained to complementary roles with distinct legal capacities; liberal critique simply applies premises the position does not share.

True and trivial. Every system is coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether those premises are defensible: whether the theological case for treating men and women as ontologically different in legal capacity survives examination. The position cannot make that case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling.

The exit-cost asymmetry

Women within the regime have legitimate paths of redress: khul' divorce, mahr enforcement, work permission, the qadi's discretion.

Khul' in most jurisdictions requires the husband's cooperation or compensation; mahr enforcement varies by court; work permission depends on his consent across Gulf and South Asian implementations; exit from Islam altogether carries apostasy consequences in many contexts. The scheme provides nothing for a Muslim woman who concludes the gender-anthropology premise is simply wrong and wants to live by a different premise without sanction. A defense of a system that requires its female subjects to accept its gender metaphysics on pain of legal or social cost is not a defense; it is a description of the coercion problem.

The Western-women-too tu-quoque

Western legal equality conceals informal subordination: the second shift, the wage gap, invisible care labor, sexualized commodification. The Islamic settlement is at least honest about role asymmetry.

True about Western informal asymmetry; irrelevant to the comparison. The question is which framework produces better measurable outcomes on metrics women themselves identify: life expectancy, education access, divorce parity, custody parity, freedom of movement and marriage choice, protection from honor-based violence. The empirical record is not close. The WEF Global Gender Gap, the UNDP Gender Inequality Index, life-expectancy gaps, female labor-force participation, age-at-first-marriage: all run sharply in one direction. The tu-quoque hides the comparison rather than answering it. The position's serious advocates know this, which is why it is the lay-defense form of the argument, not the scholarly one.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency objection

Western formal sex equality is a 20th-century achievement (US 19th Amendment 1920, Married Women's Property Acts 1870–1882, marital rape criminalized only in the 1970s–1990s in most US states); informal equality is incomplete. If the equal-citizenship framework is universal and self-evident, why did the West only discover it recently?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not ignore it. The honest answer: we were wrong before, the same way the West was wrong about slavery, religious tolerance, racial segregation, and child labor. The right reply to "why didn't you have this view in 1850?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held," not "we held a different view that was equally valid." The settlement has to be defended on its merits, not its provenance; and liberal-democratic norms continue to evolve and are not always self-consistent.

The "wrong, not different" claim

Some doctrinal positions on gender (death penalty for adultery, child marriage, half-share testimony, polygamy without consent) are wrong, not merely "another tradition." This is a substantive cross-civilizational moral claim with imperial echoes.

The claim is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can: Ayaan Hirsi Ali, Masih Alinejad, Asra Nomani, Irshad Manji, Mona Eltahawy, Taslima Nasrin, the Iranian women on the streets after Mahsa Amini, the diaspora honour-killing victims' surviving sisters, Musawah, Sisters in Islam. The argument has to be made through these voices, not over them. Acknowledge the civilizational asymmetry, defend the substantive claim on its merits, and refuse the relativist deflection that would silence Muslim women's voices in the name of respecting Muslim-majority cultures.

The cross-examination above is the project's method: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature; it has been refuted by honest engagement. That is the only refutation its serious advocates would consider worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK

Sharia councils, divorce, and women's standing

The UK's ~85 informal sharia councils handle mainly women's applications for religious divorce (khul'); a divorce a Muslim wife typically cannot get from her husband without third-party mediation. The Casey Review (2016) and subsequent inquiries found that some councils condoned wife-beating, ignored marital rape claims, and applied custody and inheritance frameworks inconsistent with English equality law.

Women use these councils because they need a religious divorce their imam will recognize, not because civil divorce is unavailable. The friction is exactly there: a parallel jurisdiction operating on inegalitarian rules, voluntarily entered by women whose alternative is community-recognized marital limbo.

Sources: UK Parliament evidence on sharia councils
US

Religious arbitration and family-court enforcement

US courts enforce religious-arbitration awards (Catholic, Jewish, Islamic) under the Federal Arbitration Act, with a public-policy carve-out: awards violating fundamental constitutional rights are unenforceable. Litigation through the 2010s established that Islamic mahr contracts are generally enforceable as prenuptials; custody and child-support awards inconsistent with state best-interest standards are not.

The live dispute in US scholarship is whether voluntary entry into a religiously-asymmetric arbitration regime should be respected as autonomy or refused as effectively coerced. Same question the UK debate raises.

Source: Multiple state-level cases since ~2000.
Japan

Low immediate friction, persistent domestic gender gap

Japan ranks 118th of 146 in the WEF Global Gender Gap 2024: the lowest among OECD economies. The Islamic 男女平等 (gender equality) fault line has not surfaced as a public issue: Japan's own gender equality is not where the West sits, and the small Muslim population (~0.18%) has produced no visible friction cases.

A future deep dive should ask whether Japan's approach to gender (less legal hierarchy, persistent informal hierarchy) gives it a different relationship to Islamic family-law norms than the European one: closer in some respects, sharper in others.

Source: WEF Global Gender Gap 2024

Case study: gender coercion from Tehran to the diaspora

Two empirical objects, one fault line. Inside Iran: the largest sustained women-led uprising in the Islamic Republic's history, sparked by Jina Mahsa Amini's death in custody (September 2022), produced ~500 protester deaths, 22,000+ arrests, and the European Parliament's first call to designate Iran's enforcement apparatus as terrorist. In the European and North American diaspora: a cluster of "honour killings" of girls whose offence was Westernization, refusal of forced marriage, or relationships with non-Muslim partners. Iran gives the state-coercion anchor; the diaspora gives the intra-Western evidence that the problem does not stop at the West's borders. The poll cards below sit on top of both.

Mahsa Amini, "Woman, Life, Freedom," and the diaspora honour-killings cluster

Iran, Sept 2022 – present · UK / Germany / Canada honour-killings cluster, 2002–2012 · scope: state coercion abroad + family coercion in The West · women-led contestation in both directions

On 16 September 2022, Jina Mahsa Amini died in a Tehran hospital three days after detention by the Gasht-e Ershad ("Guidance Patrol") for hijab non-compliance. The UN Independent Fact-Finding Mission on Iran concluded her death was "unlawful and caused by physical violence" for which the Iranian state is responsible. The protests that followed were the largest sustained women-led uprising in the Islamic Republic's history; the regime's response (mass shootings, sexual assault of detainees, public hangings) is one of the best-documented contemporary cases of state-enforced complementarianism. Inside The West, the same fault line appears in a different register: a cluster of family honour killings of girls aged 16 to 23: Heshu Yones (London 2002), Hatun Sürücü (Berlin 2005), Banaz Mahmod (London 2006), Aqsa Parvez (Toronto 2007), Shafilea Ahmed (Cheshire 2003, conviction 2012). The same coercion logic: executed by states in one geography, by families in another.

What the case shows about this fault line. Two pieces of evidence that look very different: Iranian security forces beating a 22-year-old to death for a loose headscarf, and a British father stabbing his 16-year-old daughter 17 times for dating a Christian; they sit on the same axis once you look at the polls. Pew's international baselines (74–93% "wife must obey" across the regions of origin) and ICM 2016's finding that 39% of British Muslims hold the same view (versus 5% of the wider population) are the bridge. The diaspora honour-killings statistics are systematically undercounted: the practice depends on community concealment, the victims' families are the perpetrators, and English juries returned "missing person" or "suicide" verdicts for years before Heshu Yones was correctly classified in 2002–03. Iran shows what the maximalist-state version of complementarian coercion looks like at scale; the diaspora cases show its private-family version in cities where the law in principle protects the daughter equally. The women on both sides (Mahsa Amini's funeral chanters in Saqqez, Banaz Mahmod making her fifth police report, Masih Alinejad still publishing from a Brooklyn safe-house) are the same constituency, fighting the same logic, in the same lifetime. Any honest position on this fault line has to centre them, not deflect from them.

Highest-burden Muslim-majority countries include Bangladesh (~50% of girls married before 18), Yemen, Sudan, Niger, and Mali. The Joint Programme operates in 18 focus countries.
Caveat: Child marriage in Muslim-majority contexts tracks law, custom, conflict, and poverty rather than any single doctrinal mandate. Indonesia (the largest Muslim country) has substantially lower child-marriage rates than Christian-majority Niger. The doctrinal anchor most often cited (Aisha tradition) is contested across reformist Sunni and Shi'a scholarship; the country-by-country variation is the empirical fact.

FGM · UN data
UNICEF, Female Genital Mutilation: A Global Concern, 2024 update; WHO FGM fact sheet (current); UNFPA–UNICEF Joint Programme on the Elimination of FGM, 2024 Annual Report.
Method: Aggregated DHS and MICS surveys across 90+ countries. Headline: 230+ million girls and women alive today have undergone FGM, up 30 million (15%) on the 2016 figure; 4.4 million girls at risk this year. 144 million cases in Africa; 80 million in Asia (Indonesia the largest single country pool); 6 million in the Middle East. Country prevalence among women 15–49: Somalia 99%, Guinea 96%, Djibouti 90%, Mali 89%, Sierra Leone 86%, Egypt 87%, Sudan 87%. To meet SDG 5.3, progress would need to accelerate 27×.
Caveat: The religious framing is contested. WHO records FGM as a cultural rather than doctrinal practice; the major Sunni and Shi'a juridical bodies that have ruled have either condemned it or denied it any religious requirement (al-Azhar 2006/2007 conferences and fatwa; Mauritania 34-scholar 2010 fatwa). Practice tracks geography and ethnicity more closely than confession: the largest Muslim country (Indonesia) and several Christian-majority African populations both practise it. Use the country-level data, not the regional or "Muslim world" averages.

UK statistics · Forced marriage
UK Home Office & Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office, Forced Marriage Unit Statistics 2024, published 2025.
Method: Joint-unit casework data. 2024: 240 cases supported (down 15% on 2023's 283); plus 572 enquiries signposted out. Pakistan was the focus country in 107 cases (45%); Bangladesh and India the next largest. Domestic-only cases (no overseas element) rose from 8 (2022) to 10 (2023) to 15 (2024). The 2023 raising of the legal marriage age to 18 in England and Wales is one explanation for the case-count fall, alongside outreach effects. The FMU itself flags that the data is not a prevalence measure; it counts what reaches the unit.

UK statistics · FGM
NHS England, Female Genital Mutilation Annual Report April 2023 – March 2024; Crown Prosecution Service FGM prosecution record through 2024.
Method: Mandatory FGM clinical recording across NHS Trusts since 2015. Each annual report identifies thousands of women and girls newly attending NHS services with FGM, almost all of whom underwent the procedure abroad before arrival in the UK. Prosecutions remain extremely low: three convictions in total since the offence was created in 1985. The first ever conviction for conspiracy to commit FGM in England and Wales (R v Emad Kaky, Nottingham Crown Court, September 2024) is the most recent. The clinical-volume to conviction gap is the structural prosecution problem.

Honor crimes · HRW
Human Rights Watch, Honoring the Killers: Justice Denied for "Honor" Crimes in Jordan, 2004; HRW reporting on Pakistan's 2016 Anti-Honour Killings Law and its limits (2017–present); HRW World Report 2025, Pakistan chapter.
Method: Field research, interviews, court-record analysis. Jordan: 15–20 reported cases per year (rising to 26 in 2016 per the Sisterhood is Global Association); prosecutorial leniency rooted in Jordanian penal-code articles 340 and 98. Pakistan: rights groups estimate roughly 1,000 honour killings per year; the 2016 Anti-Honour Killings Law (passed after the murder of Qandeel Baloch) only partially closed the legal-heir pardon loophole, leaving conviction rates low. The HRW record is the in-region anchor that pairs with the UK / Germany / Canada diaspora cluster already covered.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The Musawah synthesis on its merits. The Muslim-feminist case for reconciling CEDAW with classical jurisprudence deserves serious engagement: where does it succeed and where does it strain? Is "Islamic feminism" a stable settlement or a transitional position?
  • The Iran 2022–23 protests as data. Largest contemporary mass mobilization of Muslim women against the complementarian state. What does the demographic and political structure of that movement tell us about the layered picture the hub describes?
  • UK sharia council quantitative data. Beyond Casey's qualitative findings: what is the actual case-load, outcome distribution, and follow-up of women whose cases were heard? Phase 2 should locate or commission proper survey work.
  • Complementarian-vs.-equal in non-Islamic Western settings. Catholic and Orthodox Jewish family-law traditions hold structurally similar positions. Why are the comparable controversies smaller (selection, size, the specific legal mechanisms)? What does the comparison tell us?
  • Honour-based violence prosecution data. UK 2024: 2,755 honour-based abuse offences recorded; three FGM convictions in total since 1985 (the September 2024 R v Kaky verdict was the first ever for conspiracy to commit FGM). Phase 2 should triangulate with EU-level EIGE and DG-JUST data and assess the prosecution-rate gap.
  • Child marriage and the doctrinal-vs-cultural question. Indonesia (the world's largest Muslim country) has substantially lower child-marriage rates than Christian-majority Niger; Bangladesh has among the world's highest. UNICEF's country-level data refuses a single doctrinal explanation. Phase 2 should test the "law, custom, conflict, poverty" model against doctrinal-mandate claims country by country.
  • FGM as religion-vs-geography test. Practice tracks ethnicity and region more than confession; al-Azhar 2007 and Mauritania 2010 are the strongest Muslim-internal repudiations. Phase 2 should map the doctrinal pushback against FGM, the diaspora-cutting prosecution record across France, the Netherlands, Sweden, and the US, and what drives the variance between high-clinical-recording / low-prosecution UK and the higher-prosecution French model.
