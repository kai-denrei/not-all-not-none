Fault Line · 02

Religion and state / 宗教と国家: secularism / laïcité vs. sharia governance

Whether the state stays doctrinally neutral toward religious truth claims, or whether its job is to organize public life around one true religion's settled requirements.

scaffold · sources gathering Phase 1
Western liberal democracies institutionalize 政教分離 (separation of religion and state): neutral toward competing doctrines, funding and enforcing none. Political-Islamist projects hold that the state's job is to enforce sharia in public life, and that "neutrality" is itself a Western dogma pretending to be the absence of one.

Steelmanned positions

Each position at its most thoughtful, not its worst proponent on a bad day.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Sharia is public, not private

Islam, in the Mawdudi–Qutb–Banna formulation, is a nizam shamil: a comprehensive system covering law, economics, education, ritual, and governance. To privatize it into a "religion" in the post-Westphalian European sense is to amputate it. The strongest version concedes that classical Islamic states tolerated religious diversity (the dhimma system) and that no historical caliphate ever ran anything like totalitarian uniformity. What it refuses is the European move of declaring religion a private hobby with no public authority.

The critique extends to "secular neutrality" itself: a state that bans the hijab in classrooms, regulates ritual slaughter, and treats Friday as a workday is not neutral toward religion; it has chosen a particular post-Christian secular settlement and enforces it. The honest demand is that the public sphere be organized around revealed law, not around the European compromise that pretends to have no organizing principle while in fact having one.

Liberal-democratic articulation

The state is doctrine-neutral so citizens can be doctrine-free

Liberal secularism's wager, most explicit in French laïcité and more accommodating in American First-Amendment doctrine, is that the state's public-square neutrality is what makes private religious freedom possible. A state that picks a religion to enforce will sooner or later persecute those who hold the wrong one: reformist Muslims, ex-Muslims, Sufis, Shia under Sunni rule, Sunni under Shia rule, Ahmadis everywhere. The record is clear.

The strongest version concedes that "neutrality" is never perfect; the calendar still has Christmas in it, the cathedral still casts a longer shadow than the mosque. It claims only that imperfect, revisable neutrality is better than perfectionist enforcement of any single creed, because the imperfection is correctable and the perfectionism is not. That asymmetry is the entire case.

Method note. Each card presents the position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own cross-examination is propaganda, not an honest reading.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. Both arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would apply. Both sides.

Cross-examination

On this fault line, the conservative-Islamist position is most likely to win the abstract debate ("every state has an organising principle; yours just hides it") and most likely to lose on what it does in office. Both sides tested below.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "secular = comprehensive Western doctrine" conflation

A state that bans the hijab in classrooms, regulates ritual slaughter, and treats Friday as a workday is not neutral toward religion; it has chosen a particular post-Christian secular settlement and enforces it. "Neutrality" is therefore itself a doctrine, no less imposed than a confessional one; the choice is between competing comprehensive frameworks, not between a framework and its absence.

The argument equivocates between two senses of "neutrality." The liberal claim is not metaphysical neutrality (no settlement has that) but jurisdictional neutrality: the state does not adjudicate competing salvation-claims and does not deploy coercion to enforce one. A regime that lets you keep, change, or abandon your religion without legal consequence is not "imposing secularism" in the same sense that a regime that executes apostates is imposing Islam. That asymmetry is the operative content. Treating the two as symmetric framings of the same underlying coercion launders enforcement as merely a different choice of organising principle.

The "we'll govern Islamically when we're a majority" tell

In the Mawdudi–Qutb–Banna formulation the position is candid: sharia is the legitimate organising principle of public life and the long-term goal is a state that enforces it. In Western diaspora contexts the same currents typically present as merely asking for accommodation, religious courts for family law, mosque autonomy, dress freedom, within the existing constitutional order.

The two positions are not in tension; they are sequenced. The accommodation register is operative where the demographic and institutional position does not yet support the substantive one. The 2025 French Interior Ministry report on the Muslim Brotherhood, and the 38% of French Muslims (57% under 25) saying religious rules should take precedence over Republic law, is the empirical record of a movement that does have a long-term governance project and a measurable constituency for it. The question is not "is the accommodation request unreasonable?" Most are reasonable in isolation. It is whether accommodation is being requested by actors who, on their own published programme, would not extend it to dissenters once the demographic permits it. That is not a paranoid liberal projection; it is the position's own self-description in its movement-internal literature.

The dhimma-as-tolerance reframe

Classical Islamic states tolerated significant religious diversity through the dhimma system; Christians and Jews retained their faith, their courts, and their communal autonomy under Muslim sovereignty. The position can therefore claim a longer record of confessional pluralism than post-Westphalian Europe achieved.

The dhimma is a real historical institution, and the comparison to early-modern European confessional warfare is not entirely flattering to Europe. What it is not is the equal-citizenship pluralism a contemporary liberal-democratic state offers. Dhimmis paid the jizya as a discriminatory poll tax, were excluded from public office and military service, faced legal restrictions on dress, building, riding, and mixed-confession testimony, and depended on the contingent goodwill of the sovereign. The system tolerated subordination, not equality. Citing it as a model is either a concession that subordinated tolerance is the position's preferred ceiling, or a category error about what contemporary religious-liberty claims are asking for. Pick one.

The laïcité-was-written-against-Catholics deflection

The 1905 law was drafted against the Catholic Church's institutional grip on the Third Republic, and is now applied by an overwhelmingly post-Christian state apparatus disproportionately to a Muslim minority that arrived after the bargain was struck. The rule was made by one party for itself and is now being enforced against another.

The first half is true and historically important. The second does not follow. The rule that "the Republic neither recognizes nor employs nor subsidizes any cult" is the same rule applied to any cult that arrives after it. It is precisely not a confession-specific provision; the Catholic Church is no longer state-funded, Catholic religious instruction is not in public schools, Catholic clerical dress is not worn by public-school teachers on duty. The asymmetry critics name is real at the level of which religion is currently most visibly affected, because Catholic visibility had already been retracted by 1905, but it is not asymmetric at the level of the rule. Conflating "applied to us" with "designed against us" is rhetorically powerful and analytically incorrect, and the position's serious advocates know the difference.

The "every state is religious about something" relativism

Western secular states are no less doctrinal than Islamic ones; they enforce human rights as a comprehensive moral framework, take positions on gender and sexuality, regulate education in ideologically loaded ways, and punish dissent from the ruling consensus through informal social mechanisms. The choice is between competing civic religions, not between religion and its absence.

Coherent on premises, weak on evidence. The relevant comparison is not whether either system has commitments (both do) but the size of the consequences for declining to share them. A French citizen who rejects the laïcité settlement may write polemics, vote for parties opposed to it, found associations, run for office, and emigrate; the worst formal sanction is loss of public-employment access in narrow circumstances. A Saudi or Iranian citizen who publicly rejects the religion-state synthesis faces apostasy, blasphemy, and security-court provisions whose ceiling is execution. Treating these two regimes as merely "different civic religions" is a category error large enough to change everyone's risk calculation when they choose where to live. That asymmetry is the entire substantive content the relativism is designed to obscure.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "neutrality" claim that isn't quite neutral

The position holds that imperfect, revisable, jurisdictional neutrality beats perfectionist enforcement of any single creed. But the IFOP/Elmaniya 2023 finding that 78% of French Muslims experience laïcité as currently applied as discriminatory is not nothing; and the post-2004 stack of laws (hijab, burqa, abaya) was passed by an overwhelmingly post-Christian legislature applying the rule predominantly to one demographically Muslim domain.

The honest answer is that "jurisdictional neutrality" is the principle and the post-2004 application has often outrun it. The position should distinguish between (a) the 1905 law on its own terms, which 79% of French Muslims support, and (b) the more recent perimeter laws that a substantial intra-French debate (Reset DOC, the Conseil d'État Villeneuve-Loubet ruling) treats as overreach. The defensible liberal position is not "everything the French state has done in the name of laïcité is correct"; it is that the underlying principle is correct and the enforcement perimeter is itself revisable through democratic and judicial process, which is what the Conseil d'État has at points done. Treating every laïcité enforcement decision as automatically vindicated by the framework's legitimacy hands the conservative-Islamist position its strongest empirical grievance.

The competing-liberal-models problem

"Liberal-democratic" treatment of religion-state separation is presented as a single settlement, but the page itself shows three radically different versions: French laïcité (strict separation, restrictions on public religious symbols), American First-Amendment (free exercise plus disestablishment, strong accommodation), Anglo-Dutch multicultural (state recognition of communal religious institutions). The strongest conservative reply: which one is the universal liberal answer?

The reply concedes more than it should. There is no single liberal-democratic religion-state settlement. What the family of settlements shares is narrower: jurisdictional separation of religious authority and state coercion, equal citizenship across confession, and revisability through democratic process. Within that frame, France, the US, and the UK have made different defensible bets about how much religious visibility the public square should accommodate. The liberal position is the family, not any single member. Defenders should be honest that the within-liberal disagreement is real and substantive; the answer to "which model" is partly a matter of national history and partly an open empirical question about which architecture best sustains both individual conscience and social cohesion under demographic pressure. The page's case study is in part France discovering, law by law, that the answer is not as obvious as the 1905 framers thought.

The cross-examination is this project's core method: steelman, then expose. On this fault line the conservative-Islamist position lands its strongest rhetorical blow ("your neutrality is itself a doctrine") and the liberal position lands its strongest empirical one ("ours is correctable and yours is not"). Both arguments are partly right. The answer lives in the asymmetry of consequences for dissent, not in either side's preferred level of abstraction.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Laïcité, the 1905 law, and its 21st-century stress-test

France's 1905 Law on the Separation of the Churches and the State set the constitutional baseline: the Republic "neither recognizes nor employs nor subsidizes any cult." The framework was designed against the Catholic Church's institutional grip on the Third Republic. Applied to a 21st-century Muslim minority of ~5–6 million, it produces continuous flashpoints: hijab in schools (banned 2004), niqab in public space (banned 2011), abaya in schools (banned 2023).

Critics inside and outside France argue laïcité has taken an "illiberal turn," applied more strictly to Islamic visibility than to the Catholic infrastructure the law inherited. Defenders argue the rule is the same; the visibility is what changed.

Sources: 1905 law overview · Institut Montaigne on laïcité
US

Establishment Clause and the asymmetric doctrine

The US runs the opposite settlement from France: the First Amendment forbids the state from establishing any religion (including secularism as a comprehensive doctrine) but explicitly protects free exercise. Religious head-coverings are constitutionally protected in most public contexts; anti-sharia statutes targeting only Islamic law are unconstitutional (Awad v. Ziriax, 2012); state actors cannot promote any religion's claims.

The friction is around the line between accommodation and establishment: religious-arbitration enforcement, public-school release time, prison and military chaplaincy budgets, zoning for religious buildings. The line is litigated case by case rather than legislated comprehensively.

Source: ACLU — Awad v. Ziriax
Japan

Article 20 and a constitutionally-secular state with low Islamist pressure

Japan's postwar constitution (1947) Article 20 enshrines religious freedom and bars state participation in religious activity, a strict separation closer to American doctrine than to Continental establishment models. The Shinto-state nexus dismantled in 1945 is what the article was designed to prevent recurring.

The religion-state question has not become an Islamic-political question in Japan because the demographic mass is small (~0.18%) and Japan's immigration regime does not produce the second-generation religious-revival pattern documented in Europe. The constitutional baseline exists; the friction does not.

Source: Constitution of Japan, Article 20.

Case study: France's laïcité stress-test, 1989–2025

No European state has spent more political capital, across left and right governments alike, testing religion-state separation against a large Muslim minority than France has over the past three and a half decades. The arc runs from a single 1989 schoolgirl-headscarf affair in Creil to a 2025 Conseil de défense on the Muslim Brotherhood, by way of seven major laws, two Conseil d'État reversals, and a steady IFOP polling series that shows what French Muslims actually think as the legal architecture is built around them. The case is here because it is the most-documented working laboratory in the West for what happens when laïcité stops being abstract.

From Creil 1989 to the 2021 separatism law and beyond

October 1989 – May 2025 · seven major laws & two Conseil d'État rulings · ~5–6 million French Muslims · the legal architecture vs. the polling distribution it was built around

The French state's response to the visibility of Islam in public life has been a thirty-six-year sequence of incremental legislation, each round tightening the perimeter the 1905 law established against Catholic clerical power. The 2021 loi confortant le respect des principes de la République, known as the "loi séparatisme", is where the legislator stopped naming a garment or a venue and started naming a project, "le séparatisme islamiste," that the Republic was authorizing itself to disrupt. As Macron put it at Les Mureaux on 2 October 2020: "a conscious, theorised, political-religious project materialising through repeated deviations from the Republic's values, often reflected by the formation of a counter-society." Three and a half years of enforcement later, 741 establishments closed, more than 40 mosques shuttered since 2017, the CFCM dissolved and FORIF launched, the question is no longer whether the architecture exists. It is whether the polling distribution it sits on top of can support it.

  • 3 Oct 1989 The "Creil affair." Three middle-school pupils, Leïla, Fatima, and Samira, are excluded from Collège Gabriel-Havez in Creil (Oise) for refusing to remove their headscarves. The Conseil d'État issues an opinion on 27 November 1989 holding that wearing religious symbols is not by itself incompatible with laïcité. The episode opens the question that will run for thirty-five years: where is the line between private religious freedom and the school as a neutral space?
  • 15 Mar 2004 Loi 2004-228 bans the wearing of "conspicuous" religious symbols (large crosses, kippas, headscarves, turbans) in public primary and secondary schools. Drafted under the Stasi Commission. The European Court of Human Rights upholds the law against challenges (Dogru v. France, 4 December 2008; Kervanci v. France, same date).
  • 11 Oct 2010 Loi 2010-1192 bans the concealment of the face in public space (the "burqa ban"). Upheld by the Constitutional Council (decision 2010-613 DC) and by the European Court of Human Rights Grand Chamber in S.A.S. v. France (1 July 2014) on the basis of "vivre ensemble."
  • 26 Aug 2016 Conseil d'État, Villeneuve-Loubet (no. 402742). The administrative supreme court suspends mayor Lionnel Luca's burkini-on-beach ordinance, holding that without a proven public-order risk, "the emotion and anxiety resulting from terrorist attacks, and notably the one committed in Nice on July 14, could not justify the prohibition." The ruling is the textbook case for what laïcité cannot do, a constraint Macron will later describe as pointing in the wrong direction.
  • 2 Oct 2020 Macron's Mureaux speech announces a forthcoming law against "le séparatisme islamiste." Two weeks later (16 October), Samuel Paty is beheaded in Conflans-Sainte-Honorine after showing Charlie Hebdo cartoons in a class on freedom of expression, accelerating the bill's passage.
  • 24 Aug 2021 Loi 2021-1109, the "loi confortant le respect des principes de la République," is promulgated. Strengthens administrative closure of places of worship, restricts homeschooling, requires associations receiving public subsidies to sign a "contrat d'engagement républicain," and tightens controls on foreign funding of cults. The Conseil constitutionnel (decision 2021-823 DC, 13 August 2021) validates the bulk of the text and censures only two narrow provisions on association dissolution and residence permits.
  • 5 Feb 2022 The Conseil français du culte musulman (CFCM), created by Sarkozy in 2003, is effectively wound down after the Interior Ministry refuses further dialogue with it citing foreign-state influence. Replaced by the Forum de l'Islam de France (FORIF), with members directly selected by prefects rather than elected by federations.
  • 27 Aug 2023 Education minister Gabriel Attal announces by ministerial memorandum that the abaya constitutes a "conspicuous manifestation of religious affiliation" under the 2004 law and is therefore prohibited in public schools from the September rentrée. The Conseil d'État rejects two summary appeals (7 September 2023 and a second, October 2023) and confirms the ban on the merits in 2024.
  • 13 Oct 2023 Teacher Dominique Bernard is murdered at the Collège-Lycée Gambetta in Arras by a former pupil radicalised online. The IFOP/Elmaniya survey six weeks later finds that 16% of French Muslims, and 31% of Muslim secondary/higher students, stop short of total condemnation of the attacker (vs. 5% nationally).
  • 9 Apr 2024 Senate information report "Tout reste à faire" on the application of the 2021 law: 741 establishments closed temporarily or definitively, but the Senate concludes the administrative architecture is under-resourced relative to the project of "entrisme" the law was designed to disrupt.
  • 21 May 2025 Macron convenes a Conseil de défense on the Muslim Brotherhood the day after the Interior Ministry publishes its 75-page report "Frères musulmans et islamisme politique en France." The report names Musulmans de France (rebranded UOIF) and the Brussels-based Council of European Muslims as the institutional spine of an explicitly non-violent, long-term entryism strategy. New legislation promised by end of summer 2025.

Sentiment polls: what laïcité is being built on top of

The IFOP series for the Comité Laïcité République, the Fondation Jean-Jaurès, and Elmaniya runs from 2016 through 2023 with a consistent methodology: national French sample plus a Muslim oversample of ~500–1,000, online or face-to-face. The findings below are the empirical answer to the question every laïcité debate begs and few participants ask: what does the constituency on the receiving end of these laws actually think? There is no single "Muslim opinion"; there is a distribution, with substantial intra-Muslim majorities supporting the 1905 baseline, and substantial minorities (in some cases solid majorities of younger respondents) holding positions explicitly incompatible with the post-2004 stack.

What the arc shows about this fault line. The French sequence is not a story about a state cracking down on a religion. It is a story about a state discovering, law by law, that the 1905 settlement, drafted against a Catholic Church that had already lost the argument with modernity, does not automatically work against a religious tradition that has not. The 2021 law is the moment the legislator stopped pretending laïcité was neutral and started naming what it was for. The IFOP series explains why. The 79% French-Muslim support for the 1905 law and the 46% IFOP/Montaigne "secularised or integrating" cluster are the constituency for whom French laïcité is a livable settlement; their existence makes the entire enterprise defensible. The 38% saying religious rules trump Republic laws (57% under 25), the 78% calling current laïcité discriminatory, the 66% opposing caricatures in class, the 16–31% post-Arras condemnation gap: these are the illiberal-but-overwhelmingly-peaceful middle the hub describes. They are not the terrorism problem. They are the constitutional one. The Brotherhood-report defendants, the dissolved associations, the ~40 closed mosques sit on the violent-or-violence-adjacent tail the 2021 law was actually built to reach. A laïcité worth defending has to be calibrated against all three layers at once: strict enough to disrupt the entryism project the 2025 report names, generous enough not to alienate the 79% who already accept the 1905 baseline, and honest enough to admit the middle is large, growing among the young, and won't be argued out of its position by a Conseil d'État ruling. France has produced a lot of evidence. It has not yet produced consensus, even inside France, about what the evidence means.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im (Sudanese-American), Islam and the Secular State (2008): the canonical intra-Muslim case that secular state ≠ secular society and that a religiously-neutral state is the precondition for genuine Islamic faith. · Mustafa Akyol (Turkish, Cato Institute), Reopening Muslim Minds (2021): the Islamic tradition's own Mu'tazilite rationalist current supports limits on religious coercion. · Olivier Roy (French, EUI), not Muslim himself but the most-cited European theorist on the question; in Holy Ignorance argues that Islamism in Europe is a symptom of religion's privatization, not its triumph. · Tariq Ramadan (Swiss, contested figure post-2017 sexual-assault charges), earlier work argued for a "European Islam" reconciled to liberal-democratic secular framing; cited for the intellectual position, with the caveat that his credibility is in serious dispute. · Bassam Tibi (Syrian-German, Göttingen), coined "Euro-Islam" in the 1990s; argues for Muslim-democratic synthesis and against the Brotherhood/Salafi rejection of secular constitutional order. · Soheib Bencheikh (former Grand Mufti of Marseille), argued publicly that French laïcité is theologically defensible from within Islam and that French Muslim institutions should not require special accommodation against it.

  • Indonesia's Pancasila compromise. The world's largest Muslim-majority democracy rejects both pure secularism and pure theocracy. What does the constitutional and legal practice actually look like, and is it eroding under Islamist pressure?
  • The "neutrality is itself a doctrine" objection. The Islamist critique of secularism, that it's a particular post-Christian settlement enforcing its own metaphysics, has serious defenders in Western political theory (Talal Asad, Saba Mahmood). Phase 2 should engage the genealogy-of-secularism literature on its own terms rather than dismiss it.
  • Tunisia's 2014 constitution. Drafted post-Ben Ali under Ennahda's influence, balancing Article 1 ("Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state, its religion is Islam") with Article 2 ("Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship") in deliberate tension. What has the operative outcome been a decade in?
  • Japan as comparator. Article 20 establishes constitutional separation, but Japanese practice involves significant Shinto-state entanglement at the ceremonial level (imperial succession, Yasukuni). How durable is formal separation when demographic and political pressure rises?
