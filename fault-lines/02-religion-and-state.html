Fault Line · 02

Religion and state: secularism / laïcité vs. sharia governance

Whether the state is doctrinally neutral toward religious truth claims, or whether the state's job is to organize public life around one true religion's settled requirements.

scaffold · sources gathering Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Western liberal-democratic states institutionalize a separation between religious authority and state authority — neutral toward competing doctrines, neither funding nor enforcing them — while political-Islamist projects hold that the state's legitimate function is precisely to enforce sharia in public life, and that "neutrality" is itself a Western dogma masquerading as the absence of one.

Steelmanned positions

Both positions in their thoughtful form, not their bumper-sticker form.

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Sharia is public, not private

Islam, in the Mawdudi–Qutb–Banna formulation, is a nizam shamil — a comprehensive system covering law, economics, education, ritual, and governance. To privatize it into a "religion" in the post-Westphalian European sense is to amputate it. The strongest version concedes that classical Islamic states tolerated significant religious diversity (the dhimma system) and that no historical caliphate ever implemented anything resembling totalitarian uniformity. What it refuses is the European move of declaring religion a private hobby with no public authority.

The argument extends to a methodological critique of "secular neutrality": a state that bans the hijab in classrooms, regulates ritual slaughter, and treats Friday as a workday is not neutral toward religion — it has chosen a particular post-Christian secular settlement and is enforcing it. The honest demand is that the public sphere be organized around revealed law rather than around the European compromise that pretends to no organizing principle while in fact having one.

Liberal-democratic articulation

The state is doctrine-neutral so citizens can be doctrine-free

Liberal secularism's wager — most explicit in French laïcité, more accommodating in American First-Amendment doctrine, more multicultural in Anglo-Dutch settlements — is that the state's public-square neutrality is what makes private religious freedom possible. A state that picks a religion to enforce will sooner or later persecute those who hold the wrong one, including reformist Muslims, ex-Muslims, Sufis, Shia under Sunni rule, Sunni under Shia rule, Ahmadis everywhere. The historical record on this is unambiguous.

The strongest version concedes that "neutrality" is never perfect — the calendar still has Christmas in it, the cathedral still casts a longer shadow than the mosque. It claims only that an imperfect neutrality enforced through revisable democratic institutions is better than perfectionist enforcement of any single creed, because the imperfection is correctable and the perfectionism is not. The asymmetry is the entire case.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Laïcité, the 1905 law, and its 21st-century stress-test

France's 1905 Law on the Separation of the Churches and the State established the constitutional baseline: the Republic "neither recognizes nor employs nor subsidizes any cult." The framework was designed against the Catholic Church's institutional grip on the Third Republic. Applied to a 21st-century Muslim minority of ~5–6 million, it produces continuous flashpoints — hijab in schools (banned 2004), niqab in public space (banned 2011), abaya in schools (banned 2023).

Critics inside and outside France argue laïcité has taken an "illiberal turn" — applied more strictly to Islamic visibility than to the Catholic infrastructure the law inherited. Defenders argue the rule is the same, the visibility is what changed.

Sources: 1905 law overview · Institut Montaigne on laïcité
US

Establishment Clause and the asymmetric doctrine

The US runs the opposite settlement from France: the First Amendment forbids the state from establishing any religion (including secularism as a comprehensive doctrine) but explicitly protects free exercise. Result: religious head-coverings are constitutionally protected in most public contexts, anti-sharia statutes targeting only Islamic law are unconstitutional (Awad v. Ziriax, 2012), but state actors cannot promote any religion's claims.

The friction emerges around the line between accommodation and establishment — religious-arbitration enforcement, public-school release time, prison and military chaplaincy budgets, zoning for religious buildings. The line is litigated case by case rather than legislated comprehensively.

Source: ACLU — Awad v. Ziriax
Japan

Article 20 and a constitutionally-secular state with low Islamist pressure

Japan's postwar constitution (1947) Article 20 enshrines religious freedom and prohibits state participation in religious activity — a strict separation closer to American doctrine than to Continental establishment models. The Shinto-state nexus dismantled in 1945 is what the article was designed to prevent recurring.

As with most fault lines in this set, the religion-state question has not become an Islamic-political question in Japan because the demographic mass is small (~0.18%) and Japan's immigration regime does not produce the second-generation-religious-revival pattern documented in Europe. The constitutional baseline exists; the friction does not.

Source: Constitution of Japan, Article 20.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im (Sudanese-American) — Islam and the Secular State (2008): the canonical intra-Muslim case that secular state ≠ secular society and that a religiously-neutral state is the precondition for genuine Islamic faith. · Mustafa Akyol (Turkish, Cato Institute) — Reopening Muslim Minds (2021): argues the Islamic tradition's own Mu'tazilite rationalist current supports limits on religious coercion. · Olivier Roy (French, EUI) — not Muslim himself but the most-cited European theorist; in Holy Ignorance argues that Islamism in Europe is a symptom of religion's privatization, not its triumph. · Tariq Ramadan (Swiss, contested figure post-2017 sexual-assault charges) — earlier work argued for a "European Islam" reconciled to liberal-democratic secular framing; cited here for the intellectual position, with the caveat that his own credibility is in serious dispute. · Bassam Tibi (Syrian-German, Göttingen) — coined "Euro-Islam" in the 1990s; argues for Muslim-democratic synthesis and against the imported Brotherhood/Salafi rejection of secular constitutional order. · Soheib Bencheikh (former Grand Mufti of Marseille) — argued publicly that French laïcité is theologically defensible from within Islam and that French Muslim institutions should not require special accommodation against it.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The German cooperative-secularism middle path. Germany's Staatskirchenrecht recognizes religious bodies as public-law corporations and cooperates with them on tax collection, school instruction, and social services. How is the Islamic-association recognition process going (the "Islamkonferenz" since 2006), and what does it tell us about a non-French, non-American secularism model?
  • Indonesia's Pancasila compromise. The world's largest Muslim-majority democracy explicitly rejects both pure secularism and pure theocracy. What does the constitutional and legal practice actually look like, and is it eroding under Islamist pressure?
  • The "neutrality is itself a doctrine" objection. The Islamist critique of secularism — that it's a particular post-Christian settlement enforcing its own metaphysics — has serious defenders inside Western political theory (Talal Asad, Saba Mahmood). Phase 2 should engage the genealogy-of-secularism literature on its own terms rather than dismiss it.
  • Tunisia's 2014 constitution. Drafted post-Ben Ali under Ennahda's influence, balancing Article 1 ("Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state, its religion is Islam") with Article 2 ("Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship") in deliberately ambiguous tension. What has the operative outcome been a decade in?
  • Japan as comparator. Article 20 establishes constitutional separation but Japanese practice involves significant Shinto-state entanglement at ceremonial level (imperial succession, Yasukuni). What does this case tell us about how durable formal separation is when demographic and political pressure rises?
