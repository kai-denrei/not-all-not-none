Eschatology / 終末論: afterlife logic, martyrdom calculus, end-times reasoning

How belief about the next life shapes calculations in this one — particularly when the next life's reward for martyrdom enters the cost-benefit analysis of attacks on civilians.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic political thought brackets eschatology — what individuals believe about the afterlife is private, and policy reasons must be public — while certain Salafi-jihadist currents draw operational consequences from eschatological doctrine, including the explicit use of apocalyptic narrative (Dabiq, the Mahdi, the return of Isa) in recruitment and the martyrdom-reward calculation in attack planning. Most Muslims, like most Christians and Jews, hold eschatological beliefs that produce no operational violence; ISIS and AQ are the documented exceptions.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

Eschatology is settled doctrine; messianic claim is the line

The honest articulation begins by distinguishing two different eschatological claims that the position holds. The first is the broad Sunni and Shia consensus on the end-times sequence: the appearance of the Mahdi (the rightly-guided one) at the end of historical time, the return of Isa (Jesus) who will defeat the Dajjal (false messiah / Antichrist), the eschatological battles at Dabiq (Sham/Syria) and elsewhere, the abrogation of the jizya on People of the Book, the establishment of universal sharia, and the final Day of Judgment. This eschatological corpus is grounded in numerous Qur'anic passages and a substantial hadith literature (Sahih Muslim Book 41 on the signs of the Hour; Sunan Abu Dawud Book 38; Ibn Kathir's al-Nihaya fi al-Fitan wa al-Malahim). The mainstream Sunni and Twelver Shia positions hold these as doctrinally settled. The second claim is operational: that contemporary historical events — the loss of the caliphate, the mass-displacement wars in Iraq and Syria, the rise of secularism, the foundation of Israel — are signs ('alamat al-sa'a) that the end-time sequence has begun, and that participation in jihad in Sham is therefore participation in cosmically-scripted events. Will McCants' The ISIS Apocalypse (St. Martin's 2015) and Jean-Pierre Filiu's L'Apocalypse dans l'Islam (Fayard 2008; English Apocalypse in Islam, UC Press 2011) document the intensification of this operational reading among Salafi-jihadist movements since the 1990s.

The strongest version concedes that the operational-apocalyptic intensification — al-Zarqawi's deliberate injection of end-times reasoning into AQI/ISI strategy from 2004 onward, the naming of Dabiq magazine in 2014, the explicit ribat fi al-Sham framing of foreign-fighter recruitment — is innovative within the modern jihadist tradition. The strongest version draws a hard line between the eschatological corpus (settled, mainstream, religiously valid) and the operational politicization (recent, contested, often extremist). What is uniformly held — across mainstream Sunni and Twelver Shia jurisprudence — is that the doctrine of khatm al-nubuwwa (finality of prophethood with Muhammad) is a non-negotiable theological axiom, and that messianic-prophetic claim by any post-Muhammad figure (Mirza Ghulam Ahmad of Qadian in 1889, the Bahá'í Manifestation, the Druze, contemporary Mahdist claimants) is categorically outside the bounds of Islam. Pakistan's Constitution Second Amendment (1974) and Ordinance XX (1984) declared Ahmadis legal-non-Muslims on this basis; Indonesia, Malaysia, Brunei, Saudi Arabia, and most Sunni states maintain similar legal-religious classifications. The Iranian state's hidden-Imam politics (Khamenei's recurrent Mahdist appeals, Soleimani-funeral imagery, IRGC eschatological framing) is the Twelver-Shia state-level institutional expression of the same logic. The position thus holds: eschatology is real and detailed; messianic claim displaces it; the line between mainstream eschatology and Salafi-jihadist apocalyptic politicization is one most mainstream scholars are willing to defend.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Cosmic stakes do not bind other citizens' policy choices, and messianic-claim does not justify state-sanctioned exclusion

The liberal-democratic position has two limbs. On the first, eschatological beliefs are protected as private religious matters and excluded from binding public-policy reasoning. The Rawlsian formulation in Political Liberalism (1993) — that political justifications must be statable in terms of public reason accessible to citizens across comprehensive doctrines — applies symmetrically: Christian Second-Coming, Jewish messianism, Buddhist Maitreya, secular Singularity-millenarianism, and Islamic Mahdism all encounter the same constraint. Your cosmic timeline does not bind your fellow citizens' policy choices. The constraint is real and is one of the foundational political-philosophy commitments that distinguishes liberal democracy from confessional state.

On the second limb — the part the conservative articulation forces into view — the position holds that messianic claim is a religious dispute internal to a tradition, and that the secular state has no jurisdiction over the question of whether a particular figure is or is not a prophet, an Imam Mahdi, or a Manifestation of God. The state's role is to protect equal civic standing irrespective of which side of the intra-religious dispute a citizen falls on. Pakistan's 1974 Second Amendment and 1984 Ordinance XX — declaring Ahmadis legal-non-Muslims, criminalizing their use of Islamic religious vocabulary, calling their places of worship "places of worship" rather than mosques, prohibiting their use of azan — are, on the liberal view, the precise opposite of what a state should do: they take a religious-doctrinal dispute, ratify one side as state truth, and impose criminal sanctions on the other. The 2010 Lahore mosque attacks (94 Ahmadis killed in coordinated TTP/LeJ assaults on two Ahmadi mosques during Friday prayer), Indonesia's Cikeusik attacks (2011), and decades of low-grade sectarian killings of Ahmadis across South Asia are the consequence of having state machinery that ratifies the religious-doctrinal exclusion. The strongest version refuses both the relativist deflection ("this is an internal Muslim debate the West should not enter") and the universalist trap ("the Islamic position is uniquely problematic"); it argues that liberal-democratic civic standing requires that messianic-claim disputes be internal religious matters and not state-enforced legal categories, and that this requirement applies equally to Saudi Arabia (Shia status), Pakistan (Ahmadi status), Iran (Bahá'í status), and to historical Christian-state precedents (Catholic-recusant disabilities in 16th-19th century England) the West has now mostly outgrown. The argument can be made and is made from inside Muslim traditions — Ali Usman Qasmi's Ahmadis and the Politics of Religious Exclusion in Pakistan (Anthem 2014) is the model.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Case study: the Ahmadi persecution arc and the ISIS-apocalyptic data point

The most documented contemporary intersection of eschatological-doctrinal claim and state-and-mob-enforced exclusion. The Ahmadiyya Muslim Community, founded by Mirza Ghulam Ahmad of Qadian in 1889, makes a messianic-prophetic claim that mainstream Sunni and Shia jurisprudence treats as a categorical breach of the finality-of-prophethood doctrine. Pakistan's 1974 Second Amendment constitutionally declared Ahmadis legal-non-Muslims; the 1984 Ordinance XX criminalized their use of Islamic religious vocabulary. The 2010 Lahore mosque attacks killed 94 Ahmadis in coordinated TTP/LeJ assaults. Indonesia's 2011 Cikeusik attacks and the steady-state low-grade killings across South Asia continue. Paired with the ISIS Dabiq-magazine 2014–2016 datapoint, the cluster shows how eschatological doctrine — settled mainstream and operationally weaponized — produces visible costs in lives.

The dispute is real, intellectually serious, and theologically substantive within the tradition. What the case illustrates is what happens when state machinery ratifies one side of the dispute. Paired with the ISIS apocalyptic-magazine cluster (Dabiq, 15 issues 2014–2016, naming the eschatological battlefield in Sham and recruiting around the explicit end-times frame), the cluster shows two operational expressions of doctrinal-eschatological politics: state-and-mob exclusion of internal-disputed messianic claimants, and Salafi-jihadist apocalyptic mobilization. Both are real consequences of treating eschatological-doctrinal positions as politically operative rather than as intra-religious belief.

What the Ahmadi cluster and the Dabiq datapoint show together about this fault line. The cases pair because they show the same underlying logic — eschatological-doctrinal claim treated as politically operative — producing visible costs in two different shapes. The Ahmadi case is the state-and-mob expression: a legal-religious classification (Ord. XX 1984) that ratifies one side of an internal religious dispute, and the predictable cascade of mob and organized-jihadist violence (Lahore 2010, Cikeusik 2011, ongoing) that the classification enables. The ISIS-Dabiq case is the Salafi-jihadist mobilization expression: an apocalyptic frame deliberately operationalized by al-Zarqawi and his successors to convert ordinary political-military reasoning into participation in cosmically-scripted events, with documented foreign-fighter recruitment in 15–25% of cases featuring eschatological motivation prominently. The Iranian Twelver-Shia state-Mahdism rhetoric is the third, less-documented institutional expression — the operational consequences are harder to count, but the rhetorical framing of IRGC and Quds Force operations as preparation for the hidden Imam's return is documented. The fault line is not "Islam has eschatology while liberal democracy doesn't"; both have eschatology (climate apocalypse, AI Singularity, Christian Second Coming, Jewish messianism are no less present in Western public discourse). The fault line is whether eschatological-doctrinal positions are contained as private religious commitments under public-reason norms — Pew 2012 shows ~80% of Afghans believing the Mahdi will return in their lifetime without Afghanistan having state-enforced anti-Ahmadi law, demonstrating belief and operational politics can be separated — or ratified by state machinery and weaponized by movement-actors, in which case the Lahore 2010 attacks, Cikeusik 2011, and the Dabiq foreign-fighter pipeline are the documented consequences. Ali Usman Qasmi's argument from inside Pakistani Islamic studies is that the choice has been made and can be unmade. Imam Muhsin Hendricks (page 21 case) was making a parallel argument when he was assassinated in February 2025; the project is to expand the inside-critic infrastructure so the choice can be unmade through Muslim voices.

Where the argument strains

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "settled-doctrine" hardline disguised as moderation

The position presents itself as defending settled mainstream eschatology (Mahdi, Isa, Dabiq, Dajjal) and rejecting only the Salafi-jihadist operational politicization. The implicit claim: criticizing the doctrinal corpus is criticizing all Sunni and Shia Muslims; criticizing only the operationalization is the moderate position.

The position holds the line at the apocalyptic-mobilization issue while leaving the messianic-claim issue (Ahmadiyya, Bahá'í, contemporary Mahdist claimants) on the operationally-active hardline side. Pakistan's 1974/1984 legal framework, Indonesia's 2005 MUI fatwa, the Algerian 2017–2018 prosecutions, and Saudi-Iranian state policies on internal-disputed messianic claimants are contemporary operational expressions of the very logic the position claims to limit to bracketed eschatology. The "we only oppose ISIS apocalypticism" framing erases the much larger and continuously-active framework of state-enforced anti-Ahmadi, anti-Bahá'í, and anti-Shia exclusion. Will McCants and Filiu document the Dabiq case; Ali Usman Qasmi, Cassandra Balchin, and the Asian Human Rights Commission document the Ahmadi case. Both are eschatological-doctrinal politics; the position cannot disclaim one while operationally maintaining the other.

The unfalsifiable-classical-consensus claim on khatm al-nubuwwa

The doctrine of khatm al-nubuwwa (finality of prophethood with Muhammad) is presented as theologically settled across all four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia, with the Ahmadi reading therefore lying outside the bounds of legitimate intra-Islamic interpretation.

Ahmadi self-understanding is that Mirza Ghulam Ahmad's prophethood is non-law-bearing (nabi ghair tashri'i), reformist within Islam, and consistent with a particular interpretive tradition (Ibn al-'Arabi, certain Sufi articulations) of how the seal of Muhammad's prophethood operates. The dispute is therefore over which interpretation of khatm al-nubuwwa is correct, not over whether the doctrine exists. Ahmadi scholars cite Ibn 'Arabi, Mawlana Rumi, Mirza Mazhar Jan-i-Janaan, and the broader Sufi-philosophical tradition for the legitimacy of distinguishing law-bearing from non-law-bearing prophethood. Treating the mainstream Sunni interpretation as the only legitimate reading — when the dispute is precisely about interpretation within a recognized intellectual tradition — is the move that the unfalsifiable-classical-consensus framing performs. The interpretive choice has to be defended on the merits; ratifying it as state law removes the question from interpretive dispute and converts it into criminal-religious classification with the Lahore-2010-attack consequence. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of the argument, not its ceiling.

The "operational politicization is recent and external" deflection

The mainstream position holds that ISIS's apocalyptic operationalization was an innovation by al-Zarqawi (2004 onward) within the modern jihadist current — recent, contested, and rejected by mainstream contemporary Sunni scholarship including Hamza Yusuf, Yasir Qadhi, the Yaqeen Institute, al-Azhar, and the Mauritanian classical-tradition clerics opposing JNIM and ISIS-Sahel.

The mainstream-Sunni rejection of the ISIS operationalization is real and is the position's strongest defense. The Iranian Twelver-Shia state's parallel Mahdist-political framing is much harder for the position to disown. Khamenei's repeated Mahdist appeals (Friday-prayer sermons 2018, 2020, 2022; the explicit Soleimani-funeral imagery; IRGC and Quds Force eschatological framing) are state-actor operational eschatology by an Iranian establishment that has the seminary credentials of Qum, Najaf, and the Imamiyya tradition. Treating ISIS as the rogue and the Iranian state as the legitimate religious establishment requires defending why operational eschatology is illegitimate when Salafi-Sunni and operational when Twelver-Shia state-actors do it. The "recent and external" framing on the Sunni-jihadist case does not extend symmetrically to the Iranian-state case; the mainstream Sunni rejection of ISIS apocalypticism is real and the Iranian-state continuation of comparable framing is not similarly rejected by Twelver-Shia mainstream institutions.

The privacy-not-state-enforcement retreat on Ahmadis

A softer version of the position — articulated by some contemporary Sunni jurists in diaspora — holds that the Ahmadi religious-doctrinal exclusion is a religious matter for the umma, that the state should not formally enforce it, and that the Pakistan / Indonesia legal frameworks are mistakes that the broader Sunni tradition should not endorse.

The privacy-version exists but is not the operative version where the position holds state power. In every Muslim-majority jurisdiction with substantial state-establishment of religion (Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Iran on Bahá'ís, parts of Indonesia, Algeria 2017+), the messianic-claim exclusion is enforced as legal classification, not bracketed as private intra-religious dispute. The privacy-retreat is the framing offered to Western audiences; the operational behavior where the regime governs is criminalization. The rhetorical structure is identical to the same-sex-conduct strain analysis on page 21 (the moderation bait-and-switch). The mainstream-Sunni position has the resources to do this differently — the Indonesian NU 2019 Humanitarian Islam declaration shows an institutional voice on the broader-religious-pluralism side — but operative behavior in state-power contexts has not yet followed.

The metaphysical-premise dodge on cosmic stakes

The position retreats, when pressed, to the position that eschatological doctrine is coherent on its own metaphysical premises about historical time, divine providence, and the meaning of Muslim collective existence — and that liberal critique applies premises (methodological naturalism, public-reason bracketing of comprehensive doctrines) the position does not share.

The argument is partially correct and trivializing. Every comprehensive system is coherent on premises that license it; the substantive question is whether the premises survive examination — whether the case for treating internal-disputed messianic claim as criminal-state-classification (rather than internal religious matter) survives without circular appeal to the very doctrinal authority that establishes the conclusion. The position cannot make that case without invoking khatm al-nubuwwa in a particular interpretive form (the form that excludes Ahmadi self-understanding) as a non-negotiable axiom — which is exactly the move the cross-examination is testing. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling. The privacy-retreat exists as a philosophical move; the operational-state-enforcement record is the test.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The selective-comparator on millenarianism

The liberal-democratic position holds that all comprehensive doctrines — Christian Second-Coming, Jewish messianism, Islamic Mahdism, secular Singularitarianism, climate apocalypse — are equally subject to the public-reason bracketing rule, and that liberal democracy is therefore consistent in its constraints.

The constraint is real but is applied unevenly. American evangelical Christian apocalyptic-political mobilization (Tim LaHaye Left Behind influence on US foreign policy on Israel; specific Christian-Zionist political organizing) is documented at substantial scale and operates inside US politics with substantial effect on Middle East policy. Israeli religious-Zionist messianism (Gush Emunim, the contemporary settler movement, the Hardal current) operates inside an actually-functioning liberal democracy with documented consequences for occupied-territories policy. Hindu nationalist apocalyptic framing of Indian-Muslim relations under the BJP is a comparable case in the world's largest democracy. The honest liberal answer is that the public-reason constraint is aspirational and selectively applied, that liberal democracies have lived with substantial Christian-millenarian and Jewish-messianic political-electoral mobilization without enforcing the bracketing rule equally, and that pretending the rule is neutrally applied is the error. The Ahmadi-case argument therefore has to be made on the operational-consequence ground (state-enforced exclusion produces the Lahore-2010-attack pattern) rather than on a clean public-reason-symmetry ground. The honest version concedes the asymmetry and defends the substantive operational claim on its merits.

The recency objection on apocalyptic-political mobilization

The liberal-democratic constraint on apocalyptic-political reasoning consolidated in 17th–18th-century Europe in response to the Wars of Religion (1517–1648), the English Civil Wars, and the Thirty Years War. The constraint is presented as universal-applicable; its origin is the collapse of Christian-confessional politics into mass-violence under the same logic the position now warns about for Salafi-jihadist apocalyptic mobilization.

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest liberal answer is: we learned this lesson at enormous cost to our own population over 130 years of religious war, and the bracketing rule is the institutional solution we worked out. The right answer to "why didn't your law constrain Christian apocalyptic politics in 1620?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held — the disaster of mass religious-political violence forced the working-out — not because we held a different view that was equally valid." The position requires acknowledging that liberal-democratic norms continue to evolve and are not always self-consistent — and defending the current settlement on its operational-record-of-cost-avoidance, not on its provenance.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

ISIS recruitment and the European-jihadist eschatological narrative

European intelligence assessments through 2014–2018 documented the apocalyptic component of ISIS's appeal to European recruits. The 2014 Mosul declaration of caliphate, the geographic emphasis on Dabiq (in Syria, location of the prophesied final battle), and the explicit eschatological framing of the magazine of the same name were not incidental marketing — they were operational doctrine. Will McCants' The ISIS Apocalypse (2015) is the standard reference.

Counter-radicalization programs in France, the UK, the Netherlands, and Belgium have specifically targeted the eschatological frame, with mainstream-clerical refutations of the ISIS reading.

Source: "Jihad after the Fall of IS — Reframing the Apocalyptic Narrative" (academic)
US

American Muslim eschatology and the recruitment-radicalization vector

US-based ISIS recruits — the relatively small population identified through 2014–2018 — showed varying degrees of eschatological engagement. The Combating Terrorism Center at West Point and George Washington University's Program on Extremism have documented case-by-case how the apocalyptic frame functioned in specific recruitments (Tarek Mehanna case, Mohamed Khweis case, the Brooklyn cell cases). The frame was not the only motivator but figured prominently in cases where ideological vs. opportunistic recruitment can be analytically separated.

Source: GWU Program on Extremism
Japan

No documented Japanese-Muslim eschatological-recruitment cases

Japan has no significant documented case of Japanese-Muslim ISIS recruitment driven by eschatological narrative. The 2015 Goto/Yukawa hostage cases involved Japanese journalists and adventurers in Syria, not Japanese converts to apocalyptic Islamism.

A separate Japanese eschatological-violence case worth noting is Aum Shinrikyo's 1995 Tokyo subway sarin attack, motivated by Buddhist-Hindu-syncretic apocalypticism. Aum is the closest Japanese-context case study of cosmic-stakes religious violence — the structural parallels (charismatic leader, eschatological doctrine, attack on the polluted secular state) are worth noting; the religious tradition is entirely different.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Post-territorial-defeat eschatology. ISIS lost Dabiq in 2016 and the broader caliphal territory by 2019. How has the apocalyptic narrative been reframed and is it still operative in surviving affiliates?
  • The non-jihadist eschatological currents. Most Islamic eschatology is held by ordinary Muslims with no operational implications, like Christian eschatology among ordinary Christians. Phase 2 should map how mainstream-Sunni and Shia eschatological belief functions without producing political consequences — the comparison with Christianity is illuminating.
  • Iran-specific Mahdi politics. Iranian state-religious framing of the Mahdi (Twelfth Imam) doctrine has produced specific operational consequences (Khamenei's appeals, IRGC framing, Soleimani's funeral rhetoric). The Shia version of this fault line deserves its own treatment.
  • The Aum Shinrikyo comparator. Phase 2 should ask what the cross-religious-tradition study of cosmic-stakes violence (Aum, Christian Reconstructionists, certain Hindu-nationalist millenarians, ISIS) tells us about the structure of eschatological-violence motivation independent of religious content.
  • Yaqeen and counter-narrative effectiveness. The intra-Muslim refutation infrastructure has grown substantially since 2014. Phase 2 should ask what is known about its actual effectiveness in vulnerable-population de-radicalization.
