National identity vs. ummah / 国家アイデンティティとウンマ: civic citizenship vs. transnational religious community

Is the primary political identity national-civic (French, German, American) or transnational-religious, with the ummah's claims above the host 国家 (state)?

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1
Liberal democracies require operative primacy of civic-national identity in the public sphere: the citizenship oath, the loyalty assumption behind public office and military service. Political-Islamist movements (most explicitly Hizb ut-Tahrir) hold that the ummah is the believer's primary political 共同体 (community) and the nation-state a temporary scaffold to be transcended through caliphate restoration. Most European Muslims hold both identities; the Islamist current says the holding cannot last.

Steelmanned positions

Each side in its strongest form, as proponents actually defend it in writing. No euphemism.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

The ummah is the primary political community; the nation-state is a temporary scaffold

The believer's primary political community is the ummah, not the post-Westphalian nation-state, which this position reads as a European artefact (the 1648 Westphalian system, retroactively universalized) and a colonial imposition via Sykes-Picot (1916), the abolition of the Ottoman caliphate (3 March 1924), and the mandate borders that became the modern Arab states. Hizb ut-Tahrir, founded by Taqi al-Din al-Nabhani in Jerusalem in 1953, names the project openly: restore the caliphate; replace existing Muslim-majority states with an Islamic khilafa; in the interim, refuse the legitimacy of voting in non-sharia states, serving in their militaries, swearing their oaths. The Muslim Brotherhood version (Hassan al-Banna 1928, Qutb's Milestones 1964) is gradualist: institutional capture through mosques, charities, student unions, and diaspora councils, building toward an Islamic state over time.

The Diyanet/DITIB version is the state-actor form: Turkey's Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet İşleri Başkanlığı) runs mosque networks abroad. DITIB in Germany (~900 mosques), CMTGB in the Netherlands, ATIB in Austria, all staffed with imams trained, paid, and recallable by Ankara. The transnational-ummah claim and the transnational-state claim overlap: both ask European governments to accept a foreign state's religious bureaucracy as the primary address for "their" Muslim populations. Across HT, the Brotherhood, Diyanet, and major Salafi networks, one thing is held consistently: the Muslim believer's relationship to the European 国家 is conditional and revisable in light of religious obligation. Harder versions treat civic loyalty as shirk-adjacent; softer versions treat it as a temporary arrangement subordinate to the ummah.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Operative civic loyalty is the price of the citizenship bargain

Voluntary association in private religious community is fully protected. The ummah as a felt bond is no different from global Catholicism, world Jewry, or the Anglican Communion; Western democracies have lived with these transnational moral communities for centuries. The friction is not the ummah as belief; it is the active supersession of civic loyalty by organizations that explicitly treat European citizenship as a stage to transcend. Locke's Letter Concerning Toleration (1689) and Rawls's Political Liberalism (1993) give the same answer: the citizenship bargain is operative, not comprehensive. The state does not demand you love it more than God; it demands that your public-sphere conduct (voting, military service, oaths, jury duty) runs on civic terms. Catholics in 1850s America made this accommodation; American Jews under McCarthy; British dissenters in the 1820s. It has been done before; it can be done again.

Pew, Bertelsmann, and Ipsos data consistently show European Muslim national-identification rates far higher than the HT-Brotherhood position would predict. Most European Muslims hold both identities and find the transnational-supersession claim foreign. What this position refuses is funding, hosting, and legally protecting movements (HT: UK 2024, Germany 2003, France 2003; Diyanet/DITIB under BfV watch since 2017; the May 2025 French Brotherhood report) that openly articulate replacement of civic loyalty as their operating doctrine. The fight is not about belief; it is about whether institutions must subsidize their own stated replacement. Hallaq (The Impossible State, Columbia 2013) concedes the incompatibility from the Islamist side but draws the opposite conclusion: live as religious civil society within the state, not as a political project against it. Tibi's Euro-Islam makes the same move from a German liberal-Muslim standpoint.

Method note. Both cards present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language its own proponents use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that fails its own cross-examination is propaganda, not honest reading.

Case study: DITIB, the Diyanet imams, and the German “long arm of Ankara”

The clearest documented case of a state-actor transnational-religious network inside a European democracy. DITIB (Türkisch-Islamische Union der Anstalt für Religion) is the largest German Muslim umbrella (~900 mosques, ~800,000 affiliated members), formally a German registered association, operationally under the structural and personnel control of Turkey's Diyanet and through it the Turkish presidency. The 2016-2018 spy scandal, the BfV classification, and the post-Erdogan-purge imam recall are where the dual-loyalty question stops being abstract.

DITIB / Diyanet in Germany, 1984–2025

DITIB founded 1984 as Turkish-state-aligned umbrella · ~900 mosques in Germany · Diyanet personnel control documented across BfV reports 2016–2024 · imam-spying scandal 2016–2017 · German federal funding cuts 2017 · BfV begins formal observation of DITIB-aligned associations 2019–2020 · Imam-Ausbildung Germany-trained-imam programme launched 2020 · imam-recall 2024 toward German-trained replacement

DITIB is the largest Turkish-Islamic organization in Germany and the largest single Muslim federation in any Western European country. Founded in Cologne in 1984 as the German registered-association (e.V.) arm of Turkey's Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet), it became the operative religious-services provider for most Turkish-origin Sunni Muslims in Germany: Friday sermons, Qur'an classes, marriage and burial services, halal-meat certification, women's and youth associations. Its German-legal form is a non-state religious community; its operational structure is a department of the Turkish state. Until the 2024 reform, every DITIB imam was a Turkish state employee: trained at Turkish theology faculties, paid by Diyanet, posted on rotating four-to-five-year tours, recallable by Ankara, and required to deliver Friday sermons (khutba) drafted at Diyanet headquarters and distributed weekly to all DITIB mosques worldwide. After the July 2016 attempted coup against Erdogan, Diyanet instructed DITIB imams to denounce the Gülen movement (Hizmet) from their pulpits, file reports on suspected Gülen sympathizers in their German congregations, and transmit those reports to Turkish authorities. When revealed in early 2017, this triggered raids on DITIB premises in North Rhine-Westphalia, a Generalbundesanwalt criminal investigation (discontinued in late 2017 for insufficient statutory evidence), federal-funding cuts, and formal BfV observation of DITIB-aligned and Millî Görüş-aligned organizations.

  • 3 Mar 1924 Mustafa Kemal Atatürk abolishes the Ottoman caliphate; the Diyanet İşleri Başkanlığı (Directorate of Religious Affairs) is established as the secular Turkish state's instrument for managing Sunni Hanafi religion. It controls mosque appointments, Friday sermon content, and imam training. Erdogan retains and substantially expands the model from 2002 onward.
  • 1984 DITIB is founded as a German eingetragener Verein in Cologne by Turkish-state actors, formally to coordinate religious services for the Turkish-origin diaspora that arrived under the 1961-1973 Gastarbeiter agreements. Staffed and funded through Diyanet from the start.
  • 2002 onward Under the AKP government, Diyanet's foreign budget grows; imam-export programs to Germany, the Netherlands, Austria, France, and Belgium expand. Friday sermons drafted in Ankara increasingly focus on Turkish national-religious identity, the Ottoman legacy, and suspicion of the secular West.
  • 15 Jul 2016 The attempted coup against Erdogan. Within weeks, the Turkish state purges 2,700+ Diyanet personnel domestically and instructs Diyanet missions abroad to identify and report Gülen sympathizers in diaspora congregations.
  • Dec 2016 – Feb 2017 German press (Welt am Sonntag, Die Zeit, ARD) reveals that DITIB imams, on Diyanet instruction, compiled lists of suspected Gülen sympathizers in German congregations and sent them to Turkey. The Generalbundesanwalt opens a formal investigation for activity on behalf of a foreign intelligence service; raids follow at imam residences in North Rhine-Westphalia.
  • Jul 2017 The BfV publicly assesses that DITIB's structure makes it an instrument of Turkish state policy in Germany. Federal funding for DITIB integration projects is partially suspended. NRW and Bavaria review their cooperation arrangements.
  • Dec 2017 The Generalbundesanwalt discontinues the investigation for insufficient evidence of formal intelligence-service activity under German criminal law. The practice happened; it did not clear the statutory threshold. Civil-society and political reactions remain sharp.
  • 2018–2019 Dutch and Austrian intelligence services issue parallel assessments on Diyanet mosque networks. Austria's 2018 closure of seven mosques and expulsion of 40+ Turkish-paid imams under the 2015 Islam Act is the strongest European response to date.
  • Jan 2020 The German federal government and Lower Saxony launch the Islamkolleg Deutschland in Osnabrück to train Germany-resident imams and reduce Diyanet dependency. First cohorts graduate 2020–2025.
  • 2024 A Turkish-German bilateral agreement begins phased reduction of Diyanet-paid imams over five years, replacing them with Germany-trained personnel. Implementation pace and whether Diyanet retains curricular and sermon control are open as of 2025.
  • May 2025 The French Interior Ministry publishes Frères musulmans et islamisme politique en France, the most detailed European government treatment of the Brotherhood-aligned (rather than state-actor Diyanet) institutional-capture strategy. Its "entrisme" framing is methodologically separate from the DITIB / Millî Görüş question but politically linked.

What DITIB and the proscription cluster show about this fault line. The fault line has two forms, often conflated. HT, the Brotherhood network, and Salafi-jihadist organizations are the movement-actor form: civil-society organizations whose own doctrine articulates supersession of civic loyalty; European responses have been proscription (UK Jan 2024, Germany 2003, France 2003, Austria) or surveillance (Netherlands AIVD, French Interior Ministry 2025). Diyanet/DITIB and the Turkish-state network are the state-actor form: a foreign government's religious bureaucracy running mosque networks and recallable imams inside European democracies, with documented post-2016 instructions to surveil and report on diaspora congregants. The two forms call for different responses: proscription targets organizations, not foreign-state agencies. Both share the underlying friction: European citizenship assumes the primary political-loyalty address of a citizen is the domestic civic order, not a foreign 国家 or a transnational religio-political project. Bertelsmann and Policy Exchange data show most European Muslims have already made this accommodation; the proscription, surveillance, and Imam-Ausbildung debates concern the institutional-actor minority, not the population. Treating ordinary European Muslim practice as proto-Islamist is the failure mode the blue band names. Treating the documented institutional-actor cases as paranoid invention is the failure mode the black band names. Both fail; the empirical record is what remains.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. Both are presented uncensored above; below they are stress-tested. Both sides. The rainbow band is not "both positions equally defensible"; it is "both clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments above, tested against standards a serious interlocutor would apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The motte-and-bailey on dual loyalty

The version shown to liberal European audiences is the motte: every religious community has a transnational moral dimension; Catholics look to Rome, Jews to Jerusalem, Muslims to Mecca and the ummah; unobjectionable, protected by religious-liberty norms.

The version Hizb ut-Tahrir publishes in The System of Islam and The Methodology of Hizb ut-Tahrir for Change is the bailey: the existing nation-state system is illegitimate; voting and military service in non-sharia states are religiously prohibited; the operative project is caliphate restoration through three stages (cultural preparation, intellectual struggle, political seizure). Vidino's The Closed Circle (Columbia 2020) documents the Brotherhood parallel: gradualist institutional capture rather than overt secession. The motte and bailey are not the same position. The motte is for journalists; the bailey is published in movement texts and used in cell organization. When pressed, the bailey is dismissed as old material; the texts have not been retracted.

The "long-term aspiration, not current operation" deferral

Caliphate restoration is a long-term project, not a current operational program for most European Muslims. The interim demand is modest: don't require oaths in tension with religious identity; respect transnational bonds; let mosques be mosques.

The DITIB record (BfV 2017, NRW 2017 raids, Generalbundesanwalt 2017 investigation) shows "long-term aspiration" coexisting with current operational activity that does clear the dual-loyalty threshold. Diyanet-instructed imams compiling Gülen-sympathizer lists in German congregations and transmitting them to Turkey is not aspirational; it is current intelligence-adjacent activity by a foreign state through a diaspora's religious infrastructure. The "aspiration" framing absorbs present-tense operations into a future-tense category that blocks scrutiny. Austria's 2018 mosque closures and imam expulsions were made on present-tense grounds, not future ones.

The "ordinary civil society" laundering

Brotherhood-aligned organizations (France: Musulmans de France / UOIF; UK: Muslim Association of Britain; Germany: IGD) present as ordinary civil-society bodies. The 2025 French entrisme framing, on this view, criminalizes ordinary religious-community work.

The 2025 French report and Vidino's earlier work both distinguish doctrinal Brotherhood-aligned organizations (whose internal training, leadership selection, and publication record show Brotherhood commitment) from ordinary Muslim civil-society organizations. The argument is not that European Muslim federations are illegitimate; it is that some whose public face is "ordinary civil society" are, on inspection of their cadre training and doctrinal core, Brotherhood-tradition organizations whose long-term aim is institutional Islamization of the host society. The objection requires case-by-case inspection; the contested list cannot be derived in advance.

The Diyanet "neutral religious bureaucracy" claim

DITIB and Diyanet-affiliated organizations argue Diyanet is a neutral religious-services bureaucracy, comparable to the Kirchensteuer-collecting Christian denominations or the Jewish Zentralrat. Turkish-state employment is incidental; imams operate on standard pastoral lines; the spy scandal was a one-off.

Scholarship on post-2002 Diyanet (Brown's Pious Periphery; Turkish-language studies since 2017) documents a substantial shift from the secular-Kemalist model toward a Sunni-Hanafi-Turkish-nationalist project. Friday sermons distributed weekly worldwide are drafted in Ankara on topics the Turkish presidency selects. The "neutral bureaucracy" claim rests on a 1924-2002 institutional history the post-2002 AKP transformation has largely overwritten. The Islamkolleg Deutschland (2020) and the 2024 phased-recall agreement exist precisely because the neutral-bureaucracy claim is not operationally true.

The exit-cost asymmetry on civic loyalty

The position imposes no formal religious sanction on the European Muslim who chooses civic-national identification: no excommunication, no apostasy declaration. The fatwa-issuing infrastructure does not, in mainstream practice, target Muslims who vote, serve in European militaries, or swear oaths to European constitutions.

The formal-sanction absence is real and is the position's strongest point. The informal-sanction record is more mixed. HT treats voting in non-sharia states as religiously prohibited; harder Salafi networks treat civic-loyalty oaths as shirk-adjacent. Within DITIB and Brotherhood-aligned communities, the social cost of explicit Euro-Islam advocacy is documented: Tibi's career under sustained attack from Brotherhood-aligned outlets is one example. No formal apostasy mechanism for civic loyalty; a substantial informal mechanism for marking such Muslims as compromised or insufficiently committed. The burden falls on the Muslim choosing the host-state primary identification, not on the one choosing ummah primacy. The asymmetry is real.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The selective-comparator problem on transnational loyalty

Transnational religious-political loyalty incompatible with civic primacy is a categorical concern; yet the position does not apply it symmetrically: dual-citizen Israeli-American politicians' Israel-loyalty claims, AIPAC's open foreign-state advocacy, the Catholic-Vatican diplomatic corps in earlier American history, European Jewish communities holding Israeli citizenship and military-reserve obligations.

The asymmetry is real and is the position's most uncomfortable strain point. The honest answer is not denial: transnational loyalties are common; the concern is loyalty-with-supersession, not transnational affinity as such. But consistency requires that state-actor surveillance, AIPAC-style lobbying, dual-citizen public officials' foreign-loyalty rhetoric, Hindu-nationalist diaspora coordination with India's Diaspora Affairs ministry, and Chinese United Front Work Department operations in Western Chinese-diaspora communities all be judged by the same standard. The position's credibility on DITIB / Brotherhood / HT requires that standard apply across the board. The policy literatures on the Israeli, Indian, and Chinese state-actor diaspora cases are visibly thinner. Treating the Muslim cases as the unique pathology is the move. The honest version applies the same scrutiny everywhere.

The proscription-recency objection

UK proscription of HT under the Terrorism Act 2000 came in January 2024, decades after its positions were public. Germany's 2003, France's 2003, Austria's responses are all similarly late. If HT was always a threat, why did the West take until 2003-2024? The proscription looks like a contingent political move, not a principled universal rule.

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest answer: proscription thresholds are high because freedom-of-association and freedom-of-belief norms are foundational; proscription is reserved for organizations whose operational threat clears a substantial evidentiary bar. HT was not proscribed earlier because the evidentiary case for operational threat (not offensive belief) had not been built to statutory standard. Nawaz, Husain, Maher, and others made the public case for over a decade; the proscription waited for the legal package, including the post-Hamas-7-Oct UK environment. The position must acknowledge that liberal-democratic proscription standards are deliberately slow, and defend the slowness on its merits. A faster, lower-bar proscription regime catches HT earlier and also catches categories of legitimate civil-society advocacy; the threshold is set where it is for reasons internal to the rule-of-law settlement.

This cross-examination is the methodological commitment of the whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot survive its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature. It has been refuted by honest engagement. That is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK / Germany

Hizb ut-Tahrir's UK and German trajectories

Germany banned HT in 2003 on grounds of antisemitic incitement and rejection of the constitutional order. The UK proscribed HT in January 2024 under the Terrorism Act 2000, a delayed move Maajid Nawaz and others had advocated for over a decade. France banned HT in 2003. The Netherlands monitors it via AIVD without banning.

The live policy question is whether less-explicit transnational-loyalty currents (some Brotherhood-aligned organizations, some Diyanet-coordinated networks) constitute a comparable category. The 2025 French Brotherhood report argued yes; its critics argued no.

Source: UK HMG proscription announcement
US

First Amendment, the Material Support statute, and the proscription gap

The US has not designated HT as a Foreign Terrorist Organization. Brotherhood-aligned group designations have been debated under Trump 1, Biden, and Trump 2 without proceeding. The First Amendment makes ideology-based proscription difficult; conduct-based proscription (Material Support, 18 U.S.C. §2339B) requires demonstrated material assistance to a designated FTO.

Source: State Department FTO list
Japan

No salient transnational-Muslim political organization

Japan has no significant HT or Brotherhood-aligned organizational presence and has not faced the proscription debate domestically. The Japanese Muslim 共同体 is overwhelmingly first-generation, organized around national-origin associations (Indonesian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Turkish) rather than transnational political movements.

Japan's Public Security Intelligence Agency monitors international terrorism but has issued no proscriptions or significant domestic-action reports on the Islamist transnational-political category.
