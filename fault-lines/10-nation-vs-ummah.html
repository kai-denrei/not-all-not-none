Fault Line · 10

National identity vs. ummah / 国家アイデンティティとウンマ: civic citizenship vs. transnational religious community

Whether the primary political identity is national-civic — French citizen, German citizen, American citizen — or transnational-religious, with the umma's claims taking precedence over the host state's.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic states require an operative primacy of civic-national identity in the public sphere — the citizen oath, the loyalty assumption underlying public office and military service — while political-Islamist movements (most explicitly Hizb ut-Tahrir) hold that the umma is the believer's primary political community and the nation-state a temporary scaffolding to be transcended through caliphate restoration. Most European Muslims hold both identities; the Islamist current insists the holding cannot be stable.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

The umma is the primary political community; the nation-state is a temporary scaffold

The honest articulation begins with the package the position actually defends. The believer's primary political community is the umma — the global community of all Muslims — not the post-Westphalian nation-state, which is read as an artefact of European history (the 1648 Westphalian system, codified retroactively as universal) and a colonial imposition on the dar al-Islam through the Sykes-Picot partition (1916), the abolition of the Ottoman caliphate (3 March 1924 by Mustafa Kemal Atatürk), and the European-administered mandate boundaries that became the modern Arab states. The Hizb ut-Tahrir version, founded by Taqi al-Din al-Nabhani in Jerusalem in 1953, names the project openly: re-establish the caliphate; replace the existing Muslim-majority states with an Islamic khilafa on the historical-classical model; in the interim, refuse the legitimacy of voting in non-sharia states, serving in their militaries, swearing oaths to their constitutions, or affirming their flag rituals. The Muslim Brotherhood version (Hassan al-Banna 1928, Sayyid Qutb's Milestones 1964) is gradualist: institutional capture of civil society — mosques, charities, professional associations, student unions, family-of-origin diaspora councils — building toward an eventual Islamic state.

The Diyanet/DITIB version is the state-actor variant: the Turkish Republic's Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet İşleri Başkanlığı, founded 1924) operates a network of mosques abroad — DITIB in Germany (~900 mosques), CMTGB in the Netherlands, ATIB in Austria — staffing them with imams trained, paid, and recallable by Ankara, transmitting an Erdogan-era Turkish-nationalist version of Hanafi-Sunni Islam to the Turkish-origin diaspora. The transnational-ummah claim and the transnational-state claim overlap: both ask the German, Dutch, Austrian, and French states to accept that a foreign state's religious bureaucracy is the primary institutional address for "their" Muslim populations. What is uniformly held — across HT, MB, Diyanet, and the major Salafi networks — is that the relationship between the Muslim believer and the European nation-state is conditional, contingent, and revisable in light of religious obligation. The harder versions treat civic loyalty itself as shirk-adjacent (associating the nation with God's exclusive claim); the softer versions treat it as a temporary practical arrangement subordinate to the umma.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Operative civic loyalty is the price of the citizenship bargain

Adult-to-adult voluntary association in private religious community is unconditionally protected — the umma as a community of faith is the same kind of trans-state moral community as global Catholicism, world Jewry, or the Anglican Communion, and Western liberal-democratic states have lived with these for centuries. The friction is not the umma as a felt religious bond; it is the operative supersession of civic loyalty by religious-political organizations that explicitly target European citizenship as a stage to be transcended. The classical liberal answer, going back to Locke's Letter Concerning Toleration (1689) and refined by Rawls in Political Liberalism (1993), is that the citizenship bargain is operative-not-comprehensive: the state does not demand that you love it more than God; it demands that your conduct in the public sphere — voting, military service, jury duty, public-office oath — operates on civic terms. Catholics in 1850s America made the same accommodation under similar suspicion; American Jews under McCarthy; British dissenters in the 1820s. The accommodation has been made before; it can be made again.

The strongest version concedes that Pew, Bertelsmann, and Ipsos repeatedly find European Muslim populations exhibit substantially higher national-identification rates than the HT-Brotherhood position would predict — most European Muslims hold both identities, often nest them, and find the transnational-ummah-supersession claim intellectually foreign. What the position refuses is the move that asks the host society to fund, accommodate, host, and politically protect movements and state-actor networks (HT proscribed in UK 2024 / Germany 2003 / Austria / France; Diyanet/DITIB under BfV surveillance since 2017; the May 2025 French Interior Ministry Brotherhood report) that openly articulate the supersession of civic loyalty as their operating doctrine. The disagreement is not about belief, it is about whether public institutions must subsidize their own articulated replacement. The Hallaq objection (Wael Hallaq, The Impossible State, Columbia 2013) concedes from the Islamist side that the modern state is incompatible with classical sharia — but draws the opposite conclusion: live as a religious-civil-society community within the state, not as a political project against it. Bassam Tibi's Euro-Islam proposal makes the same move from a German liberal-Muslim standpoint.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Case study: DITIB, the Diyanet imams, and the German “long arm of Ankara”

The clearest documented contemporary case of a state-actor transnational-religious network operating inside a European democracy. DITIB (Türkisch-Islamische Union der Anstalt für Religion) is the largest German Muslim umbrella organization (~900 mosques, ~800,000 affiliated members), formally a German registered association but operating under the structural and personnel control of Turkey's Diyanet — and through Diyanet, of the Turkish presidency. The 2016–2018 spy scandal, the BfV (German domestic intelligence) classification, and the post-Erdogan-purge imam recall make this the case where the dual-loyalty question stops being abstract.

DITIB / Diyanet in Germany, 1984–2025

DITIB founded 1984 as Turkish-state-aligned umbrella · ~900 mosques in Germany · Diyanet personnel control documented across BfV reports 2016–2024 · imam-spying scandal 2016–2017 · German federal funding cuts 2017 · BfV begins formal observation of DITIB-aligned associations 2019–2020 · Imam-Ausbildung Germany-trained-imam programme launched 2020 · imam-recall 2024 toward German-trained replacement

DITIB is the largest Turkish-Islamic organization in Germany and the largest single Muslim federation in any Western European country. Founded in Cologne in 1984 as the German registered-association (e.V.) arm of Turkey's Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet), it grew through the 1990s and 2000s into the operative religious-services provider for most Turkish-origin Sunni Muslims in Germany — Friday sermons, Qur'an classes, marriage and burial services, halal-meat certification, women's and youth associations. The organization's German-legal form is a non-state religious community; its operational structure is a department of the Turkish state. Imams in DITIB mosques are, until the 2024 reform began, all Turkish state employees: trained at Turkish theology faculties, paid by Diyanet salaries, posted to Germany on rotating four-to-five-year tours, recallable by Ankara, and required to recite Friday sermons (khutba) drafted at Diyanet headquarters and distributed weekly to all DITIB mosques worldwide. After the July 2016 attempted coup against President Erdogan, DITIB imams in Germany were instructed by Diyanet to denounce the Gülen movement (Hizmet) from their pulpits, to file reports on suspected Gülen sympathizers in their German congregations, and to transmit those reports back to Turkish authorities — a request that, when revealed in early 2017, triggered raids on DITIB premises in North Rhine-Westphalia, criminal investigation by the German Federal Prosecutor (which in late 2017 was discontinued for insufficient evidence of intelligence-service activity), federal-funding cuts, and the formal initiation of BfV observation of certain DITIB-aligned and Millî Görüş-aligned organizations.

  • 3 Mar 1924 Mustafa Kemal Atatürk abolishes the Ottoman caliphate; the Turkish Diyanet İşleri Başkanlığı (Directorate of Religious Affairs) is established as the secular Turkish state's instrument for managing Sunni Hanafi religion. The state thereafter controls mosque appointments, the content of Friday sermons, and the training of imams — a model retained and substantially expanded under Erdogan from 2002 onward.
  • 1984 DITIB (Türkisch-Islamische Union der Anstalt für Religion) is founded as a German eingetragener Verein in Cologne by Turkish-state actors, formally to coordinate religious services for the Turkish-origin diaspora that arrived under the 1961–1973 Gastarbeiter agreements. From inception, the organization is staffed and funded through Diyanet.
  • 2002 onward Under the AKP government, Diyanet's foreign budget grows substantially; imam-export programs to Germany, the Netherlands, Austria, France, and Belgium expand. Erdogan-era Friday sermons (drafted in Ankara, distributed weekly to all Diyanet mosques worldwide) increasingly thematize Turkish national-religious identity, the Ottoman legacy, and a suspicion-toward-the-secular-West frame.
  • 15 Jul 2016 The attempted coup against President Erdogan. Within weeks, the Turkish state launches a sweeping purge of Gülen-movement-aligned officials, including 2,700+ Diyanet personnel domestically and instructions to Diyanet missions abroad to identify and report Gülen sympathizers in diaspora congregations.
  • Dec 2016 – Feb 2017 German press reporting (Welt am Sonntag, Die Zeit, ARD) reveals that DITIB imams in Germany, on Diyanet instruction, have been compiling lists of suspected Gülen sympathizers in their German congregations and transmitting them back to Turkey. The German Federal Prosecutor (Generalbundesanwalt) opens a formal investigation for activity-on-behalf-of-a-foreign-intelligence-service; raids occur on imam residences in North Rhine-Westphalia in February 2017.
  • Jul 2017 The Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution (Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz, BfV) issues a public assessment that DITIB's organizational structure makes it an instrument of Turkish state policy in Germany. German federal funding for DITIB-run integration projects is partially suspended. Multiple Land governments (NRW, Bavaria) review their cooperation arrangements.
  • Dec 2017 The Generalbundesanwalt discontinues the criminal investigation against the DITIB imams for insufficient evidence of formal intelligence-service activity within the meaning of German criminal law — a finding that confirmed the practice happened but did not meet the high statutory threshold for prosecution. Civil-society and political reactions remain sharply critical.
  • 2018–2019 Dutch and Austrian intelligence services issue parallel assessments on Diyanet-controlled mosque networks; Austria's 2018 closure of seven mosques and expulsion of 40+ Turkish-paid imams under the 2015 Islam Act marks the strongest European response.
  • Jan 2020 The German federal government and the State of Lower Saxony launch the Islamkolleg Deutschland in Osnabrück — a cooperative German-trained-imam programme intended to reduce dependency on Diyanet-imported personnel. Funding ramps over 2020–2025; first cohorts graduate.
  • 2024 A Turkish-German bilateral agreement begins phased reduction of Diyanet-paid imams in Germany over five years, replacing them with Germany-resident, Germany-trained personnel. The implementation pace and the extent to which Diyanet retains substantive curricular and sermon control are open questions as of 2025.
  • May 2025 The French Interior Ministry publishes Frères musulmans et islamisme politique en France, the most detailed government-level European treatment of the parallel Brotherhood-aligned (rather than state-actor Diyanet-aligned) institutional-capture strategy. The report's framing — "entrisme" through civil society, education, and federations — is methodologically separable from but politically connected to the DITIB / Millî Görüş question.

What DITIB and the proscription cluster show about this fault line. The fault line has two operative shapes that are often conflated. Hizb ut-Tahrir, the Muslim Brotherhood network, and Salafi-jihadist organizations are the movement-actor form: civil-society organizations that articulate the supersession of civic loyalty in their own published doctrine, and that European democracies have responded to with proscription (UK Jan 2024, Germany 2003, France 2003, Austria) or surveillance (Netherlands AIVD, French Interior Ministry 2025 report). Diyanet/DITIB and the Turkish-state network are the state-actor form: a foreign government's religious bureaucracy operating mosque networks and recallable imams inside European democracies, with documented post-2016 instructions to surveil and report on diaspora congregants. The two shapes call for different responses — proscription works against organizations, not against foreign-state agencies — but they share the underlying friction: European citizenship presupposes that the primary political-loyalty address of a citizen is the domestic civic order, not a foreign state or a transnational religio-political project. The Bertelsmann and Policy Exchange numbers show that most European Muslims have already made this accommodation; the proscription, surveillance, and Imam-Ausbildung debates are about the institutional-actor minority, not the population. Conflating the two — treating ordinary European Muslim religious practice as proto-Islamist — is the failure mode the project's blue-band framing names. Treating the documented institutional-actor cases as paranoid invention is the failure mode the project's black-band framing names. Both fail; the empirical record is what's left.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The motte-and-bailey on dual loyalty

The version offered to liberal European audiences is the motte: every religious community has a transnational moral-imaginative dimension; Catholics look to Rome, Jews to Jerusalem, Muslims to Mecca and the umma; this is unobjectionable and protected by religious-liberty norms.

The version Hizb ut-Tahrir publishes in The System of Islam and The Methodology of Hizb ut-Tahrir for Change is the bailey: the explicit doctrine that the existing nation-state system is illegitimate, that voting and military service in non-sharia states are religiously prohibited, and that the operative project is caliphate restoration through three-stage takeover (cultural preparation → intellectual struggle → political seizure). Lorenzo Vidino's The Closed Circle (Columbia 2020) documents the parallel Brotherhood version: gradualist institutional capture rather than overt secession. The motte and the bailey are not the same position; the motte is what is shown to journalists, the bailey is what is published in movement texts and operationalized in cell organization. When pressed, the bailey is dismissed as old material no longer operative; the texts have not been retracted.

The "long-term aspiration, not current operation" deferral

The position concedes that caliphate-restoration is a long-term project, not a current operational program for most European Muslim populations. The interim demand is therefore modest: do not require Muslims to swear oaths in tension with their religious identity; respect transnational religious bonds; let mosques be mosques.

The DITIB record (BfV 2017, North-Rhine-Westphalia 2017 raids, Generalbundesanwalt 2017 investigation) shows that "long-term aspiration" can coexist with current operational activity that does meet the dual-loyalty concern's threshold — Diyanet-instructed imams compiling lists of Gülen sympathizers in German congregations and transmitting them to Turkish authorities is not aspirational, it is current intelligence-adjacent activity by a foreign state through the religious-services infrastructure of a German diaspora population. The "aspiration" framing absorbs current operational activity into a future-tense category that excuses present scrutiny. Austria's 2018 closure of seven mosques and expulsion of 40+ Turkish-paid imams under the 2015 Islam Act is the strongest European response and was made on present-tense grounds, not aspirational ones.

The "ordinary parental engagement" laundering

Brotherhood-aligned organizations and federations (in France: Musulmans de France, formerly UOIF; in the UK: Muslim Association of Britain; in Germany: IGD) present themselves as ordinary civil-society organizations representing Muslim populations on integration, education, and inter-faith dialogue. The May 2025 French Interior Ministry report's framing of entrisme, on this view, criminalizes ordinary religious-civil-society work.

The 2025 French report, like Lorenzo Vidino's earlier work and Brookings analyst Shadi Hamid's documentation (in a different framing), distinguishes doctrinal Brotherhood-aligned organizations (those whose internal training, leadership selection, and publication record demonstrate Brotherhood commitment) from ordinary Muslim civil-society organizations that are sometimes confused with them. The argument is not that European Muslim federations are illegitimate; the argument is that some federations whose self-presentation is "ordinary civil society" are, on inspection of their cadre training and doctrinal core, Brotherhood-tradition organizations whose long-term aim is the institutional Islamization of the host society. The "laundering" objection requires the inspection to be done case by case; the contested-federation list cannot be derived a priori.

The Diyanet "neutral religious bureaucracy" claim

DITIB and parallel Diyanet-affiliated organizations argue that Diyanet is a neutral religious-services bureaucracy, comparable to the German Kirchensteuer-collecting Christian denominations or the Jewish Zentralrat — that its Turkish-state employer is incidental, that its imams operate on standard pastoral lines, and that the spy-scandal accusations were a one-off mishandling.

Diyanet under Erdogan is documented (Turkey-comparative-religion scholarship: Brown's Pious Periphery; Arabic-language Turkish scholarship since 2017) as having shifted substantially from the secular-Kemalist model toward a Sunni-Hanafi-Turkish-nationalist project. The Friday sermons distributed weekly worldwide are drafted in Ankara on themes the Turkish presidency selects. The "neutral bureaucracy" claim relies on a 1924–2002 institutional history that the post-2002 AKP transformation has substantially overwritten. The German Imam-Ausbildung (Islamkolleg Deutschland 2020) and the 2024 phased-recall agreement are the German federal-state institutional response to this finding; they would not have been negotiated if the neutral-bureaucracy claim were operationally true.

The exit-cost asymmetry on civic loyalty

The position imposes no formal religious sanction on the European Muslim who chooses civic-national identification — does not excommunicate, does not declare apostasy. The fatwa-issuing infrastructure that targets perceived doctrinal apostasy does not, in mainstream contemporary practice, target Muslims who vote in European elections, serve in European militaries, or swear oaths to European constitutions.

The formal-sanction absence is real and is the position's strongest defense. The informal-community-sanction record is more mixed. The Hizb ut-Tahrir tradition treats voting in non-sharia states as religiously prohibited; the harder Salafi networks treat civic-loyalty oaths as shirk-adjacent. Within DITIB and Brotherhood-aligned communities, the social cost of explicit Bassam-Tibi-style Euro-Islam advocacy is documented (Tibi's own career under sustained polemical attack from Brotherhood-aligned outlets is one example). The position has no formal apostasy mechanism for civic loyalty; it has a substantial informal mechanism for marking such Muslims as compromised, secularized, or insufficiently committed. The exit cost is not symmetric across host-state and religious-community loyalty; the burden falls on the Muslim choosing the host-state primary identification, not on the one choosing the umma primary identification.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The selective-comparator problem on transnational loyalty

The liberal-democratic position holds that transnational religious-political loyalty incompatible with civic primary loyalty is a categorical concern. It does not, however, apply this concern symmetrically to comparable cases: dual-citizen Israeli-American politicians' Israel-loyalty oaths to ASIO, AIPAC's open advocacy for foreign-state interest, the Catholic-Vatican diplomatic corps in earlier American history, and substantial fractions of the European Jewish community holding Israeli citizenship and military-reserve obligations.

The asymmetry is real and is the position's most uncomfortable strain point. The honest liberal answer is not denial — yes, transnational loyalties are common, the concern is dual-loyalty-with-supersession, not transnational-affinity per se — but the consistency requires that state-actor surveillance, AIPAC-style foreign-policy lobbying, dual-citizen public officials' Israel-loyalty rhetoric, Hindu-nationalist diaspora organizations operating in coordination with the Modi government's Diaspora Affairs ministry, and Chinese-state United Front Work Department operations in Western Chinese-diaspora communities all be evaluated by the same standard. The liberal position's credibility on the DITIB / Brotherhood / HT case requires that the standard be applied across the board — and the historical and contemporary record on the comparable other transnational cases is uneven. Treating the Muslim cases as the unique pathology is the move; the honest answer requires applying the same scrutiny to the Israeli, Indian, and Chinese state-actor diaspora cases, and the policy literatures on the latter three are visibly thinner.

The proscription-recency objection

UK proscription of Hizb ut-Tahrir under the Terrorism Act 2000 occurred only in January 2024, despite the organization's positions being publicly available for decades. Germany's 2003 proscription, France's 2003, and Austria's institutional response are similarly recent. The conservative position can press: if HT was always a national-security threat, why did the West only conclude this in 2003–2024? Doesn't this suggest the proscription is a contingent political move rather than a principled application of universal rules?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest liberal answer is: proscription thresholds are high precisely because the freedom-of-association and freedom-of-belief norms are foundational, and proscription is reserved for organizations whose operational threat clears a substantial evidentiary bar. Hizb ut-Tahrir was not proscribed earlier because the evidentiary case for operational threat (rather than offensive belief) had not been compiled to legal-statutory standard. Maajid Nawaz, Ed Husain, Shiraz Maher, and others made the case publicly for over a decade; the proscription waited for the legal package — including the post-Hamas-7-Oct UK environment — to be filed. The position requires acknowledging that liberal-democratic proscription standards are deliberately slow, and defending the slowness on its merits, not pretending the eventual proscription was inevitable from the founding moment. A faster, lower-evidentiary-bar proscription regime would catch HT earlier and would also catch substantial categories of legitimate civil-society advocacy; the threshold is set where it is for reasons internal to the rule-of-law settlement.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK / Germany

Hizb ut-Tahrir's UK and German trajectories

Hizb ut-Tahrir was formally banned in Germany (2003) on grounds of antisemitic incitement and rejection of the constitutional order. The UK proscribed HT in January 2024 under the Terrorism Act 2000 — a delayed move long advocated by Maajid Nawaz and others, opposed by some civil-liberties groups. France banned HT in 2003. The Netherlands does not ban it but monitors it via AIVD.

The broader question — whether less-explicit transnational-loyalty currents (some Brotherhood-aligned organizations, some Diyanet-coordinated networks under Turkish state direction) constitute a comparable category — is the live policy debate. The 2025 French Muslim Brotherhood report argued yes; its critics argued no.

Source: UK HMG proscription announcement
US

First Amendment, the Material Support statute, and the proscription gap

The US has not designated Hizb ut-Tahrir as a Foreign Terrorist Organization. Designations of Brotherhood-aligned groups have been discussed under multiple administrations (Trump 1, Biden, Trump 2) without proceeding. The First Amendment framework makes ideology-based proscription difficult; conduct-based proscription (Material Support, 18 U.S.C. §2339B) requires demonstrated material assistance to a designated FTO.

Source: State Department FTO list
Japan

No salient transnational-Muslim political organization

Japan has no significant Hizb ut-Tahrir or Brotherhood-aligned organizational presence and has not faced the proscription debate domestically. The Japanese Muslim community is overwhelmingly first-generation immigrant, organized around national-origin associations (Indonesian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Turkish) rather than around transnational political-Islamist movements.

Japan's Public Security Intelligence Agency monitors international terrorism but has not issued proscriptions or significant domestic-action reports on the Islamist transnational political category specifically.

Source: Public Security Intelligence Agency

Austria · 2018 Islam Act enforcement
Republic of Austria, 2015 Islam Act (Islamgesetz); 2018 federal-government action closing seven mosques and expelling 40+ Turkish-paid imams. Reuters / DW contemporary coverage.
Method: The 2015 Islam Act is the strongest European legislative instrument on foreign-state religious financing; it requires Austrian-resident religious-community organizations to obtain Austrian-source funding. Legal and political analysis from Religion Watch, Brookings, and German-language Austrian press.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Post-proscription HT in the UK. What has the operational impact of the January 2024 ban been — disrupted, displaced, or merely renamed? Phase 2 should track 18-month outcomes.
  • The Brotherhood-current proscription debate. Should European states proscribe Brotherhood-aligned organizations as the UK and Germany have proscribed HT? The 2025 French report sharpens the question without answering it.
  • Diyanet and Turkish-state-sponsored networks. The Turkish religious-affairs directorate operates abroad through DITIB (Germany), Millî Görüş, and similar organizations. The transnationalism question here has a state-actor rather than party-organization shape; Phase 2 should map it.
  • Survey data on European Muslim identification trends. The Policy Exchange 2016 data is the most-cited; what does the comparable IFOP, Bertelsmann, and Pew data through 2024 show, and is the trend toward national-civic identification or away from it?
  • The Hallaq objection again. If, per Hallaq, the modern state form is incompatible with sharia, the HT caliphate-restoration aim is itself incoherent within Hallaq's framework. Phase 2 should engage what intra-Islamic critics of HT do with the Hallaq position.
