Fault Line · 21

Same-sex conduct: death-penalty hudud, decriminalization, and the doctrinal spectrum

Where liberal-democratic legal orders treat consensual adult same-sex conduct as outside the legitimate reach of state power, and the conservative Sunni and Shia mainstream — running from active execution at one end to discreet toleration at the other — treats it as a categorical religious prohibition with state-enforceable consequences in the strictest readings.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic legal orders treat consensual same-sex conduct between adults as outside the legitimate jurisdiction of the state; classical and political-Islamist family-law traditions treat it as a hudud-class capital offence in the strictest readings, a major sin requiring punishment short of death in most contemporary jurisprudence, and an unequivocally non-permitted private behavior in essentially all mainstream readings — with the empirical record running from active state execution at one end to quiet toleration at the other, across a 1.9-billion-person population.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

Categorical prohibition, contested punishment

The honest articulation begins with the textual case the position actually makes. The Quranic narrative of the people of Lot (Surahs 7, 11, 15, 26, 27, 29) is read in mainstream classical exegesis as God's destruction of a community for same-sex sexual conduct. Hadith literature — most explicitly Sunan Abu Dawud 4462 ("If you find anyone doing as Lot's people did, kill the one who does it, and the one to whom it is done") — is read as establishing the death penalty as the consensus punishment among the four major Sunni schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) and the Twelver Shia, with disagreement only on method (stoning, throwing from a height, execution by sword, fire-burning per some Hanbalis). The position holds that this is not a marginal interpretation but the classical mainstream — that the four schools and the Imamiyya converged on the substance, and that the textual chain runs from the Quranic narrative to settled hudud jurisprudence without a credible counter-interpretation in the classical period.

The strongest contemporary version concedes that not all conservative jurists endorse capital punishment under current conditions. Some argue the hudud are suspended absent a properly Islamic state. Some emphasize the high evidentiary bar (four reliable male Muslim witnesses to the act of penetration) as effectively making prosecution impossible. Some draw the act/orientation distinction (the disposition is a trial sent by God, the act is the sin). Some emphasize sitr (the religious duty of concealment) as overriding public exposure. What is uniformly held in mainstream Sunni and Shia jurisprudence — including by every major contemporary authority from al-Azhar to Yaqeen Institute to the Iranian marja'iyya — is that consensual same-sex conduct is a categorical prohibition (haram), that no acceptance or affirmation is theologically permissible, and that "moderation" means privacy and pastoral counseling toward repentance, not normalization, equal civil status, or marriage equivalence. The position presents this as theological honesty against what it sees as a Western-cultural pressure campaign to require Muslim institutions to bless what God has forbidden.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Adult consent, private conduct, equal protection

Adult-to-adult consensual sexual conduct in private is outside the legitimate jurisdiction of the state. The argument has a procedural form (Mill's harm principle: state power constrains private acts only to prevent harm to others) and a substantive form (sexuality is a constitutive aspect of personhood; equal protection requires the state extend the same legal recognition to same-sex couples it extends to opposite-sex couples). The framework is recent in Western law — sodomy laws were on US books until Lawrence v. Texas (2003); European jurisprudence consolidated through ECtHR Dudgeon v. UK (1981), Norris v. Ireland (1988), Modinos v. Cyprus (1993). Marriage equality arrived later still: Netherlands 2001, Spain 2005, US Obergefell 2015. The position therefore concedes that the West got here recently, incompletely, and after sustained internal struggle.

What the position does not concede is the relativist counter-claim. The doctrinal view that capital punishment for consensual private conduct between adults is a legitimate exercise of state authority is incompatible with constitutional protections for life, equal treatment, and privacy as those concepts are understood in liberal-democratic legal orders. Decriminalization is not "Western imperialism"; it is the application to sexual conduct of the same private-sphere principles that protect religious belief, freedom of conscience, and family privacy from state interference — principles many Muslim-majority states themselves invoke when defending mosque autonomy, religious dress, and parental authority against Western secular pressure. The strongest version is willing to defend the substantive claim that some doctrinal positions — including the death-penalty hudud for sodomy — are simply wrong, not "different," and that a liberal democracy is entitled to refuse them legal effect within its borders without owing the position equal philosophical standing. The argument is made most credibly by LGBT Muslims themselves; it does not require Western mediation.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "moderation" bait-and-switch

The contemporary apologetic version offered to Western audiences emphasizes the act/orientation distinction, the high evidentiary bar, the duty of concealment (sitr), and the suspension-of-hudud arguments — implying these are the position's mainstream operative content.

In every jurisdiction where the conservative position has actually held state power (Iran 1979–present, Saudi Arabia, Mauritania, Sudan pre-2020, Yemen, Brunei post-2014, ISIS-controlled territory 2014–2017, Taliban Afghanistan 1996–2001 and 2021–present), the death penalty is on the books and has been applied. The "moderate" reading is a presentation strategy for diaspora and export, not a description of how the system behaves where it governs.

The unfalsifiable-consensus claim

The position rests heavily on ijma' — that the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia agreed on the death-penalty substance and disagreed only on method, and that this convergence settles the textual question.

Contemporary scholarship — Khaled El-Rouayheb's Before Homosexuality in the Arab-Islamic World, 1500–1800 (Chicago, 2005); Scott Kugle's Homosexuality in Islam (Oneworld, 2010) and Living Out Islam (NYU, 2014); Samar Habib's edited Islam and Homosexuality (Praeger, 2010) — documents that the relevant hadith are weakly transmitted by classical isnad standards, that the Quranic Lot narrative concerns rape, inhospitality, and conduct toward guests rather than consensual private adult conduct, and that the four-schools "consensus" reflects political consolidation in the early Abbasid period rather than textual necessity. The classical period contains a substantial tradition of male-male erotic poetry, sex-positive Sufi imagery, and biographical literature describing same-sex relationships among caliphs and scholars that coexisted with relatively muted enforcement. The position is one defensible reading among several available within the Islamic intellectual tradition. Treating it as the only legitimate reading is an interpretive choice that has to be defended on the merits, not asserted as a textual fact.

The four-witnesses dodge

The high evidentiary bar (four pious adult male Muslim witnesses to the act of penetration) is offered as making prosecution functionally impossible — a built-in moderation that renders the death penalty effectively dormant.

The bar's existence does not constrain the regimes that actually apply the law. ILGA World's documentation shows convictions in Iran obtained on confessions extracted under torture, on circumstantial evidence, on a single hisbah denunciation, on the judge's "knowledge" ('ilm al-qadi), and on retracted testimony. ISIS courts dispensed with the standard altogether and convicted on accusation alone. The "high bar" argument requires a functioning rule-of-law context that the regimes implementing the death penalty do not provide. It insulates the doctrine from criticism without constraining the practice.

The privacy-not-punishment retreat

The version offered to liberal audiences argues that mainstream jurisprudence requires sitr (concealment) and counsels privacy — implying the law is not aggressively pursued and that public knowledge is what triggers prosecution, not the act itself.

This concedes the structure of the criminalization regime while denying its existence. If the conduct is licit when private and criminal when known, the criminalization rests on the public/known character of the conduct — which is exactly what hostile neighbors, family members, blackmailers, security services, and morality patrols depend on for prosecution. The privacy argument does not reduce the regime; it rationalizes it. And it places the entire burden of survival on the targeted person's ability to remain undetected by state and community in perpetuity.

The metaphysical-premise dodge

The argument retreats, when pressed, to the position that the doctrine is coherent on its own metaphysical premises about sexuality, divine ordering, and the moral status of same-sex conduct — and that liberal critique applies premises the position does not share.

This is true and trivial. Every system is internally coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether the premises are defensible — whether the theological case for treating same-sex conduct as ontologically harmful (rather than merely traditionally disapproved) survives examination. The position cannot make that case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The position offers no provision for a Muslim person, born into the doctrinal regime, who concludes after honest reflection that the conclusion is wrong. The available moves are lifelong celibacy, sham heterosexual marriage, leaving Islam (with the apostasy consequences attached), or remaining publicly closeted indefinitely.

A doctrinal system that imposes lifelong existential costs on a category of its own adherents, and provides no internal mechanism for those adherents to opt out without religious or legal sanction, is not "diversity within Islam"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one. The repeated answer from mainstream conservative pulpits — "they should accept the test God has sent them" — is a position one can hold; it is not an argument that has met its interlocutor on equal ground.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency objection

Liberal Western law decriminalized same-sex conduct only between 1981 and 2003, and recognized marriage equality only after 2001. The conservative position can press: if the position is universal-and-self-evident, why did the West only discover it recently? Doesn't this suggest the framework is contingent rather than universal?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The liberal answer is: we were wrong before — the same way the West was wrong about slavery, religious tolerance, women's suffrage, racial segregation. The right answer to "why didn't you have this view in 1950?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held," not "we held a different view that was equally valid." The position requires acknowledging that liberal-democratic norms continue to evolve and are not always self-consistent — and defending the current settlement on its merits, not on its provenance.

The "wrong, not different" claim

The strongest liberal version holds that the death-penalty hudud for sodomy is wrong, not merely "another tradition." This is a substantive cross-civilizational moral claim with imperial echoes — Western law telling Islamic jurisprudence its core penal provisions are barbaric.

The claim is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can — Imaan in the UK, Muslims for Progressive Values, Scott Kugle's Living Out Islam, the substantial body of LGBT Muslim memoir literature, the late Imam Muhsin Hendricks (assassinated February 2025). The claim has to be defended through these voices, not over them. It is dishonest to pretend the universalist framing has no civilizational asymmetry baked in; the honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry and defend the substantive claim on the merits — and to refuse the relativist deflection that would silence Muslim LGBT voices in the name of respecting Muslim-majority cultures.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK

Mosque sermons, school protests, and the Birmingham flashpoint

The 2019 Birmingham primary-school protests — Muslim parents picketing Anderton Park and Parkfield Community School over LGBT-inclusive relationships education ("No Outsiders" curriculum) — produced sustained demonstrations, court orders, and a national debate about whether parental religious objection should override age-appropriate inclusion teaching. The protests included parental withdrawal of children, allegations of intimidation directed at teachers, and the broader question of whether opt-out provisions for relationships education tip into a separate-but-equal regime.

Sources: BBC — Birmingham LGBT protests · Guardian coverage
US · multiple states

Religious-exemption claims and the limits of accommodation

US litigation has tested whether religious objection — Catholic, evangelical Protestant, and Muslim — can support exemptions from anti-discrimination law in employment, school curricula, and public accommodation. Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) extended Title VII to sexual orientation and gender identity; subsequent cases (Fulton v. Philadelphia 2021, 303 Creative 2023) have carved religious-conscience exceptions whose boundary remains contested. Muslim-American organizations have generally aligned with broader religious-liberty coalitions on the exemption questions, with internal LGBT-Muslim dissent.

Sources: Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)
Japan

No criminalization, no marriage equality, low Muslim-community salience

Japan does not criminalize same-sex conduct (and has not since the Meiji-era Penal Code of 1880, with intermediate variation). It also does not provide national same-sex marriage as of 2025, though municipal partnership certificates have spread to most major cities since Shibuya's 2015 ordinance, and a March 2024 Sapporo High Court ruling held the marriage exclusion unconstitutional under Article 14.

The Japanese Muslim community (~0.18% of population) has not produced significant friction cases on this fault line — the issue's domestic salience is dominated by mainstream secular and religious-conservative debates, not Islamic ones. As with most fault lines on this site, this is a finding about demographics and immigration regime rather than a gap in research.

Source: Japan Times — Sapporo ruling 2024

Case study: the death-penalty cluster and the moratorium question

The empirical anchor for this fault line is the set of jurisdictions where the conservative position has been implemented as state law and consensual adult same-sex conduct carries the death penalty under hudud or ta'zir provisions. The cluster includes both stable state regimes (Iran since 1979) and brief but lethal non-state actors (ISIS 2014–2017). The polls below show the popular-opinion substrate that makes the regime maintainable.

  • 1979–1983 Following the Iranian Revolution, the Islamic Penal Code formally codifies the death penalty for lavat (sodomy) under hudud provisions; Articles 108–113 (later restructured in the 2013 IPC at Articles 233–241) prescribe execution by hanging for the active partner if Muslim, and for the passive partner regardless. Methods historically included stoning and throwing from a height.
  • 19 July 2005 Mahmoud Asgari and Ayaz Marhoni are publicly hanged in Edalat (Justice) Square, Mashhad, Iran. Both were aged ~17 at execution (and therefore ~15–16 at the time of the alleged conduct), making the execution a juvenile-offender case under the Convention on the Rights of the Child. The case became iconic; the underlying facts are contested: Iranian authorities charged rape of a 13-year-old; international LGBT organizations argued the underlying conduct was consensual same-sex and the rape charge a pretext. Human Rights Watch's Scott Long noted the activist sources for the consensual reading "refused to speak to anyone else, including human rights investigators." The case is real evidence of Iranian state execution under sodomy provisions and a documented example of how complex the underlying facts can be.
  • 9 Dec 2014 – 7 May 2016 ISIS executes ~41 men in territory it controls in Iraq and Syria, charged with sodomy (lawat). Methods documented: throwing from rooftops, stoning, sword execution, fire-burning. ISIS published photographs and videos as religious-policy propaganda; OutRight Action International compiled the count from open sources, ISIS's own publications, and refugee testimony. Cases include Mosul, Raqqa, Homs, Maslamah City (Aleppo), and Wilayat al-Jazirah.
  • 2017 Documented "gay purges" in Chechnya — extrajudicial detention, torture, and killings of suspected gay men by Chechen authorities, reported initially by Novaya Gazeta. Subsequent UK, US, and EU sanctions targeting Chechen officials. The Russian federal government denied and obstructed investigation.
  • 3 April 2019 Brunei's Syariah Penal Code 2014 enters full force, including death by stoning for sodomy and adultery, amputation for theft. International boycott of Brunei-owned luxury hotels (Beverly Hills Hotel, The Dorchester) led by Clooney, Elton John, and others.
  • 5 May 2019 Sultan Hassanal Bolkiah extends Brunei's existing death-penalty moratorium to cover the new Syariah provisions. The law itself is not repealed; the moratorium is unilateral and revocable. Whippings for "lesbian sex" and amputation for theft remain in force.
  • 15 Aug 2021 Taliban return to power in Afghanistan. Taliban judges restate the death-penalty position publicly; reports of arrests, beatings, and forced disappearances of LGBT people accumulate over the following years (Human Rights Watch, US State Department reporting).
  • Jan 2024 ILGA World reports the public hanging of a 31-year-old gay man in Iran.
  • Aug 2024 ILGA World reports the execution by hanging of two men in Iran for "consensual same-sex intimacy."
  • 15 Feb 2025 Imam Muhsin Hendricks — the world's first openly gay imam, founder of The Inner Circle / al-Fitrah in Cape Town, founder of the Compassion-Centered Islam mosque-network — is shot dead in Gqeberha, South Africa, on his way to officiate an interfaith marriage. Two masked gunmen blocked his vehicle with a Toyota Hilux and opened fire. The South African Police Service and ILGA World investigated the killing as a likely hate crime.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The conservative-Islamist articulation, in its strongest contemporary form, presents itself as theologically rigorous on the categorical-prohibition claim and pragmatically restrained on the punishment claim. The empirical record across the cluster — Iran 1979 to present, ISIS 2014–2017, Brunei 2019, Taliban 2021–present — shows the death penalty is not a dormant doctrinal artifact but the operative law where the position holds power. The poll data (Pew 95%+ "should be rejected" across the Sunni Arab heartland, ICM 52% UK Muslims "should be illegal," Pew 52% US Muslim "should be accepted") shows the popular substrate is real, generational shift in diaspora populations is real, and the within-Islam variance forbids any single number. The fault line is not "Islam vs. the West" — it is the conservative-jurisprudential position vs. the liberal-democratic constitutional settlement, with the entire global Muslim population distributed across that distance, the violent-state extreme killing people now, and the LGBT-Muslim reformist current arguing inside the tradition that the consensus claim is overstated. Imam Muhsin Hendricks died in February 2025 making that argument.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Generational shift in diaspora populations. The Pew US 2007→2011→2017 movement (27% → 39% → 52% acceptance) is the strongest single piece of generational-shift evidence. Phase 2 should examine whether the European pattern — where ICM 2016 found 52% UK Muslims wanting illegality — is converging on the US pattern, holding flat, or moving in the opposite direction. The contrast with the gender-equality fault line (where European second-generation re-Islamization is documented per Olivier Roy) is theoretically interesting and empirically open.
  • The Hendricks killing as inflection point. What does the assassination of the world's first openly gay imam — by masked gunmen in a country where the murder is a hate crime under domestic law — tell us about the within-Muslim political-religious dynamics around the reformist position? Is it an isolated act, the visible end of a broader intimidation pattern documented by Imaan / al-Fitrah / ILGA, or both?
  • The Birmingham 2019 protests as European-friction template. The "No Outsiders" curriculum protests crystallized the conflict between religious-parental claims and inclusive-education policy. Phase 2 should examine whether this template has spread (Belgium, Netherlands, France) and what jurisprudential settlements emerge.
  • The four-schools "consensus" reread.
