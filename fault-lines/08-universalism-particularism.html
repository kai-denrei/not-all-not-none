Fault Line · 08

Universalism vs. particularism / 普遍主義と特殊主義: universal human rights vs. religious-civilizational frame

Whether the UDHR-and-successors human-rights framework is genuinely universal, or a culturally-particular Western settlement that the OIC's Cairo Declaration was right to refuse.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

The UN human-rights system since 1948 claims to articulate norms binding on all human persons regardless of culture or creed; the OIC's 1990 Cairo Declaration on Human Rights in Islam responded by enumerating an alternative scheme explicitly bounded by sharia, where every right is qualified by "in accordance with Islamic law." Both documents claim universality. They do not mean the same thing by the word.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

The umma is universal; the territorial nation-state is the parochial settlement

The honest articulation begins by naming the doctrine the position actually inherits. Classical fiqh divided the world into dar al-Islam (the abode of Islam, where Islamic law governs) and dar al-harb (the abode of war, where it does not), with intermediate categories — dar al-'ahd (treaty), dar al-sulh (truce) — recognized but contingent. The relationship between dar al-Islam and dar al-harb was, in the dominant classical reading, a state of latent jihad until the latter accepted Islam, accepted dhimma, or was subject to it; ibn Taymiyya's Mardin fatwa (c. 1303) is the most-cited classical instrument addressing the in-between case (a territory ruled by Muslims but governed by non-sharia law). The 1990 Cairo Declaration on Human Rights in Islam, drafted by the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation, restated the universalist claim of the Islamic frame in modern form, qualifying every UDHR-style right with "in accordance with Islamic law." The OIC's 1999–2010 UN "defamation of religions" campaign was the operational extension. ISIS's 2014 caliphate proclamation was the violent maximalist version. The peaceful institutional version — pursued by the Brotherhood-derived current in Europe and North America, articulated by Tariq Ramadan as the displacement of dar al-harb with dar al-shahada ("abode of witness") — accepts territorial coexistence but maintains the umma as the primary identification.

The strongest contemporary version concedes what its serious advocates also know. ISIS's caliphate was rejected by the Sunni mainstream (the 126-scholar Open Letter of September 2014 is the institutional record). The classical dar al-Islam / dar al-harb binary was contested in the medieval period itself (the Hanafi tradition's pragmatic recognition of treaty-status; al-Shaybani's Siyar; the Ottoman millet system as a working extension). The Cairo Declaration's sharia-qualification clauses produce serious internal problems on gender equality, religious freedom, and freedom from cruel punishment that even its OIC-state signatories must defend in their bilateral diplomacy. Bin Bayyah's 2016 Marrakesh Declaration, signed by ~250 Sunni scholars from 120 countries, formally rejected the classical dhimma framework in favor of equal-citizenship under the Charter of Medina precedent. What the position does not concede is the categorical claim: that there is a universal moral order grounded in divine sovereignty rather than autonomous human reason, that the territorial nation-state is a recent and parochial settlement that European modernity universalized through colonial coercion, and that Muslim-majority societies are entitled to ground their political order in Islamic categories without owing the UDHR system equal philosophical standing. Its serious institutional advocates — al-Azhar, the OIC permanent missions, the Iranian Guardian Council, the Saudi Senior Scholars' Council, Tariq Ramadan in his pre-2017 work, and Wael Hallaq's The Impossible State (Columbia 2013) in academic register — articulate this in those exact terms. The position is the steelman of the universalism debate, not the ISIS caricature of it.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Universalism is contestable but the comparator decides — equal citizenship, exit included

The liberal-democratic position concedes substantial parts of the parochialism critique. The UDHR drafting committee was Western-dominated (Cassin, Roosevelt, Malik, Chang, Humphrey are the canonical names; the Saudi delegate Jamil Baroody refused to sign citing exactly the parochialism objection). The document carries Enlightenment assumptions about autonomous individual rights-bearers that not all moral traditions share; the application has been selective and politically convenient; the post-1948 implementation record includes Western support for monarchies, dictatorships, and torture regimes that violated UDHR norms when geopolitically expedient. The Asad-Mahmood critique (Talal Asad's Formations of the Secular 2003, Saba Mahmood's Politics of Piety 2005, Hallaq's Impossible State 2013) is real and not entirely dismissible — secular-liberal premises are themselves a tradition with its own genealogy, not a view from nowhere.

The strongest version is unflinching about the operative comparator. The Cairo Declaration as implemented — death penalty for apostasy in Saudi Arabia and Iran, mandatory blasphemy-death in Pakistan, criminalization of public conversion in over 20 OIC states, sex discrimination in inheritance and testimony across the membership, no recognition for Ahmadis or Bahá'ís, no protection for ex-Muslims, and the Pakistani Council of Islamic Ideology's 2024 advisory opinions defending child marriage and the 2-out-of-3-witnesses rule — is the alternative being offered. The defensible claim is not that the UDHR's drafters were morally pure or its implementation consistent, but that revisable equal-citizenship is the only stable settlement compatible with the right to leave one's natal religion, marry across creeds, criticize the dominant orthodoxy, and live free of state-religion entanglement — and that the operative empirical record across the Cairo-vs.-UDHR comparator runs sharply in one direction on every metric the underlying populations themselves would identify. The argument is made most credibly by ex-Muslims, Iranian Bahá'ís, Pakistani Ahmadis, the Marrakesh signatories, Indonesia's Nahdlatul Ulama, and the Iranian human-rights lawyer Shirin Ebadi — articulating from inside what the universalism debate looks like to people for whom the cost of getting it wrong falls on their own bodies. The civilizational asymmetry critique is real and the argument has to be defended through these voices, not over them, and not as relativist deflection that would silence them in the name of respecting their inherited cultures.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The dar al-harb modernization that doesn't modernize

Contemporary Brotherhood-derived and "moderate Islamist" thought has formally replaced dar al-harb with categories like dar al-shahada (Tariq Ramadan), dar al-da'wa (Yusuf al-Qaradawi), or rejected the binary altogether (the Marrakesh Declaration). The position is therefore not stuck in a 9th-century classification.

The relabeling does substantial concealment work. Ramadan's dar al-shahada retains the conceptual structure — the umma as the primary political body of identification, with the European nation-state as the territory in which Muslims bear witness — without doing what the framework would have to do to be a genuine modernization: fully accept the territorial polity as the legitimate primary unit and the natal-religion identification as secondary. The test is whether the framework treats a Muslim-born person who chooses atheism, a Muslim-born woman who marries a non-Muslim man without his conversion, or a Muslim-born gay man entering a same-sex civil marriage as a full member of the umma in good doctrinal standing. None of the major contemporary Islamist frameworks does. The relabeling allows the position to disclaim the bin-Laden caricature while preserving the categorical structure that produces the apostasy, blasphemy, and gender-asymmetric outcomes the rest of this fault-line set documents. The Marrakesh Declaration is a partial exception — and it is contested by the Saudi establishment, by the Iranian marja'iyya, and by the Pakistani Council of Islamic Ideology in exactly the terms this objection raises.

The selective comparator (Cairo's "in accordance with sharia" elasticity)

The Cairo Declaration is presented in dialogue as compatible with most UDHR articles — the operative qualification "in accordance with Islamic law" is described as a margin-of-interpretation, not a wholesale subordination.

The 1990 Cairo Declaration's Article 24 ("All the rights and freedoms stipulated in this Declaration are subject to the Islamic Sharia") and Article 25 ("The Islamic Sharia is the only source of reference for the explanation or clarification of any of the articles of this Declaration") establish the qualification as wholesale, not marginal. The 2020 OIC revision modified some article-by-article qualifications but retained the operative subordination. Whether the qualification is benign depends entirely on which sharia interpretation is operative — and the OIC's most-influential member states implement the qualification through Iran's Guardian Council jurisprudence, the Saudi senior-scholars' fatwas, and Pakistan's Federal Shariat Court. Ann Mayer's Islam and Human Rights (Westview, multiple editions) parses the article-level conflicts: Article 18 UDHR (freedom to change religion) is incompatible with the Cairo Declaration as conventionally read; Article 16 UDHR (equal marriage rights) is incompatible; Article 5 UDHR (cruel punishment) is incompatible. The "elasticity" framing only works if the strict-conservative readings can be excluded from "Islamic law" — and the very institutions that drafted Cairo defend those readings as authoritative.

The unfalsifiable-divine-source claim

The Islamic universalism is grounded in divine sovereignty rather than autonomous human reason; the substantive disagreement with UDHR is metaphysical, not procedural.

True and trivial. Every system is internally coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether the metaphysical premises are defensible — whether the case for treating divine sovereignty as binding on non-believers (the operative content of the Cairo Declaration's "in accordance with sharia" clauses applied to Bahá'ís, ex-Muslims, atheists, Hindus) survives examination. The position cannot make that case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling. The honest answer to "what binds a Hindu citizen of Pakistan, who has not accepted Islamic premises, to provisions defended on Islamic premises?" cannot be "her acceptance is not required because the premises are true." That is the unfalsifiable retreat the cross-civilizational-negotiation framing is supposed to avoid — and the framework collapses back into it whenever pressed.

The ISIS-exploitation problem

The Salafi-jihadist current's takfiri-caliphate framework is a corruption of the classical tradition, not its logical extension; the mainstream Sunni rejection (Open Letter to al-Baghdadi, Marrakesh Declaration, Amman Message) demonstrates that the dar al-Islam / dar al-harb categories do not operationally license what ISIS did.

The mainstream-rejection point is real and the position is entitled to invoke it — ISIS is genuinely heterodox by classical-Sunni standards, and the 126 signatories of the Open Letter document the disqualifications. What the position does not get to do is invoke the mainstream rejection while keeping the classical categorical structure that ISIS was able to exploit. The classical dar al-Islam / dar al-harb / takfir architecture is the conceptual material from which the maximalist program was assembled; ISIS's selective citation of Ibn Taymiyya's Mardin fatwa, the slavery doctrine, the proclamation-of-caliphate move, and the Adnani lone-wolf incitement all drew on classical-textual sources whose contemporary heirs cannot consistently endorse the source-material while disowning the assembly. The honest move is the Marrakesh Declaration's: explicit doctrinal repudiation of the dhimma framework and the dar al-harb category, replacement with the Charter of Medina equal-citizenship reading. Anything short of that leaves the categorical material available for the next maximalist-vanguard reassembly.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The umma-as-primary-identification framework is internal to Muslim self-understanding and operates only on those who voluntarily affirm it; non-Muslims and ex-Muslims are free to pursue their own civilizational identifications.

The Pakistani, Iranian, Saudi, and OIC factual records are the answer. PPC § 295-C applies to all Pakistani citizens regardless of religion; Iranian apostasy provisions in the 2013 IPC apply to Muslim-born persons regardless of contemporary belief; the Saudi anti-atheism law (2014) defines public atheism as terrorism; the OIC's two-decade UN campaign sought to internationalize the framework as binding on non-OIC states. A framework that imposes its primary-identification structure on non-adherents through state law, transnational instruments, and the threat of social and physical sanction is not "internal to Muslim self-understanding"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one. The Marrakesh signatories recognize it; the institutional mainstream of the OIC member states does not. The position cannot consistently invoke the moderate-reformist current as evidence of the framework's benignity while the operative state-implementation continues to look like Iran's Bahá'í policy and Pakistan's anti-Ahmadi laws.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The Asad-Mahmood-Hallaq parochialism critique

The UDHR system is not a view from nowhere — it carries Enlightenment-Christian-secular assumptions about the autonomous individual rights-bearer, the privatization of religion, the territorial nation-state as the primary unit of political community, and the procedural-rationality model of legitimacy. Talal Asad, Saba Mahmood, and Wael Hallaq have argued these assumptions are themselves a tradition with its own contingent genealogy, not a universal default.

The objection has bite, and the honest answer concedes it. The UDHR carries genealogical baggage; the secular-liberal frame is a tradition with its own historical contingencies; the universalism claim has to be defended on the merits of its content, not on a fictional view-from-nowhere. The right reply is twofold: (1) acknowledge the genealogy without conceding the substantive comparator question — that the operative test is what each framework produces when implemented, not whose hands drafted whose document; (2) defend the substantive content (equal citizenship across religion, sex, ethnicity; right of exit; freedom of conscience; protection from cruel punishment) on grounds the people most affected by the alternative would themselves accept. The Asad-Mahmood critique is most powerful when read as a corrective against complacent universalism; it is least powerful when read as licensing the relativist deflection that would withhold equal protection from people whose inherited cultures do not extend it. The honest move is to take the genealogical critique seriously and refuse the relativist conclusion it does not actually entail.

The selective-implementation objection

The US has not ratified CEDAW, the CRC, or ICESCR; European states have repeatedly violated UDHR norms in counter-terrorism operations, refugee handling, and surveillance regimes; the Refugee Convention has been undermined by every major Western asylum reform since 2015. The conservative position can press: the UDHR system is invoked when convenient and ignored when inconvenient.

The objection is correct and the position should not pretend it isn't. Western implementation of the UDHR-derived treaty system is selective, politically convenient, and frequently violated. The right reply is: the bright line has to hold both ways, and the same accountability that calls out OIC sharia-qualifications also calls out US Senate rejection of CEDAW, French laïcité enforcement that disproportionately falls on Muslim women, UK and EU asylum reforms that have rolled back 1951 Convention protections, and the post-9/11 black-site, drone-strike, and deportation record that violates UDHR norms by definition. The position does not require the speaker to be morally pure; it requires the speaker to apply the line consistently. The asymmetric-accountability the parochialism critique flags is real, and the honest move is to defend the substantive content of the framework while accepting that consistent application calls out one's own state's violations as well as the other side's.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · OIC vs. EU

Council of Europe response and the asymmetric ratification record

The Council of Europe's Parliamentary Assembly Resolution 2253 (2019) explicitly stated the Cairo Declaration is "manifestly incompatible with universal human rights" and called on member states to ensure their bilateral relations with OIC states do not implicitly endorse the Cairo framework. Most OIC member states are formal parties to the UDHR-derived treaty system (ICCPR, ICESCR) while having entered substantial sharia-clause reservations to CEDAW, CRC, and the Refugee Convention.

The 2020 OIC revision of the Cairo Declaration removed the most-criticized explicit sharia subordination in some articles — a partial shift worth Phase-2 examination of how operative it has been.

Source: Council of Europe PACE Resolution 2253
US

US engagement with the universalism debate

The US has historically been a strong champion of the UDHR universalism position abroad while having ratified relatively few of its operative implementation treaties (notably not CEDAW, not the CRC, not the ICESCR). The asymmetry weakens US standing to lecture, while not necessarily undermining the substantive universalism position. USCIRF and State Department religious-freedom reporting operate on the universalism premise.

Source: State Department Religious Freedom Reports
Japan

Japan's UDHR-aligned formal position with practical reservations

Japan is a party to the major UN human rights treaties (ICCPR, ICESCR, CEDAW, CRC, Refugee Convention) and consistently aligns with the UDHR-universalism position in international fora. Japan's domestic implementation has its own gaps — particularly on women's rights, foreign-resident treatment, and refugee recognition — but the formal ratification record is unambiguous.

Japan has not engaged substantially with the OIC framework either way; the universalism-vs.-particularism debate as it concerns Islam is essentially absent from Japanese domestic public discourse.

Source: MOFA Human Rights policy page

Case study: the Marrakesh Declaration 2016 and the dar al-Islam doctrine being renegotiated from inside

The empirical anchor for this fault line on its reformist side is the Marrakesh Declaration on the Rights of Religious Minorities in Predominantly Muslim Majority Communities (25–27 January 2016) — convened by the Forum for Promoting Peace in Muslim Societies under Sheikh Abdallah bin Bayyah, attended by ~250 Muslim scholars from 120 countries, and the Moroccan Ministry of Endowments and Islamic Affairs, with King Mohammed VI's institutional sponsorship. The declaration explicitly invoked the Charter of Medina (the constitutional document the Prophet Muhammad concluded with the Jewish, polytheist, and Muslim communities of Medina in 622 CE) as the model for contemporary equal-citizenship arrangements, and called for "the re-examination of the very concepts of citizenship and protection of minorities, in light of the principles of the Charter of Medina." On the same axis sits ISIS's exploitation of the dar al-Islam / dar al-harb / takfir architecture — the maximalist programme the Marrakesh Declaration explicitly arose to refuse. Together they show the fault line being actively renegotiated from inside the tradition, with the Saudi establishment and the Iranian Guardian Council on one side and the Marrakesh signatories, Indonesia's Nahdlatul Ulama, and the bin Bayyah Forum on the other.

Marrakesh 2016, the Charter of Medina retrieval, and the contested dar al-Islam categorical structure

Marrakesh, Morocco · 25–27 January 2016 · Forum for Promoting Peace in Muslim Societies · King Mohammed VI institutional sponsorship · ~250 signatories from 120 countries · explicit Charter of Medina precedent · explicit rejection of dhimma in modern application

On 27 January 2016, the closing day of a three-day convocation in Marrakesh, ~250 Muslim scholars from 120 countries — including Mauritanian Sheikh Abdallah bin Bayyah, US-based Sheikh Hamza Yusuf (Zaytuna College), Egyptian Grand Mufti Shawqi Allam, Indonesian Sheikh Yahya Cholil Staquf (Nahdlatul Ulama), and Sheikh Mustafa Cerić (former Grand Mufti of Bosnia) — signed the Marrakesh Declaration on the Rights of Religious Minorities in Predominantly Muslim Majority Communities. The declaration's substantive moves were three: explicit invocation of the Charter of Medina as a constitutional model with binding precedent for contemporary practice; explicit rejection of dhimma as a doctrinal category for contemporary Muslim-majority states ("the term dhimmi is no longer applicable"); and explicit call to "re-examine the educational curricula in Muslim majority countries with a view to identifying and rectifying any material that may foster aggression and extremism." The declaration named ISIS's takfiri-caliphate programme as the maximalist exploitation of dar al-Islam categorical structure that necessitated the institutional response. The peace conference was, in bin Bayyah's phrasing, "an act of refusal" — refusal of ISIS's claim to speak for the tradition, refusal of the dhimma categorical structure to govern Muslim-non-Muslim relations in modernity, refusal of the choice the universalism debate had been forcing between unmodified classical fiqh and externally-imposed UDHR. Around the declaration sat the institutional terrain that would absorb or resist it: Saudi Arabia maintained the senior-scholars' council's traditional readings; Iran maintained the Guardian Council's; Pakistan's Federal Shariat Court rulings continued to track the conservative classical positions on apostasy and blasphemy; and ISIS, which had proclaimed its caliphate 19 months before Marrakesh, denounced the declaration as taqut (idolatry) and a betrayal of dar al-Islam. The Charter of Medina retrieval became the clearest intra-Sunni constitutional alternative to both the ISIS reading and the Saudi establishment reading.

The document is preserved in Ibn Ishaq's Sira (mid-8th century) as transmitted by Ibn Hisham. The Marrakesh Declaration retrieves it as constitutional precedent.
  • 9th–14th c. Classical fiqh elaboration of dar al-Islam / dar al-harb / dar al-'ahd / dar al-sulh categorical structure across the four Sunni schools (Abu Hanifa pragmatic-treaty leaning, al-Shafi'i more strictly bipolar, Malik and Ibn Hanbal in between) and the Twelver Shia. Ibn Taymiyya's Mardin fatwa (c. 1303) addresses the in-between case of Mongol-ruled territory; Salafi-jihadist movements selectively cite this material as authorizing armed struggle against contemporary Muslim-majority governments deemed apostate.

The 25-article document parallels the UDHR structure but qualifies every right "in accordance with Islamic law"; Articles 24 and 25 establish the qualification as wholesale subordination.   • Jan 2004 Amman Message: King Abdullah II of Jordan convenes 200 Muslim scholars who sign a three-point statement: recognition of the legitimacy of the eight traditional schools of Islamic jurisprudence (Sunni 4, Shia 2, Ibadi, Zahiri); a strict definition of the conditions for declaring a Muslim apostate (closing the takfir door); a strict definition of who is qualified to issue fatwas. The Amman Message becomes the institutional precedent the Marrakesh Declaration extends.

The proclamation invokes the dar al-Islam / dar al-harb categorical structure in its maximalist form: takfir against all Muslim-majority governments not implementing sharia in the ISIS reading, slavery and dhimma resurrection in territory under control, lone-wolf incitement against the dar al-harb. The Sunni mainstream rejects the proclamation as theologically illegitimate.
  • 19 Sept 2014 Open Letter to Dr. Ibrahim Awwad al-Badri, alias 'Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi': 126 Sunni scholars publish a 24-section refutation of ISIS theology — point by point on takfir, slavery, jizya, the proclamation of caliphate without consultation, the killing of journalists and aid workers, the destruction of shrines. Signatories include the Grand Muftis of Egypt and Bosnia, scholars from al-Azhar, the Saudi establishment, and Western Muslim leadership.   • 25–27 Jan 2016 Marrakesh Declaration: ~250 scholars from 120 countries, convened by the Forum for Promoting Peace in Muslim Societies under bin Bayyah, with the Moroccan Ministry of Endowments and King Mohammed VI's sponsorship, sign the declaration on rights of religious minorities.   • 22 May 2019 NU Nusantara Manifesto / Declaration on Humanitarian Islam: Nahdlatul Ulama, the world's largest Muslim civil-society organization (~90 million members), formally repudiates the dhimma frame, the caliphate-restoration project, and the apostasy-execution doctrine in a programmatic statement aligning with Marrakesh principles and extending them. NU Chairman Yahya Cholil Staquf takes the document on a global tour through 2019–2024 including White House and Vatican meetings.
  • 28 Nov 2020 OIC revises the Cairo Declaration: Council of Foreign Ministers adopts a "Cairo Declaration on Human Rights" (revised) at the Niamey summit, modifying some article-level sharia-qualifications.   • Feb 2019 Document on Human Fraternity: signed by Pope Francis and Sheikh Ahmed el-Tayeb, the Grand Imam of al-Azhar, in Abu Dhabi — a Catholic-Sunni-Islamic joint declaration affirming "the freedom to choose one's religion" and condemning religious violence. What the cluster shows about this fault line. The Marrakesh Declaration is not a watershed and not a one-off. It is the most institutionally weighty contemporary intra-Sunni statement aligning with UDHR-style equal-citizenship principles, drawing on the Charter of Medina precedent (which is genuinely classical, not invented), backed by the Forum for Promoting Peace in Muslim Societies, the Moroccan crown, ~250 scholars from 120 countries, the al-Azhar establishment via el-Tayeb's 2019 Document on Human Fraternity with Pope Francis, and Indonesia's Nahdlatul Ulama with its ~90 million members. It is also explicitly contested by the Saudi senior-scholars' council, the Iranian Guardian Council, the Pakistani Federal Shariat Court, and the entire takfiri-jihadist current — all of whom continue to operate the unmodified classical dar al-Islam / dar al-harb / dhimma / takfir architecture in their institutional and legal practice. The Pew sharia-preference data shows the popular substrate runs from 8% (Azerbaijan) to 99% (Afghanistan); the Arab Barometer Wave VI data shows generational shift in MENA toward equal-citizenship principles is real and accelerating; the ICM 2016 UK Muslim data shows the umma-as-primary-identification cluster is non-trivial in diaspora populations but not the modal position. The fault line is not "Islam vs. universal human rights" — it is the conservative-classical particularist position vs. the reformist intra-Muslim universalist position with the Marrakesh signatories on one side, the Saudi-Iranian-Pakistani-Taliban institutional mainstream on the other, the entire global Muslim population distributed across the distance, ISIS and the Saudi-Iranian conservative establishment exploiting the categorical structure for their own purposes, and the bin Bayyah / NU / Akyol / Saeed reformist current arguing inside the tradition that the Charter of Medina is the actual Islamic precedent and the conservative-classical reading is one defensible reading among several. The 2016 Marrakesh signatories included Hamza Yusuf and Sheikh Mustafa Cerić; the 1985 Sudanese execution of Mahmoud Mohamed Taha for articulating the universalist position is the cost-of-articulation precedent the same tradition contains. The argument continues.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The 2020 OIC revision audit. What changed substantively, what changed cosmetically, and how have OIC member-state implementations moved (if at all) since the revision?
  • The Asad-Mahmood critique on its own terms. Talal Asad's Formations of the Secular and Saba Mahmood's Politics of Piety articulate the strongest academic version of the parochialism case. Phase 2 should engage these directly rather than dismiss them.
  • The Marrakesh Declaration's institutional follow-through. Eight years on, what has the declaration produced in member-state law or practice? Is it a watershed or a one-off?
  • The Refugee Convention reservation pattern. Some OIC member states are non-parties to the 1951 Refugee Convention; others have entered reservations. The pattern is policy-relevant given European refugee-policy debates.
  • Indonesia's position on the universalism debate. The world's largest Muslim-majority democracy aligns formally with the UDHR system while substantive practice varies. Phase 2 should map the actual gap and trend.
