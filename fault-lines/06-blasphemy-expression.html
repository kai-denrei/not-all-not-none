Fault Line · 06

Blasphemy and free expression / 冒涜と表現の自由: protected satire vs. punishable insult

Whether religious belief enjoys legal protection from offense, or whether the right to mock, criticize, and depict — including the religious — is the constitutional baseline.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic legal orders treat religious doctrine as a permitted target of satire, criticism, and depiction — even when offensive — while political-Islamist movements and several Muslim-majority states treat insult to the Prophet, the Qur'an, or core Islamic doctrine as a punishable offense, in some jurisdictions a capital one. The Charlie Hebdo / Jyllands-Posten / Samuel Paty / Salman Rushdie cases are the most-litigated examples; the Pakistani blasphemy law and Saudi shatm al-rasul jurisprudence are the codified ones.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

Insult to the Prophet is categorically prohibited and properly punishable

The honest articulation begins with the textual case the position actually makes. Classical fiqh across the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia treats shatm al-rasul (insult to the Prophet) and sabb Allah (blasphemy of God) as offenses that vitiate the contract of aman (security) for non-Muslims and constitute apostasy for Muslims, with a death penalty in the strict readings (Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali, Twelver Shia) and imprisonment-until-repentance in the most lenient (some Hanafi). Ibn Taymiyya's al-Sarim al-Maslul ala Shatim al-Rasul ("The Drawn Sword Against Whoever Insults the Messenger," c. 1294) is treated by contemporary Salafi-conservative jurists as the canonical statement; al-Qaradawi, the Pakistani Council of Islamic Ideology, and the Iranian Supreme Leader's office articulate substantially the same position in modern terms. Pakistan's PPC § 295-C — mandatory death for derogatory remarks against the Prophet, added under Zia in 1986 — is the codified contemporary version.

The strongest contemporary version concedes that Khomeini's 1989 fatwa against Salman Rushdie was procedurally irregular (no aman-revocation hearing, no jurisdictional ground over a UK citizen), that mob violence in Pakistan operates outside any classical-jurisprudential bar, and that the OIC's 1990s–2010s campaign for a "defamation of religions" UN instrument was an attempt to internationalize the Pakistani-Saudi domestic position. What it does not concede is the categorical-prohibition core: that the Prophet enjoys a status that makes mockery of him a real harm to Muslims (not metaphorical, not subjective), that Muslim-majority states are entitled to keep blasphemy and apostasy provisions as state law, and that the Western "right to offend" is a parochial Enlightenment doctrine wrongly being demanded as a universal. The position is articulated in those exact terms by its serious advocates — al-Azhar, the Pakistani Council of Islamic Ideology, the OIC's permanent UN representations, the Saudi Senior Scholars' Council, and the Iranian Guardian Council. The frame for export is "asymmetric Western hate-speech statutes already protect other categories — include Islam"; the frame at home is the unmodified classical death-penalty for shatm al-rasul.

Liberal-democratic articulation

The right to mock and contradict religion is the bright line

The liberal-democratic position concedes the asymmetry critique has bite: hate-speech statutes, Holocaust denial laws (Germany, Austria, France), royal-defamation provisions, and de-facto self-censorship around the Prophet's image do exist in Western legal systems; the Charlie Hebdo cartoonists were operating in a regulatory environment that is itself not "no protected categories." The honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry — and to roll the asymmetric protections back rather than extend them. The ECtHR's drift (Otto-Preminger 1994, Wingrove 1996, I.A. v. Turkey 2005, E.S. v. Austria 2018) has been inconsistent and is not the settled liberal-democratic baseline some defenders pretend.

The strongest version is unflinching about cost. Salman Rushdie was nearly killed in 1989 and again in 2022; his Italian translator Ettore Capriolo was stabbed (1991), his Norwegian publisher William Nygaard shot (1993), his Japanese translator Hitoshi Igarashi murdered (1991). 12 people were killed at Charlie Hebdo in 2015. Samuel Paty was beheaded in 2020 for showing the cartoons in a class on free expression. The case for protected mockery is not that these costs are imaginary; it is that the alternative — a heckler's veto enforced by murder — produces a society where the most-threatening sub-population sets the limits of public discourse for everyone, in which the people most directly silenced are not Western cartoonists but ex-Muslim writers, Pakistani Christians like Asia Bibi, Bangladeshi atheist bloggers, and reformist scholars whose sole forum is the freedom Rushdie defended. The substantive claim — that legal regimes which prescribe death for insulting a religion are wrong, not different — is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can; Maajid Nawaz, Mustafa Akyol, Hamed Abdel-Samad, Taslima Nasrin, Avijit Roy (murdered 2015) all have. The argument has to be defended through their voices, not over them.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "moderation" bait-and-switch

The contemporary apologetic version offered to Western audiences emphasizes scholarly disagreement on the death penalty, the "high bar" of proof in classical procedure, the Hanafi imprisonment-rather-than-execution position, and the demand for "parity" with Holocaust-denial laws — implying these are the operative content of the position.

In every jurisdiction where the position has actually held state power (Iran 1989 fatwa onward, Saudi Arabia, Pakistan post-1986, Sudan pre-2020, Mauritania, Yemen, Taliban Afghanistan, ISIS-controlled territory), blasphemy and shatm al-rasul provisions are on the books, in active use, and produce executions and lynchings. The Pakistani Centre for Social Justice tracked 247 blasphemy cases registered in 2023 alone — the highest annual figure since the law was last amended. The "moderate" reading is a presentation strategy for diaspora and export, not a description of how the system behaves where it governs.

The Holocaust-denial-tu-quoque

European hate-speech and Holocaust-denial statutes already constitute de-facto blasphemy law for one category; the demand to include Islam is therefore consistency, not special pleading.

The comparator collapses on inspection. Holocaust denial laws criminalize the assertion of a specific historical falsehood about an event with extraordinarily well-documented historical record; blasphemy laws criminalize critique, satire, mockery, or even academic discussion of religious doctrine. The categories are not symmetrical even on the position's own framing. The honest version of the argument has to defend a per-religion privilege — that Islam specifically deserves protection from satire and depiction that Christianity, Judaism, Hinduism, and atheism do not — which is not a parity claim, it is a special-status claim. Even granting the argument's strongest form, the operative remedy (extend protection to all religions equally) is not what Pakistan's § 295-C, Saudi Arabia's anti-atheism law, or the OIC's defamation-of-religions campaign has ever proposed in practice.

The mob-violence externality

The position distinguishes between principled doctrinal disagreement (which it endorses), state prosecution under proper sharia procedure (which it accepts), and mob violence (which it formally disavows).

The disavowal does not survive the empirical record. The killers of Salmaan Taseer (2011), Mashal Khan (2017), and Priyantha Kumara (2021) acted on the doctrinal premise the position teaches; Mumtaz Qadri's funeral drew hundreds of thousands and a shrine was built at his grave that became a pilgrimage site. If the doctrinal frame produces mob violence everywhere it is taught, even where the formal teaching disavows mobs, the doctrinal frame is part of the answer, not an external variable. The same Pakistani society that produced Qadri was unable to produce a single major mosque willing to refuse him funeral rites. Calling this a "mob problem distinct from doctrine" treats the empirical record as separable from the framework — a separation the framework's own theology rejects when it claims the framework is divinely revealed and complete.

The "real harm" claim

Insult to the Prophet causes real harm to Muslims — not metaphorical or subjective, but a real injury to the community's spiritual integrity that warrants legal response.

The position cannot define "real harm" without circularity. If "real harm" means measurable injury (death, bodily harm, property loss), insult does not produce it. If "real harm" means subjective offense felt by the believer, the standard licenses any believer's offense at any expression as ground for legal response — a regime under which Hindus might criminalize beef advocacy, Catholics criminalize anti-Vatican satire, atheists criminalize public prayer. The "real harm" framing only works if the privileged metaphysical premise is granted, and the premise that licenses it is the same one the system asks the broader liberal-democratic order to take on faith. The substantive claim collapses to: insult to our sacred is real harm in a way that insult to your sacred is not. That is not a parity argument; it is the asymmetry it accuses the West of.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The blasphemy frame is internal to Islamic discourse and operates on Muslims who voluntarily submit to it; non-Muslims and ex-Muslims are free to disagree.

The Pakistani factual record is the answer. PPC § 295-C applies to all citizens regardless of religion: Asia Bibi was Christian, Mashal Khan was Muslim, Priyantha Kumara was Buddhist. The Iranian fatwa against Rushdie applied to a UK citizen on UK soil. The de-facto enforcement extends through diaspora networks (the 2022 attack on Rushdie in New York was carried out by a 24-year-old US-born attacker citing the 1989 fatwa) and through corporate self-censorship that affects everyone (Yale University Press redacted the Jyllands-Posten cartoons from a 2009 academic book on the controversy). A doctrinal regime that imposes its norms on non-adherents through state prosecution, transnational fatwa, and the threat of violence is not "internal to Islamic discourse"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The asymmetric-protection objection

The liberal-democratic free-expression principle is unevenly applied: Holocaust denial is criminal in 19 European jurisdictions, Article 130 StGB ("incitement of the people") is widely used in Germany, France's Gayssot Act criminalizes denial of crimes against humanity, the UK Equality Act and Public Order Act limit "religiously aggravated" speech. Several ECtHR judgments (Otto-Preminger, Wingrove, I.A., E.S.) have upheld restrictions on anti-religious expression. The conservative position can press: if expression is the bright line, why is the West already drawing several?

The objection has bite, and the honest answer concedes it. Western legal systems do contain asymmetric protections that produce real chilling effects — the publishers who declined to reprint the Jyllands-Posten cartoons, the Yale Press redactions, the de-facto omertà around drawing the Prophet's image in mainstream European publications post-2015 — and these are not consistent with the strongest free-expression position. The right reply is: roll the asymmetric protections back, not extend them; defend the current settlement on its merits as a transitional state and not as a settled doctrine; and acknowledge that the chilling-effect critique of European publishing is a real phenomenon that weakens the formal-protection case if not honestly named. The conservative position's substantive claim — that Islam should join the protected list — is not the same as the conservative position's procedural critique that the West is hypocritical, and the procedural critique can be granted without granting the substantive demand.

The "wrong, not different" claim

The strongest liberal version holds that the death-penalty for blasphemy and the Khomeini-fatwa transnational-enforcement regime are wrong, not "another tradition." This is a substantive cross-civilizational moral claim with imperial echoes.

The claim is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can — Salman Rushdie himself (born in Bombay, raised in a Muslim family), Maajid Nawaz, Mustafa Akyol's Reopening Muslim Minds retrieving the Mu'tazilite tradition, Hamed Abdel-Samad's classical-textual critique, Taslima Nasrin since 1993, Avijit Roy (murdered in Dhaka 2015), the four other Bangladeshi secular bloggers killed in 2015–16, and the contemporary Pakistani journalists and lawyers who continue to defend blasphemy-accused at lethal personal cost. The argument has to be defended through these voices, not over them. It is dishonest to pretend the universalist framing has no civilizational asymmetry baked in; the honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry, defend the substantive claim on the merits, and refuse the relativist deflection that would silence ex-Muslim and dissident voices in the name of respecting Muslim-majority cultures.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Charlie Hebdo (2015), Samuel Paty (2020), and ten years on

The 7 January 2015 attack on Charlie Hebdo killed 12 people including the editor and lead cartoonists, in retaliation for the magazine's republication of Jyllands-Posten Muhammad cartoons. The 2020 beheading of teacher Samuel Paty in Conflans-Sainte-Honorine, after he showed the cartoons in a class on free expression, demonstrated the threat had moved from publication to pedagogy. The 2022 stabbing of Salman Rushdie in New York closed a 33-year arc opened by Khomeini's 1989 fatwa.

French law (1881 Press Law as amended) does not criminalize blasphemy; the question is whether the legal baseline is sufficient when the practical baseline is enforced by attack.

Sources: Brookings on European blasphemy · Ten years after Charlie Hebdo
US

First Amendment doctrine and Rushdie's New York attack

US constitutional law forecloses any state-level blasphemy criminalization (the last US blasphemy conviction was 1928, and Joseph Burstyn v. Wilson (1952) effectively barred such prosecutions). Salman Rushdie was attacked on stage at the Chautauqua Institution in New York in August 2022 by a 24-year-old US-born attacker citing the 1989 fatwa; Rushdie lost an eye and use of one hand. The constitutional protection of speech is intact; the practical security question for individual writers, cartoonists, and translators is harder.

Source: English PEN — Rushdie statement
Japan

No blasphemy statute, no high-profile Islamic-blasphemy case

Japan has no general blasphemy criminal statute and the relevant Islamic-context case law is essentially empty — a function both of the small Muslim population and of the broader Japanese cultural pattern of absorbing religious-symbolic provocation through avoidance rather than legal contest. The Yasukuni Shrine and Imperial-symbol controversies that do generate Japanese friction are domestically Shinto-centered, not Islamic.

A future deep dive could ask what Japan's response would be to a domestically-published equivalent of the Jyllands-Posten cartoons; the honest answer is that no Japanese mainstream publication has tested it, and the absence of testing is itself a finding.

Case study: Rushdie 1989–2022 and the codified legal architecture

The 33-year arc of the Rushdie fatwa is the longest-running test case of how transnational blasphemy enforcement actually behaves — issued 1989 against a UK citizen for an English-language novel, killing a Japanese translator in 1991, nearly killing Rushdie himself in New York in 2022. Around it sits the codified architecture: Pakistan's PPC §§ 295-A/B/C and 298 (Ahmadi-specific) under which 247 cases were registered in 2023; Indonesia's 1965 Anti-Blasphemy Law (PNPS Law No. 1) used against Ahok in 2017 and against Sukmawati Sukarnoputri in 2019; the OIC's two-decade UN campaign for a "defamation of religions" instrument that ran until 2011 and morphed into Resolution 16/18 on incitement to hatred. The Charlie Hebdo and Samuel Paty cases are documented separately on Page 20 — this case study focuses on the legal-instrument architecture and the Rushdie thread.

The Rushdie fatwa, the Pakistani PPC architecture, and the OIC's two-decade UN campaign

UK / India 1988 publication · Iran 14 Feb 1989 fatwa · Japan / Italy / Norway 1991–1993 attacks · New York 12 Aug 2022 attack · Pakistan PPC 1986 amendments · OIC defamation campaign 1999–2011

On 14 February 1989, Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini issued a fatwa on Tehran Radio sentencing British-Indian novelist Salman Rushdie to death for his fourth novel The Satanic Verses (Viking Penguin, September 1988) — a magical-realist work whose title refers to a contested early-Islamic narrative about Quranic verses ostensibly recited by the Prophet then withdrawn. The Iranian state-affiliated 15 Khordad Foundation placed a $2.8 million bounty (later raised to $3.3 million in 2012). The fatwa applied to "the author of the book entitled The Satanic Verses, which is against Islam, the Prophet, and the Qur'an, and all those involved in its publication, who were aware of its content." The transnational enforcement campaign that followed killed his Japanese translator, stabbed his Italian translator, shot his Norwegian publisher, killed protesters in Pakistan and India, and culminated 33 years later when a 24-year-old US-born attacker stabbed Rushdie 12 times on stage at the Chautauqua Institution. He survived. Around the fatwa sits a codified architecture: Pakistan's PPC § 295-C as added by the 1986 amendment (mandatory death for derogatory remarks against the Prophet); Indonesia's 1965 Anti-Blasphemy Law as activated against the Christian Jakarta governor Basuki Tjahaja Purnama ("Ahok") in 2017; and the Organization of Islamic Cooperation's sustained 1999–2011 UN campaign to internationalize a "defamation of religions" instrument under the Universal Declaration framework.

Pakistan, Bangladesh, and South Africa follow. Six people are killed in protests in Islamabad on 12 February 1989; one is killed in Kashmir on 13 February.
  • 14 Feb 1989 Ayatollah Khomeini issues the fatwa on Radio Tehran, calling on "all valiant Muslims, wherever they may be in the world, to kill" Rushdie and the publishers. The 15 Khordad Foundation announces a bounty of $2.8 million (later raised). Rushdie enters protective custody in the UK under the codename "Joseph Anton" (memoir title); he remains under police protection for nine years.
  • 3 Aug 1989 A bomb-maker named Mustafa Mahmoud Mazeh dies prematurely while preparing a bomb intended for Rushdie at the Beverly House Hotel in Paddington Central, London. The Tehran Behesht-e Zahra cemetery later erects a monument hailing him as a martyr.
  • 11 July 1991 Hitoshi Igarashi, the Japanese translator of The Satanic Verses (Tsukuba University, professor of comparative Islamic culture), is found stabbed to death in his university office. The case has never been solved.
  • 3 July 1991 Ettore Capriolo, the Italian translator, is stabbed multiple times in his Milan apartment; he survives. The attacker, an Iranian national, escapes.
  • 11 Oct 1993 William Nygaard, the Norwegian publisher, is shot three times outside his Oslo home; he survives. Two suspects are charged in 2018 — too late for prosecution under Norwegian statute of limitations — and the Norwegian Police Security Service publicly attributes the attack to Iranian state involvement.
  • 3 Mar 1989 / 2 July 1993 Sivas massacre, Turkey: an Islamist mob, organized partly around Aziz Nesin's planned Turkish translation of excerpts from The Satanic Verses, sets fire to the Madımak Hotel during the Pir Sultan Abdal Cultural Festival, killing 33 (mostly Alevi intellectuals) and 2 hotel workers. Nesin escapes.
  • 1986 / amended through 1992 Pakistan codifies the contemporary architecture. PPC § 295-A (insulting religious feelings, 1927 colonial-era), § 295-B (defiling the Quran, life imprisonment, 1982), § 295-C (derogatory remarks against the Prophet, mandatory death, 1986 under General Zia, made non-bailable 1991, mandatory death only after Federal Shariat Court ruling October 1990), § 298-A (Ahl al-Bayt insult), § 298-B and § 298-C (Ahmadis cannot call themselves Muslim or their houses of worship mosques, 1984 Ordinance XX). The architecture is the most extensive codified blasphemy regime among Muslim-majority states.
  • 25 March 1999 OIC introduces "Defamation of Islam" resolution at UNCHR (Resolution 1999/82). Renewed annually with broadening "defamation of religions" framing through 2010. Western states and human-rights NGOs (HRW, ARTICLE 19, IHEU) critique the resolutions as backdoor blasphemy law; vote margins narrow each year as Latin American and African states defect. In 2011 the OIC accepts a US-brokered compromise: Resolution 16/18 on "combating intolerance" replaces "defamation" with "incitement to imminent violence" — substantively a defeat for the OIC position. Pakistan and Egypt have continued attempting reframings since.
  • 9 May 2017 Basuki Tjahaja Purnama ("Ahok"), the Christian Chinese-Indonesian Governor of Jakarta, is sentenced to 2 years' imprisonment under Indonesia's 1965 Anti-Blasphemy Law (PNPS Law No. 1) for a campaign-trail comment quoting Quran 5:51 in a way conservative clerics framed as insult. The case is preceded by mass mobilizations of 200,000+ in Jakarta organized by FPI and the 212 Movement. Ahok loses the gubernatorial runoff and is jailed; the Indonesian-pluralism narrative takes a recognized hit.
  • 12 Aug 2022 Hadi Matar, a 24-year-old US-born New Jersey resident of Lebanese-Shia background, attacks Rushdie on stage at the Chautauqua Institution in western New York moments before his lecture. Rushdie is stabbed 12 times; he loses sight in his right eye and use of his left hand. Matar tells the New York Post he had read "two pages" of The Satanic Verses and was acting in support of the Khomeini fatwa. He is convicted on attempted-murder charges in February 2025; sentenced May 2025 to 25 years.
  • 16 Apr 2024 Salman Rushdie publishes Knife: Meditations After an Attempted Murder (Random House) — first-person account of the attack, recovery, and the longer arc since 1989. The book becomes the contemporary statement of the position by its central figure.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The Rushdie fatwa is the diagnostic instrument — issued by a sovereign state against a foreign citizen, never formally rescinded (Iran's 1998 statement that the regime would not "support or hinder" enforcement was not a withdrawal), still operationally active 33 years later when a 24-year-old US-born attacker recited the 1989 grounds back to police. Around the fatwa sits the codified architecture: Pakistan's PPC § 295-C (mandatory death, applied across religions, 247 cases registered in 2023), Indonesia's PNPS 1965 (used against the Christian governor Ahok in 2017 with broad popular support), and the OIC's 1999–2011 UN campaign to internationalize a defamation-of-religions instrument that Western states defeated only by negotiating Resolution 16/18 as a substantive replacement. The popular substrate is real (76% Pakistan and 84% Egypt for death-for-apostasy in Pew 2011, 62% Indonesia for the Ahok verdict in LSI 2017) and the diaspora variation is real (ComRes 2015 — 11% UK Muslims agreeing publishers "deserve to be attacked"). The fault line is not Western expression versus Muslim sensitivity — it is the doctrinal-state position that licenses execution and transnational fatwa for blasphemy, vs. the constitutional settlement that protects the right to mock and contradict religion, with the entire global Muslim population distributed across the distance, the maximalist-state regimes killing now, and the reformist current — Akyol, Saeed, the Marrakesh signatories, the British Muslim majority who reject the violent reading — arguing inside the tradition that the consensus claim is overstated. Hitoshi Igarashi died in 1991. Avijit Roy died in 2015. The argument continues.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The ECtHR's inconsistent line. Phase 2 should map the full case sequence — Otto-Preminger, Wingrove, I.A. v. Turkey, E.S. v. Austria, Sekmadienis v. Lithuania (2018), and any post-2020 cases — and ask whether the Court has converged on a defensible doctrine or is still drifting.
  • The Pakistani enforcement landscape. 295-C prosecutions have soared since 2010. What is the current case-load, conviction rate, and mob-violence-vs.-state-prosecution split? Phase 2 should locate the most current academic and human-rights-org tracking.
  • The de-facto blasphemy regime in Europe. No legislative blasphemy prohibition, but publishers self-censor (Yale Press redacted the cartoons from a 2009 academic book on the controversy). Phase 2 should document the chilling-effect side honestly — it is a real phenomenon and weakens the formal-protection case if not acknowledged.
  • Hate-speech vs. blasphemy distinction. The honest case for European hate-speech statutes is that they target incitement, not insult. Whether the distinction holds in practice — particularly in French and German prosecutions of the 2010s — is genuinely contested.
  • Asia Bibi and the case-study question. Her 2018 acquittal by the Pakistani Supreme Court is the most-cited contemporary case of the system delivering a non-conviction. What does the post-acquittal landscape — her exile, the assassination of two officials who defended her, the Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan mobilization — tell us about the operating equilibrium?
