Fault Line · 06

Blasphemy and free expression: protected satire vs. punishable insult

Whether religious belief enjoys legal protection from offense, or whether the right to mock, criticize, and depict — including the religious — is the constitutional baseline.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic legal orders treat religious doctrine as a permitted target of satire, criticism, and depiction — even when offensive — while political-Islamist movements and several Muslim-majority states treat insult to the Prophet, the Qur'an, or core Islamic doctrine as a punishable offense, in some jurisdictions a capital one. The Charlie Hebdo / Jyllands-Posten / Samuel Paty / Salman Rushdie cases are the most-litigated examples; the Pakistani blasphemy law and Saudi shatm al-rasul jurisprudence are the codified ones.

Steelmanned positions

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Blasphemy is real harm, not free speech

The strongest version argues that the European Enlightenment's blasphemy-protection regime was forged against Christianity in a context where Christianity held political power and could absorb the criticism without existential damage. To extend the same regime to Islam in a Muslim-minority context, where Muslim communities hold no equivalent power and where deliberate provocations function as collective insult, is not principled neutrality — it is enforced asymmetry. The strongest version concedes a robust right to scholarly critique, theological disagreement, and earnest argument; what it refuses is the right to gratuitous mockery whose function is humiliation.

The argument extends to the methodological objection that Western states already enforce de-facto blasphemy norms — Holocaust denial laws, hate-speech statutes, lèse-majesté in monarchies, contempt-of-court — and selectively designate only the Muslim-protective version as illegitimate. The honest demand is parity: either no protected categories, or include Islam in the protected list.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Free expression includes the right to offend

The liberal-democratic position concedes the asymmetry argument has bite: hate-speech statutes, Holocaust denial laws, and de-facto taboos do exist in Western legal systems, and the Charlie Hebdo cartoonists were freer to mock Islam than they would have been to mock other categories under those same statutes. The defensible response is to roll the asymmetric protections back, not to extend them. The European Court of Human Rights has been moving inconsistently in this direction over the 2010s and 2020s.

The strongest version is unflinching about cost: cartoonists were murdered, a teacher was beheaded, an author was nearly killed for a 1988 novel, several others have been killed since. The case for protected satire is not that the costs are imaginary; it is that the alternative — a heckler's veto enforced by murder — produces a society where the most-threatening sub-population sets the limits of public discourse for everyone. That trade is unacceptable, and the reformers and ex-Muslims who most need free critique to be available are the people the alternative most directly silences.

The friction in The West

EU · France

Charlie Hebdo (2015), Samuel Paty (2020), and ten years on

The 7 January 2015 attack on Charlie Hebdo killed 12 people including the editor and lead cartoonists, in retaliation for the magazine's republication of Jyllands-Posten Muhammad cartoons. The 2020 beheading of teacher Samuel Paty in Conflans-Sainte-Honorine, after he showed the cartoons in a class on free expression, demonstrated the threat had moved from publication to pedagogy. The 2022 stabbing of Salman Rushdie in New York closed a 33-year arc opened by Khomeini's 1989 fatwa.

French law (1881 Press Law as amended) does not criminalize blasphemy; the question is whether the legal baseline is sufficient when the practical baseline is enforced by attack.

Sources: Brookings on European blasphemy · Ten years after Charlie Hebdo
US

First Amendment doctrine and Rushdie's New York attack

US constitutional law forecloses any state-level blasphemy criminalization (the last US blasphemy conviction was 1928, and Joseph Burstyn v. Wilson (1952) effectively barred such prosecutions). Salman Rushdie was attacked on stage at the Chautauqua Institution in New York in August 2022 by a 24-year-old US-born attacker citing the 1989 fatwa; Rushdie lost an eye and use of one hand. The constitutional protection of speech is intact; the practical security question for individual writers, cartoonists, and translators is harder.

Source: English PEN — Rushdie statement
Japan

No blasphemy statute, no high-profile Islamic-blasphemy case

Japan has no general blasphemy criminal statute and the relevant Islamic-context case law is essentially empty — a function both of the small Muslim population and of the broader Japanese cultural pattern of absorbing religious-symbolic provocation through avoidance rather than legal contest. The Yasukuni Shrine and Imperial-symbol controversies that do generate Japanese friction are domestically Shinto-centered, not Islamic.

A future deep dive could ask what Japan's response would be to a domestically-published equivalent of the Jyllands-Posten cartoons; the honest answer is that no Japanese mainstream publication has tested it, and the absence of testing is itself a finding.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Salman Rushdie (British-Indian) — The Satanic Verses (1988); two attempts on his life, the second nearly successful in 2022. The defining case. · Maajid Nawaz (British-Pakistani) — public defender of Charlie Hebdo, called for British Muslims to defend free expression on Islamic grounds. · Hamed Abdel-Samad (Egyptian-German) — under fatwa for his 2010 book on Islamic Fascism; lives under permanent police protection. · Taslima Nasrin (Bangladeshi) — exiled under multiple fatwas since 1993. · Avijit Roy (Bangladeshi-American) — secular blogger, hacked to death in Dhaka 2015; his death is the case study in how blasphemy enforcement reaches across borders. · Asia Bibi (Pakistani Christian, blasphemy death-row case 2010–2018) — not Muslim, but the most prominent contemporary blasphemy-case survivor; freed by Pakistani Supreme Court 2018, escorted into exile in Canada. · The Charlie Hebdo cartoonists who survived — Riss, Coco, Catherine Meurisse — continued publication; Meurisse's La Légèreté (2016) is the most-read first-person account of surviving the attack.

Sources collected

Court case · ECtHR
E.S. v. Austria, ECtHR, App. No. 38450/12, judgment 25 October 2018. Upheld an Austrian conviction for "disparagement of religious doctrines" arising from a 2009 lecture characterizing the Prophet Muhammad's marriage to Aisha as paedophilia.
Method: European Court of Human Rights judgment. Important because the Court found the conviction did not violate Article 10 (freedom of expression). Read alongside Otto-Preminger-Institut v. Austria (1994) and Wingrove v. UK (1996) for the ECtHR's inconsistent line.
Caveat: ECtHR doctrine in this area is not a settled liberal-democratic baseline. Wingrove (1996), Otto-Preminger (1994), and E.S. (2018) all uphold restrictions; I.A. v. Turkey (2005) likewise. The court has not converged on the strong free-expression position.
HUDOC E.S. v. Austria
Pakistani blasphemy law
Pakistan Penal Code §§ 295-A, 295-B, 295-C (Sections expanded under Zia-ul-Haq, 1980s). 295-C makes derogatory remarks against the Prophet Muhammad punishable by death.
Method: Primary legislation, in active use. The Asia Bibi case (2010–2018) was the highest-profile prosecution; mob-violence enforcement (Mashal Khan 2017, Priyantha Kumara 2021) operates in parallel.

Method: Telephone survey, 1,000+ UK Muslim respondents. Cited in the hub. The methodology has been debated; the magnitude of the offense-norm finding has been broadly replicated.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The ECtHR's inconsistent line. Phase 2 should map the full case sequence — Otto-Preminger, Wingrove, I.A. v. Turkey, E.S. v. Austria, Sekmadienis v. Lithuania (2018), and any post-2020 cases — and ask whether the Court has converged on a defensible doctrine or is still drifting.
  • The Pakistani enforcement landscape. 295-C prosecutions have soared since 2010. What is the current case-load, conviction rate, and mob-violence-vs.-state-prosecution split? Phase 2 should locate the most current academic and human-rights-org tracking.
  • The de-facto blasphemy regime in Europe. No legislative blasphemy prohibition, but publishers self-censor (Yale Press redacted the cartoons from a 2009 academic book on the controversy). Phase 2 should document the chilling-effect side honestly — it is a real phenomenon and weakens the formal-protection case if not acknowledged.
  • Hate-speech vs. blasphemy distinction. The honest case for European hate-speech statutes is that they target incitement, not insult. Whether the distinction holds in practice — particularly in French and German prosecutions of the 2010s — is genuinely contested.
  • Asia Bibi and the case-study question. Her 2018 acquittal by the Pakistani Supreme Court is the most-cited contemporary case of the system delivering a non-conviction. What does the post-acquittal landscape — her exile, the assassination of two officials who defended her, the Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan mobilization — tell us about the operating equilibrium?
