Fault Line · 07

Violence and political change: ballots vs. jihadist strands

Whether legitimate political change is achieved through elections, courts, and persuasion, or whether — within explicit Islamist sub-traditions — armed jihad against the present order is theologically valid and operationally indicated.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal democracies define legitimate political change as ballot-based, court-mediated, and persuasion-driven; the Salafi-jihadist tradition (al-Qaeda, ISIS, AQAP, JNIM, ISWAP, ISIS-K) holds that armed jihad against governments deemed apostate is theologically obligatory and operationally available, while the Brotherhood-derived Islamist tradition formally rejects violence in Western contexts and pursues institutional change instead. The split between these two strands inside political Islam is real and policy-relevant.

Steelmanned positions

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Jihad against tyranny is theologically obligatory

The Salafi-jihadist position, in its strongest articulation, draws on Ibn Taymiyya, Sayyid Qutb, Abdullah Azzam, and Abu Bakr Naji to argue that contemporary governments — including most Muslim-majority ones — fail the test of legitimate Islamic governance and must be confronted. Voting within the Western system is, in this view, ratification of an illegitimate order; participation in Western liberal democracy a more sophisticated form of jahiliyya than the colonial regimes that preceded it.

The strongest version concedes that violence against civilians is forbidden by classical jurisprudence and concedes the historical absence of suicide-attack tactics in pre-modern Islamic warfare. Its operative argument is that in conditions of asymmetric power, the classical rules require modification, and that the umma's obligation of self-defense extends to taking the fight to the source of aggression. The position is consciously revisionist within the tradition, not classical — which is one of the strongest internal arguments against it.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Ballots, courts, and the bright line on violence

The liberal-democratic position is straightforward at its baseline: political change happens through votes, court rulings, public persuasion, and lawful protest. Armed political violence against civilian targets is criminal regardless of cause. The strong-form position adds that this principle holds even for causes the speaker sympathizes with — the test is the means, not the goal.

The strongest version concedes that liberal democracies have themselves used massive violence in pursuit of political change (decolonization wars, Iraq 2003, Afghanistan 2001–2021, Vietnam, the colonial project itself) and that the moral authority to lecture is therefore complicated. What it refuses to concede is the equivalence move that converts complicated moral standing into licence for civilian-targeting attacks — the bright line on civilian targeting holds even when the lecturer's hands are dirty, because the alternative is no line at all.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

The 2015–2017 European jihadist wave and after

Charlie Hebdo (Jan 2015), Bataclan (Nov 2015), Brussels (Mar 2016), Nice (Jul 2016), Berlin Christmas market (Dec 2016), Manchester Arena (May 2017), Las Ramblas (Aug 2017), and dozens of smaller attacks defined the European jihadist period. Most operationally tied to ISIS or AQ networks; most attackers EU-citizen second-generation. Frequency declined sharply 2018–2024 due to ISIS territorial collapse, intelligence cooperation, and prosecutorial response. Sporadic attacks continue (Vienna 2020, Paris Notre-Dame area 2020, Reading UK 2020, Brussels 2023).

The Brotherhood-aligned Islamist current, distinct from the jihadist one, pursues institutional and electoral strategies — the focus of the May 2025 French Muslim Brotherhood report cited in the hub. Both currents are real; conflating them is the analytical error.

Source: EU Radicalisation Awareness Network factbook
US

9/11, the homeland-attack drought, and post-2014 returnee question

The US has experienced very few successful Islamist-directed mass-casualty attacks since 9/11 — Fort Hood (2009), Boston Marathon (2013), San Bernardino (2015), Orlando Pulse (2016), Manhattan truck (2017) being the principal cases. Numbers are an order of magnitude lower than European frequencies in the comparable period, attributed by analysts to a mix of geographic separation, an overwhelmingly first-generation Muslim immigrant population with high-skill selection, intelligence-community resourcing, and the FBI's controversial sting-operation methodology.

Source: Global Terrorism Database (START / U. Maryland)
Japan

Effectively zero domestic Islamist political violence

Japan has had essentially no domestic Islamist political violence — the 2015 ISIS hostage execution of two Japanese citizens (Kenji Goto, Haruna Yukawa) was an attack on Japanese nationals abroad, not on Japanese soil. The 1970s Japanese Red Army cooperated with Palestinian groups including the 1972 Lod Airport attack — that is the closest historical Japan-Islamist-violence linkage and was leftist, not Islamist on the Japanese side.

Honest finding: very low salience for the standard reasons — small population, immigration regime, geographic distance. Japan participates in counter-terrorism intelligence cooperation but does not experience domestic Islamist attacks.

Source: MOFA counter-terrorism policy

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Maajid Nawaz (British-Pakistani) — formerly Hizb ut-Tahrir, served prison time in Egypt; later co-founded Quilliam (now closed); de-radicalization advocate. · Hassan Hassan (Syrian-American) — co-author ISIS: Inside the Army of Terror (2015); intra-Arab journalism documenting the jihadist current from inside. · Ed Husain (British) — The Islamist (2007); former Hizb ut-Tahrir member. · Hamed Abdel-Samad — Islamic Fascism (2014); under fatwa. · Salah al-Ansari and the broader al-Azhar reform current — institutional Sunni-orthodox condemnations of jihadist hermeneutics, particularly post-2014. · The Sahel-region traditional clerics opposing JNIM and ISIS-Sahel — most have not survived the experience but their fatwas and recorded sermons are documentary.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The decline-of-jihadism question. European jihadist attack frequency is sharply down 2018–2025 vs. 2014–2017. How much is durable (territorial defeat of ISIS, intelligence cooperation, prosecution backlog clearing) and how much is cyclical (returnee waves, Sahel expansion, Gaza-conflict spillover risk)?
  • The Brotherhood / jihadist boundary. Is the institutional-Brotherhood path genuinely separate from the jihadist path, or does it function as a recruitment funnel for some sub-population? The hub's framing implies the former; some analysts (Vidino partially) argue partial overlap. Phase 2 should weigh the evidence.
  • Sahel as the new center. JNIM (al-Qaeda affiliate) and ISIS-Sahel have grown rapidly; the GTI 2024 ranks the Sahel as the world's deadliest region for terrorism. What is the implication for European security through migration and Mediterranean spillover?
  • The Pew suicide-bombing numbers a decade later. Pew has not repeated the World's Muslims survey at scale. What does the most-current country-level data (Arab Barometer waves, IFOP, Policy Exchange) show on this measure? Has it moved?
  • Returnee management. European intelligence services estimate ~5,000+ EU-citizen ISIS travelers, of whom ~30% returned, ~20% killed, ~50% remaining in detention or unaccounted. The recidivism rate, prosecution rate, and rehabilitation-program effectiveness are critical Phase-2 questions.
