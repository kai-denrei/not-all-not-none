Fault Line · 07

Violence and political change / 暴力と政治変革: ballots vs. jihadist strands

Legitimate political change: elections, courts, persuasion. Or, within explicit Islamist sub-traditions, armed jihad (ジハード) against the present order as theological obligation.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1
Liberal democracies define legitimate political change (政治改革) as ballot-based, court-mediated, and persuasion-driven; the Salafi-jihadist tradition (al-Qaeda, ISIS, AQAP, JNIM, ISWAP, ISIS-K) holds that armed jihad against governments deemed apostate is theologically obligatory and operationally available, while the Brotherhood-derived Islamist tradition formally rejects violence in Western contexts and pursues institutional change instead. The split between these two strands inside political Islam is real and policy-relevant.

Steelmanned positions

Each side in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Salafi-jihadist articulation

Armed jihad against the present world order is individual obligation

Name the inheritance first. Ibn Taymiyya's 1303 Mardin fatwa (read by twentieth-century jihadists as authorizing jihad against rulers who claim Islam but govern by non-sharia law) is the medieval anchor. Qutb's Ma'alim fi-l-Tariq (1964) supplies the modern reading: contemporary Muslim societies are in jahiliyya, and the vanguard's obligation is hijra-then-jihad to overturn the apostate order. Azzam's Defence of the Muslim Lands (1984) reframes jihad as fard al-'ayn (individual, not collective obligation) when Muslim land is occupied. Naji's Idarat at-Tawahhush (2004) supplies the operational doctrine: provoke disproportionate Western response, exhaust the enemy's will, administer territory through "vexation" until state-building is possible. The near-enemy/far-enemy debate (Faraj 1981 vs. the 1998 World Islamic Front declaration) is the operational variation on the same theological frame.

The strongest version concedes what its serious advocates know: classical fiqh prohibits targeting non-combatants; the Sunni mainstream (al-Azhar, the Saudi Senior Scholars' Council, the four traditional schools' contemporary heirs) has formally rejected the takfiri move and condemns ISIS specifically. What the position refuses to concede: the umma faces asymmetric existential threat from a Crusader-Zionist alliance, Muslim-majority governments ruling by non-sharia codes are apostates whose blood is licit, and civilian-targeting is justified under tatarrus doctrine. Bin Laden's 1996 and 1998 declarations and Baghdadi's 2014 Mosul khutba say exactly this. The position is consciously revisionist; it claims not to be the Sunni mainstream but rather that the mainstream is corrupted and the vanguard sees correctly.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Ballots, courts, persuasion; and a bright line on civilian targeting

The baseline is simple: political change happens through votes, court rulings, public persuasion, and lawful protest (非暴力). Armed political violence against civilians is criminal regardless of cause. The strong-form version adds that this holds even for causes the speaker sympathizes with; the test is the means, not the goal. The obligation applies to liberal democracies themselves when they cross the line (Abu Ghraib, civilian casualties in Iraq and Afghanistan, drone-strike collateral damage, the broader colonial-violence record are all part of the same accounting).

The strongest version concedes the moral-standing asymmetry. Western states fought decolonization wars in Algeria, Kenya, and Indo-China; deposed governments in Iran (1953), Guatemala (1954), Chile (1973); invaded Iraq in 2003 on grounds the post-war record discredits; killed civilians in counter-terrorism operations from Pakistan to Yemen that the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Airwaves, and ACLED have documented. The bright-line argument cannot rest on clean hands. The defensible claim: the bright line holds across the asymmetry, not because the speaker is morally pure, but because the alternative (civilian-targeting acceptable for whichever side claims sufficient grievance) is a position no civilian on any side survives. The same line that condemns ISIS executing aid workers condemns disproportionate strikes killing civilians in Mosul, Raqqa, Gaza, and Aleppo. The claim is the line itself, not the speaker's credibility. It is made most credibly by those most directly hit: Iraqi Yazidis, Bataclan survivors, Sahel village elders murdered by JNIM and ISIS-Sahel, and the Muslim-majority of victims (Pew, START, ACLED converge: roughly 85% of jihadist victims since 2001 are Muslim civilians in Muslim-majority states).

Method note. Both cards present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides, because intellectual honesty requires it. The standard: both positions clear the same evidentiary bar.

Cross-examination

The arguments above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the Salafi-jihadist articulation

The takfir-laundering move

The position formally prohibits targeting Muslims and concedes the classical-fiqh civilian-immunity rule, presenting itself as fighting only the apostate state and its non-Muslim allies.

The empirical record is the answer. ISIS killed more Muslims than non-Muslims in absolute numbers between 2014 and 2019, by an order of magnitude. JNIM and ISIS-Sahel kill village imams, traditional-Sufi clerics, and Muslim civilians who refuse cooperation, in volumes ACLED documents weekly. The takfir doctrine, declaring fellow Muslims apostates so that classical civilian-immunity does not apply, is the operational mechanism that converts the formal civilian-immunity rule into license for the actual violence the movement does. The Saudi Senior Scholars' Council's 2014 statement against ISIS, al-Azhar's repeated declarations, and the November 2014 "Open Letter to al-Baghdadi" signed by 126 Sunni scholars all identify takfir-laundering as the position's signature move and reject it as theologically illegitimate. The position cannot consistently hold both the formal prohibition and the operational practice its takfiri framework licenses.

The "umma under siege" frame

The umma faces an existential Crusader-Zionist threat that justifies emergency derogation from classical limits; bin Laden's 1996/1998 declarations and Baghdadi's 2014 khutba ground the violence in this defensive-jihad frame.

The empirical claim is testable and fails. Muslim-majority populations grew from roughly 880 million in 1980 to 2 billion in 2024; Islam is the world's fastest-growing religion by demographic projection (Pew 2024); Muslim-majority states control significant oil reserves, possess nuclear weapons (Pakistan), hold permanent and rotating UN Security Council seats, and conduct independent foreign policies. The "siege" frame is not a description of the umma's actual position; it is a mobilization narrative requiring constant external-enemy provision to sustain. If the diagnosis is wrong, the emergency derogation built on it has no grounds. The serious advocates know this, which is why the rhetorical work aggregates Muslim-civilian deaths from disparate conflicts (Iraq, Syria, Palestine, Kashmir, Chechnya, Bosnia, Xinjiang) into a unified causal frame, suppresses the local political variables in each, and presents the result as a single ongoing aggression. That move requires exactly the civilizational-bloc framing it accuses the West of imposing.

The "Islamization of radicalism" diagnosis

The position presents itself as a theologically grounded movement acting on textual obligation. Recruitment narratives emphasize religious awakening, study of classical sources, and submission to scholarly authority.

Roy's Jihad and Death (Hurst 2017) compiles the empirical pattern from European jihadist case files: shallow religious knowledge, second-generation alienation, criminal background prior to radicalization, conversion-to-violence trajectory typically under 18 months. The pattern fits "Islamization of radicalism" (the radical disposition precedes and selects the religious frame) better than "radicalization of Islam." Filiu's Apocalypse in Islam (California 2011) and McCants's The ISIS Apocalypse (St. Martin's 2015) document the heavy reliance on millenarian narratives the contemporary scholarly mainstream rejects as marginal. Kepel's competing thesis (the radicalization is genuinely religious; third-generation re-Islamization is real) has empirical support in different sub-populations. The honest reading is that both phenomena exist. But the position cannot defend itself by pointing to religious-vanguard self-understanding when the personnel pattern shows recruits are not drawn from religiously-trained sub-populations and the doctrine has to be packaged for them after the disposition is already in place.

The unfalsifiable-vanguard claim

The mainstream Sunni rejection (al-Azhar, Saudi Senior Scholars, the 2014 Open Letter, the Amman Message 2004) is the establishment's failure to recognize what the vanguard sees correctly; mainstream rejection is therefore evidence of the mainstream's corruption, not of the vanguard's error.

The move is structurally unfalsifiable. Any position that reads all institutional rejection as confirmation of its diagnosis cannot be tested. The serious-advocate version must either accept that scholarly consensus is evidentiary in theology (in which case the takfiri framework loses) or accept that no scholarly consensus is evidentiary (in which case the appeal to Ibn Taymiyya, Qutb, and Azzam becomes selective citation, not tradition-recovery). The position cannot have both: using classical authority where convenient and discarding its contemporary heirs where inconvenient is the unfalsifiable retreat the Open Letter to al-Baghdadi names with section-by-section citations.

The casualty-asymmetry the position cannot account for

The vanguard fights for the umma and accepts that some Muslim civilians will die; the tatarrus (human-shields) doctrine provides the jurisprudential cover.

The numbers are not "some." START Global Terrorism Database, IEP Global Terrorism Index, ACLED, and UN Monitoring Team data converge: between 2001 and 2024, jihadist violence killed roughly 200,000–300,000 people, of whom about 85% were Muslim civilians in Muslim-majority states (Iraq, Syria, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Somalia, Yemen, Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Nigeria). Western-target attacks are a fraction of total jihadist activity. A movement that has killed Muslim civilians on the scale that ISIS and JNIM have cannot ground itself in defense-of-the-umma framing without contradiction; the framing licenses the violence, but the violence refutes the framing. The serious advocates acknowledge the casualty pattern privately and deflect publicly by redefining the targeted Muslims as apostates ex post; that returns to the takfir-laundering move and the unfalsifiable-vanguard claim.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The dirty-hands objection

Liberal democracies have killed civilians on substantial scale: Iraq 2003 (Lancet, IBC, Costs of War estimates between 200,000 and over 1 million civilian deaths), Afghanistan 2001–2021, Vietnam, the colonial project itself. The objection presses: the bright line is drawn against attacks targeting Western civilians and consistently relaxed for attacks producing Muslim ones.

The objection has bite; the honest answer concedes it. Western counter-insurgency in Iraq and Afghanistan killed civilians in volumes that complicate the moral standing of any Western lecture on civilian-immunity. The Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Airwaves, and ACLED have tracked the operational record. The right reply: the bright line has to hold both ways. Western military operations that violate civilian-immunity standards are illegitimate by the same principle that makes ISIS attacks illegitimate. The position does not require clean hands; it requires consistent application, and consistent application will call out attacks the speaker's own state has carried out. The post-2003 Iraq War accounting, the Chilcot Report, and the broader War on Terror retrospectives are partial examples; incomplete, and the position should not pretend the asymmetry-of-accountability is settled.

The Roy-Kepel debate is not closed

Roy's "Islamization of radicalism" explains European second-generation jihadist recruitment as a fundamentally non-religious pathology finding in jihadism an available transcendence-of-the-self. Kepel's competing reading: genuine third-generation re-Islamization in European banlieues producing genuinely religious radicalization, empirically defended in different case studies.

Both phenomena exist; overstating either is the analytical error. This project leans toward Roy because European case files predominantly fit it; the Sahel-region cases (Northern Mali, Burkina Faso, the Lake Chad Basin) show recruits whose trajectories are much closer to Kepel's reading: religious-school formation, traditional-cleric apprenticeship, gradual radicalization through Salafi networks rather than rapid criminal-to-jihadist conversion. The liberal-democratic articulation has to stay analytically open here. Dismissing Kepel's evidence to preserve the Roy framing is a non-rigorous move; the geographic-variance reading also implies that European deradicalization programs and Sahel counter-insurgency programs are not the same problem.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature; it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

The 2015–2017 European jihadist wave and after

Charlie Hebdo (Jan 2015), Bataclan (Nov 2015), Brussels (Mar 2016), Nice (Jul 2016), Berlin Christmas market (Dec 2016), Manchester Arena (May 2017), Las Ramblas (Aug 2017), and dozens of smaller attacks defined the European jihadist period. Most operationally tied to ISIS or AQ networks; most attackers EU-citizen second-generation. Frequency declined sharply 2018–2024 due to ISIS territorial collapse, intelligence cooperation, and prosecutorial response. Sporadic attacks continue (Vienna 2020, Paris Notre-Dame area 2020, Reading UK 2020, Brussels 2023).

The Brotherhood-aligned Islamist current pursues institutional and electoral strategies, distinct from the jihadist one; that is the focus of the May 2025 French Muslim Brotherhood report cited in the hub. Both currents are real; conflating them is the analytical error.

Source: EU Radicalisation Awareness Network factbook
US

9/11, the homeland-attack drought, and post-2014 returnee question

The US has had very few successful Islamist-directed mass-casualty attacks since 9/11: Fort Hood (2009), Boston Marathon (2013), San Bernardino (2015), Orlando Pulse (2016), Manhattan truck (2017) are the principal cases. Numbers are an order of magnitude below European frequencies in the comparable period, attributed to geographic separation, a predominantly first-generation Muslim immigrant population with high-skill selection, intelligence-community resourcing, and the FBI's controversial sting-operation methodology.

Source: Global Terrorism Database (START / U. Maryland)
Japan

Effectively zero domestic Islamist political violence

Japan has had essentially no domestic Islamist political violence (暴力). The 2015 ISIS hostage execution of Kenji Goto and Haruna Yukawa was an attack on Japanese nationals abroad. The 1970s Japanese Red Army cooperated with Palestinian groups, including the 1972 Lod Airport attack; that is the closest historical Japan-Islamist-violence linkage, and the Japanese side was leftist, not Islamist.

Honest finding: very low salience for the standard reasons; small population, immigration regime, geographic distance. Japan participates in counter-terrorism intelligence cooperation but does not experience domestic Islamist attacks.

Source: MOFA counter-terrorism policy

Case study: the ISIS arc 2014–2019 and the European urban-attack wave

The empirical anchor: ISIS's 2014–2019 trajectory. Caliphate proclaimed in Mosul (June 2014); peak control of roughly 110,000 km² across Iraq and Syria with about 10 million people under administration (late 2014 to early 2015); rollback by the Global Coalition 2015–2017; fall of Mosul (July 2017), fall of Raqqa (October 2017), final territorial defeat at Baghuz (March 2019). Around that arc, the European urban-attack wave: Paris 13 November 2015 (130 dead), Brussels 22 March 2016 (32 dead), Nice 14 July 2016 (86 dead), Berlin December 2016, Manchester May 2017, Barcelona August 2017, Vienna 2020. Charlie Hebdo and Samuel Paty are documented separately on Page 20; this case study covers the mass-casualty urban-attack wave and the ISIS organizational arc behind it.

ISIS 2014–2019, the Bataclan/Paris/Brussels/Nice cluster, and the deradicalization question

Iraq/Syria territorial caliphate Jun 2014 – Mar 2019 · European urban-attack wave Jan 2015 – Aug 2017 · ~10,000 EU citizens travelled to ISIS territory; ~30% returned · GW Program on Extremism returnee tracking · ICCT & RAN reintegration data

On 29 June 2014, an ISIS spokesman declared the caliphate from the al-Nuri Mosque in Mosul, with Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi as caliph. At its territorial peak in early 2015, ISIS controlled an area larger than Britain and ruled an estimated 10 million people. January 2015 to August 2017: an interconnected European urban-attack wave killed over 350 people in Paris (Bataclan-Stade de France-cafés 13 Nov 2015), Brussels (airport-Maelbeek 22 Mar 2016), Nice (Promenade des Anglais 14 Jul 2016), Berlin (Breitscheidplatz Christmas market 19 Dec 2016), Manchester (Manchester Arena 22 May 2017), and Barcelona (Las Ramblas 17 Aug 2017). Most attacks were carried out by EU-citizen second-generation Muslim men under 30, with operational links to or directives from Raqqa-based ISIS networks. The 13 November 2015 Paris attack, a coordinated multi-team Kalashnikov and suicide-vest assault on a concert hall, sports stadium, and street-cafés, is the single deadliest jihadist attack on European soil since the 2004 Madrid train bombings. Roy's Jihad and Death (2017) parsed the European case files: shallow religious knowledge, criminal background, second-generation alienation, conversion-to-violence under 18 months. Filiu and McCants traced the millenarian organizational doctrine; Kepel argued the genuinely religious component is real and underestimated. Frequency declined sharply after 2017 due to ISIS territorial collapse, intelligence cooperation, returnee prosecution, and online platform restrictions; the returnee question and the Sahel expansion remain open.

Civilian deaths estimated 9,000–11,000 (Associated Press / Mosul reconstruction office); ISIS uses civilians as human shields, executes those attempting to flee, deploys industrial-scale suicide-VBIED tactics. Western Mosul is destroyed.
  • 22 May 2017 Manchester Arena bombing: Salman Abedi, 22, British-Libyan, detonates a shrapnel-packed suicide vest in the foyer as concert-goers leave an Ariana Grande performance. Kills 22 (mostly teenagers and parents waiting for them, including 8-year-old Saffie-Rose Roussos); wounds 1,017. Abedi had returned from Libya 4 days earlier; his brother Hashem is convicted of conspiracy in 2020 (life with 55-year minimum). The Manchester Arena Inquiry runs 2020–2023 and produces the most comprehensive UK public-inquiry record of a jihadist attack to date.
  • 17 Oct 2017 Fall of Raqqa: SDF and Coalition retake the de-facto ISIS capital after a four-month siege. ~80% of the city is destroyed. Foreign fighters surrender, are killed, or attempt to flee through SDF lines.
  • 23 Mar 2019 Battle of Baghuz: SDF declares territorial defeat of ISIS at the village of Baghuz Fawqani in Deir ez-Zor; final pocket of ISIS-held territory falls. ~70,000 ISIS dependents (mostly women and children) are processed into al-Hol detention camp where ~50,000 remain in 2025 in Kurdish custody. Foreign-fighter spouses and children, including the European-citizen cohort, become the unresolved "returnee" question.
  • 26 Oct 2019 US Special Operations raid in Barisha, Idlib province, kills Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, who detonates a suicide vest. Successor Abu Ibrahim al-Hashimi al-Qurashi is killed in February 2022. ISIS-K (Khorasan) and Sahel franchises expand as the central organization weakens.
  • 2 Nov 2020 / 7 Oct 2023 onwards Vienna attack (4 killed by ISIS-aligned shooter Kujtim Fejzulai); 2023–2024 attack cluster following the 7 October 2023 Hamas attacks on Israel and the Gaza war: Brussels 16 Oct 2023 (2 Swedish nationals killed by Tunisian assailant referencing Quran-burning incidents), Arras teacher Dominique Bernard 13 Oct 2023, Paris RER 4 Dec 2023, Solingen Aug 2024 (3 dead, ISIS claim), Magdeburg Dec 2024. The post-Gaza period sees a measurable uptick from the 2018–2022 baseline, though still well below the 2014–2017 wave.

Sentiment polls: the popular substrate

The Pew 2013 country breakdown of "suicide bombing in defense of Islam can be sometimes/often justified" is the canonical international substrate measure. Arab Barometer Wave VI (2020–21) shows substantial declines from earlier waves; the legitimacy of jihadist groups in Muslim-majority states fell sharply after 2014–2015 ISIS atrocities, particularly in Tunisia, Iraq, and Lebanon. The Pew US-Muslim 2017 and ICM 2016 UK figures provide the diaspora baseline. The 126-scholar Open Letter and the Amman Message 2004 are the institutional-rejection instruments.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The 2014–2019 ISIS arc and the 2015–2017 European urban-attack wave are the empirical anchor for the Salafi-jihadist position's operational content. The position can be stated in scholarly terms (Ibn Taymiyya, Qutb, Azzam, Naji); when implemented by an organization controlling territory, it produced the Yazidi genocide, the slave markets in Raqqa, the destruction of Mosul's old city, and a directed European-attack wave whose victims were teenagers leaving an Ariana Grande concert, concert-goers at the Bataclan, families at a Bastille Day fireworks display. Pew international polling shows the popular-sympathy substrate exists; non-trivial in some MENA jurisdictions, very low in others. The Arab Barometer post-2015 decline is one of the most legible single findings in the literature: where ISIS actually governed, popular support for Islamist political projects collapsed. The 126-scholar Open Letter to al-Baghdadi (September 2014) is the substantive Sunni-mainstream rejection: section by section, citing Quran and hadith, refuting the takfir laundering, the slavery doctrine, the territorial-caliphate claim, and the lone-wolf incitement. The fault line is not "Islam is jihadism" or "Islam has nothing to do with jihadism." It is the Salafi-jihadist current against the constitutional-political settlement, with the entire global Muslim population distributed across that distance; the violent vanguard killing now (and killing primarily Muslim civilians); the Sunni mainstream institutionally rejecting it; Roy, Filiu, McCants, and Kepel parsing the recruitment patterns; the deradicalization programs (Aarhus, RAN, EXIT-Sweden) doing the long-cycle work of getting individual returnees back across the line. The al-Hol camp population, roughly 30,000 children mostly under 12 mostly born to foreign-fighter parents, is the unresolved fifteen-year case.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The decline-of-jihadism question. European jihadist attack frequency is sharply down 2018–2025 vs. 2014–2017. How much is durable (ISIS territorial defeat, intelligence cooperation, prosecution backlog clearing) and how much is cyclical (returnee waves, Sahel expansion, Gaza-conflict spillover risk)?
  • The Brotherhood / jihadist boundary. Is the institutional-Brotherhood path genuinely separate from the jihadist path, or does it function as a recruitment funnel for some sub-population? This project's framing implies the former; some analysts (Vidino partially) argue partial overlap. Phase 2 should weigh the evidence.
  • Sahel as the new center. JNIM (al-Qaeda affiliate) and ISIS-Sahel have grown rapidly; GTI 2024 ranks the Sahel as the world's deadliest region for terrorism. What is the implication for European security through migration and Mediterranean spillover?
  • The Pew suicide-bombing numbers a decade later. Pew has not repeated the World's Muslims survey at scale. What does the most-current country-level data (Arab Barometer waves, IFOP, Policy Exchange) show on this measure? Has it moved?
  • Returnee management. European intelligence services estimate roughly 5,000+ EU-citizen ISIS travelers: about 30% returned, about 20% killed, about 50% remaining in detention or unaccounted. The recidivism rate, prosecution rate, and rehabilitation-program effectiveness are critical Phase-2 questions.
