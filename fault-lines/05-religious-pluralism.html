Fault Line · 05

Religious pluralism: coexistence vs. dhimmi/apostasy frameworks

Whether religious diversity is a settled equality of citizens, or a hierarchy of protected status (Muslims first, "people of the book" tolerated second, others third) with apostasy as a one-way door.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic states treat religious affiliation as a citizen's private matter producing no civic consequence; classical Islamic political theology treated non-Muslims as a tolerated dhimmi class with reduced legal status and special tax (jizya), and treated departure from Islam as either a capital crime or, at minimum, a civil-status revocation. Contemporary political-Islamist movements range from advocating modernized dhimma to advocating equal citizenship to repudiating both — that variation is the subject.

Steelmanned positions

Each side, in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamist articulation

Hierarchical pluralism, with apostasy as the boundary

The honest articulation begins by naming the package the position actually defends. Classical fiqh across the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia recognized a tiered status order: full Muslim citizenship; ahl al-kitab (Jews, Christians, Zoroastrians, sometimes Sabaeans) admitted to dhimmi protection in exchange for jizya and acceptance of the second-tier status (no proselytizing, no construction of new houses of worship taller than mosques, sumptuary marking in the strictest readings, no public consumption of pork or wine, ban on Muslims marrying out except Muslim men marrying kitabi women); polytheists, atheists, and apostates outside the protection altogether. The classical apostasy rule is mainstream across the four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia: an adult sane Muslim man who renounces Islam after due process of repentance is to be executed; the woman is imprisoned until she returns (Hanafi) or executed (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali, Twelver Shia in their majority readings).

The strongest contemporary version concedes that the explicit-tier dhimma frame is not realistically reimplementable in pluralist polities and that diaspora Muslims operate as a minority, not as the protectors of one. It concedes that the apostasy-execution rule is contested by reformists like Abdullah Saeed and Mustafa Akyol on classical-jurisprudential grounds. What it does not concede is the categorical-prohibition core: that public renunciation of Islam, public proselytizing of Muslims by non-Muslims, and the establishment of doctrinally unbounded religious pluralism (where Islam holds the same legal-civic position as Bahá'í, Ahmadiyya, atheism, or paganism) are theologically rejected, that the wider community has a duty of hisbah to discourage them, and that Muslim-majority polities are entitled to keep apostasy and blasphemy provisions as state law against Western pressure. The position presents itself as theological honesty against a Western secular project that demands Muslim institutions ratify the privatization of religion as a matter of universal principle. Its serious advocates — al-Azhar, Yaqeen Institute, the Iranian marja'iyya, the Pakistani Council of Islamic Ideology — articulate it in those exact terms.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Equal citizenship across the religion question, exit included

The liberal-democratic position concedes the historical comparison: dhimma was often more humane than its actual contemporaries (Visigothic Spain expelling Jews, Crusader pogroms, the Holocaust). The argument is not that the West got there first or honestly; it is that "tiered protected status" is structurally inferior to equal citizenship, regardless of which religion writes the tiers, that the West's own Catholic-vs.-Protestant and Catholic-vs.-Jewish hierarchies are the case for the equal-citizenship principle and not against it, and that Article 18 of the UDHR on freedom to change religion is the operative test — a test the Cairo Declaration explicitly fails by qualifying every right with "in accordance with sharia."

The strongest version is unflinching about the contemporary cost. The right to leave Islam, to publicly say one has left, to argue against it, and to live without state or family sanction is the bright line — not because exit is the goal of liberal pluralism but because the ability to exit is what distinguishes voluntary belief from coerced compliance. The defensible claim is that doctrinal positions licensing the death penalty for apostasy, the criminalization of public conversion, the persecution of Bahá'ís and Ahmadis as heretics, or the systematic exclusion of non-Muslims from senior public office are simply wrong, not "different," and that a liberal democracy is entitled to refuse them legal effect within its borders without owing them equal philosophical standing. The argument is made most credibly by ex-Muslims, Iranian Bahá'ís, Pakistani Ahmadis, and Coptic Christians themselves; it does not require Western mediation, and it is dishonest to pretend that the universalist framing has no civilizational asymmetry baked in. The honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry and defend the substantive claim on the merits.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, in the language and on the premises its own proponents actually use, without smoothing. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own internal cross-examination is propaganda for the position, not an honest reading of it.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides — because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the rainbow band is not "both positions are equally defensible," it is "both positions have to clear the same evidentiary bar."

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamist articulation

The "compared to its contemporaries" comparator

Classical dhimma is offered as more humane than its actual contemporaries — Visigothic Spain, the Inquisition, Crusader pogroms — and contemporary Muslim-majority pluralism is implicitly evaluated against the Holocaust ceiling rather than the UDHR floor.

The comparator is doing concealed work. The relevant question is not "was 9th-century dhimma better than 9th-century Christendom?" (often, yes) but "is contemporary tiered status better than contemporary equal citizenship?" The position smuggles a 1,000-year time-shift into the comparison so that classical Andalusia is set against modern Western pluralism and modern Iranian theocracy is set against medieval European pogroms. Pew, the World Values Survey, and the Religious Restrictions Index document the present-day distribution: among the 20 states ranking highest on government restrictions on religion, 14 are Muslim-majority. The selective comparator hides where the doctrine actually sits in 2025.

The Bahá'í problem

The position frames the religious-pluralism question as Muslim-majority states managing Christian and Jewish minorities, where the dhimma category technically applies and the historical record contains real coexistence.

The contemporary stress test is not the ahl al-kitab but the post-Islamic Iranian Bahá'í community — ~300,000 people the regime has classified as apostate-derivative since 1979, denied higher education, denied employment in the public sector, denied legal marriage recognition, with cemeteries bulldozed and over 200 community members executed. The Iranian regime's own published guidance — the 1991 Golpaygani memorandum endorsed by Khamenei — instructs ministries to "block the progress and development" of the community without naming the policy publicly. The Bahá'ís are the case the dhimma framework cannot accommodate because they post-date it doctrinally; they are also the empirical answer to the claim that the framework is benign on its own premises. A pluralism settlement that has no place for the religion the system regards as inadmissibly post-Islamic is not pluralism; it is the management of recognized minorities only.

The unfalsifiable-classical-consensus claim

The death penalty for apostasy rests on hadith material treated as classical ijma' across all four Sunni schools and the Twelver Shia, with reformist counter-readings dismissed as modern revisionism.

Abdullah and Hassan Saeed's Freedom of Religion, Apostasy and Islam (Ashgate 2004) walks systematically through the classical sources to show the rule rests on a small number of weakly transmitted hadith and an over-extension of war-context Quranic verses, that the Hanafi school itself imprisons rather than executes the apostate woman (and historically extended this leniency), and that Sufyan al-Thawri and several early jurists held minority positions against capital punishment for private apostasy. Mahmoud Mohamed Taha's Mecca/Medina distinction was a substantive theological argument; the Sudanese state's response in 1985 was to hang him, which is documentary evidence that the doctrinal "consensus" is enforced, not just observed. Treating the conservative reading as the only legitimate one is an interpretive choice that has to be defended on the merits, not asserted as a textual fact.

The metaphysical-premise dodge

The doctrine is internally coherent on its own metaphysical premises about divine sovereignty, the umma's collective duty to preserve revelation, and apostasy as fitna against the community — premises liberal critique does not share.

True and trivial. Every system is internally coherent on premises that license it. The substantive question is whether the premises are defensible — whether the theological case for treating exit from Islam as ontologically equivalent to treason against the polity (rather than simply a change of belief) survives examination. The position cannot make that case without circular appeal to the same scriptural authority that establishes the conclusion. Coherence-on-premises is the floor of an argument, not its ceiling, and the people on whom the doctrine actually falls — the Iranian Bahá'í child denied a university place, the Pakistani Ahmadi who cannot legally call her mosque a mosque, the Egyptian ex-Muslim whose ID card cannot be amended without a fatwa — are not bound to accept the metaphysical premises that license the harm done to them.

The exit-cost asymmetry

The rule against apostasy applies only to public, public-order-disturbing renunciation, not to private doubt or private exit; the high evidentiary bar (witnesses, due process of repentance) makes prosecution rare in practice; the operative regime is not coercive.

The high bar's existence does not constrain the regimes that actually apply the law. Pew's 2013 conditional question shows that, among Muslims who first endorse sharia as official law, support for the death penalty for leaving Islam runs 88% Egypt, 79% Afghanistan, 76% Pakistan, 62% Palestinian territories — the popular substrate for the legal substrate is real. The 2017 Hashtag Generation/Khaled Abou El Fadl/Mustafa Akyol survey of self-described former Muslims documents the actual exit cost: family disownership, employment loss, custody loss, threats, violence, exile. A doctrinal system that imposes lifelong existential costs on adherents who conclude its premises are wrong, and that provides no internal mechanism for them to opt out without religious or legal sanction, is not "diversity within Islam"; it is an unaddressed coercion problem the system has chosen not to recognize as one. The pulpit answer — "they should reflect more deeply and return" — is a position one can hold; it is not an argument that has met its interlocutor on equal ground.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The recency-and-asymmetry objection

Western states criminalized blasphemy and limited Catholic-Protestant-Jewish pluralism well into the 20th century, and selectively prosecute hate-speech against some religions while leaving others fully exposed. The conservative position can press: if equal-citizenship-across-the-religion-question is universal-and-self-evident, why did the West only get there in the 1970s, and why is the implementation still asymmetric?

Recency is not refutation, but the position should not pretend it isn't there. The honest answer is: we were wrong before — the same way the West was wrong about slavery, about Jewish emancipation, about religious-test acts. The right reply to "why didn't you have this view in 1850?" is "because we had not yet worked out the implications of premises we already held." The asymmetry critique has more bite: the de-facto blasphemy regime around Islam in some European jurisdictions (E.S. v. Austria 2018, the Yale Press redactions of the Jyllands-Posten cartoons in a 2009 academic book) is a real chilling effect that the position should defend against on its merits, not deny. The honest move is to roll the asymmetric protections back, not to extend them, and to defend the current settlement on the merits — not on its provenance.

The "wrong, not different" claim

The strongest liberal version holds that the death-penalty for apostasy and the systematic persecution of Bahá'ís and Ahmadis are wrong, not merely "another tradition." This is a substantive cross-civilizational moral claim with imperial echoes — Western law telling Islamic jurisprudence its core penal provisions are barbaric.

The claim is defensible only if the relevant insiders can make it without Western mediation. They can — Abdullah Saeed and Hassan Saeed working classical-jurisprudentially, Mustafa Akyol's Reopening Muslim Minds, the Marrakesh Declaration of 250 Muslim scholars in 2016, Indonesia's Nahdlatul Ulama "Humanitarian Islam" declaration, Asma Afsaruddin's textual work, and the Iranian Bahá'í community itself articulating from inside what its own situation costs. The argument has to be defended through these voices, not over them. It is dishonest to pretend the universalist framing has no civilizational asymmetry baked in; the honest move is to acknowledge the asymmetry, defend the substantive claim on the merits, and refuse the relativist deflection that would silence ex-Muslim and Bahá'í voices in the name of respecting Muslim-majority cultures.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this whole project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature — it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would themselves consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

Asylum cases for ex-Muslims and apostates

European receiving states grant asylum on the ground of well-founded fear of persecution for apostasy from Islam. UK Home Office, German BAMF, Dutch IND, and French OFPRA all maintain country-of-origin guidance documenting which Muslim-majority states criminalize apostasy or fail to protect apostates from non-state violence (Iran, Pakistan, Afghanistan, Saudi Arabia, Sudan, Somalia, parts of Nigeria). The pluralism fault line is institutionalized in European refugee law: the receiving state recognizes that the sending state's pluralism settlement is incompatible with the receiving state's.

Source: UK Home Office country policy notes
US

USCIRF designations and the apostasy-blasphemy nexus

The US Commission on International Religious Freedom annually designates Countries of Particular Concern, with several Muslim-majority states recurring on the list (Pakistan, Iran, Saudi Arabia). Pakistan's blasphemy law, Iran's apostasy provisions, Saudi Arabia's prohibitions on non-Muslim public worship feature prominently. The framing for the US position is that religious-pluralism conditions in the sending society are a legitimate object of US foreign policy.

Source: USCIRF annual reports
Japan

Religious pluralism via syncretic indifference

Japan's religious-pluralism baseline is unusual: the modal Japanese person is simultaneously Shinto, Buddhist, and culturally indifferent. State-religion separation is constitutional. Christian, Muslim, Buddhist, Shinto, and Tenrikyō communities coexist with low public friction.

The fault line in this set has not surfaced as a domestic question but does appear in immigration adjudication: Japan grants asylum to vanishingly few applicants annually (under 100 in most years), so apostasy and pluralism cases that would be heard in Europe are largely never adjudicated in Japan because the applicants do not arrive.

Source: UNHCR Japan asylum statistics

Case study: Asia Bibi, Iranian Bahá'ís, and the apostasy-blasphemy lock

Two empirical objects on the same axis. The 2009–2018 Asia Bibi case in Pakistan — Christian farm-worker, blasphemy death sentence, two of her defenders assassinated, eventual Supreme Court acquittal, exile to Canada — is the highest-profile contemporary documentation of how the blasphemy-apostasy legal architecture actually behaves under stress. The Iranian Bahá'í community's post-1979 persecution — the largest non-Muslim-Abrahamic religious minority in Iran, classified outside dhimma protection because it post-dates Islam doctrinally — is the case the framework cannot accommodate. Together they show the lock: blasphemy law for the recognized minority, apostasy-derivative classification for the post-Islamic one, and no exit door for the Muslim who concludes her faith was inherited rather than chosen.

Asia Bibi 2009–2018, the Iranian Bahá'í 1979–present, and the apostasy-execution overhang

Pakistan PPC §§ 295-A/B/C and 298 (Ahmadis) · Iran post-1979 Bahá'í policy · Tunisia, Egypt, Sudan apostasy contestation · ~13 jurisdictions retain death penalty for apostasy or blasphemy (USCIRF 2024)

On 14 June 2009, Asia Noreen ("Asia Bibi"), a Pakistani Christian farm-worker in Ittan Wali, Punjab, drew water for her co-workers and was accused by Muslim co-workers of contaminating the well; an argument followed in which she was alleged to have insulted the Prophet. Five days later, a local imam led a mob to her home; she was beaten and a complaint was filed under PPC § 295-C, which prescribes death for derogatory remarks against the Prophet Muhammad. She spent the next 3,427 days — nine years and four months — in custody, eight of those on death row. The two senior officials who publicly defended her were assassinated: Punjab Governor Salmaan Taseer (4 January 2011, by his own bodyguard Mumtaz Qadri, who was hailed as a national hero by tens of thousands at his funeral) and Federal Minister for Minorities Shahbaz Bhatti (2 March 2011). The Supreme Court of Pakistan acquitted her on 31 October 2018; the verdict triggered Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan blockades that paralyzed the country for three days; she was secretly exiled to Canada in May 2019. The Iranian Bahá'í case runs in parallel on a longer arc — outside the dhimma protection from the start, classified as the misguided sect, denied the right to higher education, the right to government employment, the right to legal marriage recognition, with cemeteries bulldozed and over 200 community members executed since 1979.

  • 19 Aug 1955 Pakistan inserts blasphemy provisions into the colonial-era Penal Code (originally drafted by Macaulay 1860). PPC §§ 295-A and 298 (insulting religious feelings) date from the 1860–1927 period; the maximalist provisions are added later under Zia.
  • 21 Aug 1991 Following constitutional amendments under General Zia-ul-Haq (1980–1986), § 295-B (defiling the Quran, life imprisonment) and § 295-C (derogatory remarks against the Prophet, mandatory death) are added. § 298-B and § 298-C target Ahmadis specifically — making it a criminal offence for them to call themselves Muslim, call their houses of worship mosques, or use the standard Islamic greeting. The 1984 Ordinance XX is the foundational anti-Ahmadi instrument.
  • 25 Feb 1991 Iran's Ayatollah Khamenei endorses a confidential memorandum drafted by the Supreme Cultural Revolution Council (the Golpaygani memorandum) instructing all government bodies to "block the progress and development" of the Bahá'í community: deny higher education, deny government employment, deny licenses, "deal with the Bahá'í question in such a way that their progress and development are blocked." The document leaks in 1993 via the UN Special Rapporteur on Iran.
  • 14 June 2009 Asia Bibi (Christian, ~38, mother of five) is accused by Muslim co-workers of insulting the Prophet during a dispute over drinking water. She is later beaten by a mob led by a local imam and arrested under PPC § 295-C. She is convicted and sentenced to death by hanging on 8 November 2010 by a Punjab district court — the first Pakistani Christian woman to receive a § 295-C death sentence.
  • 4 Jan 2011 Salmaan Taseer, Governor of Punjab, who had publicly defended Asia Bibi and called the blasphemy law a "black law" requiring reform, is shot 27 times at point-blank range in Islamabad by his own elite-force bodyguard Mumtaz Qadri. Qadri immediately surrenders. At his sentencing, lawyers shower him with rose petals. After his execution in February 2016, hundreds of thousands attend his funeral; a shrine is later built at his grave that becomes a pilgrimage site.
  • 2 Mar 2011 Shahbaz Bhatti, Federal Minister for Minorities (a Christian), the only Christian in the federal cabinet, who had also defended Asia Bibi and worked on blasphemy-law reform, is shot dead outside his mother's house in Islamabad. Tehreek-e-Taliban Pakistan claims responsibility. He had recorded a video four months earlier saying he expected to be killed and would not stop.
  • 13 Apr 2017 Mashal Khan, a 23-year-old journalism student at Abdul Wali Khan University in Mardan, is beaten and shot to death by fellow students in his dormitory after being accused of online blasphemy. The accusation is later found to have no evidentiary basis. The lynching is filmed; 57 are charged.
  • 31 Oct 2018 The Supreme Court of Pakistan, three-judge bench (Chief Justice Saqib Nisar, Asif Saeed Khosa, Mazhar Alam Khan Miankhel), acquits Asia Bibi unanimously, finding "glaring contradictions" in the prosecution evidence. The verdict cites the Quran ("There shall be no compulsion in religion") and notes the witnesses' material inconsistencies. The court takes the unusual step of placing her under state protection. Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan blockades major cities for three days, demanding she be hanged anyway.
  • 3 Dec 2021 Priyantha Kumara, a Sri Lankan Buddhist factory manager working in Sialkot, Pakistan, is beaten to death and his body burned by his own factory's workers after being accused of removing a poster bearing Quranic verses (he was clearing a wall for repainting). The killing is filmed and widely shared. Six are sentenced to death and over 80 to prison terms.
  • 2017–2024 Iranian authorities arrest at least 1,025 Bahá'ís in waves of detention (Bahá'í International Community 2024 figures), including the entire seven-member Yaran ("Friends") informal leadership group serving 10-year sentences 2008–2018; in 2022 several elderly women in Shiraz are sentenced to 10 and 11 years specifically for "membership of the Bahá'í organisation." Bulldozing of Bahá'í cemeteries (Tehran's Khavaran cemetery 2014–2024, Najafabad 2007) is documented by satellite imagery analyzed by USCIRF and BIC.
  • 7 May 2025 The European Parliament adopts a resolution condemning the continuing persecution of the Bahá'í community in Iran and the recent wave of arrests of Christian converts; UN Special Rapporteur Javaid Rehman's reporting through 2024 documents the systematic-policy character of the persecution. Pakistan's blasphemy-law prosecutions reach record levels (the Centre for Social Justice tracks 247 cases registered in 2023, the highest annual figure since the law was amended).

Sentiment polls: the popular substrate

The legal architecture rests on popular substrate. The Pew 2013 conditional question (death for apostasy among those who first endorse sharia) shows the support rate runs 86–88% across the South Asian and MENA Sunni heartland and falls below 20% in Indonesia, Tunisia, Lebanon, and Bosnia. The ICM 2016 UK survey shows substantial European-diaspora variation. Generational shifts in US data are real. The Iranian Bahá'í case is not a popular-opinion story — it is a state-policy story — but the broader pattern follows the same axis.

What the cluster shows about this fault line. The Pakistani blasphemy regime and the Iranian Bahá'í policy are different mechanisms producing the same outcome: religious-pluralism settlements that hold only for the categories the doctrine recognizes, with apostasy and blasphemy provisions as the boundary fence. Asia Bibi survived because the Pakistani Supreme Court, in a moment of judicial courage, applied the law against the mob — and her two senior defenders did not survive because the same legal regime that the court reformed in her case is the regime that licensed her detention for nine years and the killers of Taseer and Bhatti as religious heroes. The Iranian Bahá'í community survives at all because of remarkable internal resilience — BIHE running an underground university through home-tutoring chains, the seven Yaran serving 10-year sentences without renouncing — but the Iranian state's policy is documentary: the framework cannot accommodate them and is not designed to. The fault line is not "Islam vs. the West" — it is the conservative-jurisprudential settlement that uses apostasy and blasphemy provisions as gate-keeping vs. the equal-citizenship settlement that does not, with the entire global Muslim population distributed across that distance, the maximalist-state regimes killing and detaining people now, and the reformist current — Saeed, Akyol, the Marrakesh signatories, Nahdlatul Ulama, the Iranian Bahá'í educators — arguing inside the tradition that the consensus claim is overstated.

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Abdullah Saeed (Australian, University of Melbourne) — Freedom of Religion, Apostasy and Islam (Ashgate 2004, with Hassan Saeed); the most rigorous classical-jurisprudential intra-Muslim refutation of the apostasy-execution doctrine. · Mustafa Akyol (Turkish, Cato Institute) — Reopening Muslim Minds: A Return to Reason, Freedom, and Tolerance (St. Martin's 2021); systematic case for liberty of conscience in Islamic theology, drawing on Mu'tazilite and Maturidi sources. · Khaled Abou El Fadl (Egyptian-American, UCLA) — The Great Theft: Wrestling Islam from the Extremists (HarperOne 2005); insider critique of how puritan readings displaced the classical pluralist tradition. · Asma Afsaruddin (Indiana University) — The First Muslims (Oneworld 2008); historian arguing that classical sources contain robust pluralist materials suppressed by political Islamism. · Mahmoud Mohamed Taha (Sudanese, executed 1985 for apostasy at age 76) — The Second Message of Islam; distinguished Mecca-period Qur'an (universalist, voluntary) from Medina-period (particularist, coercive); his execution by Nimeiri remains the costliest articulated reformist position in modern Sunni Islam. · Mahmoud Mohamed Taha's student An-Na'im (Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na'im, Emory) — Toward an Islamic Reformation (Syracuse 1990) carrying his teacher's argument forward. · Indonesian Nahdlatul Ulama — world's largest Muslim civil-society organization (~90 million members); 2019 "Nusantara Manifesto" / "Declaration on Humanitarian Islam" formally repudiating dhimma, caliphal-restoration, and apostasy-execution doctrines. · Maajid Nawaz (British-Pakistani) — defended Asia Bibi publicly in 2010s; complicated subsequent trajectory but the early-period Quilliam interventions on apostasy are documentary. · The Iranian Bahá'í community itself — Yaran-i-Iran ("Friends of Iran") leadership; Bahá'í Institute for Higher Education (BIHE) educators; the open letters of the Mahvash Sabet poetry written from Evin Prison.

Centre for Social Justice (Lahore), annual tracking of PPC §§ 295-A/B/C and 298 prosecutions; USCIRF, Pakistan: Apostasy, Blasphemy, and Hate Speech Laws factsheet, 2020 (and 2024 update).
Method: CSJ tracks every public blasphemy case registered in Pakistan. The 2023 figure of 247 registered cases is the highest annual figure since the law was last amended; the dataset includes accused, district, religion, and outcome. USCIRF factsheet provides the legislative-history view.
Caveat: CSJ figures track registered cases, not all of which proceed to trial; the conviction rate is low at the trial level but high in summary proceedings. Mob-violence killings (Mashal Khan 2017, Priyantha Kumara 2021, the 2023 Jaranwala church burnings) operate in parallel to the formal-prosecution track.

Iranian Bahá'ís · primary documentation
Bahá'í International Community, The Bahá'ís of Iran: A Documentary Account (continuing); Golpaygani Memorandum (Iranian Supreme Cultural Revolution Council, 25 February 1991, endorsed by Khamenei) — released by UN Special Rapporteur Reynaldo Galindo Pohl, 1993.
Method: BIC maintains case-level tracking of arrests, sentences, property confiscations, denial of education and employment, and cemetery destruction. The Golpaygani memorandum is the smoking-gun policy document — Iranian state policy is to "block the progress and development" of the community while denying public persecution. Cited extensively in UN Special Rapporteur reporting on Iran from Galindo Pohl through Asma Jahangir, Javaid Rehman, and Mai Sato.

Survey · UK
ICM Unlimited / Channel 4, What British Muslims Really Think, April 2016 (presented by Trevor Phillips).
Method: 1,081 face-to-face interviews with self-identifying British Muslims. The dual-loyalty (35%), sharia-zone preference (23%), and obey-husband (39%) findings are the British-Muslim equivalent of the Pew international baseline; the integration findings (86% strong sense of belonging) are the counter-data points.
Caveat: ICM's 2016 sampling was contested at publication — critics argued the screen for "areas with 20%+ Muslim population" over-represented religiously engaged respondents. ICM defended the method publicly. Subsequent UK surveys have broadly confirmed the directional findings.

Survey · Arab Barometer
Arab Barometer (Princeton), Wave VI, 2020–2021 — religiosity and trust-in-religious-leaders modules; Wave VII (2021–2022).
Method: 26,000+ face-to-face interviews across 12 MENA countries. Trust-in-religious-leaders has fallen across most of MENA between 2013 and 2020; "not religious" identification has risen sharply in Tunisia, Lebanon, and Iraq. The shift is not pro-Western — it is intra-MENA generational disaffiliation.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Indonesian Pancasila pluralism on its own terms. NU's Humanitarian Islam declaration is the most ambitious contemporary intra-Muslim repudiation of dhimma. What is its actual institutional reach inside Indonesia, and is it eroding under Salafi pressure or holding?
  • The Cordoba/Ottoman counter-evidence honestly weighed. Phase 2 should engage the strongest version of the historical-tolerance argument — Mark Cohen, Maria Rosa Menocal — without dismissing it and without overstating it.
  • Persecution of Ahmadis. Pakistani anti-Ahmadi laws (Ordinance XX, 1984) are the clearest contemporary case of Sunni-majority states applying dhimma-like restrictions to a Muslim minority deemed heretical. The case is important precisely because it is intra-Muslim — refutes the framing that the pluralism question is a Muslim-vs.-non-Muslim issue.
  • European Muslim attitudes toward apostasy. Pew's data is on origin-country Muslims; what do European-resident Muslim populations show? Some IFOP and Policy Exchange numbers exist; Phase 2 should triangulate.
  • The asylum-recognition data. What share of asylum claims by ex-Muslims are recognized, in which European countries, on what evidentiary standards? This is a real, available dataset that would put quantitative texture under the qualitative claim.
