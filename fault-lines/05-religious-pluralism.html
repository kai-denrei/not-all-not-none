Fault Line · 05

Religious pluralism: coexistence vs. dhimmi/apostasy frameworks

Whether religious diversity is a settled equality of citizens, or a hierarchy of protected status (Muslims first, "people of the book" tolerated second, others third) with apostasy as a one-way door.

scaffold · sources gatheringPhase 1

The fault line, in one sentence

Liberal-democratic states treat religious affiliation as a citizen's private matter producing no civic consequence; classical Islamic political theology treated non-Muslims as a tolerated dhimmi class with reduced legal status and special tax (jizya), and treated departure from Islam as either a capital crime or, at minimum, a civil-status revocation. Contemporary political-Islamist movements range from advocating modernized dhimma to advocating equal citizenship to repudiating both — that variation is the subject.

Steelmanned positions

Extremist-Islamist articulation

Dhimma was tolerance, not persecution

The strongest defense of the classical dhimma frame argues that Christian, Jewish, and Zoroastrian communities under Muslim rule retained internal autonomy — their own family law, their own courts, their own clergy — at a time when European Christendom was expelling Jews entirely and burning heretics. Cordoba's Jewish golden age, the long Christian presence under the Ottomans, and the survival of Coptic, Maronite, and Assyrian communities through fourteen centuries of Muslim rule are the documentary evidence.

The strongest version concedes that jizya was a tax non-Muslims paid and Muslims did not, that conversion was a one-way door, and that periods of intense persecution did occur. What it argues is that comparing dhimma to modern equal citizenship is anachronistic, and that compared to its actual contemporaries (Inquisition, Crusader pogroms, the Holocaust) the system was the more humane available settlement. The argument is for retrieving the principle of plural protected status, not literal jizya.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Tiered citizenship is incompatible with citizenship

The liberal-democratic position concedes the historical comparison: dhimma was indeed often more humane than European Christian rule of the same period. The argument is not that the West was always better; the argument is that "tiered protected status" is structurally inferior to equal citizenship, regardless of which religion writes the tiers, and the West's own Catholic-vs.-Protestant and Catholic-vs.-Jewish hierarchies are the case for the principle, not against it.

The strongest version is uncomfortable with the contemporary record on its own side — discrimination against Muslims in some Western settings is real — but distinguishes between informal social discrimination (everywhere) and formal legal hierarchy (which Western states have officially repudiated and Islamist projects propose to re-introduce). The non-negotiable is not Christian-vs.-Muslim equality, it is the principle that no one's civic standing depends on their answer to the religion question.

The friction in The West

EU · multiple

Asylum cases for ex-Muslims and apostates

European receiving states grant asylum on the ground of well-founded fear of persecution for apostasy from Islam. UK Home Office, German BAMF, Dutch IND, and French OFPRA all maintain country-of-origin guidance documenting which Muslim-majority states criminalize apostasy or fail to protect apostates from non-state violence (Iran, Pakistan, Afghanistan, Saudi Arabia, Sudan, Somalia, parts of Nigeria). The pluralism fault line is institutionalized in European refugee law: the receiving state recognizes that the sending state's pluralism settlement is incompatible with the receiving state's.

Source: UK Home Office country policy notes
US

USCIRF designations and the apostasy-blasphemy nexus

The US Commission on International Religious Freedom annually designates Countries of Particular Concern, with several Muslim-majority states recurring on the list (Pakistan, Iran, Saudi Arabia). Pakistan's blasphemy law, Iran's apostasy provisions, Saudi Arabia's prohibitions on non-Muslim public worship feature prominently. The framing for the US position is that religious-pluralism conditions in the sending society are a legitimate object of US foreign policy.

Source: USCIRF annual reports
Japan

Religious pluralism via syncretic indifference

Japan's religious-pluralism baseline is unusual: the modal Japanese person is simultaneously Shinto, Buddhist, and culturally indifferent. State-religion separation is constitutional. Christian, Muslim, Buddhist, Shinto, and Tenrikyō communities coexist with low public friction.

The fault line in this set has not surfaced as a domestic question but does appear in immigration adjudication: Japan grants asylum to vanishingly few applicants annually (under 100 in most years), so apostasy and pluralism cases that would be heard in Europe are largely never adjudicated in Japan because the applicants do not arrive.

Source: UNHCR Japan asylum statistics

Inside critics & reformers worth naming

Yusuf al-Qaradawi (deceased 2022) — controversial figure who nonetheless argued for reform of the apostasy rule (death only for "treasonous" public apostasy, not private exit), illustrating that even within mainstream-Brotherhood-aligned scholarship the death-for-apostasy rule has internal contestation. Cited carefully — his other positions are illiberal in the directions the rest of this set documents. · Abdullah Saeed (Australian, Melbourne) — Freedom of Religion, Apostasy and Islam (2004): systematic intra-Muslim case against apostasy laws on classical-jurisprudential grounds. · Tariq Ramadan (early work) — argued for replacing dhimma frame with equal-citizenship frame for contemporary Muslim-minority and Muslim-majority polities. · Nadia Marzouki (Tunisian, Sciences Po) — sociologist of contemporary North African and European debates on Muslim-Christian-secular pluralism. · Asma Afsaruddin (Indiana) — historian arguing that classical sources contain robust pluralist materials suppressed by political Islamism. · Indonesian Nahdlatul Ulama — the world's largest Muslim civil society organization, has formally repudiated the dhimma frame in its 2019 "Declaration on Humanitarian Islam," advocating equal citizenship in pluralist polities.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • Indonesian Pancasila pluralism on its own terms. NU's Humanitarian Islam declaration is the most ambitious contemporary intra-Muslim repudiation of dhimma. What is its actual institutional reach inside Indonesia, and is it eroding under Salafi pressure or holding?
  • The Cordoba/Ottoman counter-evidence honestly weighed. Phase 2 should engage the strongest version of the historical-tolerance argument — Mark Cohen, Maria Rosa Menocal — without dismissing it and without overstating it.
  • Persecution of Ahmadis. Pakistani anti-Ahmadi laws (Ordinance XX, 1984) are the clearest contemporary case of Sunni-majority states applying dhimma-like restrictions to a Muslim minority deemed heretical. The case is important precisely because it is intra-Muslim — refutes the framing that the pluralism question is a Muslim-vs.-non-Muslim issue.
  • European Muslim attitudes toward apostasy. Pew's data is on origin-country Muslims; what do European-resident Muslim populations show? Some IFOP and Policy Exchange numbers exist; Phase 2 should triangulate.
  • The asylum-recognition data. What share of asylum claims by ex-Muslims are recognized, in which European countries, on what evidentiary standards? This is a real, available dataset that would put quantitative texture under the qualitative claim.
