Hygiene and ritual purity / 衛生と儀礼的清浄: wudu, foot-washing stations, water-vs-paper, female-only swim hours

Islamic ritual purity (清浄 / taharah) requires wudu before prayer, water-based bathroom hygiene, and — in much Sunni jurisprudence — avoidance of dog saliva. Western public infrastructure assumes dry-paper hygiene, mixed-gender pools, service-dog defaults, and no ablution facilities. Friction is mostly low-temperature, concentrated at foot-washing retrofits, female-only pool hours, and service-dog interactions.

canonical · Phase 1Phase 1
Islamic ritual purity (浄不浄 / taharah) requires specific ablution (wudu, ghusl), treats dog saliva as ritually impure in most Sunni schools, and supports gender separation in some ritual and social contexts; Western public infrastructure assumes mixed-gender facilities, normalizes service-dog access, and treats hygiene as personal preference. The friction is operational and mostly low-temperature, sharpest around police-dog interactions and gender-segregated public-space requests.

Steelmanned positions

Each side in the strongest form its own proponents would actually defend in writing. No euphemism, no smoothing.

Conservative-Islamic articulation

Wudu is the entry gate to prayer; infrastructure should accommodate

Wudu (rinsing mouth and nose, washing face, arms to elbows, wiping head, feet to ankles) is Quranic in basis (5:6) and consensus across all four Sunni schools and Twelver Shia as the binding precondition (shart) for valid prayer. Takes 2–4 minutes; the infrastructure is access to flowing water and the ability to wash feet, which is awkward at a standard porcelain sink. Ghusl (full-body washing after intercourse, menstruation completion, post-natal bleeding) is private and irrelevant to public-infrastructure questions. Istinja (water-based cleaning after toilet use) is preferred to dry paper alone in classical jurisprudence; a bidet, lota, or hand-held sprayer satisfies it.

The strongest version reframes the political question. Foot-washing stations at airports (Indianapolis 2007), universities (Michigan-Dearborn 2008), and workplaces are not "Sharia accommodation"; they are the same infrastructure adjustment Western public buildings have always made for the dominant religious population — Catholic confessionals in Boston General Hospital, kosher kitchens at Brandeis, Sabbath elevators in Manhattan. The harder cases — female-only pool hours (Berlin, Bremerhaven, Sweden), gender-segregated bathing, service-dog refusals — are negotiable case-by-case on the same reasonable-accommodation principle that covers Sikh kirpans in school, Catholic-Lenten cafeteria menus, and Jewish-Sabbath workplace adjustments. Not categorically problematic; natural friction in multi-religious public infrastructure, handled through dialogue.

Liberal-democratic articulation

Wudu yes, gender-segregated public space no

The liberal position is largely sympathetic on operational accommodations: wudu sinks at workplaces and airports, prayer rooms at universities, bidet or sprayer installations, bathroom-stall privacy for menstrual hygiene. Low-friction adjustments comparable to wheelchair ramps and lactation rooms. The position draws three lines. First, at gender-segregated access to shared public infrastructure (women-only pool hours, gender-segregated university seating, refusal of treatment by a professional of the "wrong" gender), where the religious-accommodation argument collides with equal public-space access. Second, at refusal to interact with service dogs in a professional capacity, where disability-rights protection is not negotiable. Third, at modesty arguments that transfer female-bodily-management from the individual woman to family or community authority.

The strongest version concedes the lines are contested at the margins (a pool hour is not the same as gender-segregated medical care; a women-only swim class is not the same as a women-only pool day) and acknowledges Western analogues (women's-only fitness clubs, sex-segregated dormitories, sex-segregated bathrooms). The defensible settlement: reasonable accommodation in private and ritual contexts, mixed-gender access as the public-infrastructure baseline with narrow time-limited exceptions that don't function as exclusion, full disability-rights protection of service animals, and the same standard applied across traditions. Ultra-Orthodox Jewish gender-segregation requests have met similar pushback in Israel and parts of New York; the principle has to apply consistently, not only to Muslim cases.

Method note. The two cards above present each position in its strongest defensible form, on the premises its own proponents actually use. The next section stress-tests both. A steelman that cannot survive its own cross-examination is advocacy, not analysis.

Where the argument strains

A steelman is not a verdict. The arguments above are presented uncensored; below they are stress-tested. Both sides, because the rainbow band here is wide: most operational accommodation is uncontroversial, and the friction concentrates at the foot-washing-station, female-only-pool, and gender-segregated-bathing edges.

Cross-examination

The arguments named above, tested against the standards a serious interlocutor would actually apply.

Testing the conservative-Islamic articulation

The "comparative-religion parity" claim and the actual installation pattern

The position frames foot-washing stations and prayer rooms as the same accommodation Western public infrastructure has always made for the dominant religious population: chapel installations, kosher kitchens, Sabbath elevators.

The framing holds for the accommodations themselves. What it ignores is the asymmetric political reception. Indianapolis Airport's 2007 foot-washing installation (~$23,000, two units serving ~3,000 daily Muslim travelers and taxi drivers) attracted national-media controversy, Indiana state-legislature hearings, and a church-state lawsuit threat that never materialized. Comparable Catholic-chapel installations at the same airport had been there since 1965 without comment. The accommodation is structurally identical; the controversy is asymmetric and is about Muslim visibility, not wudu sinks. The position can hold this is the West's problem, not Islam's; it cannot present "we want infrastructure parity" as if that parity were already operative when each new installation requires sustained political defense.

The "ritual purity is private" claim and the female-only-pool request

Female-only public-pool hours are reasonable accommodation comparable to women's-only fitness clubs; municipalities can negotiate hour-allocation as a routine equity matter.

The argument has substance — many German, Swedish, and UK municipalities have settled it by allocating limited female-only hours without excluding mixed-gender access to the broader pool schedule. What the position has to confront is the operational record where the request has been pressed into exclusion territory. Berlin's Neukölln pool 2018, Bremerhaven 2019, and the Sweden public-pool disputes have repeatedly involved escalating requests: from one women-only swimming class per week, to women-only days, to men barred from the locker-room corridor, to female-only lifeguard rotations. Aasim Padela's Medicine and Shariah framework is clear that operational necessity overrides modesty-preference; that flexibility has not always reached the actual community-level requests. The position can defend the modest version but cannot defend it without acknowledging the maximalist version has been pressed in specific documented cases and produces rather than resolves equity friction.

The dog-saliva ruling and the historical disagreement

Dog saliva is ritually impure, requiring vessel-washing seven times (one with earth) after dog contact; Muslims should generally avoid keeping dogs. Offered as a settled four-schools position.

The ruling is real; the "settled four-schools" framing overstates the classical record. Khaled Abou El Fadl's work in The Search for Beauty in Islam (Rowman & Littlefield, 2006) and Reasoning with God (Rowman & Littlefield, 2014) documents that the Maliki school in classical Andalusia held the only impure part is the saliva; the dog itself is pure (tahir); and the seven-washings rule applies only to the contact-vessel, not to the dog. Contemporary Salafi-Hanbali readings have flattened that classical pluralism into "Muslims do not interact with dogs," which is not the historical mainstream. The position can defend the most-conservative reading on its own merits; it cannot present it as the only Islamically-available reading. The Maliki tradition, several Sufi orders, and contemporary scholars including Abou El Fadl and Padela have made the accommodation case. This is one defensible reading among several inside the tradition.

The honour-modesty frame and the responsibility-transfer problem

Female-only pool hours, gender-segregated bathing, and modesty-driven swimwear are protective of Muslim women, letting them participate in public-pool culture they would otherwise be excluded from.

The "protective" framing has a structural problem. The same frame justifies, in adjacent jurisdictions, the burqini at French municipal pools (page 17), the female-only section at gender-segregated weddings, the women's-only entrance at conservative mosques, and ultimately the claim that women's bodies in public space require special management that men's do not. Kecia Ali's Sexual Ethics and Islam (Oneworld, 2016) and Mona Eltahawy's work both argue that the modesty frame transfers responsibility for male sexual self-management onto female bodies and presents the transfer as a benefit. The position can hold modesty-as-religious-virtue without accepting modesty-as-state-or-community-managed-responsibility; the operative request often does not respect that distinction. The honest version names that the protection-of-Muslim-women frame can be genuinely useful (when the woman herself prefers segregated bathing) and structurally problematic (when it constrains women who would prefer mixed bathing).

The exit cost from observance

The position offers no provision for a Muslim woman who does not personally hold the modesty-segregation requirement, does not perform wudu, does not avoid dog contact, but is in a community that maintains these as social-religious norms.

The accommodation framework assumes the observance is genuinely held. Smaller in scale than its parallels on pages 04 (women) and 21 (LGBT Muslims), but real: community-level wudu installations, gender-segregated facility hours, and dog-avoidance norms make non-observance more visible. Yasmine Mohammed and the Council of Ex-Muslims of Britain have documented Muslim women under family pressure to maintain segregated-bathing observance after they no longer privately accept it. The position can call this a community-internal problem; it cannot pretend the visible-observance / visible-non-observance asymmetry does not exist.

Testing the liberal-democratic articulation

The "narrow exceptions" that don't survive operational pressure

The liberal lines (no gender-segregated public space, no female-only public-pool days, full service-dog access) are narrow, principled, applicable across religions, defensible on equality grounds.

The principles are clean; the practice is less so. Western public swimming pools have long run single-sex hours, swim-class periods, and school-PE-period gender separation without triggering the religious-modesty or equality arguments. Women's-only fitness clubs (Curves, Pure Gym Women) operate as private commercial spaces without challenge. The "no gender segregation in shared public space" line has been applied more aggressively to Muslim-modesty requests than to comparable secular or Christian requests for the same arrangement. UK Equality and Human Rights Commission 2014 guidance on higher-education gender-segregation was applied consistently; the public-pool-hours question has been less consistent. The position would hold better if it acknowledged that the line is drawn at variable thresholds with implicit cultural-acceptability filters, and explained why women's-only commercial fitness clubs are acceptable and women's-only public-pool hours are not.

The "she's not really choosing" claim and the autonomy paradox in pool hours

A subset of the liberal position holds that Muslim women requesting female-only pool hours are not "really" exercising autonomy, that the request is family- or community-imposed, and the Western state should not enable a paternalist arrangement that mainstreams male control of female bathing.

This is the autonomy paradox: "we will free Muslim women from male-imposed modesty by refusing the bathing arrangement they say they want." Not defensible on liberal premises. Either the woman is a competent adult whose stated preferences deserve respect, or she is not. If she is not, the state has substituted one paternalist authority (her family, her imam) for another (the equality officer at the municipal pool) without solving the problem. The position that survives liberal premises is narrower: facilitate access for women who want mixed-gender bathing, facilitate access for women who want female-only bathing where it can be arranged without exclusion, and treat the choice as the woman's. Asma Lamrabet, Mona Eltahawy (in her hijab-wearing phase), and the substantial body of pro-segregated-bathing Muslim-feminist writing argue that female-only-hour bathing can be a freely-chosen feminist practice. The liberal position has to take that seriously rather than dismiss it as managed false consciousness, even where skepticism about family-pressure cases is also warranted.

The cross-examination above is the methodological commitment of this project: steelman, then expose. A position whose strongest form cannot withstand its own implications has not been refuted by hostile caricature; it has been refuted by honest engagement, which is what its serious advocates would consider the only refutation worth answering.

The friction in The West

EU · UK / Germany

Police service-dog interactions, gender-segregated swimming, and the wudu-installation question

UK and German police use service dogs routinely. A 2017 UK case involving a Pakistani-British resident refusing to interact with a search dog produced operational guidance on alternative search procedures where feasible. Gender-segregated pool hours have been negotiated case-by-case in UK municipalities (Tower Hamlets, Bradford, parts of Birmingham) and some German cities (Cologne, parts of Berlin); the 2018 Cologne case was the highest-profile.

Wudu installation at workplaces, universities, and airports is now standard across much of Western Europe: low-controversy, low-cost.

US

ADA service-animal protections and the religious-accommodation interaction

US ADA service-animal protections are categorical: a service animal cannot be excluded from public accommodations regardless of religious objection from other users (28 CFR § 36.302). Religious accommodation in private settings is permissible; in public-accommodations contexts the ADA controls. Wudu installation in public buildings is increasingly common; gender-segregated pool hours have been negotiated at municipal level in some communities.

Japan

Low salience, mostly accommodative practice

Japan's communal-bath, gender-separated-bathing tradition makes some Islamic gender-separation requests easier to accommodate than in mixed-bathing-default European contexts. Service dogs are less ubiquitous than in the US; the friction case has not surfaced significantly. Wudu-friendly facilities (清潔な洗浄設備) have appeared at major airports, university campuses, and Muslim-friendly hotels through commercial accommodation rather than legal mandate.

Case study: the foot-washing-station retrofit arc, 2007–2024

The clearest US-and-Europe operational record runs from Indianapolis Airport's August 2007 installation through the rolling US-university and German-workplace retrofit pattern, with the Berlin / Bremerhaven pool controversies as the parallel European friction case. Together they show what the conservative side calls infrastructure parity and what its critics call asymmetric political reception.

The 2007–2024 retrofit arc supports the substantive parity claim and complicates the political-reception one. Indianapolis Airport's installation was small (~$23,000, two units) but attracted sustained national-media controversy and Indiana state-legislature hearings; Catholic-chapel installations at the same airport had been there since 1965 without comment.   • Aug 2007 Indianapolis International Airport installs two foot-washing stations in restrooms near the cab driver lounge, primarily for the airport's Somali Muslim taxi-driver workforce. Cost ~$23,000. The Indianapolis Star and conservative outlets cover it as "taxpayer-funded Sharia accommodation"; the airport defends on hygiene-and-safety grounds (employees washing feet in regular sinks were producing slip-and-fall injury risk). The American Center for Law and Justice files a public-records request; the church-state lawsuit threat does not materialize. The installation becomes the US national-media template for the question.
  • 2008 University of Michigan-Dearborn installs four foot-washing stations at ~$25,000, citing the same hygiene-and-safety rationale. The Mackinac Center for Public Policy challenges on Establishment Clause grounds; the university defends successfully on the same ground. Challenge dies on the merits; installation persists.
  • 2010–2018 Rolling US-university retrofits with substantially reduced controversy as precedent stabilizes. Boston University, Stanford, George Washington, NYU, University of Toronto, University of Pennsylvania and dozens of others retrofit existing restrooms or include wudu-compatible fixtures in new construction. CAIR maintains an informal list. Standard cost has dropped to ~$3,000–$8,000 per installation.
  • 2014 Boston University's Marsh Chapel renovation includes wudu-compatible washing stations alongside existing Christian-chapel infrastructure. The chapel had served Catholic, Protestant, and Jewish students for decades; the Muslim addition produces minimal media controversy.
  • 2010s German Bundesarbeitsgericht (Federal Labour Court) handles a series of workplace ablution-accommodation cases; the rulings establish that employers must provide reasonable wudu-compatible facilities where adaptation cost is proportionate and operational impact is low. Operates under EU 2000/78/EC and applies equally to Muslim, Jewish, and Christian observance.
  • 31 Dec 2015 Cologne New Year's Eve mass-sexual-harassment events (page 04 covers in detail) produce a sustained European debate about gender segregation in public infrastructure that shapes subsequent female-only-pool hour discussions. The connection is more political-discursive than directly causal.
  • 2018 Berlin Neukölln pool introduces female-only swim hours twice per week, open to all women (Muslim, Jewish, secular, all backgrounds). Local CDU politicians criticize the change as Islamic segregation; Greens and SPD defend on equal-access grounds. The arrangement persists. This is the modest version of the request, settled successfully.
  • 2019 Bremerhaven facility-closure debate: an indoor pool is briefly closed after escalating requests for women-only-day extensions, men barred from locker-room corridors, and female-only lifeguard rotations. The pool reopens with a modest female-only-hour arrangement (limited slots, no exclusion) after municipal-council mediation. This is the maximalist version pressed beyond the operational jurisprudence and rolled back.
  • 2010s–2020s Sweden public-pool gender-hour disputes in Malmö, Gothenburg, and Stockholm follow the German pattern: most municipal pools settle on limited female-only hours that don't function as exclusion; friction concentrates where requests escalate beyond what the European Council for Fatwa and Research and Aasim Padela's work would themselves license.
  • 2017 The 2013–2014 UK Universities UK guidance permitting voluntary gender-segregated seating at student-society events is formally rescinded after EHRC 2014 intervention. The settled UK higher-education baseline is mixed-gender access; wudu-and-prayer-room infrastructure is unaffected. Gender-segregated public space is settled in the UK negative for higher-education seating; female-only swimming hours at municipal pools remain administered case-by-case.
  • 2019–2024 Wudu-friendly facilities at major airports — Heathrow, Frankfurt, Amsterdam Schiphol, Dubai (built-in), Doha (built-in), Kuala Lumpur, Tokyo Haneda's prayer rooms — become standard at large international airports, on the same operational footing as Christian-chapel installations and kosher-meal availability.

What the arc shows. The 2007–2024 retrofit pattern is the cleanest single record of operationally successful Western Muslim accommodation: over 50 US universities with installed foot-washing stations, every major Western international airport with prayer-room infrastructure, German Federal Labour Court doctrine permitting reasonable workplace adaptation, and Boston University-style integration of wudu fixtures alongside existing Christian-chapel infrastructure. It also documents the asymmetric political reception: Indianapolis Airport's 2007 installation faced state-legislature hearings while Catholic-chapel installations at the same airport had been there since 1965 without comment. The European pool arc is the parallel friction case: modest-version requests (limited hours, no infrastructure exclusion) settled successfully (Berlin 2018); maximalist-version requests (women-only days, locker-room exclusion, lifeguard-rotation control) pressed and rolled back (Bremerhaven 2019). Friction concentrates at three edges: foot-washing-station political reception (substantive issue: the accommodation; framing issue: Muslim visibility), the female-only-hour pool debate (substantive issue: operational arrangement; framing issue: equality vs. modesty), and the dog-saliva ruling (where Khaled Abou El Fadl's Maliki-Andalusian textual scholarship and Aasim Padela's Islamic-medical-ethics framework provide internal jurisprudential resources for accommodation that the operative request often does not invoke). The accommodation framework is mostly working; the friction is mostly operational; the harder questions on this index are at the gender-equality, family-law, and same-sex-conduct fault lines where doctrinal positions and constitutional principles directly collide.

Open questions for Phase-2 deep dive

  • The gender-segregated higher-education question. The 2013–2014 UK university controversy settled for the equality baseline; Phase 2 should examine whether it has resurfaced and how that baseline has held.
  • Police service-dog operational data. How often does the cultural-religious-sensitivity accommodation apply, and at what cost to investigation effectiveness? Phase 2 should locate any actual empirical study.
