CSE base rates: read the denominator first / 基準率・分母を読む

"Most child-abuse offenders are white British" is true, and on its own it is nearly empty. England and Wales is about 82% white; a white majority of offenders is arithmetic, not propensity. The sentence that carries information is a rate: a group's share of offenders divided by its share of the population. This page does that division. It shows where the disproportion vanishes, and the one place it does not.

methodology · base rates · Phase 12026-06-17
Most offenders everywhere belong to the majority; that is a fact about who lives in the country, not about who offends. Only a per-capita rate says otherwise. Divide all child sexual abuse by the population and the disproportion is gone; divide group-based exploitation in the few places it is recorded properly, and it is not.

Why this page exists

Two answers to the same question, the panic and its rebuttal, fail in the same place: neither divides by the denominator.

The grooming-gangs debate has a stock rebuttal. Confronted with Rotherham, Rochdale, Telford, a common reply is that there are just as many white offenders, so the ethnic pattern is a myth; the court and prosecution data do show roughly 85 to 90% of recorded child-abuse defendants are white. The reply is arithmetically fine and analytically empty. England and Wales is about 82% white (2021 Census); a white majority of offenders is what pure composition predicts before any propensity enters. "Most offenders are white" describes who lives here, not who offends.

The panic and the rebuttal both skip the same step. Each quotes a raw count and stops, one to indict a community, the other to acquit it, and neither divides the count by the group's share of the population. Do the division and the picture splits in two. Across all child sexual abuse the white share is base-rate-explained; the disproportion vanishes. In the narrow subset of group-based exploitation, in the few forces that record it well, it does not: Greater Manchester Police found 52 to 54% of group-based CSE suspects of Asian heritage against a 20.9% Asian local population, after the base-rate correction. The aggregate finding and the subset finding are about two different phenomena, and both survive.

Method. Three categories are held apart and never swapped: all child sexual abuse, child sexual exploitation, and group-based "grooming-gang" exploitation. Every number is divided by the population base rate before it is read, because a raw count of offenders mostly reproduces who lives in the country. Both findings are carried without a thumb on the scale; ethnicity is unrecorded for roughly two-thirds of group-based perpetrators nationally (Home Office 2020; Casey 2025; IICSA), and every proportion here describes recorded or reported cases, not all abuse.

The one distinction everything rests on

Three things are constantly swapped: all child sexual abuse, child sexual exploitation, and group-based "grooming gang" CSE. They are nested, one inside the next, and they sit at opposite ends of the same funnel. A figure for one is not a figure for another; most public argument about grooming gangs runs on quietly substituting one for another.

(a)

All child sexual abuse

Every form of sexual abuse of under-18s: intra-familial, online, lone opportunistic, institutional, peer-on-peer, with CSE sitting inside it. The widest box. Prevalence dwarfs anything the police record; about 500,000 children a year experience sexual abuse of some kind, an estimate, not a crime count.

~500,000 children/year (prevalence) · Casey 2025, p.6
(b)

Child sexual exploitation

The subset of CSA involving exchange or coercion: status, gifts, drugs, money. In police data CSE is a flag laid on a CSA offence, not a standalone crime. Roughly 15% of contact CSA carries it.

Quote an ethnicity finding about (c) as though it answered (a), or read the aggregate of (a) back onto (c), and you have committed the single error this page exists to stop.

One caveat sits above every number below. CSA is largely a hidden crime; the CSA Centre puts it at roughly 1 in 10 children, and Casey notes that for the vast majority "their abuse is not identified," with only about 7% of victims telling police at the time. Every proportion on this page therefore describes recorded, reported, or surveyed cases, never all abuse.

The base-rate principle

In any country the majority population commits the majority of most crimes, automatically, by composition. "Most offenders are [majority group]" is a fact about who lives there, not about how any group behaves; it describes the denominator, never the numerator.

Only one number carries propensity information: a group's per-capita rate, its share of offenders divided by its share of the population. Until you do that division, you have said nothing about whether any group offends more or less than expected.

England and Wales was 81.7% white at the 2021 Census, so a white plurality of offenders in almost any common offence is near-automatic before propensity enters at all. The converse holds with equal force: a group can be a small share of all offenders, a small count drawn from a small denominator, and still be over-represented in a specific subset, where its rate runs above its population share. Both can be true at once; the raw share and the rate answer different questions.

The denominator: who lives here, over time

A white plurality of offenders is mostly the population reappearing in the offender column. To see whether any group offends more or less than expected, you first need the share a pure random draw would give you; that share is not fixed. It falls decade on decade, and it falls further for the young.

So the base-rate expectation moves with the era you are asking about. A random draw in the early 1990s predicts ~94 to 95% white offenders, a single White category, Great Britain. By 2021 it predicts 74.4% White British. For a young cohort it predicts lower still: state-school pupils are ~60.3% White British (DfE, 2024/25), because the white group has the oldest age structure of any (2021 Census median age 43) and offending concentrates in younger bands.

Group-based CSE suspects skew young, so the 74.4% population stock figure overstates the White-British share you would expect among recent young offenders; for a young-cohort comparison the right denominator is the ~60.3% school-age figure, not 74.4%.

Two flags travel with the early columns and must stay attached. 1981 has no measured figure at all; the census carried no ethnic-group question until 1991, so any pre-1991 minority count is an estimate from country-of-birth, not a count. And the 1991 figure is Great Britain, not England and Wales (England alone was 93.8% white); the geography differs from every later row, though the direction holds while the value barely moves.

What "85% white" actually measures

The headline number is real, solid, and narrower than how it travels. It describes one category only: all child sexual abuse, and within that, the prosecuted defendants whose ethnicity a court bothered to record. Three caveats ride with it everywhere it goes.

"~85 to 90% white" is a finding about all CSA, recorded defendants only; it tracks the population almost exactly, so it carries no propensity information; and it is not a measured figure for group-based grooming-gang CSE. Carrying it across that line is the error this page exists to stop.

The rounder "~85 to 88%" reaches the same place by a different route. Full Fact, working CPS data on child-abuse-flagged sex-offence cases for 2015/16 (~6,200 defendants), records white 67%, Asian 4%, Black 3%, not recorded 24%; strip the 24% unrecorded and ~88% of the remainder is white (67 of 76). The "~85 to 88%" only appears once the unknowns leave the denominator. A 2017 cross-check on MoJ data points the same way: 11% of recorded-ethnicity CSA defendants were from minority-ethnic backgrounds against 14% in the 2011 Census, so if anything CSA defendants are slightly less minority-ethnic than the population, not more.

Caveat 1: the denominator

The 90% is of the 69% with ethnicity recorded, not of all ~9,215 CSA defendants that year. The 31% where ethnicity went unrecorded is silently dropped before the percentage is taken. Every version of the headline (90% in 2023, 92% in 2019/20, ~88% of recorded CPS 2015/16) is a recorded-only figure; the unknowns are never small.

Caveat 2: who is caught, not who offends

The CSA Centre attributes part of the white majority to under-identification: CSA is under-reported in minority-ethnic communities, so the prosecuted population reflects who is caught and prosecuted, not necessarily who offends. The figure measures the output of the reporting-and-conviction pipeline, with all the pipeline's blind spots.

Caveat 3: it is not a grooming-gang number

This is a measurement of all CSA. It is not a measured figure for group-based CSE, where ethnicity is unrecorded for roughly two-thirds of perpetrators nationally (Home Office 2020; Casey 2025; IICSA). Lifting "85% white" out of the all-CSA box and dropping it into the grooming-gang box answers a question the data never asked.

Who actually abuses children

The abuser is almost always someone the child already knows. Most recorded abuse happens inside the family environment or online, by a lone adult, increasingly by another child. Group-based exploitation is the rare exception on every axis at once; that is exactly what makes it distinctive, and why a figure about it cannot be read off the bulk.

Stranger abuse is the minority everywhere it is measured. The dominant pattern is a single perpetrator the child trusts, inside the home; group-based CSE inverts almost every feature of that pattern, which is the point.

NSPCC / Radford 2011, drawing on 6,196 UK interviews, found over 90% of children who suffered contact sexual abuse were abused by someone they knew; roughly 1 in 10 by a stranger. The Crime Survey for England and Wales, year ending March 2019, asks adults abused before 16 and lets them tick more than one perpetrator, so its categories overlap and do not sum to 100: friend or acquaintance 37%, stranger 30%, a family member somewhere between a quarter and a third. The two surveys use different methods and define "stranger" differently, so the 30% and the 10% are not a clean contradiction; both land on the same finding. Known perpetrators dominate, and no tidy three-way split exists.

The National Police Chiefs' Council put it at scale in 2023: abuse in the family environment is almost half of all CSA reported to police in England and Wales. The other shift is the screen. Online offending is now about 40% of recorded sexual-abuse crime; of roughly 100,000 CSA and CSE offences in 2024, about 60% were contact and 40% online (Casey 2025). That growth, with more child-on-child offending, has pulled the recorded offender age down: VKPP 2023 gives an average CSAE offender age of 24, and in the COCAD 2023 data 57% of suspects were under 18.

Against that bulk, group-based "grooming-gang" CSE is small in the recorded numbers. The funnel ends at roughly 700 recorded group-based-CSE offences in 2023, well under 1% of recorded CSA, and the data-owners call even that a snapshot undercount.

Rare in count does not mean indistinct in kind. Where group-based CSE is recorded, it differs from the dominant intra-familial and online patterns on several axes at once. These features, not the headcount, are what make it a separate phenomenon.

Multiple offenders

Connected men, not one trusted adult

Two or more offenders acting together, with what the Home Office calls a "disinhibiting effect" of the group. This is the inverse of the overwhelmingly single-perpetrator familial abuse that makes up most recorded CSA.

Distinctive MO

The "boyfriend" model and peer recruitment

A recognisable method: the "boyfriend" hook, parties with drugs and alcohol to lower resistance, victims passed between men, and coerced into introducing peers so the abuse self-propagates. None of this describes the lone familial abuser.

Shared background, victims "othered"

Groups described as "broadly homogenous in age, ethnic background and socioeconomic status," with misogyny and an "othering" of victims that lets perpetrators disregard them. This shared-background structure has no parallel in scattered intra-familial offending.

The recording trap, by design. Ethnicity is poorly recorded for CSA generally; national police data captures perpetrator ethnicity for only about 31% (VKPP) to 34% (COCAD) of suspects, so none of the relationship base rates above carry a reliable ethnicity breakdown. The largest CSA volumes, intra-familial at about half and online at about 40%, have worse or absent ethnicity recording than group-based CSE. No comparison of ethnicity between abuse types is possible on national data; anyone offering one is filling the gap, not reading it. IICSA §B.5; VKPP 2023; Casey (2025).

The Pakistan mirror / パキスタンの鏡

Run the same sentence in a country that is almost one group and its emptiness shows on its face. This is logic, not a claim about Pakistan; the country is the cleanest available demonstration of what a denominator does to a count.

Pakistan's foreign-born population is about 1.5% (UN/IOM, 2019), so roughly 98 to 99% of everyone there is a Pakistani national; it follows by arithmetic that about 98 to 99% of any category of offender there is Pakistani, child abusers included, and that sentence carries zero information about how anyone behaves.

Pakistan's international migrant stock was roughly 3.26 million in 2019, about 1.5% of the population on the UN/IOM count, the band robust across UN DESA, IOM, and the World Bank even where the exact per-year decimal does not resolve; that stock is overwhelmingly Afghan refugees, around 1.3 to 1.5 million registered with UNHCR. Near-homogeneity does the rest. When 98 to 99% of a country is one group, 98 to 99% of its abusers belong to that group automatically, the same way a near-monopoly of plumbers, teachers, or bus drivers would. "Most UK CSE offenders are white British" is the same sentence wearing different clothes; the roughly 74 to 82% white population reappearing in the offender column. Neither sentence proves over-representation, and neither rules it out. Only a per-capita rate does that.

What is known of Pakistani child sexual abuse comes mainly from Sahil, an Islamabad NGO whose Cruel Numbers compiles press-reported cases; a floor, not a prevalence rate. The 2023 edition records 4,213 cases, about 11 children a day, 53% girls and 47% boys, with perpetrators most often acquaintances or relatives. That is the same known-perpetrator pattern the UK data shows: abuse by someone the child knows, not by gangs of strangers.

Flag · missing by design

Pakistan keeps no perpetrator-ethnicity statistic, and has no reason to

In a country that is about 99% one group, "ethnicity of offender" is as empty as "nationality of offender"; the answer is fixed before any crime is counted. So the variable is simply not collected. The lesson runs back to the UK: where ethnicity recording is poor or absent, a recorded share is a recording artefact, not a propensity.

Pakistan and Denmark are the same axis from opposite ends. Pakistan makes raw counts self-evidently meaningless by homogeneity; nearly everyone is one group, so every count is that group, and no division is even possible. Denmark makes rate differences visible by standardising; it publishes the ancestry-by-crime cross-tab and an age- and socio-economically adjusted criminality index, doing the per-capita division that raw counts hide. Same principle, two extremes; one country where the denominator swallows everything, one where it is held to the light.

Where this points. The Pakistan mirror is a thought experiment with real numbers, not evidence about any group's propensity. It exists to make one habit automatic: before reading a "most offenders are X" sentence as information, ask what share of the population is already X. For the same question run on a country that does publish the rate, see Denmark · Honest Data.

Two findings, two phenomena, both true

The whole discipline is holding two true things at once: the aggregate is base-rate-explained, and the group-based subset is a separate, real over-representation. Drop either half and you are lying by omission.

Both true at once

The aggregate and the subset answer different questions. "Most child abusers in Britain are white" and "grooming-gang suspects are disproportionately Asian" are not in conflict; they describe two different rows of the funnel. Holding both, without letting either erase the other, is the entire point.

Half one: the aggregate is base-rate-explained

Across all CSA (a), "most offenders are white" is true.

And uninformative on its own. With England & Wales ~82% White (2021), it is mostly the population reappearing in the offender column; the same structural fact as "~99% of Pakistan's abusers are Pakistani." A fact about who lives there, not about how anyone behaves.

The CSA-wide data confirms no over-representation.

The CSA Centre's 2017 cross-check: 11% of recorded-ethnicity CSA defendants were BAME, against 14% BAME in the 2011 Census. CSA-in-general tracks the population; no minority is over-represented in the aggregate.

Casey states the aggregate plainly.

"If you look at the data for child abuse, it is not disproportionate, it is white men." (Casey 2025.) Half the sentence. The other half is below, in the same breath.

Half two: the group-based subset is a separate, real signal

Because the aggregate is base-rate-explained, it cannot answer the question about group-based CSE.

A figure for (a) is not a figure for (c). The aggregate dissolves into the denominator precisely so it can say nothing about the small, distinct subset nested inside it; you have to measure (c) directly.

The Home Office (2020) reached a two-part conclusion, not one.

Para 81 is an inference, not a measurement: with the group-based data unusable, it judged offender ethnicity "most likely... in line with CSA more generally... with the majority of offenders being White." Para 17 simultaneously recorded that studies "indicated an over-representation of Asian and Black offenders in group-based CSE," that the high-profile cases mainly involved men of Pakistani ethnicity, and that likely "no one community or culture is uniquely predisposed to offending." Both halves, one document.

Casey's matching line, same breath as the first.

"If you look at the data on child sexual exploitation, suspects and offenders, it is disproportionately Asian heritage." (Casey 2025.) Paired with the white-men line, this is the whole synthesis in two sentences.

One force settles the apparent contradiction. The over-representation is real where Greater Manchester records it well, and it is specific to the group-based subset; the same force, across all 781 of its CSA cases, tracks its own population. Two phenomena, visible side by side.

Then the honesty number, in the other direction. Nationally, ethnicity is unrecorded for roughly two-thirds of group-based perpetrators; put the missing data back into the denominator and the published "88% white" inverts to only ~28% recorded white (Casey 2025). National data cannot conclude in either direction, which is exactly why recording offender ethnicity in CSA cases is being made a statutory requirement. The local picture is strong; the national one is too gap-ridden to carry any claim, for or against.

The aggregate finding and the subset finding are about two different phenomena, and both are true: across all child abuse the offender profile is base-rate-explained and broadly white, while in the group-based subset, where it can be measured, Asian and Pakistani-heritage men are over-represented even after base-rate correction. The full cross-examination of the group-based cases sits on the UK Grooming Gangs · Cross-Examined page.

Where the data is missing, and other caveats

Every proportion on this page describes recorded, reported, or surveyed cases, not all abuse. Across the underlying data, perpetrator ethnicity is routinely absent. The honest reading is neither "we know it is white men" nor "we know it is one group"; it is that for two-thirds of group-based perpetrators nationally the ethnicity column is blank, and that the aggregate base-rate finding and the local subset signal both survive that gap.

Prevalence is not recorded crime

Casey notes only about 7% of victims tell police at the time, and that for the vast majority their abuse is never identified. So each percentage describes who is caught, not who offends: the seven-in-a-hundred whose case enters the data, not the hundred.

Two key figures sit in image-only PDFs

The CSA Centre's headline shares (90% white of recorded 2023 defendants; 92% in 2019/20) come from documents that are image-based and did not text-extract in our pass. They are internally consistent and match the centre's public framing; treat the sub-rows as primary-but-unverified-in-pass rather than independently re-rendered.

The ~700 group-based figure is an admitted undercount

About 700 recorded group-based-CSE offences in 2023 is a manual quarterly snapshot across 43 forces with inconsistent systems. The data-owners say so: Casey calls it "highly unlikely that this accurately reflects the true scale" and "may be an undercount"; IICSA earlier logged over 70 live investigations with returns incomplete. The true number is higher than 700, not lower.

Casey rejects certainty in both directions

The audit refuses two moves at once: using flawed national data to dismiss the over-representation claim, and asserting unevidenced certainty about it. Its verified wording is the milder "it is not possible to draw any conclusions on the representation of ethnic groups" from the published national set, paired with the reconfigured 28%-recorded-white figure; not the "at best misleading" phrase that circulates in secondary press, which is not the audit's own. As of 2025, recording offender ethnicity in CSA cases is being made a statutory requirement, precisely because the gap was this wide. IICSA B.5 reaches the same verdict: poor or non-existent data collection makes it impossible to know whether any group is over-represented.
