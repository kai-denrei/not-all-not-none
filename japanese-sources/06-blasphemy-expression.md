# Fault line 06 — Blasphemy and free expression

## Framing in Japanese context

Japan has no blasphemy law and has not had a domestic blasphemy controversy. The Japanese debate has been driven entirely by imported flashpoints: the 1989 Rushdie *fatwā* (and the murder of his Japanese translator, Igarashi Hitoshi 五十嵐一, July 11, 1991, at Tsukuba University), the 2005 Danish cartoons, and the January 7, 2015 Charlie Hebdo attack. The Igarashi murder is the single most domestically resonant moment — an unsolved murder on Japanese soil, on a Japanese university campus, of a Japanese academic, over a translation. It is the ground-truth Japanese case for this fault line.

## Sources

### 五十嵐一事件 (Igarashi Hitoshi case) — primary press record
- Publishers: Asahi Shimbun, Mainichi Shimbun, Yomiuri Shimbun, Sankei.
- Date: July 11–13, 1991, with anniversary retrospectives.
- URL: paywalled archives 朝日新聞デジタル / 聞蔵 Kikuzo II / Yomidasu Rekishikan.
- Summary: Igarashi, an Islamic-studies scholar at Tsukuba University and translator of *The Satanic Verses* into Japanese (Shinsensha 新泉社, 1990), was found stabbed to death on July 11, 1991. The murder is unsolved. The link to the Khomeini *fatwā* is universally assumed but never legally established.
- Relevance: The single most resonant Japanese-soil case for the blasphemy fault line. Anchor citation.

## Japan-specific cases

- **Igarashi Hitoshi murder, July 11, 1991**, Tsukuba University. Unsolved. The Igarashi case is the single concrete Japanese-soil incident on this fault line and should anchor any Japanese version of page 06.
- **Salman Rushdie's Japanese publisher Shinsensha** (新泉社) continued publication of the Japanese *Akuma no shi* (悪魔の詩) translation despite the threat — itself a free-expression case study.
