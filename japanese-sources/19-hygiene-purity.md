# Fault line 19 — Hygiene and ritual purity

## Framing in Japanese context

Japan has its own deep ritual-purity tradition (Shintō *kegare* and *misogi*, Buddhist *shōjin* dietary rules) and a high-cleanliness public-hygiene culture. This makes Islamic *ṭahāra* practice (wudū before prayer, ghusl, post-toilet washing with water) less culturally legible at the *theological* level but more easily *practically* accommodated than in Europe — Japanese public toilets have included washlet/bidet functions for decades, and prayer-room wudū facilities at airports and shopping malls (post-2010 halal-tourism push) are uncontroversial. There is essentially no Japanese-language scholarly treatment of Islamic ritual purity as a fault line.

## Japan-specific cases

- **Airport and shopping-mall wudū facilities** as a halal-tourism subgenre: Narita Terminal 2, Haneda, Kansai International all have prayer-and-wudū facilities; Tokyo Midtown, AEON Mall sites and others followed. JNTO directories.
- **Mosque washroom design**: Tokyo Camii, Otsuka, Kobe Mosque all maintain wudū-area washrooms.
