# Fault line 01 — Political authority: caliphate vs. constitutional rule

## Framing in Japanese context

Japan has no live debate about Islamic political authority on its own soil; the question is academic. But Japanese Islam-studies has an unusually deep treatment of the caliphate question, going back to Itagaki Yūzō's generation and synthesized in Kosugi Yasushi's *Gendai Isurāmu sekai-ron*. The 2014 ISIS caliphate declaration sharpened Japanese-language coverage; Ikeuchi Satoshi's *Isurāmu-koku no shōgeki* (2015) became the standard Japanese reading on the caliphate-as-political-project. Nakata Kō's writings — controversial because of his role as informal interlocutor during the 2015 hostage crisis — are also primary reading, since he is the rare Japanese academic who has argued for the legitimacy of a caliphate.

### 現代イスラーム世界論 (*Gendai Isurāmu sekai-ron*)
- Author/Publisher: Kosugi Yasushi (小杉泰), Nagoya University Press.

## Japan-specific cases

- **2015 hostage crisis as caliphate-question moment**: the Yukawa/Goto crisis forced Japanese television to explain *what the Islamic State claimed to be*. The resulting popular-press explainers (NHK Close-up Gendai, Asahi Globe) constitute a primary record of the Japanese reading public's first sustained encounter with the caliphate concept. (See page 07 for the security/violence frame.)
- **Nakata Kō's role as interlocutor** during the 2015 hostage crisis is a domestic case study in academic-state relations under the caliphate question.

## Gaps

- Outside the ISIS-caliphate moment, Japanese-language treatment of constitutional Islam (Tunisia 2014, Indonesia, Malaysian constitutional debates) is thinner. JIME and *Ajia keizai* cover it episodically; there is no Japanese-language monograph synthesizing the constitutional-Islam debate. **Phase 2: revisit.**
