# Fault line 02 — Religion and state: separation vs. integration

## Framing in Japanese context

Japan's church-state baseline is Article 20 (政教分離, *seikyō bunri*, separation of religion and state) of the 1947 Constitution. The Japanese debate around religion-and-state is dominated by Shintō (the Yasukuni question, the 2001 Ehime Tamagushi-ryō decision) and by new-religious-movement controversies (Aum Shinrikyō, the post-2022 Unification Church reckoning), not by Islam. But the Japanese constitutional-law literature on *seikyō bunri* is the lens through which Japanese Islam-studies frames mosque-state relations, halal certification, and school accommodation.

## Japan-specific cases

- **Religious-corporation (宗教法人) status for mosques**: most Japanese mosques operate as 宗教法人 under the Religious Corporations Act (1951). Tanada documents the spread.
- **Yasukuni and *seikyō bunri* doctrine**: while not Islam-related, the doctrinal frame (Tsu *jichinsai* 1977; Ehime Tamagushi-ryō 1997; Sora-Yasukuni 2010) is what would govern any future Islam-related accommodation case.
- **Halal certification and the state**: Japan does *not* have a state halal authority (unlike Malaysia's JAKIM); certification is handled by private bodies (Japan Halal Association, Muslim Professional Japan Association, etc.). This is itself a religion-and-state position. METI white papers acknowledge the gap.
