# Fault line 20 — Music, image, depiction

## Framing in Japanese context

The Japanese-language literature on Islamic visual and aural culture is led by aesthetics-and-architecture scholars (Tonaga Yasushi at Kyoto, Hatano Kumiko / 羽田正's broader Islamic-history-of-art writings) rather than by values-politics scholars. The fault-line *political* dimension — the prohibition of figurative depiction of the Prophet, music-banning by certain Salafi authorities, the Bamiyan Buddha destruction (2001), the Mosul museum destruction (2015) — appears in Japanese coverage of those incidents but not as a sustained values-debate.

## Japan-specific cases

- **Japanese conservation work at Bamiyan post-2001**: Japan funded major UNESCO restoration efforts at Bamiyan; documented in MOFA and JICA reports. The Taliban-2021 return and subsequent statements gave the case renewed Japanese press attention.
- **Tokyo Camii's calligraphic interior**: a domestic example of the *figurative-image-avoidance* aesthetic in a Japanese Islamic religious-architectural context.
