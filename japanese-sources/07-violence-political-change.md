# Fault line 07 — Violence as instrument of political change

## Framing in Japanese context

This is the Japan-Islam fault line with the deepest domestic primary record. The January 2015 ISIS hostage murders of Yukawa Haruna (湯川遥菜) and Goto Kenji (後藤健二) are the most-documented Japan-Islam-extremism flashpoint and the moment when "Islamic State" entered the Japanese household vocabulary. Earlier reference points are the 1977 Dacca hijacking (in which the JRA — Nihon Sekigun, with no Islamist program — exchanged hostages for prisoners with Japanese-government cooperation, an action that reshaped Japan's terrorism doctrine), the 2002–2004 Iraq hostage crises, and the 2013 In Amenas (Algeria) gas-plant attack in which ten Japanese JGC employees were killed. Each shaped the Japanese debate on jihadi violence and on whether Japan can be neutral in the global counter-terrorism architecture.

## Japan-specific cases

- **The 2015 ISIS hostage murders** — January 20 / January 24 (Yukawa) / January 31 / February 1 (Goto), 2015. Anchor case. The video releases on the ISIS Twitter and on the *al-Furqān* media wing channel were the first time the modern Japanese reading public saw Islamist execution video framed by their own government's name.
- **The 2013 In Amenas (Algeria) gas-plant attack**, January 16–19, 2013. Ten JGC (日揮) employees were killed. AQIM-aligned militants. The Japanese government-corporate response (negotiations through Algerian and Japanese channels) is documented in the Cabinet Office post-incident review.
- **The 2002–2004 Iraq hostage crises** (Imai/Takato/Kōriyama April 2004; Kōda Shōsei October 2004). Set the *jiko sekinin* (自己責任) frame in which the 2015 hostage discussion played out.
- **The 1977 Dacca hijacking** (Japan Airlines Flight 472, September 28, 1977). JRA hijackers exchanged hostages for prisoners; Prime Minister Fukuda's "human life is heavier than the Earth" decision (人命は地球より重い) is foundational to Japanese hostage-policy memory. Not Islamist but central to Japanese terrorism doctrine.
- **The 2017 共謀罪 conspiracy law** (organized-crime treaty implementation, *Kyōbōzai*). Asahi and *Sekai* (Iwanami) editorial opposition; passed June 2017. The post-Charlie / post-Yukawa-Goto context shaped the debate.
