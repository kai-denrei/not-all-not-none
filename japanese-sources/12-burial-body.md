# Fault line 12 — Burial and the body

## Framing in Japanese context

This is one of the densest Japan-Islam fault lines in the catalog because Japan is functionally a cremation society (over 99.9% of Japanese deaths are followed by cremation), and Islamic *janāza* practice mandates earth burial. Japan has roughly 7–8 cemeteries permitting earth burial nationwide; only one (the Yamanashi *Isuramu Bochi*, opened 1980s by the Japan Muslim Association) is specifically Islamic. The **Beppu (Ōita prefecture) cemetery dispute** and the parallel **Hokuto (Yamanashi prefecture) controversy** are the two most-documented domestic flashpoints — both pitting Muslim community plans for new earth-burial cemeteries against neighborhood opposition citing groundwater concerns. Both received NHK, Asahi, and Mainichi coverage from 2018 forward, with the Beppu case continuing through the early 2020s.

## Japan-specific cases

- **The Yamanashi Muslim Cemetery** (Hokuto City, Yamanashi), opened 1980s by the Japan Muslim Association, is Japan's only purpose-Islamic earth-burial cemetery. Its near-capacity state from the 2010s drove the expansion debates.
- **The Beppu / Hiji-machi cemetery dispute** (Ōita, 2018–) is the most-documented case, generating sustained press coverage. Anchor citation.
- **The Hokuto cemetery expansion controversy** (Yamanashi).
- **Repatriation of remains as default practice** for many foreign-national Muslims who die in Japan — itself a domestic-policy reality documented in Tanada's work.
